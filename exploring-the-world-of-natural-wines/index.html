Exploring the World of Natural Wines


Wine has been an integral part of human culture for thousands of years. Whether it’s enjoyed at a casual dinner, a festive celebration, or as part of a fine dining experience, wine plays an important role in our social rituals. Yet, in recent years, a new movement within the wine industry has emerged — the rise of natural wines. This growing trend is more than just a passing fad; it’s a return to the roots of winemaking, embracing traditional methods while rejecting the modern practices that dominate the global wine industry today.

In this blog post, we will dive deep into the world of natural wines, exploring what makes them unique, the principles behind their production, and how they are reshaping the landscape of wine culture. We’ll discuss the key differences between natural and conventional wines, the benefits of choosing natural options, and how you can start incorporating these wines into your own drinking experience.

What Are Natural Wines?

Natural wine is essentially wine made with minimal intervention — in both the vineyard and the winery. The concept centers around working with nature rather than against it. This means using organic or biodynamic farming methods, which avoid synthetic pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers, while focusing on soil health and biodiversity. In the winery, natural winemakers typically forgo additives like sulfites, yeast, and other chemicals used to stabilize and preserve wine in traditional winemaking processes.

To produce a natural wine, the winemaker’s goal is to let the grapes and the environment express themselves in the final product, with as little human interference as possible. Natural wine is often created using spontaneous fermentation, where the wild yeasts naturally present on the grape skins are allowed to ferment the wine, instead of using cultivated commercial yeast strains.

This philosophy stands in stark contrast to the practices of conventional winemaking, which relies heavily on technology, additives, and mechanical methods to control the wine’s flavor, texture, and aging process. Natural wines, however, aim for authenticity and uniqueness, showcasing the true character of the grape variety and the terroir (the environment in which the grapes are grown).

The Characteristics of Natural Wines

Natural wines are distinct from their conventional counterparts in many ways, with their appearance, aroma, flavor profile, and texture often varying significantly. One of the most noticeable features of natural wines is their unpredictability. Since these wines are not manipulated with additives, each bottle can be a unique expression of the vintage, the terroir, and the winemaker’s philosophy. This can lead to a wide range of flavors, from funky and earthy to bright and fresh.

Appearance

Natural wines may appear cloudy or hazy, which is a result of minimal filtration and the lack of fining agents. Fining is a process used in traditional winemaking to clarify the wine and remove any suspended particles, but natural winemakers choose not to use it, allowing the wine to retain more of its natural texture and character. While some people may find the cloudy appearance unusual, it’s a sign of the wine’s authenticity and purity.

Aroma and Flavor

The aromas and flavors of natural wines can be quite diverse. They tend to have more complex and nuanced aromas compared to conventional wines. You might notice earthy or floral notes, hints of barnyard or funk, and a sense of freshness that reflects the natural fermentation process. The flavors can range from bright and zesty to deep and savory, often with more pronounced acidity and a rustic quality. Natural wines are also known for their lower sulfite content, which can result in a softer, more delicate profile compared to wines with higher levels of sulfites.

Texture and Mouthfeel

Natural wines often have a distinct mouthfeel. The absence of additives and minimal intervention in the winemaking process can create a wine with more texture and body. Some natural wines may be slightly effervescent, while others may have a more tannic or grippy feel, especially reds. The acidity in natural wines can also be more pronounced, which contributes to their vibrant, fresh character.

The Winemaking Process: Organic, Biodynamic, and Minimal Intervention

The process of creating natural wines is grounded in a philosophy of sustainability, respect for nature, and minimal intervention. Let’s explore the key components of this process: organic farming, biodynamic farming, and minimal intervention winemaking.

Organic Farming

Organic farming is the cornerstone of natural wine production. It involves cultivating grapes without the use of synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or chemical fertilizers. Instead, organic vineyards rely on natural methods to promote soil health, control pests, and manage disease. Cover crops, composting, and crop rotation are commonly used to improve soil fertility, while natural predators and beneficial insects are encouraged to keep harmful pests in check.

Organic viticulture focuses on creating a balanced ecosystem within the vineyard. This can lead to healthier grapes, improved biodiversity, and ultimately, better wine. Many natural wine producers also take a hands-off approach to irrigation, relying on natural rainfall and the health of the soil to nourish the vines.

Biodynamic Farming

Biodynamic farming is a more holistic approach that takes organic principles a step further. It’s based on the teachings of Rudolf Steiner and views the vineyard as a self-sustaining organism that must work in harmony with the surrounding environment. Biodynamic practices incorporate organic farming methods but also include the use of preparations made from herbs, minerals, and animal manures, which are believed to enhance soil fertility and promote plant health.

One of the most unique aspects of biodynamic farming is the use of the lunar calendar to determine the best times for planting, pruning, harvesting, and other vineyard activities. The phases of the moon are thought to influence the growth cycle of plants, and biodynamic farmers use this knowledge to optimize the vineyard’s productivity and vitality.

Minimal Intervention Winemaking

Once the grapes are harvested, natural winemakers take a minimal intervention approach in the winery. This means they avoid the use of additives such as commercial yeasts, enzymes, and fining agents, which are often used to manipulate the wine’s flavor, clarity, and stability in conventional winemaking.

Instead, natural winemakers rely on wild fermentation, allowing the natural yeasts present on the grape skins to ferment the wine. This can result in more unpredictable fermentation processes, but it also leads to more authentic and expressive wines. In some cases, winemakers may add a small amount of sulfites at bottling to preserve the wine, but the levels are much lower than in conventional wines.

Why Choose Natural Wines?

As natural wines have gained popularity, many wine drinkers have found themselves drawn to the unique qualities and ethical principles behind these wines. Below are a few reasons why you might want to consider exploring the world of natural wines.

Better for the Environment

One of the main appeals of natural wines is their environmentally friendly production methods. Organic and biodynamic farming practices promote biodiversity, improve soil health, and reduce the use of harmful chemicals that can harm the environment. By choosing natural wines, you’re supporting sustainable farming practices that prioritize the long-term health of the land.

Healthier for You

Many people turn to natural wines for health reasons. Conventional wines can contain a range of additives, preservatives, and sulfites that some individuals are sensitive to. Natural wines, on the other hand, are free from synthetic chemicals and are often lower in sulfites, which can help reduce headaches and allergic reactions. Although natural wines are not necessarily “health drinks,” they do offer a more pure and authentic product, with fewer artificial ingredients.

Unique Flavor Profiles

Natural wines are often described as being more complex and expressive than their conventional counterparts. Because they are less manipulated in the cellar, natural wines reflect the true character of the grapes and the terroir. This can lead to a wide range of flavors, textures, and aromas that make each bottle a unique experience. If you’re someone who enjoys discovering new and interesting wines, natural wines provide endless opportunities for exploration.

Supporting Small, Independent Producers

Many natural winemakers are small, independent producers who prioritize quality over quantity. By choosing natural wines, you’re often supporting small-scale operations that are committed to producing wines with integrity. These winemakers tend to focus on sustainable practices, craftsmanship, and personal expression, rather than mass production and profit.

How to Start Exploring Natural Wines

If you’re new to natural wines, it can be overwhelming to know where to start. But fear not — here are some tips for diving into the world of natural wines:

  1. Visit a Natural Wine Shop or Bar: Many wine shops and bars now specialize in natural wines, so you can taste a variety of wines before committing to a full bottle. Staff at these establishments are usually knowledgeable and can guide you through the selection process.

  2. Start with Lighter Wines: Natural wines can range from light and crisp to bold and full-bodied. If you’re just starting out, it’s a good idea to begin with a light, refreshing white or rosé, which tends to be more approachable.

  3. Seek Out Recommendations: Natural wines can be a bit intimidating due to the wide variety of styles and flavors. Ask for recommendations from friends, sommeliers, or natural wine experts to help you navigate the world of natural wines.

  4. Experiment with Different Regions and Varieties: The world of natural wine is diverse, and different regions and grape varieties produce distinct flavors. Don’t be afraid to try wines from various parts of the world to broaden your understanding and find what you enjoy.

  5. Embrace the Funk: Natural wines are known for their “funk” — earthy, barnyard, and sometimes funky aromas that reflect the wild fermentation process. Don’t be put off by these unique characteristics; instead, embrace them as part of the charm of natural wine.

Conclusion

Natural wines offer a fascinating journey into the world of wine, inviting drinkers to embrace authenticity, sustainability, and the raw beauty of the land. Whether you’re drawn to the environmental benefits, the unique flavor profiles, or the sense of adventure, exploring natural wines can open up a whole new world of discovery. By supporting natural winemakers, you’re helping to preserve traditional winemaking practices and promoting a more sustainable future for the industry.

So, next time you raise a glass, consider choosing a natural wine. You might just find that the world of natural wines is richer, more diverse, and more rewarding than you ever imagined.
