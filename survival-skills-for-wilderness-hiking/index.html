Survival Skills for Wilderness Hiking


Wilderness hiking can be one of the most exhilarating and rewarding outdoor experiences. The fresh air, breathtaking views, and the challenge of navigating nature’s terrain create an adventure that is unparalleled. However, the wilderness can also be unforgiving if you’re not properly prepared. Whether you’re an experienced hiker or just starting out, having survival skills can make the difference between a successful, enjoyable trip and a potentially dangerous situation.

In this guide, we will cover essential survival skills for wilderness hiking, focusing on preparation, navigation, shelter building, fire making, and other critical techniques to help you stay safe and enjoy your adventure to the fullest.

1. Proper Preparation: The Foundation of Survival

Before you set out on your hike, the most important survival skill is preparation. Many hikers overlook this step, but knowing what to expect in terms of weather, terrain, and potential dangers is crucial. A well-prepared hiker is a safe hiker, and this starts with adequate planning.

Know the Terrain

Research the area where you’ll be hiking. Obtain a topographic map, study trail reviews, and familiarize yourself with the terrain. Understanding elevation changes, potential hazards like rivers or cliffs, and any seasonal challenges (like snow or flooding) will help you make better decisions while on the trail.

Packing the Essentials

When packing for a wilderness hike, make sure you bring everything you might need to survive for a longer period than initially planned. Include a backpack with essential items such as:

  • Water: Carry sufficient water, or bring a filter/purification tablets to source water from streams or lakes.
  • Food: Pack lightweight, non-perishable food that provides energy, such as trail mix, energy bars, or dehydrated meals.
  • First Aid Kit: Include bandages, antiseptics, tweezers, pain relief medications, and any personal medications.
  • Multi-tool: A reliable multi-tool can help with various tasks, from cutting rope to opening cans.
  • Fire Starter: Fire is critical for warmth, cooking, and signaling for help.
  • Emergency Shelter: A compact emergency shelter (like a bivy sack or tarp) can be a lifesaver if the weather turns or if you become lost.

Leave Your Itinerary with Someone

Let someone know your planned route and expected return time. This simple step can save your life if you go missing or encounter unexpected issues during your hike. If you don’t return as expected, rescuers will have a starting point for their search.

2. Navigation: Knowing Where You Are

Navigation is one of the most vital skills for wilderness hiking. Getting lost is a common concern for hikers, especially in dense forests or unfamiliar areas. Here’s how you can ensure you always know where you are:

Map and Compass

Despite the prevalence of GPS devices and smartphones, traditional navigation tools like maps and compasses are still essential. GPS batteries can die, and satellite signals can be unreliable in dense forests or deep canyons. Learning how to read a topographic map and use a compass can save you when technology fails.

Practice before your hike to become proficient at navigating with these tools. Key skills to learn include orienting your map to the terrain and determining your bearing with a compass.

GPS Devices and Apps

While a traditional map and compass should always be your first choice, modern GPS devices or smartphone apps with offline maps can provide an extra layer of confidence. Many apps allow you to download maps beforehand, so you can still track your location if you lose signal.

Trail Markers and Landmarks

Pay attention to trail markers, signs, and natural landmarks along your route. They can help you stay oriented and guide you back to the trail if you start to veer off course. Familiarize yourself with common trail marking systems in the area you’re hiking.

3. Building Shelter: Protection from the Elements

The wilderness can be unpredictable. Whether it’s an unexpected storm, dropping temperatures, or simply exhaustion, knowing how to build a shelter is one of the most essential survival skills for wilderness hiking.

Natural Shelters

If you find yourself in a bind, you can take advantage of natural structures. Large rocks, fallen trees, or caves can provide some shelter from the elements. Always ensure that these structures are safe and not prone to collapse or inhabited by wildlife.

Tarp Shelters

If you have a tarp or emergency shelter, use it to create a quick shelter. Set up a simple lean-to or A-frame shelter using trees, poles, or even rocks to anchor the tarp. Make sure the shelter is weatherproof, with an elevated floor to avoid ground moisture.

Debris Shelters

In a more extreme survival situation, you can use natural materials like branches, leaves, and grass to construct a debris shelter. A debris hut is excellent for insulation, but it requires time and effort to construct. Focus on building a structure that protects you from wind and rain, and insulate the interior with as much natural material as possible.

4. Making Fire: A Lifesaver in the Wilderness

Fire is one of the most important tools you have for survival. It provides warmth, allows you to cook food, purify water, and can signal for help. Mastering fire-making techniques is essential for any hiker.

Fire Starting Kits

A fire starter kit should be part of your essential hiking gear. Always bring waterproof matches, lighters, and a fire steel or flint. Pack tinder like cotton balls soaked in petroleum jelly or fire starter sticks. These can be lifesaving when conditions are wet or windy.

Building a Fire

To successfully build a fire in the wilderness, you need to gather three types of materials: tinder (light, easily ignitable material), kindling (small sticks), and fuel (larger logs or branches). Find dry wood, as wet wood can be difficult to ignite. Start by creating a small nest of tinder and kindling, then add larger pieces of wood as the fire grows.

If you’re struggling to ignite a fire due to wet conditions, consider using a firestarter like a magnesium block, which can create sparks even in damp weather.

5. Water Procurement and Purification

Water is a critical resource in the wilderness, and knowing how to find and purify it is crucial for survival. Carry enough water to last you through your hike, but always have a backup plan.

Finding Water Sources

Look for running water, such as streams or rivers. Avoid stagnant water sources like ponds or puddles, as they may contain harmful bacteria or parasites. When hiking in areas with limited natural water sources, carry extra water or bring a water filter to allow you to source water from available streams.

Purifying Water

You should never drink water directly from a source without purifying it. Boiling water for 1-3 minutes is the most reliable method of purification. If you can’t boil water, use water purification tablets or a portable water filter. These methods kill bacteria, viruses, and parasites that could make you sick.

6. Wildlife Awareness: Staying Safe Around Animals

The wilderness is home to many animals, some of which can pose a threat to hikers. Understanding how to stay safe and avoid conflicts with wildlife is an essential skill.

Bears and Large Carnivores

In regions with bears, it’s important to be aware of bear behavior and know how to store food safely. Use bear canisters or hang your food in a tree at least 10 feet off the ground and 4 feet away from the trunk. If you encounter a bear, remain calm and do not run. Slowly back away while maintaining eye contact.

Snakes and Insects

Be cautious around snakes and insects. Avoid stepping over logs or rocks without looking first, as snakes may be hiding underneath. Wear long pants and boots to protect yourself from bites. If bitten, stay calm and seek medical help immediately.

Insects and Other Pests

Insects like mosquitoes and ticks can also pose risks, particularly in the warmer months. Wear insect repellent, and check your body for ticks regularly, especially after walking through tall grass or wooded areas. If you’re in a high-risk area for Lyme disease or other tick-borne illnesses, take extra precautions.

7. Signaling for Help: Getting Noticed

If you’re lost or in danger, signaling for help is critical. Knowing how to attract attention and get rescued could save your life in an emergency situation.

Emergency Whistle

A loud, shrill whistle can attract attention from a long distance. Carry a whistle in a place that’s easily accessible, such as your pack or on a lanyard around your neck.

Signal Mirror

A signal mirror can reflect sunlight over long distances, making it highly effective in daylight conditions. It’s small, lightweight, and can be used to signal aircraft or rescuers.

Fire for Signaling

A large, smoky fire can also be used to signal for help. If you’re near a road or trail, consider making a signal fire with green leaves or branches to produce smoke that can be seen from a distance.

8. Mental Toughness: The Ultimate Survival Skill

In the wilderness, your mindset is just as important as your physical skills. Stay calm and focused, especially in emergencies. Panic can lead to poor decisions, so maintaining a positive attitude and thinking critically are essential.

In situations where you feel lost, remember that staying put and waiting for help might be the best decision. Don’t exhaust yourself by wandering aimlessly; instead, conserve your energy and focus on building shelter, making fire, and signaling for help.

Conclusion

Survival skills are essential for anyone venturing into the wilderness. From preparation and navigation to shelter building, fire-making, and water procurement, each skill plays a critical role in keeping you safe and comfortable during your hike. Above all, remember that a successful wilderness hike starts with knowledge and preparation. The more you know, the better equipped you’ll be to handle any challenges nature throws your way.

By practicing these survival skills and continuously improving your outdoor knowledge, you can confidently embrace the wilderness and enjoy your hiking adventures to the fullest.
