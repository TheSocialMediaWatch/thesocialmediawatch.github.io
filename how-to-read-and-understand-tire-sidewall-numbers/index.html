How to Read and Understand Tire Sidewall Numbers


Tire sidewall numbers can be intimidating, even for the most experienced drivers. These cryptic codes, etched into the sidewall of every tire, contain vital information about the tire's design, capabilities, and compliance with industry regulations. To fully comprehend the world of tire buying, it's essential to crack the code of these mysterious numbers. In this comprehensive guide, we'll delve into the intricacies of tire sidewall numbers, demystifying the complexities and empowering you to make informed decisions when purchasing or repairing your vehicle. The Basics: Tire Sidewall Features A typical tire features a series of alphanumeric characters on its sidewall, split into several sections. The sequence of numbers and letters may vary depending on the tire manufacturer, but there are some standard attributes to be aware of:

Tire Size: The first few characters indicate the tire's diameter in inches, followed by the width of the tire's outer sidewall in millimeters. For example, "225" represents a diameter of 225 millimeters and a width of 57.1 millimeters. Your tire size is essential when selecting the correct replacement or matching your existing tires. Rim Diameter: Next, the sidewall displays the diameter of the rim to which the tire is designed to be mounted, usually in inches (e.g., "17" for a 17-inch rim). Load Rating: The load rating is the maximum weight capacity of the tire and the vehicle it will support, usually measured in pounds. Look for the "Maximum Load" or "Load Index" code, which may be displayed as a single number (87) or with a letter (H, J, etc.). Common load ratings vary: H (1300 pounds), J (1700 pounds), and V (2950 pounds). Speed Rating: The speed rating indicates the maximum speed your tire can safely handle, usually measured in kilometers per hour (km/h) or miles per hour (mph). You might see an X-speed rating (e.g., "N" for 145 mph, "Q" for 99 mph). Tread Pattern: The "T" letter in the tire sidewall highlights the tire's tread design, reflecting the intended traction features for different terrain types. Max Inflation Pressure: Some manufacturers display the maximum allowable inflation pressure, usually measured in pounds per square inch (PSI), to achieve optimal tire performance. Additional Codes: Depending on the manufacturer, other designations may be displayed, focusing on safety, performance, or specific tire qualities.
