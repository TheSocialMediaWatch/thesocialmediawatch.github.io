How to Start Composting Without a Backyard


Composting is one of the most rewarding ways to reduce waste, enrich your soil, and contribute positively to the environment. But what happens if you live in an apartment or a city dwelling without access to a traditional backyard compost pile? The good news is that you don’t need a sprawling outdoor space to enjoy the benefits of composting. Urban living and small spaces can seamlessly accommodate composting through creative and practical approaches.

In this extensive guide, you’ll discover a variety of methods to compost without a backyard, tips to troubleshoot common challenges, ideas for utilizing your finished compost, and ways to weave composting into your daily life — no matter how compact your living arrangement is.

Why Composting Matters

Before diving into city-friendly composting methods, it’s helpful to understand why composting is so impactful. Food scraps and yard waste comprise a significant portion of household trash, yet these items are rich in organic material that can be recycled back into the earth. Instead of landfilling kitchen scraps, composting turns them into nutrient-rich humus that promotes healthy plants and reduces the need for chemical fertilizers.

Moreover, composting curbs methane emissions, a powerful greenhouse gas produced by organic matter decomposing in anaerobic landfill conditions. By composting, you help reduce your carbon footprint, conserve natural resources, and foster sustainable living practices.

Challenges of Composting Without Outdoor Space

Not having a backyard does present a set of challenges:

  • Limited space: Balconies, small patios, or indoor corners constrain compost container size.
  • Odor control: Without outdoor ventilation, managing smells indoors requires careful technique.
  • Pests: Fruit flies and other insects might be attracted to scraps.
  • Moisture and aeration: Proper balancing of moisture and air flow in confined setups can be tricky.

Thankfully, innovative solutions and a bit of know-how help anyone compost successfully in apartments or shared living.

1. Vermicomposting: The Worm-Composting Wonder

One of the most popular and efficient methods to compost indoors is vermicomposting—composting with worms, typically red wigglers.

How It Works

Worms consume food scraps and break them down into nutrient-rich castings, the “black gold” of compost. A vermicomposting bin usually stays indoors, making it ideal for apartments.

Setting Up a Worm Bin

  • Container: Use a plastic or wooden box around 10–20 gallons. It should be opaque to avoid light, which worms dislike.
  • Drill holes: Small holes for air circulation and drainage.
  • Bedding: Shredded newspaper, cardboard, coconut coir, or aged compost. Keep bedding moist like a wrung-out sponge.
  • Adding worms: Red wigglers (Eisenia fetida) thrive here.
  • Feeding: Feed small amounts of kitchen scraps like fruit and vegetable peels, coffee grounds, eggshells, and paper products. Avoid meat, dairy, oily foods, and citrus in big quantities.

Care and Maintenance

  • Temperature: Keep between 55 and 77°F.
  • Moisture: Maintain moist conditions, not soggy.
  • Turning: Gently mix bedding weekly to aerate.
  • Harvest: After 3–6 months, harvest dark, crumbly worm castings for your plants.

Benefits for Small Spaces

  • Compact and odorless if maintained properly.
  • No outdoor space required.
  • Produces high-quality compost perfect for houseplants or container gardens.

2. Bokashi Composting: Fermentation for All Spaces

Bokashi is a fermentation-based method developed in Japan that turns your kitchen scraps into pre-compost without odor problems.

What Is Bokashi?

It’s an anaerobic process where food waste is fermented inside a sealed container with the help of beneficial microbes (bokashi bran). The result isn’t fully composted material but a fermented pre-compost that you can later bury or add to a conventional compost to finish decomposition.

How to Set Up Bokashi Composting

  • Bokashi bucket: A two-part airtight container with a spigot for draining liquid.
  • Bokashi bran: Inoculated bran added over scraps.
  • Process: Layer food scraps, sprinkle bran, compact layers to remove oxygen, close the lid tightly.
  • Drain liquid: Bokashi tea, nutrient-rich liquid, can be diluted as a plant fertilizer.

Pros for Apartment Composting

  • Odorless or minimal smell.
  • Takes up very little space.
  • Handles all food scraps, including meat and dairy.
  • Fast fermentation, usually two weeks.

Aftercare

Once fermentation is done, bury the contents in soil or add it to your garden potting mix. The food scraps will break down further rapidly.

3. Indoor Compost Tumblers and Containers

If worms or fermentation don’t appeal to you, consider dry compost containers or tumblers designed for limited space.

Features to Look for

  • Compact size and airtight seals.
  • Aeration vents or mechanisms to mix contents.
  • Removable trays for liquid runoff.
  • Composting worms can be added in some models to speed up breakdown.

How to Use

  • Add balance of green (nitrogen-rich) materials like food scraps and brown (carbon-rich) such as shredded paper or dried leaves.
  • Regularly turn or agitate contents to encourage aeration.
  • Maintain moisture; materials should feel like a wrung-out sponge.

Managing Odors and Pests

  • Avoid overfeeding or adding wet items excessively.
  • Keep lid tightly sealed.
  • Freeze scraps briefly before adding to reduce fruit flies.
  • Use fine mesh covers on ventilation holes.

4. Composting in Community Gardens or Shared Spaces

If your building or neighborhood has community gardens or green spaces, they might encourage or allow compost contributions.

Steps to Get Started

  • Inquire about composting programs or garden clubs nearby.
  • Organize a compost drop-off with neighbors.
  • Volunteer for maintenance to learn and share composting tips.

Community composting can be an excellent way to access composting if personal space is lacking.

5. Compost Collection Services

Urban areas increasingly offer curbside or neighborhood compost pickup services for organic waste.

How It Works

  • Place food scraps and compostable paper in designated bins.
  • Collectors transport waste to municipal or commercial compost facilities.
  • Ends waste in landfills and supports large-scale compost production.

Check if your city or service providers offer this option — it’s a convenient way to divert organic waste with minimal effort.

What to Compost When You Don’t Have a Backyard

Even in small spaces, your scraps can be composted safely with awareness of what is suitable:

  • Vegetable and fruit scraps, peelings, and cores.
  • Coffee grounds and filters.
  • Tea bags (ensure compostable materials).
  • Eggshells.
  • Shredded paper and cardboard.
  • Small amounts of bread or grains.
  • Avoid large amounts of meat, dairy, oily fats unless using bokashi or specialized methods.

Tips for Success in Small-Space Composting

  • Balance green and brown materials: Prevent moisture issues and odors by mixing wet kitchen scraps with dry, carbon-rich material like paper or sawdust.
  • Control moisture: Compost should feel damp but not soggy; add dry material if too wet.
  • Aerate regularly: Stir or mix compost to provide oxygen.
  • Use food prep techniques: Chop scraps finely to speed decomposition.
  • Store scraps properly: Use a sealed container in the fridge/freezer if collecting over several days.
  • Start small: Begin with a manageable volume to avoid overwhelming the system.
  • Educate household members: Composting is easier when everyone knows what to add or avoid.

Using Finished Compost in Tight Spaces

Once your compost is ready, you can put it to excellent use without a yard:

  • Houseplants: Mix finished compost with potting soil or use as a top dressing.
  • Container gardens: Add nutrients for herbs, vegetables, or flowering plants grown on balconies or windowsills.
  • Community gardens and planter boxes: Donate or contribute your compost enrichment.
  • Gifts: Share nutrient-rich soil amendments with fellow plant lovers.

Common Questions About Urban Composting

How to Avoid Fruit Flies?

  • Freeze or refrigerate food scraps before adding to your bin.
  • Cover fresh scraps with brown materials.
  • Use sealed containers.
  • Deploy fruit fly traps near compost bins if necessary.

Can I Compost in Winter?

Yes, indoor composting is especially useful in winter when outdoor composting slows down. Maintain warmth and moisture inside.

What to Do If Compost Smells Bad?

Usually, it means too much moisture or nitrogen-rich scraps. Add more dry materials, aerate the pile, and reduce fresh food inputs temporarily.

Is It Safe to Compost Meat or Dairy?

Generally no for traditional compost bins due to pests and odor problems. Bokashi fermentation handles these scraps well indoors.

Wrap-Up

Living without a backyard is no longer a barrier to effective composting. With vermicomposting, bokashi fermentation, compact tumblers, community programs, or collection services, urban dwellers can turn kitchen scraps into valuable soil amendment while reducing landfill waste. Composting indoors or in small spaces takes some planning and maintenance but rewards you with healthier plants, fewer odors, and environmental impact reduction.

Take the leap today—your countertop scraps have a new purpose waiting just inside your kitchen. Happy composting!
