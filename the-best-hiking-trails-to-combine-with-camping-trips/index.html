The Best Hiking Trails to Combine with Camping Trips


Camping combined with hiking is an unbeatable way to immerse yourself in nature’s beauty, recharge your soul, and challenge your endurance. Whether you’re a weekend warrior or a seasoned trekking enthusiast, choosing the right trail to compliment your camping trip can transform your outdoor experience from ordinary to spectacular. In this comprehensive guide, we explore the very best hiking trails around the world that seamlessly blend incredible scenery, manageable terrains, and perfect camping spots — making your next adventure unforgettable.

Why Combine Hiking with Camping?

Before diving into specific trails, it’s worth highlighting what makes the combination of hiking and camping so special. Hiking allows you to move through diverse landscapes, each footstep revealing new vistas, wildflowers, or wildlife. Camping, on the other hand, gives you a chance to slow down, soak in the environment, and experience sunset and dawn in the heart of wilderness. Together, they create a dynamic rhythm of exploration followed by peaceful rest.

Multiday hikes ending at or incorporating campsite stays maximize the experience, letting you live fully within nature rather than just pass through. When you plan a hike with camping in mind, your choice of trail must offer not just breathtaking views and trail features but also reliable, safe, and scenic campsites along or near the route.

Top Trails Perfect for Hiking and Camping

1. John Muir Trail, California, USA

Stretching over 210 miles through the Sierra Nevada mountain range, the John Muir Trail (JMT) is legendary among hikers and campers alike. This trail showcases some of the most stunning high-altitude landscapes in the United States — including alpine lakes, granite cliffs, and expansive wildflower meadows.

Camping Highlights:
The trail is dotted with abundant backcountry camping spots, many near pristine lakes or by rivers. Permits are required, but once secured, you’re allowed to camp almost anywhere along the trail, allowing you to tailor your nightly stops to your preference. Popular camping spots include sites near Garnet Lake and Thousand Island Lake.

Trail Difficulty: Moderate to strenuous, recommended for experienced hikers, with an average completion time of two to three weeks.

2. West Highland Way, Scotland

For those seeking an immersive hiking-camping experience infused with rich history and scenic variety, the West Highland Way presents a fantastic option. Covering approximately 96 miles from Milngavie near Glasgow to Fort William in the Scottish Highlands, this trail offers everything from tranquil lochs and ancient forests to rugged mountain passes.

Camping Highlights:
There are numerous well-maintained campsites along the route, as well as opportunities for wild camping in accordance with Scotland’s Outdoor Access Code. The landscapes provide perfect sunset views and serene settings ideal for pitching a tent.

Trail Difficulty: Moderate, suitable for novices looking to step up to longer multi-day hikes.

3. The Overland Track, Tasmania, Australia

Australia’s Overland Track spans roughly 40 miles of Tasmania’s wilderness, running through Cradle Mountain-Lake St Clair National Park. The blend of dense rainforests, alpine meadows, and dramatic peaks make this trail one of the best for experiencing diverse ecosystems on foot.

Camping Highlights:
The trail has designated huts and campsites spaced about one day’s hike apart. This makes it easy to plan your daily distances and ensures a comfortable overnight stay immersed in the wild—with the possibility to spot Tasmanian devils and other unique wildlife.

Trail Difficulty: Moderate, with some steep sections and variable weather conditions to prepare for.

4. The Torres del Paine “Circuito” Trail, Patagonia, Chile

For adventurers seeking something more remote and dramatic, the Torres del Paine Circuito in Chilean Patagonia is a must. This trail encircles the Torres del Paine massif, covering around 80 miles of jaw-dropping glaciers, turquoise lakes, and granite peaks.

Camping Highlights:
Throughout the circuit, several campsites with essential facilities are available, allowing trekkers to carry less weight while still enjoying comfortable stops. The campsites are perfectly situated, offering stunning panoramic views that make the overnight experience magical.

Trail Difficulty: Strenuous, with unpredictable weather and challenging terrain that reward those well-prepared.

5. The Long Trail, Vermont, USA

Known as the oldest long-distance hiking trail in the United States, the Long Trail offers 272 miles traversing the entire length of Vermont from Massachusetts to Canada. It’s a trail steeped in history with a rugged charm reminiscent of classic Appalachian hiking.

Camping Highlights:
The Long Trail features numerous campsites and shelters about a day’s hike apart for backpackers. These campsites provide a basic accommodation solution for long-distance hikers and create an engaging challenge for camping novices and veterans alike.

Trail Difficulty: Moderate to strenuous, variable depending on season and weather.

Planning Tips for Hiking and Camping Trips

Prioritize Permit and Regulation Research

Many popular trails with camping options require permits, especially to control environmental impact and manage crowds. Researching and securing these ahead of time ensures a smooth trip. Some areas have quotas, season restrictions, or designated camp zones.

Gear Smart – Balance Weight and Function

Packing for hiking plus camping means striking a balance: you need enough gear to camp comfortably without overburdening yourself on the trail. Lightweight tents, efficient sleeping bags, compact cooking setups, and versatile clothing layers are key.

Water Sources and Food Logistics

Identify reliable water sources along the trail or plan to carry a sufficient supply. Water purification methods such as filters or tablets are essential. Also, consider foods that are calorie-dense, easy to prepare, and durable under outdoor conditions.

Safety First – Be Prepared For Emergencies

Even on the best trails, weather and terrain can turn challenging quickly. Leave your itinerary with someone reliable, carry maps (physical and electronic), a first aid kit, and knowledge of local wildlife and hazards.

Why These Trails Stand Out

All the trails featured here offer something unique — whether it’s the sheer grandeur of California’s Sierra Nevada, the cultural richness of Scotland, the exotic wilderness of Tasmania, the rugged isolation of Patagonia, or the historic charm of Vermont. What they share is excellent infrastructure or possibilities for camping, accessibility to pristine natural beauty, and options suited to a range of skill levels.

Camping alongside hiking deepens the connection with nature, allowing you to witness the transformative magic of twilight in wild settings and wake with crisp mornings that bring the forest, mountains, or lakes right to your tent door. Selecting trails that balance challenge, aesthetics, and comfortable camping spots ensures that each day ends with satisfaction and each night brings restful restoration.

Final Thoughts

If you’re envisioning a hiking and camping adventure, choose a trail that matches your physical fitness, desired scenery, and camping style. Research, prepare thoughtfully, and embark on a journey that not only tests your stamina but nourishes your spirit. Whether you conquer the demanding John Muir Trail or enjoy the gentle enchantment of the West Highland Way, the blend of trekking by day and camping by night is an unbeatable recipe for outdoor bliss.

Ready to lace up your boots, pack your tent, and hit the trail? The world is full of amazing hiking and camping combinations waiting to be explored. Your next great adventure begins on the path, under the stars, surrounded by the kind of peace that only nature can give.
