What to Do if You Are Wrongfully Terminated from Your Job


Getting fired from your job is always a stressful experience, but when the termination is wrongful, the situation can feel even more overwhelming. Whether you were fired without cause, discriminated against, or wrongfully accused, understanding your rights and knowing how to respond is crucial in protecting your career and your well-being. If you believe you have been wrongfully terminated, there are steps you can take to ensure that you respond in the right way, secure your rights, and possibly even recover damages for the wrongful act.

In this guide, we’ll explore the steps to take if you are wrongfully terminated, how to gather evidence, and what legal options are available to you.

Understanding Wrongful Termination

Before diving into the steps you should take if you believe you’ve been wrongfully terminated, it’s important to understand what wrongful termination is. Wrongful termination occurs when an employee is fired for illegal reasons, such as violating federal or state labor laws or violating the terms of a contract. The most common reasons for wrongful termination include:

  • Discrimination: Being fired due to race, gender, age, religion, disability, or any other protected characteristic.
  • Retaliation: Being fired after engaging in legally protected activity, such as whistleblowing or filing a workers’ compensation claim.
  • Breach of Contract: If you have a contract that specifies terms of employment, including how or when you can be terminated, firing you in violation of that contract may constitute wrongful termination.
  • Violation of Public Policy: Termination for reasons that violate state or federal law, such as firing an employee for taking family leave or reporting unsafe working conditions.

Understanding what constitutes wrongful termination is crucial to determining if your case qualifies. If any of these reasons align with your situation, you may be entitled to compensation or other remedies.

Immediate Steps to Take After Wrongful Termination

If you have been wrongfully terminated, it’s essential to stay calm and take immediate action. Here’s a step-by-step guide on what you should do.

1. Stay Calm and Professional

Although it may be difficult, the first thing you should do is stay calm. Losing your job can cause a flood of emotions, but acting out of anger or frustration might harm your chances later on. Whether you were called into a meeting or handed a termination letter, it’s important to maintain a professional demeanor. This will not only protect your reputation but also give you a clearer mind to navigate the situation ahead.

2. Request an Explanation in Writing

In some cases, employers may offer a verbal explanation for your termination, but it’s important to get this in writing. A formal letter explaining the reasons for your termination will provide a record of what was said. This written statement could prove to be crucial in your wrongful termination case, especially if the explanation is inconsistent with what was previously communicated or if there is no valid reason for your dismissal.

3. Review Your Employment Contract or Employee Handbook

If you have a written employment contract, review it thoroughly to check for any clauses that may provide protection against wrongful termination. Many contracts will specify that an employee cannot be fired without just cause or that certain procedures must be followed before termination can occur. If the termination violates the terms of your contract, it may provide the foundation for a wrongful termination claim.

Even if you don’t have a formal contract, you should still check your company’s employee handbook or any other written guidelines that outline the company’s policies on termination. This can help you understand if the termination was carried out in accordance with company rules or if they violated their own procedures.

4. Document Everything

Documentation is key when dealing with wrongful termination. Keep track of all communication and documentation related to your firing. This includes emails, letters, meeting notes, and anything else that could provide evidence of the circumstances leading to your termination.

Start by documenting the events leading up to your termination. Were there any incidents where you were treated unfairly, or were you given any warning about performance issues? Were there previous instances where you were disciplined, and if so, was it consistent with the company’s policies?

It’s also important to keep track of any conversations or communications with your employer after your termination. For instance, if you reached out to your employer seeking clarification on the reason for your firing, document their responses.

5. Consider Talking to a Lawyer

One of the most important steps you can take after being wrongfully terminated is consulting with an experienced employment attorney. An attorney specializing in labor law can help you understand your rights and advise you on the best course of action. They will be able to assess your case and tell you if you have a legitimate claim for wrongful termination.

An attorney can also help you navigate the process of filing a formal complaint with the relevant authorities or pursuing a legal case against your employer. Many lawyers offer free consultations, so you don’t have to commit financially until you’ve decided on the best course of action.

6. File a Complaint with the Appropriate Agency

If your wrongful termination involves discrimination, harassment, or retaliation, you may be able to file a complaint with a government agency such as the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC). The EEOC enforces federal laws prohibiting workplace discrimination, and they can investigate your claim to determine if it warrants further action.

For non-discriminatory wrongful terminations, you may be able to file a claim with a state labor board or commission. Each state has its own laws regarding wrongful termination, and your state’s labor department can help guide you through the process.

7. Explore Your Options for Reinstatement or Settlement

In some cases, your attorney may recommend pursuing a settlement with your former employer. This could involve negotiating a severance package or other compensation in exchange for not pursuing legal action. Keep in mind that settlements are often confidential, meaning you may not be able to disclose the terms to anyone.

Alternatively, you may wish to pursue reinstatement to your job. This is a rare remedy but is sometimes available if the wrongful termination was particularly egregious or if you have a strong case for why your firing was unjust.

Legal Remedies for Wrongful Termination

If you can prove that you were wrongfully terminated, there are several legal remedies available to you, including:

1. Back Pay

If you were fired without just cause and are able to prove it, you may be entitled to back pay. This means that your former employer may have to pay you the wages you would have earned if you hadn’t been wrongfully terminated.

2. Reinstatement

In some cases, a court may order that you be reinstated to your former position, especially if the termination violated a contract or public policy.

3. Compensatory Damages

If you suffered emotional distress or other non-financial damages due to your wrongful termination, you may be able to claim compensatory damages. This could include things like pain and suffering, emotional distress, or damage to your reputation.

4. Punitive Damages

In cases where your employer acted with malice or in bad faith, punitive damages may be awarded. These damages are designed to punish the employer and deter others from engaging in similar behavior.

5. Attorney’s Fees and Court Costs

If you prevail in your wrongful termination case, you may be able to recover the costs of hiring an attorney as well as any court fees associated with your case.

Preventing Future Issues

While no one can predict when or why they may face wrongful termination, there are steps you can take to protect yourself in the future:

  • Maintain a Good Record: Always strive to maintain a good work record, including punctuality, productivity, and professionalism. This helps protect you against unfair claims of misconduct.
  • Know Your Rights: Familiarize yourself with your rights under local and federal labor laws. Understanding what constitutes wrongful termination can help you recognize it if it happens to you.
  • Keep Personal and Work Boundaries Clear: Avoid situations that could lead to personal conflicts with your employer or coworkers. Professional boundaries can help avoid misunderstandings that might lead to wrongful termination.

Conclusion

Being wrongfully terminated from your job is undoubtedly a stressful and emotionally challenging experience, but it is important to remember that you have options. By staying calm, gathering evidence, and seeking legal advice, you can increase your chances of successfully navigating this difficult situation. Whether you seek reinstatement, compensation, or simply to understand what went wrong, taking proactive steps can help you recover from a wrongful termination and move forward in your career.
