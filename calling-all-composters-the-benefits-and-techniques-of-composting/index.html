Calling All Composters: The Benefits and Techniques of Composting


Composting is an eco-friendly way to transform your kitchen and garden waste into nutrient-rich soil for your plants. This resourceful practice not only aids in waste management but also contributes to sustainable gardening and farming. In today’s blog post, we’ll dive into the benefits of composting and explore various composting techniques to guide you on your green journey.

The Hidden Benefits of Composting

  1. Reduce Household Waste: Rather than throwing organic waste into the trash bin, composting turns it into a usable product that can be used in the garden. By composting food and yard waste, you can significantly reduce the amount of garbage your household produces each week.

  2. Rich Soil Nutrient: Compost enhances the structure and fertility of your garden’s soil by increasing water retention, providing essential nutrients, and preventing soil erosion.

  3. Environmental Conservation: Composting reduces methane and other greenhouse gas emissions that result from the decomposition of organic waste in landfills. Additionally, creating compost saves energy by diverting waste from being transported to and processed in landfills.

  4. Disease and Pest Control: The beneficial microorganisms found in compost help control pests and diseases in the garden. When plants thrive in healthy, compost-rich soil, their natural defenses to pests and diseases are strengthened.

Composting Techniques: Making Compost is Both Simple and Rewarding

1. Backyard Composting

This method involves creating a compost bin in your yard, where food scraps and yard waste are decomposed naturally by microorganisms. The basic steps for backyard composting are as follows:

a. Choose the Right Bin

Select a bin made of recycled plastic, wood, or metal. A size of 3’x3’x3’ is perfect for a family of four.

b. Prepare the Compost Pile

Start with a layer of browns (leaves, straw, or hay), followed by a layer of greens (vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, etc.), and sprinkle some water on top. Remember the ratio: browns to greens should be about 2:1.

c. Turn the Pile

Aerating the compost pile every week helps accelerate the decomposition process. Simply use a pitchfork or shovel to mix the compost layers.

d. Monitor and Harvest

Your compost will be ready in 2-6 months, depending on the temperature and how often you turn it. It will have an earthy scent and a dark brown, crumbly texture.

2. Bokashi Composting

Bokashi composting is a fermentation process that can break down food waste, including meat and dairy, within weeks. Unlike traditional composting, Bokashi composting does not rely on microorganisms to decompose waste. Instead, it involves a specific strain of beneficial microbes.

a. Obtaining Bokashi Supplies

You’ll need a Bokashi bucket, a lid, Bokashi bran, and an outdoor container for composting the finished waste.

b. Befriending Bokashi

First, place a layer of Bokashi bran at the bottom of the bucket. Add food waste, and then sprinkle more bran on top. Alternate layers of waste and bran until the bucket is full, ensuring that the waste is submerged in the bran at all times.

c. Curing the Waste

Once the bucket is full, seal it with the lid and let it sit for 1-2 weeks. The waste should turn an acidic pink color during this period. Then, bury the compost in the ground or place it in a compost tumbler.

3. ** Vermiculture (Worm Composting)**

Worm composting, or vermicomposting, involves using red worms (Red Wigglers or Eisenia fetida) to break down organic material into nutrient-rich worm castings. Worm bins can be placed indoors or outdoors, making it an ideal option for apartments and small spaces.

a. Building the Bin

Create a bin with two sections – one for waste and one for worms. Add a layer of bedding material (such as shredded paper or coconut coir) to the worm section before adding the worms.

b. Feeding the Worms

Feed the worms a balanced diet of green waste (fruit and vegetable scraps) and brown waste (newspaper, coffee filters, etc.). Avoid composting meat, dairy, and diseased plants.

c. Harvesting Castings

Worm castings are ready to use when they cover the bottom of the bin. To harvest, remove the worm bin’s bottom panel and gently scoop out the castings.

Embrace the Art of Composting

With these techniques and a spirit of sustainability, you can become a pro compost maker in no time. Not only does this eco-friendly practice reduce waste, but it also enriches the soil and supports the environment. Go ahead, call all the composters, and join the composting community in creating a greener world!
