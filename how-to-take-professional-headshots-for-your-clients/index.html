How to Take Professional Headshots for Your Clients


In today’s digital age, a professional headshot is more than just a photo; it’s a key asset in personal branding, job applications, and business networking. For photographers, delivering high-quality headshots that meet client expectations is essential. Whether you’re a seasoned professional or a beginner, understanding the nuances of professional headshot photography is critical in ensuring your clients look their best. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about taking professional headshots that leave a lasting impression.

1. Understand the Purpose of a Professional Headshot

Before you even begin setting up your camera, it’s crucial to understand the purpose of a professional headshot. A headshot isn’t just a picture; it’s a representation of your client’s personal brand. Headshots are commonly used for:

  • Corporate Websites: An image that represents a person in a professional manner.
  • LinkedIn Profiles: The go-to platform for professionals, where a clean and polished headshot can make a big difference.
  • Company Bios: Team pages often feature individual headshots that need to convey professionalism and approachability.
  • Marketing Materials: Headshots may also appear in brochures, email signatures, or other promotional items.

By understanding the context in which the headshot will be used, you can tailor your approach to suit the client’s needs.

2. Discuss Expectations with the Client

One of the most important steps in shooting professional headshots is having a clear understanding of the client’s expectations. Every client may have a different vision for their photo, so it’s crucial to establish the following:

  • Style and Mood: Do they want a formal, corporate look, or a more relaxed, casual approach?
  • Clothing: Are there any wardrobe guidelines, such as specific colors, patterns, or logos to avoid?
  • Background Preferences: Would they prefer a studio background, an environmental shot, or an outdoor setting?

Having a conversation about these aspects ensures that both you and the client are on the same page before the session begins.

3. Choose the Right Gear

While the creativity of your shoot is paramount, the right equipment plays a significant role in achieving professional results. Here’s what you’ll need:

Camera

For professional headshots, a camera with a full-frame sensor is ideal as it allows for superior image quality and shallow depth of field, which creates that beautiful blurred background. However, a high-quality crop sensor camera can still produce excellent results.

Lens

The most common lens for headshots is a portrait lens in the 85mm to 135mm range. A lens in this range helps create flattering images by compressing features and avoiding distortion, which is especially important when working with close-up portraits.

Lighting

Good lighting is key in creating professional headshots. You can use natural light, but it’s more reliable to use artificial lighting in the form of softboxes or light modifiers to control the light.

  • Key Light: This is your main light source, typically positioned at a 45-degree angle to your subject.
  • Fill Light: This light fills in the shadows created by the key light.
  • Background Light: If you’re shooting against a seamless background, adding a light source for the background can help separate the subject from it, creating depth.

Tripod

A tripod helps maintain stability, especially when using slower shutter speeds or ensuring consistent framing.

Backdrops

You can opt for a plain backdrop (white, grey, or black) for a professional look, or you might use a textured or environmental background to convey a specific mood or setting.

4. Lighting Setup

Lighting can either make or break a headshot, so understanding how to light your subject properly is essential. The key to great lighting is using soft, diffused light that minimizes shadows and highlights the subject’s face in a flattering way. Here are some lighting setups to consider:

Natural Light

If you’re shooting outdoors or in a well-lit space, make the most of natural light. Position your subject near a large window or in open shade. Avoid harsh, direct sunlight, which can create unflattering shadows and squinting.

Softboxes and Diffusers

If you’re shooting indoors or need to have more control over lighting, softboxes and diffusers are indispensable. These tools soften the light, preventing harsh shadows on the subject’s face and creating a more even light distribution.

Rembrandt Lighting

Rembrandt lighting is a classic portrait lighting style that creates a triangle of light on the subject’s cheek. This lighting setup is achieved by positioning the key light at about 45 degrees to one side and slightly above the subject’s face, which creates dramatic, yet flattering shadows.

Butterfly Lighting

Butterfly lighting is named after the butterfly-shaped shadow that forms beneath the subject’s nose when a light is placed directly in front and above their face. This is a great setup for highlighting facial features and is particularly flattering for women.

5. Posing Your Client

Posing is one of the most challenging aspects of taking professional headshots. The goal is to create a pose that feels natural while also showcasing the client’s personality and professionalism. Here are some tips to get the perfect pose:

Angle of the Face

Instead of having the client look straight into the camera, ask them to slightly turn their face to one side. This creates a more dynamic shot and adds depth to the image. Typically, you’ll want your client to face at a 45-degree angle to the camera.

Posture

Encourage your client to stand or sit with their shoulders back, chin slightly up, and body slightly turned toward the camera. Avoid stiff poses; instead, suggest relaxed movements like shifting weight onto one leg or gently tilting their head to create a natural flow.

Eye Contact

Eye contact is crucial in conveying confidence and engagement. Depending on the desired result, your client can either look directly into the lens for a stronger presence or look slightly off-camera for a more thoughtful or approachable look.

Hand Placement

Be mindful of hand placement. You don’t want hands to look stiff or awkward in a headshot. If your client has visible hands, they can lightly touch their lapel or fold their arms in a relaxed position. If no hands are in the shot, ensure that the arms are subtly positioned to avoid slouching.

6. Framing and Composition

The way you frame your subject can impact the mood and professionalism of the headshot. Here are some tips for good framing:

Head and Shoulders

For the majority of headshots, it’s typical to frame your subject from the chest or shoulders up, focusing on their face. A slight zoom-in will often work better than framing too wide.

Eye Level

Position your camera at the subject’s eye level to avoid distortions. Shooting from below or above can introduce unflattering angles.

The Rule of Thirds

The rule of thirds can help you compose more visually appealing headshots. Instead of centering the subject in the frame, place them slightly off-center, drawing the viewer’s attention toward their face and eyes.

Space Around the Subject

Ensure there’s enough space around your client’s head and shoulders to avoid a cramped feeling. You don’t want their head too close to the edge of the frame.

7. Communication and Client Comfort

A successful headshot session is not only about technical expertise but also about making your client feel comfortable. Many people are not used to being in front of a camera, so ensuring they feel at ease is critical to capturing their best look.

Build Rapport

Before you start the shoot, take some time to chat with your client. Ask about their goals for the session and make them feel comfortable by engaging in light conversation. A relaxed client will naturally produce a more authentic expression.

Direct the Client

Many people aren’t sure how to pose for a professional photo, so giving clear, easy-to-follow directions is key. Don’t be afraid to guide them on where to look, how to position their body, or what to do with their hands. Positive reinforcement throughout the shoot will help maintain a confident and relaxed vibe.

Capture Multiple Shots

Don’t be afraid to take multiple shots from different angles, with varying expressions, and under different lighting conditions. The more variety you capture, the higher the likelihood of getting that perfect shot. Also, allow the client to review the shots as you go to ensure you’re meeting their expectations.

8. Post-Processing and Editing

Once the photoshoot is over, the editing process begins. Post-processing is essential for refining your images and enhancing their quality. Here are some key tips for editing headshots:

Retouching

When retouching, aim to remove minor blemishes or distractions, but avoid over-editing. Professional headshots should still look natural, so don’t go overboard with smoothing skin or altering facial features.

Color Correction

Ensure that the skin tones are accurate and that the colors in the image look natural. White balance adjustments can help correct any color cast that might result from the lighting.

Cropping and Framing

Make sure the final headshot is well-composed and cropped correctly. Most professional headshots are composed with the subject’s face centered and their eyes positioned near the top third of the frame.

Sharpening

Apply a gentle sharpening effect to ensure the image looks crisp and clear, particularly in the subject’s eyes and facial features.

9. Delivering the Final Product

Once the images are edited, it’s time to deliver the final product to your client. Ensure that the files are high resolution and properly formatted for various uses, whether it’s for web profiles, print, or social media.

Consider providing your client with:

  • A selection of images: Offer a range of shots so your client can choose the one that best fits their needs.
  • Different file sizes: Include both web-ready files (smaller resolution for websites) and high-resolution files (for print and large formats).
  • A branded delivery folder: If you’re delivering the images online, make sure the delivery process feels professional and organized, perhaps with a custom folder that includes your logo or contact information.

Conclusion

Taking professional headshots is an art that requires technical expertise, good communication, and a strong understanding of what your clients need. With the right equipment, setup, and approach, you can deliver headshots that not only meet your client’s expectations but exceed them. Whether for a corporate website, LinkedIn profile, or personal branding, a high-quality headshot can be a powerful tool in making a lasting first impression.
