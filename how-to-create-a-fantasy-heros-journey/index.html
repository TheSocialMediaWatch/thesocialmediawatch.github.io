How to Create a Fantasy Hero's Journey


Creating a compelling hero’s journey in a fantasy setting is one of the most rewarding challenges for writers. This timeless narrative framework, made popular by Joseph Campbell in The Hero with a Thousand Faces, offers a rich structure to craft stories that resonate deeply with readers. Whether you’re drafting an epic novel, screenplay, or even a tabletop RPG campaign, mastering the hero’s journey can transform your ideas into unforgettable adventures.

Let’s delve into the essential steps to design a thrilling and emotional hero’s journey, while also incorporating unique elements to elevate your fantasy story.

1. Establish the Ordinary World

Every great journey begins with grounding the hero in a familiar, ordinary world. This serves as the baseline for understanding the hero’s situation and personality before their adventure begins. In fantasy stories, this world could range from a bustling medieval village, a treetop elven community, or even a dystopian future city cloaked in magical energy.

The ordinary world is where you introduce the hero’s flaws, their relationships, routine, and the forces that hold them back from pursuing greatness. Think about how this setting can emphasize contrast with the extraordinary world they will eventually encounter.

Questions to Ask:

  • What makes the hero’s ordinary world unique?
  • What does the hero yearn for, but feel unable to reach?
  • What relationships or circumstances tie the hero to this world?

Consider giving the ordinary world its own flavor of magic or charm, keeping it distinct yet tantalizing enough to make readers curious about what lies beyond.

2. The Call to Adventure

The call to adventure is the inciting event that disrupts the hero’s status quo and sets the story in motion. The hero might receive a mysterious invitation, stumble upon a forbidden artifact, or realize they possess a hidden power. This moment should introduce stakes that compel the hero to leave their comfort zone.

For a fantasy story, the call can be monumental or subtle—a prophecy foretold by a cryptic sorcerer or a sudden attack on the hero’s village that reveals a brewing war. The key is to create a compelling reason why the hero cannot ignore this call.

Tips for Making the Call Powerful:

  • Use foreshadowing to hint at the significance of the call throughout the ordinary world.
  • Tie the call to the hero’s emotional longing or deepest fear.
  • Ensure there are stakes—whether personal, societal, or cosmic—that make ignoring the call impossible.

3. Refusing the Call (Optional)

Although not mandatory, a refusal adds depth to the hero’s character, illustrating their internal conflict and reluctance to embrace change. In fantasy stories, this refusal could stem from fear, self-doubt, or loyalty to people in their ordinary world.

For example, a magic-averse farmer may hesitate to wield the enchanted blade they’ve uncovered, or a scholarly mage may refuse a dangerous quest because of their fear of failure. Highlight the tension between the hero’s motivations and their reluctance, which will make their eventual acceptance of the call more satisfying.

4. Meeting the Mentor

The mentor is a guide or teacher who helps the hero prepare for their journey and navigate the first steps of the unknown. In fantasy stories, this figure could be a wizard, warrior, ancient spirit, or even a magical creature.

The mentor’s role is to highlight the hero’s potential and provide them with tools, advice, or wisdom to confront the challenges ahead. However, the mentor doesn’t always need to be a physical guide—it could also be a symbolic artifact, an ancient text, or even memories of a deceased loved one.

Crafting a Memorable Mentor:

  • Give the mentor quirks or flaws—they don’t need to be perfect.
  • Create tension between the mentor’s advice and the hero’s choices.
  • Ensure the mentor’s wisdom adds insight into the hero’s growth or goals.

5. Crossing the Threshold

Crossing the threshold signifies the hero’s first step into the extraordinary world, where their familiar reality is replaced by mystery, danger, and wonder. This moment represents significant commitment; there’s no turning back.

For a fantasy hero, the threshold could be a portal into another dimension, an uncharted magical forest, or an oath to an ancient order of warriors. Lay down vivid details that convey the surreal, awe-inspiring nature of this new realm while also foreshadowing the challenges ahead.

Ideas for Threshold Moments:

  • An encounter with a mythical guardian who tests the hero’s worth.
  • Learning a harsh lesson about the dangers of their new environment.
  • The hero leaving a symbolic item behind (e.g., an ordinary cloak, a childhood keepsake) as a sign of transformation.

6. Tests, Allies, and Enemies

Here is where the hero starts to evolve and face small challenges that prepare them for greater trials. This phase not only builds the world but also expands the cast of characters. Allies can provide invaluable support, guidance, and camaraderie, while enemies can add layers of conflict and suspense.

Introduce a variety of tests to reinforce the stakes of the journey. The hero might solve riddles of an ancient oracle, face traps in a cursed labyrinth, or battle with renegade sorcerers. These events should gradually push them to learn, adapt, and grow stronger.

Tips for Writing Compelling Challenges:

  • Use the tests to force the hero to confront their weaknesses or insecurities.
  • Create allies with conflicting personalities and motivations—this adds a layer of realism.
  • Make enemies multidimensional by exploring their motivations, desires, and flaws.

7. The Supreme Ordeal

The supreme ordeal is the climax of your hero’s journey—the ultimate test of their strength, courage, and growth. This might involve a world-altering battle against a tyrannical overlord, a perilous journey into the heart of a cursed volcano, or the mastery of forbidden magic.

This trial should serve as a culmination of the hero’s arc, demanding that they integrate all the lessons learned along the way. It also paves the way for their full transformation.

Writing Tips for the Supreme Ordeal:

  • Ensure the stakes are at their highest—what is the hero risking in this moment?
  • Add moments of doubt and despair to heighten tension.
  • Reveal final secrets of the story’s world or characters to amplify emotional resonance.

8. The Reward (Seizing the Sword)

The reward encapsulates what the hero gains after succeeding in the ordeal. In fantasy narratives, this can be a physical object, such as a magical artifact or weapon, or a more abstract reward, like deeper understanding or peace between warring factions.

Make it clear how this reward completes the hero’s internal journey. For example, if they struggled with self-doubt, the reward might represent the confidence they needed all along.

9. The Road Back

The road back begins the journey toward resolution, allowing the hero to face lingering challenges and consequences. They must reconcile who they’ve become with the place they came from.

As the hero returns to the ordinary world, complications might arise. Perhaps their new status threatens existing relationships, or enemies from the extraordinary world follow them home.

10. The Resurrection

This is the hero’s final test, often symbolizing their ultimate transformation. The resurrection challenges them to rise above their fears, doubts, or flaws for one last magnificent act. In fantasy stories, this might involve saving their world from total annihilation, confronting corruption within their people, or fighting a reborn adversary.

11. Returning with the Elixir

The hero returns home as a changed person, bringing with them the “elixir”—a gift, insight, or newfound power that benefits not only themselves but also the world they left behind. This could be literal magic that revives their homeland or an abstract lesson that strengthens their community.

Building a fantasy hero’s journey requires careful attention to the character’s growth, the world’s intricacies, and the narrative’s emotional impact. With Campbell’s framework as your guide, along with opportunities to innovate, you can craft an epic story that captivates audiences and leaves a lasting impression. Dive into your world, let imagination take hold, and ensure your hero’s journey becomes one of legend.
