How to Install a New Kitchen Faucet


Few home upgrades deliver a faster boost in daily comfort and style than swapping out an old kitchen faucet for a new one. Whether your current fixture is leaking, corroded, or just dated, installing a new kitchen faucet instantly updates the heart of your home. If thoughts of plumbing projects feel intimidating, don’t worry—most homeowners can handle this task without calling in a professional. With the right tools, a little patience, and the steps spelled out, you’ll be enjoying your upgraded kitchen sink in no time.

Let’s dive into a no-nonsense, thorough walkthrough for installing a new kitchen faucet, covering everything from preparation and old faucet removal to installing your new fixture and troubleshooting the most common problems.

1. Preparation: What to Know Before You Begin

Assess Your Sink and Faucet Style

Before you even dream about shiny new faucet finishes, take a close look at your current setup. Kitchen sink faucets typically come in a few installation styles:

  • Single-hole faucets: The faucet and handle are mounted through one hole in the countertop or sink.
  • Center-set (three-hole) faucets: These use three holes—one for the spout and two for handles.
  • Widespread faucets: Separate handles and spout are spaced apart, each requiring its own hole.
  • Pull-out or pull-down sprayer faucets: These often use a single hole but sometimes have separate sprayer hoses.

Examine how many holes your sink has and their spacing. This is crucial when shopping for a replacement fixture that will actually fit without modification.

Gather Your Faucet Installation Materials

You’ll avoid last-minute hardware store trips (and headaches) by collecting everything you need in advance. Typical supplies include:

  • Adjustable wrench
  • Basin wrench (makes it much easier to work in tight spaces)
  • Plumber’s tape (Teflon tape)
  • Plumber’s putty (sometimes required for the base gasket)
  • Bucket or small container (to catch residual water)
  • Rag or sponge, towel
  • New supply lines (if needed—sometimes included with faucet, sometimes not)
  • Flashlight

Check your faucet box for included parts—gaskets, mounting hardware, and hose adapters often come with the fixture.

Turn Off Water Supply

Locate the shut-off valves under your sink. These typically look like small knobs or levers on the supply lines leading to the faucet. Turn them fully clockwise. If they don’t budge, use a towel for extra grip. Once off, open the faucet at the sink to drain out any water still in the lines.

2. Removing the Old Kitchen Faucet

Unhook Water Supply Lines

Place a bucket under the connections where the water supply lines meet the bottom of the faucet. With your adjustable wrench, loosen the nuts—expect a few drips. If your supply lines are old and stiff, consider replacing them to avoid future leaks.

Disconnect Sprayer (if Present)

If your faucet had a side sprayer, locate its hose and unscrew it from the faucet body or diverter.

Loosen Mounting Hardware

Reach behind the sink basin—this is where a basin wrench shines. There will be nuts or mounting plates holding the faucet tight against the sink or countertop. Unscrew these, which can be tedious depending on how corroded they are or how cramped the space is. Apply penetrating oil to stubborn nuts, wait a few minutes, and try again.

Lift Out the Old Faucet

Once freed, gently wiggle the old faucet and pull it up and out. Inspect the area for mineral build-up, mold, or grime—now’s the perfect time to scrub the surface clean before installing your new fixture.

3. Installing Your New Kitchen Faucet

Check the Fit and Layout

Dry-fit your new kitchen faucet without tightening any hardware. Make sure the gasket or putty ring sits flush on the sink. If using plumber’s putty, roll it into a rope and press under the faucet base perimeter; otherwise, a rubber or foam gasket is usually sufficient.

Secure the Faucet

Thread the faucet tailpieces through the sink holes. Go beneath the sink and slide on the mounting hardware as directed in the faucet instructions—often a washer then a mounting nut. Hand-tighten, then use your wrench for a snug fit. If the fixture wobbles, adjust the position on the sink surface and retighten until secure.

Connect Supply Lines

Most modern faucets use threaded connectors—wrap threaded ends with plumber’s tape to help prevent leaks. Attach hot (left) and cold (right) supply lines to corresponding valves. Tighten with an adjustable wrench, but don’t over-torque; you want a secure, but not stripped, connection.

Tip: If your faucet didn’t come with supply lines, use braided stainless steel hoses for flexibility and durability. Match the connection size to your plumbing and faucet (typically 3/8” compression fittings on one end, 1/2” or 1/4” fittings on the other).

Attach Sprayer Hose (if applicable)

If your new faucet includes a pull-out or side sprayer, connect the hose per the manufacturer’s instructions. Ensure all quick-connect clips or washers are seated to avoid leaks. Some hoses “snap” onto the faucet body; double-check the connection for a reassuring click.

Reinstall Counterweights

Pull-down faucets have a counterweight that must be attached to the hose below the sink. Position the counterweight about halfway down the exposed hose unless installation instructions specify otherwise.

4. Finishing Up: Testing and Troubleshooting

Turn On Water Supply

Slowly open each supply valve under the sink. Watch for leaks at every joint—especially around new connections and hoses.

Test Faucet Operation

Open the faucet handle to flush out debris (remove the aerator from the spout first, if possible, to avoid clogging it with loose grit). Check both cold and hot water functions. Test the sprayer thoroughly for smooth action and leaks.

Tighten and Adjust

If you discover a drip under the sink, tighten connections by hand, then slightly with a wrench. If it persists, turn off water again, disassemble, and inspect for misaligned washers or cross-threaded nuts.

Replace the faucet aerator once the water runs clear.

5. Pro Tips for Smooth Kitchen Faucet Replacement

Read the Instructions

Not all faucets are created equal. Always glance through the specific manufacturer guidelines even if you’ve done this before—they often have unique clips, mounting systems, or washer requirements.

Address Tough Installations

Narrow cabinet spaces and unusually thick countertops may call for a helper or longer basin wrenches. If your sink is double-bowled, work from the side with the most room. For undermount sinks, try placing a folded towel to cushion your back and arms.

Plan for Disposal

Wrap your old faucet in newspaper to keep sharp edges contained, and check if local recycling regulations accept metal fixtures.

6. Common Kitchen Faucet Installation Questions

What if my new faucet doesn’t match my sink holes?

Adapters or hole covers (escutcheons) can mask unused holes. Just make sure the cover is wide enough for your sink style.

How do I avoid leaks after installation?

Always use Teflon tape on male-threaded pipe fittings and tighten connections just until firm—you want to avoid over-tightening, which can crack connectors.

Can I install a touchless faucet myself?

Yes—if you’re comfortable wiring battery packs or low-voltage adapters. These faucets have extra wiring but the mounting process is similar. Just follow the instructions carefully.

Is swapping a faucet a one-person job?

Usually, yes. However, if the old faucet is rusted or you have minimal under-sink access, a second set of hands helps.

7. Troubleshooting Tips for Common Issues

Low Water Pressure

If you notice weak flow, check for a clogged aerator or debris in the supply lines. Flush the lines and clean or replace the aerator. If both sides (hot and cold) are equally weak, double-check the supply valves are fully open.

Persistent Leaks

Tighten connections incrementally. If this doesn’t fix the issue, take apart the connection, re-tape the threads, and reseat any washers or O-rings. Persistent leaks from the faucet base mean the gasket or putty isn’t properly sealed—lift the faucet and reapply.

Faucet Handle Feels Loose

Under-sink mounting nuts may require an extra turn. For handle play at the fixture itself, consult the manufacturer instructions for tightening a set screw—often accessible under a decorative cap.

8. Enjoy Your Upgraded Sink

A new kitchen faucet can refresh your entire kitchen look—often in under an hour, and with a satisfying sense of accomplishment. The process is well within the reach of most DIYers. With the steps above and a little attention to detail, you can enjoy smoother operation, leak-free performance, and a fresh new look at the busiest spot in your kitchen.

So, whether you’re simply replacing a worn-out fixture or transforming your kitchen’s focal point, installing a new kitchen faucet is one upgrade that pays off fast. Roll up your sleeves, gather your tools, and see just how easy a major kitchen improvement can be.
