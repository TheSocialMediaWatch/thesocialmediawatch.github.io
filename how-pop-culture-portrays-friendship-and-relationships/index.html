How Pop Culture Portrays Friendship and Relationships


Pop culture—through TV shows, movies, books, songs, and even memes—serves as both mirror and architect of our social bonds. When we talk about how popular media depicts friendship and relationships, we’re really talking about how our collective imagination grapples with love, trust, loyalty, betrayal, and everything in between. The portrayal of these connections not only colors our expectations but often shapes how we experience and understand relationships in the real world. Take a seat: we’re about to break down how pop culture frames, challenges, romanticizes, and sometimes even warps the way we see our friends and our partners.

The BFF Trope: From Supportive Sidekick to Complex Character

For decades, one of the most enduring images in film and television has been the best friend. Think of Samwise Gamgee in “The Lord of the Rings,” always ready to carry Frodo (literally and emotionally) when the journey gets tough. Or the wittier, modern duo of Tina Fey and Amy Poehler, whose real-life friendship seeps into their on-screen chemistry and comedic timing. Early portrayals of best friends often painted them as loyal support systems with little narrative agency of their own. The protagonist took center stage, while the friend played confidante, comic relief, or designated driver.

However, today’s pop culture landscape isn’t content with sidekick stereotypes. There’s a greater hunger for emotionally complex, messy, and dynamic friendships. In series like “Insecure,” Issa and Molly’s friendship is just as central—and as deeply analyzed—as any romantic subplot. Their conflicts, vulnerability, and growth together reflect the reality that friendship isn’t always about unshakable loyalty but can be complicated, uncomfortable, and even transformative. This pivot shines a light on friendship as a form of intimacy on par with romance—a refreshing recognition that often resonates with audiences who’ve experienced similar highs and lows.

Sitcom Bonds: Laugh Tracks and Life Lessons

No discussion of friendship in pop culture would be complete without mentioning the tectonic influence of sitcoms. From “Friends” and “How I Met Your Mother” to “The Big Bang Theory” and “Parks and Recreation,” these shows build universes on the foundation of close-knit groups, where apartments and coffee shops become the backdrop for life’s most important conversations.

Sitcom representations of friendship often glamorize spontaneous hangouts and unwavering loyalty through life’s changes, inviting viewers into a stylized version of togetherness. But there’s a flip side: these portrayals sometimes set unrealistic standards. The constant presence, the six-person friend group with perfect chemistry, or the notion that your college buddies will remain your core support system into your thirties—all presented with quick problem resolution and enduring humor—can sometimes make real-life friendships, with their ebbs, flows, and periods of distance, seem less than optimal. Yet, the enduring popularity of these tropes signals how much audiences crave community, laughter, and a sense of belonging.

Romance on Screen: From Fairy Tales to Fleabag

Love stories are the beating heart of much of pop culture. Early narratives leaned toward simple dichotomies: the Disney princess finds her prince, opposites attract, or the high school underdog wins over the prom queen. These classic arcs still surface, but representation is shifting. Audiences want more honest, sometimes raw stories.

Shows like “Fleabag” and “Normal People” dig into the awkwardness, the miscommunication, and the profound longing found in romantic relationships. Films such as “Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind” challenge viewers to reflect on memory, regret, and the cyclical nature of love. Modern romance narratives recognize the impermanence and imperfection of relationships; they’re just as likely to end in mutual growth as in forever-after marriage.

Pop songs, once obsessed with everlasting love (“I Will Always Love You,” “Endless Love”), now often focus on messy breakups, desire, and personal boundaries. There’s a trend toward acknowledging the parts of romance that are less about the relationship and more about individual growth.

The Role of Diversity: Expanding the Relationship Canon

Not too long ago, mainstream pop culture was notorious for offering a narrow lens on relationships—predominantly heterosexual, often white, rarely nuanced. That’s changing, if slowly. Today, you find a much broader spectrum of partnerships, friendships, and family bonds depicted, thanks to creators pushing for both representation and authenticity.

Series like “Pose,” “Sex Education,” and “Master of None” take on love, intimacy, and friendship through queer, trans, and non-Western lenses. These stories validate experiences that long went unseen or were caricatured, fostering empathy and reflection among wider audiences. Friendship groups in shows like “Brooklyn Nine-Nine” and “Orange Is the New Black” foreground diversity as a dynamic reality, shaping interactions and conflicts in a way that feels truer to modern life.

This broader representation challenges the “one-size-fits-all” model of friendship and romance. As viewers see themselves reflected in varied experiences—long-distance romance, chosen family, asexual or aromantic connections—they gain permission to write their own scripts, forging relationships that suit their needs, not just societal expectations.

The Influence of Social Media: #RelationshipGoals and Real Talk

Social platforms have added another layer to how we process pop culture narratives about friendship and love. Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter don’t just amplify existing stories—they create micro-celebrities and meme-able moments from relationship milestones, breakups, and best-friend banter. The hashtag culture—think #SquadGoals or #CoupleGoals—often sets aspirational standards for what companionship “should” look like, even as users increasingly call out the unrealistic or staged nature of many posts.

Viral videos and user-generated content sometimes subvert traditional narratives. Instead of glossy perfection, there’s space for awkward, real moments: group chats gone wrong, messy roommate drama, or brutally honest stories about the ups and downs of loving (and sometimes loathing) your people. These stories can be more relatable than what’s on TV, but they come with their own pressures to perform or curate friendship for public consumption.

Toxicity and the Dark Side of Friendship: Frenemies and Betrayal

Pop culture isn’t all about support and loyalty. From mean-girl cliques in “Heathers” and “Mean Girls” to betrayals in dramas like “Gossip Girl,” the depiction of toxic friendship dynamics makes for compelling drama. These stories tap into universal fears—being excluded, outgrown, or hurt by those closest to you.

The rise of the “frenemy” archetype reflects a cultural awareness of the grey area between friend and rival. Shows like “Killing Eve” or “Big Little Lies” put complex, sometimes even dangerous, relationships under the microscope, revealing how power, competition, and envy can poison partnerships that should, in theory, be supportive.

Yet, even these stories can serve a purpose: they offer cautionary tales, spark conversation about conflict resolution, red flags, and the ambiguous territory between closeness and competition. They remind us that not all friendships are meant to last and, sometimes, breaking away is the healthiest option.

Family as Friends: Chosen Family and Found Community

Not every profound relationship is romantic or rooted in childhood friendship. Pop culture increasingly spotlights “chosen family”—those friends, colleagues, or neighbors who become just as central as any blood relation. Sitcoms like “The Golden Girls,” “Will & Grace,” and “Schitt’s Creek” delight in the chaos and love of cobbled-together communities.

This focus aligns with the reality that, for many, traditional family structures may be absent, strained, or insufficient. Pop culture’s embrace of chosen family provides comfort and validation: it’s okay to build your own circle of trust. Stories about unconventional families offer hope that belonging and love are possible outside prescribed molds.

How These Portrayals Shape (and Sometimes Skew) Our Expectations

Pop culture doesn’t just imitate real life—it shapes what viewers expect from friendship and romance. Binge-watching shows that resolve decades of drama in a 22-minute episode can set up the false notion that conflict is always easily fixed. Sweeping reunions at airports—cue the John Hughes music—fuel the belief that grand gestures are the soul of true love, when quietly showing up day after day often matters more.

Repeated exposure to certain storylines—endless forgiveness, ride-or-die loyalty, picture-perfect proposals—can make it hard to accept the messiness and imperfection that come with real connections. Just as often, though, stories offer new models: friends who drift but reconnect, couples who communicate honestly, or people who thrive in communities they built themselves. Pop culture gives us permission to hope, mess up, try again, and love in ways that work for us individually, not just collectively.

The Future: Where Do Friendship and Relationship Narratives Go Next?

As new platforms, voices, and genres enter the pop culture stage, what does the future look like? Audiences want, and increasingly demand, stories that reflect the wide variety of friendships and relationships they experience. That means more space for stories about non-traditional companionships—platonic life partnerships, cross-generational friendships, polyamory, co-parenting, or simply the joy (and struggle) of deep friendships that defy easy categorization.

The best and boldest creative works will keep wrestling with questions as old as time: What does it mean to belong? How do we forgive each other? When is it time to walk away? With a growing appetite for authenticity, nuance, and diversity, pop culture’s take on friendship and relationships is poised to become even more layered, humane, and satisfying for the generations to come.

Friendship and love will always be the lifeblood of our stories. The more honestly we tell them, the more we learn about ourselves. Now, as ever, pop culture is along for the ride—sometimes leading the way, sometimes lagging behind, but always reflecting our hope that no one has to face the world alone.
