The Underworld in Mythology: A Journey to the Afterlife


Sprawling beneath the roots of ancient worlds, the underworld stands as a realm of profound mystery and eternal influence. No matter the civilization or era, the question lingers: what happens after death? For centuries, myths and legends have painted vivid pictures of journeys beneath the living soil—shadowy domains where hope and dread intermingle, and the souls of the departed seek their destinies.

Within the vast landscape of mythology, the underworld is not just one place. It is a thousand lands shaped by a thousand imaginations. From the sunless plains of Hades to the labyrinthine depths of the Egyptian Duat, every culture has shaped its underworld according to its dreams, fears, and moral codes. To undertake a journey to the afterlife in myth is not simply to pass from life to death but to traverse a landscape of the soul, face cosmic judgment, and encounter both dread and wonder.

Let’s embark on our own mythic voyage—a guided tour through the myriad underworlds that populate humanity’s stories, examining their origins, rulers, notable residents, and the meaning these shadowy lands hold.

The Origins of Underworld Myths

Long before the written word, ancient peoples sat around fires and wondered: “Where do we go when the breath leaves our bodies?” The underworld arose as a response to that eternal question—part explanation, part cautionary tale.

Early burial practices suggest that belief in an afterlife predates civilization itself. Graves lined with goods, tools, and artworks hint at preparation for a journey after death. As cultures evolved, so did their ideas of what lay beneath. The underworld became not only an answer to the great unknown but a mirror reflecting a society’s values and fears.

The Greek Underworld: Hades and the Rivers of Sorrow

Few mythological landscapes are as recognizable as the Greek underworld. Situated below the surface world, the domain of Hades is a shadowy, complex territory governed by strict laws and overseen by memorable figures.

Geography of the Greek Underworld

Imagine a land sheltered in permanent twilight, traversed by five rivers encircling the terrain like serpentine barriers:

  • Styx: The river of hatred, why even the gods swore oaths on its relentless current.
  • Acheron: Called the river of woe, it ferried souls across its deep, resonant waters.
  • Lethe: The river of forgetfulness, whose draught erased memories of mortal life.
  • Phlegethon: A flaming torrent, forever burning but never consumed.
  • Cocytus: The river of lamentation, where the wails of the damned never cease.

At the gates stands Cerberus, the three-headed dog, ever-watchful and hungry for souls wishing to escape. Newly arrived spirits pay Charon, the ferryman, before crossing into the land of the dead—a practice echoed in Greek burial rituals, with coins placed upon the eyes or mouths of the deceased.

Rulers of Hades

At the center sits Hades himself, stern but not evil, married to Persephone, the queen whose annual return to the surface brings spring to the living. Together, they preside over a court where notable judges—Minos, Rhadamanthys, and Aeacus—decide the fates of arriving souls.

Afterlife and Judgment

In myth, the underworld is not only a resting place but sometimes a trial. Great sinners receive punishment in Tartarus, an even deeper abyss reserved for the wicked. Meanwhile, those of virtue may find themselves in the Elysian Fields, a land of eternal delight. For most, however, existence in the asphodel meadows is dull and dreamlike—a pale reflection of mortal life.

Egyptian Duat: A Journey Through Darkness

Egyptian mythology offers a stunningly detailed vision of the afterlife. Unlike the Greek underworld’s static gloom, the Egyptian Duat is dynamic, perilous—a journey that every soul must undertake, equipped with spells and moral righteousness.

Passage Through the Duat

The journey begins at death and follows a soul as it sails through a night-long passage fraught with monsters, gates, and tests. Protected by the spells contained in the Book of the Dead, the soul must answer riddles, recite passwords, and avoid terrors lurking in darkness.

The climax of this perilous journey is the weighing of the heart. In the Hall of Ma’at, the goddess of truth, the heart of the deceased is balanced against the feather of Ma’at. Souls too heavy with sin are devoured by the monster Ammit, erased from existence. Those found worthy join Osiris in the Field of Reeds, a blissful version of everyday life perfected.

Order and Chaos

Underlying Egypt’s afterlife journey is a cosmic battle between order and chaos. Osiris, lord of the dead, is a symbol of renewal, reigning with a silent dignity over a realm that is ever-shifting but always guided by rules. The journey through the underworld is thus not only personal redemption for the soul but also a reaffirmation of the cosmic order the Egyptians so prized.

Norse Realms: Hel and the Many Faces of the Dead

The Norse cosmos is a sprawling, nine-world tree, with the underworld known as Hel resting in its shadowed roots. Governed by Hel, daughter of Loki, this land is both literal and metaphoric—a cold domain for those who did not die in battle.

The Structure of Hel

Hel is a vast, misty region, neither a place of endless torment nor absolute peace. Unlike Valhalla, reserved for warriors, Hel receives ordinary souls—those who died of sickness or old age. Here, existence continued, though often bleak and comfortless.

But not everyone in the North is destined for Hel or Valhalla. Poetic sagas mention lands like Folkvangr, ruled by Freyja, and even realms of utter darkness and punishment for the truly wicked. Yet, unlike the strict moral judgments found in other traditions, Norse afterlife is less concerned with moral worth and more about fate—the hand the Norns have dealt.

The Journey to Hel

The way to Hel is described as a descent along the Helveg (Hel-way), a road traveled by all, regardless of their life, save the warriors whisked away by Valkyries. Along the way appear guardian hounds, bridges of gold, and formidable gates. The dead arrive not to be judged for sins, but simply to dwell in the company of Hel and her kin, waiting for the world’s end at Ragnarök.

The Mesopotamian Underworld: Land of No Return

Among the world’s oldest myths, Mesopotamian tales describe the afterlife as a somber and immutable fate.

The Descent of Inanna

Perhaps the most famous story is the descent of Inanna (later known as Ishtar), goddess of love and war. Driven by desire, pride, or the need to witness power, she passes through seven gates, each removing her worldly possessions. Within the Great Below, ruled by her dark sister Ereshkigal, Inanna is stripped bare, powerless and subject to judgment. Her eventual return is a tale of sacrifice, death, and rebirth, echoing the annual cycle of fertility and the land.

Irkalla: The Great Below

For ordinary souls, the afterlife is the “Land of No Return.” Here, shades eat dust and drink muddy water, stripped of individuality. The Mesopotamian underworld is a place of faded memory, where even kings and heroes cannot escape their fate. There is little hope of redemption—but much wisdom to be found in the acceptance of mortality.

Mesoamerican Underworlds: Xibalba and Mictlan

Moving across the globe, Mesoamerican myth paints its own vibrant images of the underworld.

Xibalba: The Maya Place of Fear

For the ancient Maya, Xibalba (“Place of Fright”) beckoned from beneath the earth. Here, a realm ruled by sinister gods challenged the souls of the dead with cruel games and obstacles. Only the cunning would survive, as told in the Popol Vuh, where heroic twins Hun Hunahpu and Xbalanque trick and outwit the lords of Xibalba.

The journey was grueling—trials by darkness, by sharp blades, by blood rivers and scorpions. Only those who possessed the right knowledge, and perhaps a share of luck, could reach the place of rest beyond.

Mictlan: The Aztec Underworld

The Aztec afterlife, Mictlan, stretched across nine levels, each demanding the departing soul to overcome challenges. Death was less a punishment and more a rite of passage, a test of spirit on the way toward final sleep. Aztec mythology maps out this journey with vivid imagery: mountains that clash together, winds that cut flesh, obsidian hills. The souls’ destination depended on how they died—warriors could ascend to the sun, while most made their slow way to Mictlan.

Themes and Motifs: What the Underworld Means

Despite their differences, underworld myths share striking similarities:

  1. A Great Journey: Whether a hero descends in life (Orpheus, Odysseus, Inanna) or every soul must travel after death, the journey is as important as the destination. The afterlife is not a static existence but a progression—sometimes perilous, sometimes redemptive.

  2. Rites and Rituals: Myths are reflected in rituals—funerary offerings, guide spells, appeasements to chthonic (underworld) gods. The living seek to aid the dead, even influence their fate.

  3. Judgment and Justice: Many underworlds are places of judgment—sins weighed, deeds remembered, the soul rewarded or punished. Yet, the form of justice differs. Greek souls might wander forever, Egyptians face the scales, and Norse souls drift, anonymous, until the world ends.

  4. Mirror to the Living: The underworld always mirrors the hopes, ethics, and fears of its creators. Where societies value order, the underworld is ruled by codes. Where life is uncertain, the land of the dead may be capricious or bleak.

The Hero’s Descent: Katabasis and Transformation

The so-called “descent to the underworld,” or katabasis, is a classic theme in myth and literature. This is no mere death but an archetypal journey—sometimes literal, sometimes symbolic. Heroes and gods alike enter the underworld seeking wisdom, power, or lost loved ones. Few return unchanged.

  • Orpheus braves Hades to reclaim Eurydice, turning back for a forbidden glance.
  • Odysseus visits the shades to consult the prophet Tiresias, gaining essential knowledge for his voyage home.
  • Inanna/Ishtar descends and is reborn, ensuring fertility and cosmic renewal.

These stories emphasize that confrontation with death, with the shadow self or the unknown, can yield transformation. The underworld is a crucible—its fires shape those who survive.

Enduring Influence: The Underworld Today

Why do these stories persist? The idea of the underworld runs deep—it anchors our fears of oblivion and expresses our hope for meaning. Whether through Dante’s Inferno or modern tales of ghosts and vampires, the world below remains a source of fascination.

Contemporary literature, films, and religions continue to revisit themes of afterlife journeys, cosmic justice, and encounters with death. The imagery of rivers, gates, judges, and journeys shape everything from popular song lyrics to philosophical essays.

Even as science seeks empirical answers to death, the metaphorical underworld endures as a way to reckon with our own mortality. Myths give shape to questions too deep for data alone—What do we leave behind? What must we face? What, if anything, comes next?

Conclusion: The Journey Continues

The underworld, in all its guises, is less a destination than a journey through the imagination. Each culture sculpts its own map, peopled with ancient gods, fierce guardians, and ordinary souls driven by the universal desire to defy oblivion and find meaning in the transition from life to death.

To study the underworld in mythology is to walk alongside countless others on the greatest journey of all. These stories do not claim to explain every mystery, but they remind us that, across time and place, humanity has always faced the unknown with courage, creativity, and a longing for understanding—and in the stories of the underworld, found a way to keep journeying, even after the last breath is drawn.
