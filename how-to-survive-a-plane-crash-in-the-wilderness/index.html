How to Survive a Plane Crash in the Wilderness


Imagine the unimaginable: your plane goes down in the wilderness. Whether it’s a dense forest, a vast desert, or an isolated mountain range, surviving a plane crash in such conditions requires quick thinking, practical skills, and a calm mindset. While it’s a rare and frightening scenario, knowing how to respond can drastically improve your chances of survival. Here’s a comprehensive guide to help you prepare for the worst and increase your chances of making it out alive.

Step 1: Surviving the Initial Impact

The first few moments after a crash are crucial. The moment you realize the plane is going down, your priority should be minimizing injuries. Here’s what to do:

Brace for Impact

  • Sit upright: In most crashes, sitting upright with your back against the seat is the safest position. Tuck your head down and place your hands over your head or secure them under your legs.
  • Strap yourself in: If you’re seated, ensure your seatbelt is securely fastened. Tighten it as much as possible to reduce the chances of being thrown from your seat during the crash.
  • Protect your head: Use any available clothing or padding to shield your head from impact. If possible, wrap your arms around your torso to brace against sudden forces.

Escape the Wreckage

Once the plane has come to a stop, it’s essential to exit quickly:

  • Stay calm: The initial shock of the crash might disorient you. Take a deep breath and assess your surroundings.
  • Locate the nearest exit: Know where the nearest emergency exits are. If the cabin is filled with smoke or flames, it’s important to stay low to the ground as you move toward the exit.
  • Don’t hesitate: The faster you get out of the wreckage, the better. However, avoid rushing blindly, as debris or fire may pose a threat.

Step 2: Assess Your Injuries

Once you’ve exited the plane, your next task is to assess yourself and others for injuries. It’s critical to check for broken bones, deep cuts, or head trauma. Here’s how to handle injuries in a survival scenario:

Self-Assess and First Aid

  • Check for bleeding: Apply pressure to any visible wounds to stop bleeding. If you have a shirt or any cloth, tear it into strips to create makeshift bandages.
  • Splint fractures: If you or a fellow survivor has a broken limb, improvise a splint using nearby materials like sticks, belts, or clothing.
  • Keep wounds clean: Infection can be a major concern, so try to keep any wounds as clean as possible using available resources like water or clean clothing.

Prioritize Care

If there are multiple survivors, prioritize the most seriously injured. Basic first aid can mean the difference between life and death, so don’t hesitate to help others even if you’re injured yourself.

Step 3: Make a Shelter

The wilderness can be harsh, and you need shelter to survive. Whether it’s day or night, exposure to the elements can quickly cause hypothermia or dehydration. The first shelter you create doesn’t have to be perfect, but it should provide some form of protection from the elements.

Location, Location, Location

  • Find high ground: Try to avoid low-lying areas that could flood if it rains. A higher spot will also make it easier for rescuers to spot you.
  • Consider wind direction: You don’t want to build your shelter in the wind’s path, so pick a spot that is sheltered by rocks, trees, or other natural barriers.

Building a Shelter

  • Use plane debris: The wreckage of the plane can be used to build shelter. Pieces of metal or fabric can help create a protective cover.
  • Use natural materials: Branches, leaves, and grass can be used to construct a makeshift shelter. A lean-to, a small hut made by leaning branches against a tree or rock, can be a great option.
  • Insulation is key: Gather as many leaves, grass, and other plant materials as you can to insulate the shelter and protect against the cold.

Step 4: Signal for Help

One of your primary concerns after a plane crash is making sure rescuers can find you. The first step is to assess your surroundings for any obvious signs of rescue. But if you’re in a remote wilderness, you will need to take the initiative to create signals.

Smoke Signals

  • Use fire carefully: Fire is one of the most effective signals, but it can also be dangerous. Gather dry materials and build a fire away from your shelter. The smoke will help signal for help. Use green vegetation like pine needles to create thick smoke.
  • Create a smoky signal: If possible, make sure the fire is large enough that it can be seen from a distance.

Reflective Surfaces

  • Mirrors and shiny objects: If you have anything shiny, use it to reflect the sun’s light. Even a metal watch or a piece of the wreckage can be used to catch the sun’s rays and signal an aircraft or ground searchers.
  • Create large, visible signs: If you’re near the wreckage, use large pieces of the plane’s body or fabric to spell out SOS or another recognizable distress signal.

Use Sounds to Alert Rescuers

  • Whistles or loud noises: A whistle can travel much farther than your voice. Blow it every few minutes to maintain your presence. If you don’t have a whistle, yell every so often.

Step 5: Gather Water

Dehydration is one of the leading causes of death in wilderness survival situations. Without water, you can only survive for a few days at best. Here’s how to secure clean drinking water:

Find Water Sources

  • Look for streams or rivers: If you’re near a water source, it’s the best place to get fresh water. Avoid stagnant water as it may contain harmful bacteria.
  • Rainwater: In the absence of visible water sources, rainwater is your next best option. Create a container by using any materials from the wreckage or natural resources to catch rain.

Purify Water

Even if you find water, it may not be safe to drink without purifying it. If you can make a fire, you can boil the water to kill harmful bacteria. Alternatively, if you have any purification tablets or filters, now is the time to use them.

Step 6: Find Food

After ensuring you have water, your next priority is food. While you can survive for weeks without food, it’s crucial to find something to eat to maintain energy.

Foraging for Edible Plants

  • Know what’s edible: If you are familiar with the plants of the area, start foraging for fruits, nuts, and edible plants. Avoid anything that looks unfamiliar or suspicious. If you’re not sure, it’s better to leave it alone.
  • Insects as food: If plant options are limited, insects like ants, grasshoppers, or beetles can be a great source of protein. While not ideal, they are highly nutritious in a survival situation.

Trapping and Fishing

  • Improvise traps: If you have the tools or resources, you can create basic traps to catch small animals like rodents or birds. Create snares with cords or string if available.
  • Fishing: If you’re near a water source, fishing can provide a reliable food source. You can improvise fishing hooks or use sharp objects to spear fish.

Step 7: Maintain Mental Toughness

Surviving a plane crash in the wilderness isn’t just about physical survival; your mental state plays a huge role. Staying calm and positive is vital in such a stressful and life-threatening situation. Here’s how you can manage:

Stay Focused on the Present

Focus on the immediate tasks at hand, such as securing shelter, water, and food. Breaking the situation down into manageable tasks can reduce anxiety.

Keep Hope Alive

Remember that the chances of rescue are high in modern-day aviation, especially if you’ve been able to signal. Keep your morale up by telling yourself that help will come.

Build a Routine

Having a routine can help you maintain a sense of normalcy. Set goals for each day, whether it’s finding a better shelter or checking your signals.

Step 8: Be Prepared for the Long Haul

The longer you remain stranded in the wilderness, the more challenging your survival becomes. Be prepared to endure for weeks if necessary. Plan ahead, ration your food and water, and stay as healthy as possible.

Conclusion

Surviving a plane crash in the wilderness is a daunting experience, but with the right mindset and preparation, you can vastly improve your chances of survival. By focusing on your immediate needs—shelter, water, food, and signaling for help—you can ensure that you’ll be able to stay alive until rescue arrives. While it may feel like an overwhelming challenge, remember that survival is a matter of resourcefulness, calmness, and determination.
