How to Increase Your Running Speed for Track and Field


Whether you’re a seasoned sprinter aiming for a personal best or a young athlete eager to make an impact on the track, increasing your running speed is a priority for success in track and field. Achieving faster speeds requires more than just showing up for practice—it’s about optimizing your training, technique, and overall fitness. Here are actionable strategies to help you boost your running speed and maximize your athletic potential.

Perfect Your Running Form

The foundation of faster running lies in impeccable form. Inefficient movements can waste energy, slowing you down. Focus on the following elements to refine your technique:

Posture

Maintain an upright posture with your head held high and your eyes looking straight ahead. Avoid slouching or leaning too far forward, as this can affect your balance and stride efficiency.

Arm Movement

Your arms play a crucial role in propelling you forward. Keep your elbows bent at approximately 90 degrees, swinging them forward and backward in sync with your legs. Ensure your hands remain relaxed—tight fists or rigid movements can waste energy.

Foot Strike

Fine-tune the way your feet hit the ground. Aim for a midfoot strike instead of landing on your heels, as this helps you generate more force while minimizing impact. Push off powerfully with your toes to create explosive forward motion.

Cadence

Incorporate a fast, consistent cadence into your stride. Studies show that quicker, shorter steps reduce the risk of injury and allow you to maintain speed for longer durations.

Develop Explosive Strength

Speed on the track is heavily dependent on power. Athletes who excel in sprinting and mid-distance events often prioritize strength training to complement their running workouts. Here are some critical approaches to building strength:

Weight Training

Incorporate lower-body strength exercises like squats, lunges, deadlifts, and leg presses. These moves strengthen your glutes, hamstrings, quads, and calves—muscles vital for sprinting and accelerating. Compound lifts like cleans and snatches also develop explosive power and can improve your explosiveness off the starting blocks.

Plyometric Exercises

Plyometric training focuses on executing powerful movements in a short period, helping you develop the elasticity and strength needed for speed. Exercises like box jumps, bounding, and single-leg hops can improve your reaction time and stride length.

Core Workouts

While much of the focus in running speed is on the legs, a strong core allows for better stability and transfer of power. Implement workouts like planks, Russian twists, and medicine ball rotations to improve your overall running mechanics.

Enhance Your Aerobic and Anaerobic Endurance

Track and field athletes, especially those competing in events longer than sprints, require a mix of aerobic and anaerobic capabilities. Improving both aspects will not only help you run faster but also help you sustain speed over time.

Interval Training

One of the most effective ways to increase your running speed is through interval workouts. Alternate between periods of extreme effort and recovery; for example, sprint for 30 seconds followed by 90 seconds of jogging. These training sessions improve both your speed and endurance while mimicking the rigors of competition.

Hill Sprints

Short hill sprints build strength and explosive power in your legs while challenging your cardiovascular system. Make sure to run at full effort uphill, then use the downhill jog as active recovery.

Tempo Runs

Incorporate tempo runs into your routine to increase your lactate threshold—essential for maintaining speed over time. These runs should be at a “comfortably hard” pace for 20–30 minutes, allowing your body to adapt to sustained effort.

Focus on Speed Drills

Speed drills are vital in maximizing your ability to sprint and accelerate. Incorporate these exercises regularly into your training routine to fine-tune your mechanics and increase efficiency.

Start Drills

Powerful starts can make all the difference in short sprint events. Practice explosive starts by focusing on driving your legs and extending your stride quickly right off the blocks.

Strides

Strides are controlled sprints that help you develop proper mechanics at higher speeds. Run about 50–100 meters at approximately 85–90% of your maximum speed, focusing especially on form.

Overspeed Training

Overspeed drills, such as running on a slight downhill slope, encourage faster turnover and stride length. Be cautious during these exercises to avoid injury and maintain control.

Nutrition and Recovery Practices

While training hard is important, optimizing your nutrition and recovery practices is equally crucial for speed gains. The demands of track and field require a comprehensive approach to fueling and caring for your body.

Fuel Your Body

Provide your muscles with the fuel they need by consuming a balanced diet rich in carbohydrates, lean protein, and healthy fats. Carbs supply energy for intense workouts and races, while protein supports muscle repair and growth.

Hydration

Dehydration can significantly undermine your performance. Ensure you’re drinking enough water throughout the day and add electrolyte-rich drinks during or after intense workouts to replenish lost minerals.

Sleep

Quality sleep is essential for muscle recovery and energy restoration. Aim for 7–9 hours per night to allow your body to recover from training sessions and build strength.

Active Recovery

Schedule active recovery days where you engage in low-impact activities like cycling, swimming, or yoga. This keeps your muscles loose and helps prevent burnout, allowing you to maintain peak performance over time.

Mental Preparation and Goal Setting

Your mindset plays a significant role in setting new speed records on the track. Being mentally prepared and setting realistic yet ambitious goals will help you stay motivated and focused.

Visualize Success

Spend time visualizing yourself running with perfect form and achieving new personal bests. Mental imagery can help you feel prepared and focused before a race or challenging workout.

Set SMART Goals

Define Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound goals to fuel your progress. Rather than simply aiming to “run faster,” break it down. For example, you could aim to lower your 400-meter sprint time by 3 seconds in 4 weeks.

Positive Self-Talk

Replace doubt with confidence through positive affirmations and self-talk. Telling yourself you’re capable of improvement can solidify your belief in your abilities and translate into faster performance.

Consistency is Key

Finally, remember that improvements in running speed take time. Consistently follow your plan, listen to your body, and make adjustments as needed. Monitor your progress and celebrate small victories, as these incremental gains will eventually lead to significant improvements.

Running faster for track and field doesn’t come easily, but through dedicated training, proper technique, and a smart approach to nutrition and recovery, you can reach new heights. Commit to the process, trust your abilities, and watch yourself surpass your limits on the track!
