How to Use Dreams and Visions in Fantasy Storytelling


Dreams and visions hold a special place in fantasy storytelling, serving as powerful narrative tools to deepen character development, foreshadow events, and enrich world-building. They allow writers to explore intangible realms beyond reality, where symbolism, subconscious fears, and mystical revelations intermingle. Creating compelling dream sequences or haunting visions requires balancing surrealism with narrative clarity—something that every aspiring fantasy author can master with practice and understanding.

The Unique Appeal of Dreams and Visions in Fantasy

Fantasy worlds naturally embrace elements beyond the ordinary, making dreams and visions an organic extension of their mystical and otherworldly qualities. These elements can:

  • Reveal internal conflicts: Dreams provide insight into a character’s psyche, showing fears, desires, or memories they might suppress.
  • Foreshadow future events: Visions often hint at what’s to come, creating suspense and guiding the plot.
  • Introduce magical or prophetic elements: In many fantasy settings, visions are gifts or curses from supernatural forces, connecting the mundane world with the mystical.
  • Expand lore and world-building: Dreams can contain symbolic imagery that reflects the mythology or history of the fantasy world.

Using dreams and visions effectively makes your storytelling richer and more immersive, helping readers connect emotionally and intellectually.

Crafting Dreams: The Gateway to the Subconscious

Dreams in fantasy can serve many functions, from vivid nightmares that unsettle a hero to cryptic messages from the spirit world. To craft memorable dream sequences, consider the following:

1. Define the Purpose of the Dream

Start by asking what the dream accomplishes in your story. Is it an emotional release, a puzzle, or a foreshadowing device? A dream that simply confuses the reader without adding value risks breaking immersion. For example:

  • Revealing character background: A character might relive a forgotten trauma or glimpse a hidden desire.
  • Introducing mystery: Strange, symbolic images can plant questions that intrigue readers.
  • Catalyzing plot action: A prophetic dream could push a hero toward a quest or reveal a villain’s plan.

2. Use Symbolism and Imagery Thoughtfully

Dreams thrive on symbolism, but it should be meaningful rather than random. Let recurring symbols reflect themes or character traits:

  • A broken sword may symbolize lost honor.
  • A flickering flame could represent hope or life’s fragility.
  • Shadows might hint at hidden dangers or the character’s dark past.

Employ sensory details—sights blurred by mist, echoing voices, or unsettling silences—to evoke the dream’s mood and immerse readers fully.

3. Play with Surrealism—but Maintain Clarity

The dream world is a space where the laws of reality can bend or break. Objects can shift shapes, time can flow strangely, and logic can twist. However, for readers to stay invested, provide anchors that prevent the dream from becoming impenetrable.

Techniques include:

  • Familiar elements: Incorporate recognizable settings or faces to ground the dream.
  • Emotional consistency: Even if the events are surreal, the emotions should ring true.
  • Repeating motifs: Use motifs to guide readers through the dream’s meaning.

4. Connect the Dream to the Waking World

A dream gains significance when it impacts the character outside of sleep. After the dream, show how it affects their decisions, emotions, or beliefs. Perhaps a hero wakes with a sense of urgency or confusion that propels the narrative.

Using Visions: Mystical Glimpses into Fantasy Worlds

Visions differ from dreams in that they often have a divine or supernatural origin, bestowed upon the character consciously or involuntarily. In fantasy, they help weave the mystical into the everyday realm.

1. Establish the Source and Stakes of the Vision

Clarify why your character experiences visions in your fantasy world. Is it a gift from a god, a curse from a witch, or a byproduct of magical artifacts? Understanding this foundation shapes how characters interpret and respond to their visions.

Visions often carry stakes—warnings about disaster or glimpses of victory. Highlighting these stakes creates tension:

  • Ignoring a vision could have dire consequences.
  • Deciphering the vision’s cryptic symbols might unlock crucial information.

2. Vary the Forms of Visions

Visions need not be limited to static images. They can manifest through:

  • Auditory hallucinations: Voices speaking prophecies or secrets.
  • Physical sensations: A sudden chill or warmth signaling magic nearby.
  • Symbolic dreams: Visions that overlap with dream states.
  • Omniscient insight: A fleeting but overwhelming awareness of hidden truths.

These variations keep visions fresh and engaging.

3. Impact on Character Arcs

Define how visions shape your character’s growth. Do they embrace their role as seers or resist the burden of prophecy? Perhaps the visions lead to self-doubt or madness, adding depth and complexity.

4. Use Visions to Drive Plot and Worldbuilding

Visions can reveal hidden layers of your fantasy world, such as ancient histories, secret societies, or cosmic forces at play. For example:

  • A vision might show the ruins of a lost civilization, prompting an expedition.
  • It could expose a villain’s motive hidden from other characters.
  • Spiritual visions might convey the cosmology or ethical framework of your world.

Balancing Dreams and Visions with Storytelling Dynamics

When integrating dreams and visions into fantasy stories, balance is key. Overusing these elements can disrupt pacing and frustrate readers, while underusing them wastes their potential.

1. Timing and Frequency

Introduce dreams and visions at moments when insight is needed or tension peaks. Avoid excessive sequences that do not push the narrative forward. For instance, a single crucial vision before a climactic battle is more impactful than frequent ambiguous dreams.

2. Maintaining Narrative Clarity

Ensure that the dream or vision sequences serve the reader’s understanding. Avoid heavy-handed exposition or overly obscure symbolism. Aim for a balance between mystery and meaning.

3. Integration with Character POV

Filter dreams and visions through your character’s perspective. Their personality, fears, and culture influence interpretation. A superstitious character may see dreams as omens, while a skeptic may dismiss them, which can create internal conflict.

Examples of Dreams and Visions in Fantasy Literature

To better understand the effective use of dreams and visions, consider how renowned authors employ them:

  • J.R.R. Tolkien’s Middle-earth: Characters sometimes experience prophetic dreams that foreshadow battles or reveal dark secrets, using symbolism and emotional resonance rather than direct exposition.
  • George R.R. Martin’s A Song of Ice and Fire: Bran Stark’s visions tie deeply into the mythology and magic of the world, offering glimpses across time and space that guide key plot developments.
  • Patrick Rothfuss’s The Kingkiller Chronicle: Dreams provide intimate access to the protagonist’s inner life, blending poetic imagery with narrative insight.

These examples show how dreams and visions enrich fantasy storytelling without overwhelming the core plot.

Practical Tips for Writers

  • Plan your dream/vision sequences ahead: Know their purpose and place in the story arc.
  • Avoid clichés: Steer clear of overused dream tropes like the “it was all a dream” trope, which can feel like a cop-out.
  • Use dreams/visions to reveal, not just tell: Show emotions and clues through imagery instead of exposition.
  • Experiment with form: Consider writing a dream as a poem, a fragmented narrative, or a stream-of-consciousness piece to reflect its surreal nature.
  • Make them memorable: Strong, evocative dreams stick with readers long after the story ends.

Conclusion

In fantasy storytelling, dreams and visions act as magical threads weaving through the narrative fabric, connecting the mystical to the personal. Mastering their use lets you craft stories that resonate on multiple levels—emotional, thematic, and fantastical. When thoughtfully integrated, these ethereal experiences invite readers to explore your fantasy world’s deeper mysteries while engaging intimately with your characters’ journeys.

By understanding their purpose, employing vivid imagery and symbolism, and anchoring their mysterious qualities with clarity and emotional truth, you can transform dreams and visions from mere narrative devices into unforgettable storytelling elements. Whether to hint at destiny, reveal secrets, or reflect the hero’s inner turmoil, these mystical glimpses will enrich your fantasy tales and captivate your audience’s imagination.
