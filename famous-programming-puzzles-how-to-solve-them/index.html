Famous Programming Puzzles: How to Solve Them


Programming puzzles are a unique and challenging way to test your skills as a programmer. These brain-teasing problems can be a great way to build your critical thinking and problem-solving abilities. In this article, we’ll explore some famous programming puzzles, how they work, and how you can solve them.

  1. The Infinite Phrases Puzzle

The Infinite Phrases Puzzle originated from a PhD Thesis in Computer Science in the 1980s. It goes like this:

You have two people, Alice and Bob, each starting at a computer screen displaying a phrase (“Alice” or “Bob” will always be in the phrase). Every minute, Alice copies her screen’s phrase to Bob, and Bob immediately changes his phrase to whatever he saw on Alice’s screen.

Question: When can Alice show Bob her initial phrase again?

  1. The Mozart Chess Clock Puzzle

Chess enthusiasts and programmers know about the unique puzzle presented by the Mozart Chess Clock. In this clock, both players are allowed to make up to four seconds of thinking time, but there’s a catch: both players must agree before the start of the game. What should the time setting be to ensure a fair game?

The trick here is in understanding the rules of the game. Think carefully and try to solve it on your own!

  1. The Prisoner’s Dilemma Puzzle

This isn’t a programming problem, but it has a profound impact on decision-making in computer science and beyond. Imagine two prisoners who have been arrested for a crime. They’re being interrogated separately, and they can either betray or cooperate with each other. They don’t know what the other will do. Solve this puzzle by considering the best strategy for the prisoners.

  1. The Fizz Buzz Challenge

This classic problem is often used as a programming interview question. It’s simple - you have an array of numbers from 1 to 100. Your goal is to create a new array from the input array and return the same array, but replace every multiple of 3 with “Fizz”, every multiple of 5 with “Buzz”, and every multiple of both with “FizzBuzz”.

To solve this, you’ll need to use control structures and conditional statements in programming. Can you get it right?

  1. The Tower of Hanoi Puzzle

This famous puzzle dates back to the 13th century. It involves three rods and a number of disks of different sizes, which can slide onto any rod. The puzzle starts with the disks in a neat stack in ascending order of size on one rod, with the smallest at the bottom, and the largest at the top. The objective is to move the entire stack to another rod, obeying the following simple rules:

  1. Only one disk can be moved at a time.
  2. Each move consists of taking the upper disk from one of the stacks and placing it on top of another stack or on an empty rod.
  3. No larger disk may be placed on top of a smaller disk.

So, dive into these puzzles and have fun!
