The History of the Hundred Years' War: England vs. France


The Hundred Years’ War stands as one of the most significant and transformative conflicts in medieval European history. Spanning from 1337 to 1453, this protracted series of battles, skirmishes, and political maneuvers between England and France shaped the national identities, political landscapes, and military tactics of both countries for centuries to come. Understanding the causes, key events, and lasting consequences of the Hundred Years’ War offers valuable insight into the complex medieval rivalry between these two European powers.

Origins: Seeds of Conflict Between England and France

The roots of the Hundred Years’ War trace back to a tangle of dynastic disputes, territorial claims, and feudal allegiances that characterized medieval Europe. At the heart of the conflict was the question of rightful succession to the French throne. The immediate trigger occurred following the death of Charles IV of France in 1328, who left no male heir to succeed him. The French nobility invoked the Salic Law, which barred inheritance through the female line, to exclude Edward III of England from the line of succession—despite his being a grandson of Philip IV of France through his mother.

Instead, the French crown passed to Philip VI, a cousin from a junior branch of the Capetian dynasty. Edward III, feeling his claim was more legitimate, stalled before asserting it formally. This dynastic dispute became entwined with longstanding tensions over English-held territories in France, particularly the Duchy of Aquitaine. The English monarchs had retained significant territorial holdings on French soil since the Norman Conquest, leading to an ongoing tug of war over sovereignty, taxation, and feudal rights.

Another key factor was shifting alliances and feudal obligations, where vassals owed allegiance to multiple lords, often across borders. Economic rivalries, particularly over the prosperous wool trade between England and Flanders (a French vassal), added further strain, turning what might have been localized disputes into all-out war.

Early Phases of War: Edward III and the English Advantage

The first phase of the war (1337–1360) saw Edward III take the offensive by formally claiming the French crown in 1340, marking the official start of hostilities. Edward leveraged his naval strengths and alliances with Flemish cities to disrupt French commerce and assert control over coastal regions.

A major English victory came with the Battle of Crécy in 1346, where innovative use of the longbow and defensive positioning decimated the French knights. This battle exemplified the evolving military tactics where heavily armored cavalry began to lose dominance to more mobile infantry and ranged weapons.

Following Crécy, the English king captured Calais in 1347, providing England with a vital foothold on the continent for decades to come. The capture of Calais allowed the English to launch attacks and maintain supply lines deep within France, shifting the strategic balance.

The war suffered a brief pause under the Treaty of Brétigny in 1360, which ceded large territories to England and released the French king John II from captivity. However, the treaty struggled to secure lasting peace, primarily because it did not resolve the underlying tensions over sovereignty and succession.

The Struggle Continues: French Resurgence and Renewed Warfare

From the 1360s to the 1410s, the war ebbed and flowed with both sides experiencing victories and setbacks. The French nobility engaged in internal power struggles while trying to fend off English aggression. Notably, the French introduced military reforms, including improvements to infantry tactics and the use of artillery, which gradually challenged the English advantages.

The conflict resumed in full force with the advent of Henry V on the English throne. His military leadership culminated in the famous Battle of Agincourt in 1415, where, despite being outnumbered, the English forces utilized longbows and muddy terrain to crush the French army. Agincourt became emblematic of English martial skill and valor.

Following Agincourt, Henry V pressed his claim further, culminating in the Treaty of Troyes (1420). This controversial agreement recognized Henry as heir and regent of France, effectively disinheriting the Dauphin Charles (later Charles VII). However, this treaty was deeply unpopular amongst the French populace, who saw Henry’s rule as illegitimate and foreign.

Joan of Arc and the French Revival

One of the most dramatic turning points in the Hundred Years’ War came in the 1420s and 1430s with the emergence of Joan of Arc, a peasant girl who claimed divine guidance to support Charles VII’s claim to the throne. Joan rallied French troops and helped lift the Siege of Orléans in 1429, revitalizing French resistance and reversing years of English gains.

Her impact extended beyond the battlefield. Joan of Arc became a symbol of French nationalism and divine favor, swaying public sentiment and boosting morale. Despite her capture and execution by the English in 1431, the momentum had clearly shifted.

The French began conducting a series of successful campaigns to reclaim lost territories, capitalizing on growing internal divisions among the English and their allies. By the 1450s, English possessions in France had diminished considerably, restricted mainly to the port of Calais.

The End of the War and Its Aftermath

The Hundred Years’ War formally ended in 1453 with the Battle of Castillon, largely considered the first significant engagement where gunpowder artillery played a decisive role. The French victory sealed the expulsion of the English from almost all French territories except Calais, which England lost two centuries later.

The war had far-reaching consequences. It marked the decline of feudal armies and the rise of professional standing forces, shifting military power towards centralized monarchies. The conflict also fostered a stronger sense of national identity in both England and France, helping to lay the foundation for the modern nation-states.

Additionally, the war inflicted widespread devastation on many French regions and contributed to social and economic changes within both kingdoms. The English also grappled with political instability that helped precipitate the Wars of the Roses, a dynastic conflict for control of the English throne.

Legacy of the Hundred Years’ War

The Hundred Years’ War remains a landmark in medieval history for its scale, complexity, and enduring impact. It exemplified the transition from medieval to early modern warfare and politics, showcasing innovations in arms, strategy, and statecraft.

From a cultural perspective, the war inspired numerous chronicles, poems, and legends—most famously those surrounding Joan of Arc and the chivalric ideals of knighthood. It also influenced the English and French languages, laws, and governance systems as both countries sought to consolidate power.

Ultimately, the Hundred Years’ War was more than a protracted dynastic conflict; it was a transformational epoch that reshaped Europe and echoes in the histories of two of its most storied nations.

In Summary:

  • The war began over disputed claims to the French throne and tensions related to English-held territories.
  • Early English victories leveraged new military tactics and alliances.
  • The Treaty of Troyes temporarily favored England but failed to end hostilities.
  • Joan of Arc’s leadership revitalized French resistance and helped secure Charles VII’s reign.
  • The war concluded with French forces reclaiming most territories and ushering in new military and political eras.
  • The conflict significantly influenced the national identities, military strategies, and political structures of England and France.

Understanding the Hundred Years’ War uncovers the interplay of power, culture, and warfare that shaped medieval Europe and left an indelible mark on history.
