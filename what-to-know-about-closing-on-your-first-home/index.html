What to Know About Closing on Your First Home


Closing on your first home is an exciting milestone, but it can also be a bit overwhelming if you’re not familiar with the process. From signing documents to receiving the keys, closing is the final step in the home-buying journey. It marks the moment you officially become a homeowner. To help you navigate this process with ease, we’ve put together a comprehensive guide to what you should know before, during, and after closing on your first home.

What is Closing?

In real estate, “closing” refers to the process in which the ownership of the property is transferred from the seller to the buyer. It’s the last step in the home-buying journey, following the negotiations, offers, home inspections, and mortgage approval process. When you close on a home, you’ll sign a series of legal documents, finalize your mortgage details, pay closing costs, and receive the keys to your new property.

While closing is a straightforward process, it requires careful preparation and attention to detail. In this guide, we will walk you through every aspect of the closing process to ensure you understand what to expect and how to make the most of it.

Understanding the Closing Process

The closing process typically takes place in an office with a real estate attorney, title agent, or closing agent, depending on where you are located. This individual will facilitate the meeting and ensure that all necessary documents are signed, payments are made, and that the transfer of ownership is completed smoothly.

In most cases, you will be expected to attend the closing meeting in person. However, some states allow for remote closings or electronic closings (eClosings). Regardless of how the closing takes place, there are key steps you’ll need to understand and prepare for.

1. The Pre-Closing Checklist

Before you even sit down at the closing table, there are several things you need to do to prepare for the big day. Here’s a checklist of tasks to complete in the weeks leading up to closing:

  • Schedule a Final Walkthrough: A final walkthrough is an essential part of the closing process. It allows you to inspect the home one last time before the sale is completed. This is your opportunity to ensure that any agreed-upon repairs have been made and that no new issues have arisen since the home inspection.

  • Review Your Closing Disclosure: This document outlines all of your closing costs, including your loan terms, interest rate, taxes, insurance, and other fees. You should receive this document at least three days before the closing date, and it’s important to review it carefully to avoid any surprises.

  • Secure Your Funds: Closing typically requires a significant amount of money, so it’s essential to make sure you have your finances in order. The amount you need to bring to closing will vary, but it generally includes your down payment, closing costs, and any other fees. These funds are usually paid via a certified or cashier’s check, or through a wire transfer.

  • Get Your Documents Ready: There are several documents you’ll need to bring to closing, such as proof of identity, your mortgage agreement, your closing disclosure, proof of homeowner’s insurance, and any other necessary paperwork. Make sure you have everything in order before the big day.

  • Schedule Utilities and Services: Before you close on your home, make sure to schedule the transfer of utilities and services, including electricity, gas, water, internet, and cable. You don’t want to be left without essential services after closing.

2. The Closing Day: What to Expect

The closing meeting is where all the formalities take place. During this meeting, you’ll sign numerous documents that finalize the purchase of your home. Here’s an overview of what you can expect on closing day:

  • Signing the Documents: The primary event at closing is signing the purchase and loan documents. This includes your mortgage agreement, a deed of trust, and other paperwork that formalizes your ownership of the property. The seller will also sign their documents, and the closing agent will ensure that everything is in order.

  • Paying Closing Costs: You will be required to pay closing costs at this time. These fees can vary widely depending on the state, lender, and type of loan, but they typically range from 2% to 5% of the purchase price of the home. Some of the costs may be rolled into your loan, while others will need to be paid upfront. Be sure to review the closing disclosure to understand exactly what you owe.

  • Receiving the Keys: Once the paperwork is signed and all funds are exchanged, you’ll receive the keys to your new home! This marks the official transfer of ownership from the seller to you. At this point, you are officially a homeowner.

  • Transfer of Title: After the closing, the title to the home will be transferred to you, and the deed will be recorded with the local county office. This officially documents that you are the new owner of the property.

Key Documents You’ll Sign at Closing

During the closing process, there will be several documents that you will need to review and sign. These documents may vary based on location and type of loan, but they generally include:

  • The Deed of Trust or Mortgage Agreement: This document outlines your promise to repay the loan and gives the lender a lien on the property in case of default.

  • The Closing Disclosure: This is a document that outlines all the financial details of the transaction, including the purchase price, loan terms, interest rates, closing costs, and other fees.

  • The Promissory Note: This document is your formal agreement to repay the mortgage loan according to the terms specified.

  • The Deed: This is the official document that transfers the title of the property from the seller to you.

  • The Affidavit of Title: This document confirms that the seller has the legal right to sell the property and that there are no liens or legal issues tied to it.

Common Closing Costs to Expect

Closing on a home involves various fees, both one-time costs and recurring costs. Here are some of the most common closing costs you should expect:

  • Down Payment: This is the amount you pay upfront toward the purchase of the home. The down payment is typically 20% of the home’s purchase price, but it can be lower depending on the type of loan you secure.

  • Loan Origination Fee: This is a fee charged by the lender to cover the cost of processing your mortgage application. It usually ranges from 0.5% to 1% of the loan amount.

  • Appraisal Fee: Before your lender approves the loan, an appraisal is conducted to determine the fair market value of the property. You’ll typically pay for this service, and it usually costs between $300 and $500.

  • Title Insurance: Title insurance protects both the buyer and lender in case there are any issues with the title, such as liens or ownership disputes. The cost of title insurance can vary but is usually between 0.5% and 1% of the home’s purchase price.

  • Home Inspection Fee: While not required, a home inspection is a wise step to ensure the home is in good condition. The inspection fee usually costs between $300 and $500.

  • Escrow Fees: Escrow is a neutral third party that manages the funds and documents during the transaction. There may be fees associated with this service, which can range from $500 to $2,000.

  • Property Taxes: Property taxes are often prorated at closing. You may be required to reimburse the seller for any property taxes they have already paid for the time you’ll own the home.

What Happens After Closing?

Once the closing process is complete and you have the keys in hand, there are a few important steps to take to ensure a smooth transition into homeownership:

  • Change Your Address: Update your address with the post office, your bank, and other important services.

  • Set Up Home Insurance: You should have your home insurance policy in place before closing. Make sure it is effective as soon as you become the owner of the home.

  • Make Your First Mortgage Payment: Depending on your loan terms, your first mortgage payment will typically be due on the first day of the following month after closing.

  • Start Maintaining Your Property: Now that you’re the owner, it’s important to start maintaining your new home. This includes performing regular maintenance tasks, handling repairs, and making any improvements you have planned.

Final Thoughts

Closing on your first home is an exciting but intricate process that requires careful attention and preparation. By understanding the steps involved and knowing what to expect, you can navigate the process with confidence. Remember to review all documents carefully, stay organized, and communicate with your real estate agent, lender, and attorney throughout the process.

With this knowledge in hand, you can approach the closing day with the understanding that it’s just the beginning of your journey as a homeowner. Enjoy this moment and embrace the opportunity to create lasting memories in your new home.
