Survival Skills for Remote Hiking and Camping


Hiking and camping in remote areas offer an unparalleled sense of freedom, adventure, and connection with nature. However, they also present unique challenges, especially when the wilderness is far from civilization. Whether you’re a seasoned adventurer or a beginner, it’s essential to be prepared with the proper survival skills to ensure a safe and enjoyable outdoor experience.

In this guide, we’ll cover the essential survival skills for remote hiking and camping. From navigation and shelter-building to fire-starting and first aid, we’ll explore the key techniques that will help you thrive in the wild, no matter how far off the beaten path you go.

1. Planning and Preparation: The Foundation of Safe Hiking and Camping

The key to a successful and safe trip is thorough planning. Remote hiking and camping can be unpredictable, so it’s important to prepare for every possible scenario. Here are some critical steps to take before setting out:

1.1 Research Your Destination

Researching your destination is essential, especially in remote areas where you may encounter unpredictable weather, wildlife, or terrain. Key things to consider include:

  • Topography and Elevation: Know the terrain you’ll be navigating, including any significant elevation changes, difficult passes, or obstacles.
  • Weather Conditions: Remote locations often experience extreme and rapidly changing weather conditions. Check the seasonal weather patterns and plan accordingly.
  • Wildlife: Understand the types of wildlife you might encounter and how to handle encounters safely. This may include bears, snakes, or other potentially dangerous animals.
  • Access Points: Understand where the nearest access points are in case of emergency, such as trails, roads, or river crossings.

1.2 Gather the Right Gear

Your gear is your lifeline in the wilderness. Prioritize lightweight, durable, and multifunctional equipment. Some essential items include:

  • Backpack: A well-fitting, durable backpack will allow you to carry your gear comfortably.
  • Tent or Shelter: A lightweight tent, tarp, or bivy sack will provide protection from the elements.
  • Sleeping Bag: Choose a sleeping bag suitable for the temperatures you’ll encounter.
  • Water Filtration System: Carry a reliable filtration system to ensure a clean water supply.
  • Food and Snacks: Pack enough non-perishable food to last the duration of your trip.
  • Navigation Tools: A map, compass, or GPS device are crucial for navigating remote areas.
  • First Aid Kit: A basic first aid kit can make all the difference in an emergency.

1.3 Leave a Trip Plan

Before venturing into a remote area, always leave a trip plan with a trusted friend or family member. This plan should include:

  • Your destination and route
  • Estimated departure and return times
  • Contact information and emergency numbers
  • A description of your equipment and gear

2. Navigating Remote Terrain: Map, Compass, and GPS

In remote areas, it’s easy to lose your bearings. To ensure you stay on course, you need to master the art of navigation using both traditional and modern methods.

2.1 Map and Compass

A map and compass are your foundational tools for navigation in the wilderness. Here’s how to use them effectively:

  • Orient Your Map: To properly read your map, orient it with your surroundings by aligning the map’s north with magnetic north using a compass.
  • Identify Landmarks: Recognize prominent landmarks on your map such as rivers, mountains, or lakes. These can help you identify your position in real-time.
  • Use a Compass: Learn how to take bearings using your compass and follow a specific azimuth to reach your destination.

2.2 GPS Devices

Modern GPS devices or smartphone apps provide precise navigation and can track your position in real-time. However, they rely on battery power, so always bring a power bank to keep them running. When using a GPS, ensure you have pre-loaded maps for offline use in case of signal loss.

2.3 Natural Navigation

Sometimes, technology fails. In such cases, you can rely on natural navigation techniques such as:

  • Sun Position: During the day, the sun rises in the east and sets in the west, which can help you orient yourself.
  • Star Navigation: At night, the North Star (Polaris) indicates true north in the Northern Hemisphere.
  • Vegetation and Water Flow: Vegetation and the flow of water can give clues to direction, as rivers often flow downhill, and certain plants grow in specific environments.

3. Building Shelter: Protection from the Elements

In remote areas, you may need to construct a temporary shelter to protect yourself from harsh weather conditions, whether it’s rain, snow, or intense sun.

3.1 Tarp Shelters

A tarp shelter is a versatile and lightweight option for creating shelter in the wilderness. Use trees or trekking poles to support the tarp and ensure it’s pitched tightly to avoid water pooling. There are various configurations to create shelter based on the weather conditions, such as a simple lean-to or an A-frame setup.

3.2 Improvised Shelters

In an emergency, you may not have a tarp or tent. In such cases, you can build a shelter from natural materials:

  • Snow Shelters: If you’re in a snowy area, you can dig into the snow to create an igloo or snow cave for insulation.
  • Debris Shelters: Use fallen branches, leaves, and other debris to create a structure that shields you from wind, rain, and cold.

3.3 Choosing the Right Location

When choosing a campsite, look for a flat, elevated area away from potential hazards like flooding, falling rocks, or unstable terrain. Avoid setting up your shelter too close to water sources to prevent exposure to rising water levels or unwanted animal encounters.

4. Fire Making: A Crucial Skill for Warmth and Cooking

A fire can provide warmth, light, and a way to cook food, but starting one in a remote area can be challenging, especially in wet conditions. Knowing how to make fire in all conditions is an essential survival skill.

4.1 Fire Starting Materials

Carry several fire-starting materials with you, including:

  • Waterproof Matches: Store these in a waterproof container.
  • Fire Starter Kits: Cotton balls soaked in petroleum jelly, magnesium fire starters, or flint and steel are all excellent options.
  • Tinder: Dry leaves, grass, or bark work well as tinder. Collect them beforehand, especially if the ground is wet.
  • Fuel: Once your fire is started, you’ll need small, dry twigs, followed by larger branches, to keep the fire going.

4.2 Building a Fire

There are several methods for building a fire, but one of the most reliable is the teepee method:

  1. Arrange small tinder in the center.
  2. Build a teepee structure with small twigs around the tinder.
  3. Gradually add larger wood as the fire builds.

Make sure to keep the fire small and manageable to conserve resources and avoid causing a wildfire.

4.3 Fire Safety

Always build your fire away from overhanging branches, dry grass, or leaves that could catch fire. Once you’re done, ensure the fire is completely extinguished before leaving the area to prevent accidental wildfires.

5. Water: Sourcing, Purifying, and Storing Water in the Wilderness

Water is essential for survival, but in remote areas, fresh water sources may be contaminated or difficult to find. Knowing how to source, purify, and store water is crucial for any outdoor adventure.

5.1 Finding Water Sources

Look for clear, flowing water sources like rivers or streams. If you’re in a dry environment, you may need to dig for water near the roots of plants or follow animal tracks to find hidden water sources.

5.2 Purifying Water

Even if the water looks clean, it’s always safest to purify it before drinking. Some effective methods include:

  • Boiling: Bring water to a rolling boil for at least 1-3 minutes to kill harmful microorganisms.
  • Water Filters: Carry a portable water filter to remove bacteria, protozoa, and viruses.
  • Water Purification Tablets: These are lightweight and easy to use, though they may leave an unpleasant taste.

5.3 Storing Water

Always carry extra water if possible, especially when hiking in remote areas where sources may be scarce. Use durable, leak-proof water bottles or hydration bladders to store your water.

6. First Aid: Handling Injuries and Illnesses

Accidents can happen, even in the most controlled environments. Having basic first aid knowledge is crucial for managing injuries and preventing complications in the wild.

6.1 Basic First Aid Kit

A well-stocked first aid kit is essential for remote hiking and camping. Some key items to include are:

  • Bandages and gauze
  • Antiseptic wipes and ointments
  • Pain relievers (e.g., ibuprofen or aspirin)
  • Tweezers (for removing splinters or ticks)
  • A space blanket (for warmth in case of hypothermia)
  • An emergency whistle

6.2 Dealing with Common Injuries

In remote areas, you may encounter injuries like sprained ankles, cuts, or blisters. Here’s how to deal with some common injuries:

  • Sprained Ankles: Rest, ice, compression, and elevation (R.I.C.E.) are key to managing sprains. Immobilize the ankle using a splint if necessary.
  • Cuts and Scrapes: Clean the wound with antiseptic wipes and apply a sterile bandage to prevent infection.
  • Blisters: Keep the blister clean and cover it with a bandage or moleskin to reduce friction.

6.3 Dealing with Hypothermia and Heatstroke

In extreme weather conditions, hypothermia and heatstroke are serious risks. Learn the symptoms and treatment for these conditions:

  • Hypothermia: Symptoms include shivering, confusion, and slurred speech. Warm the person gradually with dry clothing, warm fluids, and by creating a shelter.
  • Heatstroke: Symptoms include dizziness, nausea, and rapid breathing. Move the person to a cooler area, hydrate them, and use cold compresses to lower body temperature.

7. Wildlife Safety: Avoiding Dangerous Encounters

Remote areas are home to a variety of wildlife, some of which can pose serious risks. Knowing how to safely interact with wildlife can prevent dangerous encounters.

7.1 Bear Safety

In bear country, always store your food in bear-proof containers and hang it at least 10 feet off the ground. If you encounter a bear, remain calm, make yourself appear larger, and slowly back away.

7.2 Snake Safety

If you encounter a snake, do not approach it. Most snakes will not attack unless provoked. Wear protective boots and be cautious when navigating rocky or tall grassy areas.

7.3 Other Wildlife

Be cautious of other animals like mountain lions, wolves, or moose. Always give wildlife plenty of space and avoid startling them. If you’re camping in an area with dangerous animals, consider using noise devices like bells to alert them to your presence.

8. Emergency Protocols: What to Do When Things Go Wrong

In the wilderness, things don’t always go as planned. Having a set of emergency protocols can make all the difference. If you’re lost or in danger:

  • Signal for Help: Use a whistle, flare, or mirror to signal rescuers.
  • Stay Put: If you’re lost, stay where you are to conserve energy and increase your chances of being found.
  • Make a Shelter: If you can’t make it back to camp, build a shelter to protect yourself from the elements.

By following these survival tips and acquiring the necessary skills, you’ll increase your chances of staying safe and comfortable during remote hiking and camping trips. The wilderness can be a challenging environment, but with the right knowledge, you can navigate its challenges and emerge stronger, more capable, and more connected with nature.
