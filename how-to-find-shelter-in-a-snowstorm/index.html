How to Find Shelter in a Snowstorm


When a snowstorm strikes, the situation can quickly escalate from mild discomfort to life-threatening conditions. The wind howls, the snow blinds your vision, and the temperature drops significantly, making it crucial to find shelter quickly. In these conditions, finding a safe haven is not just a matter of convenience but of survival. In this guide, we’ll explore effective strategies for finding shelter during a snowstorm, how to stay safe, and what to do if you’re stranded in a blizzard.

Understanding the Dangers of a Snowstorm

Snowstorms can be deceptively dangerous. While snow itself may seem harmless, the combination of snow, wind, and freezing temperatures can create hazardous conditions. A snowstorm is often accompanied by blizzards, which are powerful snowstorms with high winds and low visibility. These conditions can result in hypothermia, frostbite, or disorientation due to a lack of visibility and extreme cold.

The immediate dangers during a snowstorm include:

  • Low Visibility: Snow, combined with strong winds, can reduce visibility to near zero. This makes navigation extremely difficult and increases the risk of getting lost.
  • Cold Exposure: Prolonged exposure to cold temperatures can lead to hypothermia and frostbite, particularly if you’re wet or inadequately dressed.
  • Disorientation: The relentless snow and wind can confuse your sense of direction. This is especially true in remote or unfamiliar areas.

It’s vital to prepare for a snowstorm by having the right gear, knowing how to recognize a dangerous storm, and understanding how to find shelter when you’re caught in one.

The Importance of Shelter in a Snowstorm

Finding shelter in a snowstorm is critical because it serves as protection from the cold, wind, and wetness. Whether you’re caught in the middle of a snowstorm while outdoors, stranded in a vehicle, or trapped in a remote area, a shelter can save your life by keeping you warm and dry.

Shelter provides several essential benefits:

  1. Protection from the Wind: Wind chill can make the temperature feel much colder than it actually is. A shelter blocks the wind, reducing the risk of frostbite and hypothermia.
  2. Insulation: Proper shelter traps heat, allowing you to maintain body warmth. In snowy environments, even a minimal amount of insulation can make a significant difference.
  3. Reduced Exposure to Snow and Wetness: Being dry is essential for staying warm. Snow can quickly soak through clothing, lowering your body temperature and increasing the risk of hypothermia.
  4. A Safe Space for Rest: Resting in a safe and sheltered area allows your body to recover and conserve energy.

1. Finding Shelter in the Wilderness

If you’re outdoors and caught in a snowstorm, the first priority is to seek shelter immediately. The wilderness presents several challenges, including the difficulty of finding a shelter and the risk of becoming lost. Here are the steps to take:

Look for Natural Shelters

The first step in seeking shelter is to look for natural features that can protect you. These could include:

  • Caves or Overhangs: If you’re in a mountainous area, look for natural rock formations or caves that can protect you from the elements.
  • Dense Trees or Bushes: Evergreen trees can offer a surprising amount of shelter. Their dense branches can block snow and wind, providing a sheltered space underneath. Look for a cluster of trees to increase your chances of finding a good spot.
  • Hollows or Depressions in the Ground: In certain terrain, depressions or hollows in the landscape may provide some protection from the wind and snow. If you’re in a forest or open area, look for a natural indentation in the ground that could shield you.

Build an Emergency Shelter

If you cannot find a natural shelter, you will need to build one. The key to building a successful snow shelter is to use materials that can insulate and protect you from the elements.

  • Snow Cave: If you’re in an area with deep snow, digging a snow cave can be an excellent way to protect yourself from the cold. Snow provides natural insulation, so a well-built snow cave will trap warmth and keep you safe. Look for a snowbank that is stable enough to tunnel into. When digging, be sure to create a small entrance that you can cover to keep the warmth in.

  • Quinzhee: A quinzhee is a simple snow shelter you can create by piling snow in a mound, then hollowing it out to form a small cave. This is a more advanced technique, but it can provide a larger space for shelter if built correctly. A quinzhee requires time to construct, so it’s essential to begin building it as soon as possible if you’re caught in a snowstorm.

  • Tree Well Shelter: If you find dense evergreen trees, you can build a small shelter beneath them using the snow and branches. Pack snow against the base of the tree to create walls, and use branches and more snow to build a roof. The tree canopy will help protect the shelter from falling snow.

2. Finding Shelter in an Urban Area

If you’re in a city or town during a snowstorm, the situation changes significantly. The presence of buildings, public facilities, and transportation routes can offer various shelter options.

Seek Out Public Buildings

The best places to seek shelter are typically public buildings, such as:

  • Shopping Malls
  • Train Stations or Bus Terminals
  • Libraries
  • Hotels or Motels

These buildings are usually equipped with heating and can offer protection from the storm. In an emergency, many public spaces will allow individuals to take shelter inside. If you can reach one of these locations, make your way there as soon as possible.

Use a Vehicle for Shelter

If you’re driving and get caught in a snowstorm, your vehicle can serve as an excellent temporary shelter. To ensure your vehicle remains a safe shelter, follow these steps:

  • Stay in the Vehicle: Unless there is an immediate threat to your safety (such as a fire or flooding), stay inside your vehicle. It’s easier to stay warm in a car than it is to venture out into the storm.
  • Turn On the Engine Sparingly: Use the car heater for warmth, but avoid running the engine for extended periods. Running it intermittently will help conserve fuel and keep you warm.
  • Clear the Exhaust Pipe: Make sure that the tailpipe is clear of snow to avoid carbon monoxide poisoning. A blocked exhaust can lead to dangerous buildup of toxic fumes inside the vehicle.
  • Stay Visible: If you’re stranded in a snowstorm, it’s essential to stay visible. Keep your lights on and use a brightly colored piece of clothing to signal for help.

3. Dealing with Extreme Cold in Shelter

Once you’ve found shelter, the next challenge is staying warm. Extreme cold can quickly drain your body’s energy reserves, so it’s essential to keep your core temperature stable.

Layer Your Clothing

Wear multiple layers of clothing to trap heat. The key is to keep warm air close to your body while also allowing moisture to escape. Start with a moisture-wicking base layer, followed by insulating layers like fleece or wool, and finish with a waterproof outer layer to block wind and snow.

Conserve Your Energy

Rest as much as possible while in your shelter. If you’re caught in a snowstorm for an extended period, energy conservation is crucial. Avoid unnecessary movement, which can increase heat loss, and focus on staying warm and hydrated.

Insulate Your Shelter

If you’re in a natural shelter, you can insulate it further by packing snow against the walls or covering the roof with branches or a tarp if available. The more insulation you have, the better your chances of staying warm.

Stay Hydrated and Nourished

Even if you’re stranded, it’s important to drink water and eat if possible. Your body needs energy to stay warm, and hydration helps prevent frostbite and dehydration. Avoid eating snow directly, as it can lower your body temperature. Instead, melt the snow and drink the water once it’s warm enough.

Conclusion

Surviving a snowstorm requires quick thinking, resourcefulness, and preparation. Finding shelter in a snowstorm is the first and most critical step in ensuring your safety. Whether you’re in the wilderness, a city, or stranded in a vehicle, having a plan in place can make the difference between life and death. Remember to stay calm, assess your environment, and seek shelter immediately. With the right knowledge and survival strategies, you can endure even the most severe winter storms.
