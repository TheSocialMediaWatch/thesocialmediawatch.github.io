Hume's Philosophy of Morality and Sentiment


David Hume, a prominent 18th-century Scottish philosopher, is renowned for his contributions to empiricism, skepticism, and moral philosophy. His primary musings revolve around the interplay of reason and emotion in moral thinking. While many fascinating theories on morality have emerged throughout history, Hume’s philosophy of morality and sentiment paints a distinct picture of moral development and decision-making.

Hume’s philosophy emphasizes the role of sentiment in shaping moral judgments. Sentiment, derived from the Latin word ‘sentire’ which means ’to feel’, is the episodes of emotion Hume deems instrumental to reaching moral choices. Hume asserts that morality depends not on pure rationality but on the emotional responses, or sentiments, stirred by any given act.

Displaying skepticism towards the prominent views of the rationalist philosophers of his time, Hume insists that reason alone cannot dictate moral choices. Instead, moral decisions are more influenced by ‘moral sentiments’, which are emotions evoked by the perception of the morality of an act.

The grounding of Hume’s moral theory lies in his concept of conscience, an inner sense that distinguishes right from wrong. Hume elucidates that moral distinctions arise from our agreements and disagreements with others’ sentiments. In Hume’s words, “Moral sense theory holds that we acquire our moral intuition, or conscience, by having our sentiments shaped by our cultural and communal experiences.”

An important aspect of Hume’s moral philosophy is the concept of the ‘moral sense,’ which he believes to be an inherent human attribute. This ‘moral sense’ acts as our internal moral compass, guiding us in our moral decisions. Hume contends that despite cultural, societal, or generational differences, there exists an underlying universal moral sense.

Hume’s alternativist approach also breaks from the prevalent philosophical views of his era. Instead of seeking absolute moral truths, Hume argues for moral relativity, where what is considered right or wrong relies on sentiment shared among people. This interplay of sentiments stresses compassion, benevolence, and empathy in determining moral value.

Hume’s philosophy thus appears to be an insightful perspective on morality, inclining us to consider sentiment in our moral judgments. It urges us to examine not only the reason but also the emotions stirred in moral decision making. It brings into perspective that moral phenomena are more nuanced than merely relying on reason and adhering to a pre-defined moral code.

Hume’s philosophy encourages reflection on our moral instincts and beliefs. It urges us to understand that the evolution of morality is a progression shaped by shared sentiments and societal contexts. Finally, it calls for a deeper examination of our moral decisions, emphasizing the importance of empathy, compassion, and benevolence beyond our rational judgments.

Significantly, Hume’s moral philosophy doesn’t refute the role of reason in moral development. Rather Hume argues for inclusiveness, rendering both reason and sentiment equally valuable in the moral decision-making process. His philosophy urges us to once again embrace empathy, altruism, and humanity, alongside logical reasoning.

To conclude, Hume’s philosophy of morality and sentiment sheds light on the importance of considering emotions, or ‘sentiments,’ in our moral decision-making. Empathy, benevolence, and the moral sense act as essential ingredients in shaping our moral judgments. As we traverse through the complex labyrinth of moral philosophy, Hume’s wisdom serves as a vital cornerstone in understanding the ebb and flow of human morality.
