The Ethics of Cultural Appropriation: Philosophical Perspectives


Cultural appropriation is a highly debated topic across various disciplines. It is essential to understand the philosophical perspectives surrounding this issue to properly evaluate and discuss its implications. Let’s dive into this intricate topic to delve into the heart of the matter.

Cultural appropriation can be defined as the adoption of elements from one culture by individuals of another culture, often without adequate recognition or respect for their historical and social significance. This act often results in the perpetuation of power imbalances and the marginalization of the original culture. In this post, we will explore three philosophical perspectives on cultural appropriation: the relativist, cosmopolitan, and nationalist perspectives.

The Relativist Perspective

The relativist perspective posits that cultural practices and norms are contextual and consequently, cannot be judged universally. Cultural appropriators, from this perspective, are not guilty of wrongdoing since they are merely adopting aspects of a culture in a non-threatening, non-exploitative manner. According to this perspective, cultural boundaries are fluid and flexible, meaning that appreciating elements from different cultures enriches and diversifies one’s own culture.

However, this relatively passive approach may overlook the power dynamics and complexities at play. The emphasis on context and perpetuation of cultural norms may inadvertently exploit the culture they are supposedly celebrating.

The Cosmopolitan Perspective

The cosmopolitan perspective rejects cultural boundaries, promoting an interconnected world where people freely exchange culture without borders or barriers. In this view, cultural appropriation is not problematic, as it encourages cross-cultural understanding and exchange. This perspective considers cultural appropriation a means of transcending cultural boundaries and breaking down barriers between people from different backgrounds.

While this perspective seemingly advocates for the fusion of cultures and global unity, it does not take into account the historical and social traumas that have shaped these cultural practices. Furthermore, the power imbalances between the appropriator and the appropriated remain unaddressed.

The Nationalist Perspective

The nationalist perspective emphasizes the importance of respecting and preserving the cultural heritage of a specific nation or group of people. Cultural appropriation is viewed as a violation of this heritage, devaluing and disrespecting the original culture. From this perspective, cultural elements are seen as belonging to the people of that culture, and permission for their use should be granted by the original culture or its representatives.

While this perspective acknowledges the value of cultural preservation and respect for others, it can be restrictive, potentially diminishing the growth of intercultural understanding. It also poses challenges in determining who should be allowed to appropriate cultural elements and how this decision should be reached.

In conclusion, the three philosophical perspectives on cultural appropriation provide valuable insights into the complexities of this issue. The relativist perspective encourages flexibility and appreciation, while the cosmopolitan perspective promotes unity and global exchange. Meanwhile, the nationalist perspective emphasizes respect for cultural heritage and boundaries. Achieving a balanced understanding and response to cultural appropriation may require a synthesis of these perspectives—that is, recognizing the interconnectedness of cultures, respecting cultural heritage, and fostering mutual understanding.
