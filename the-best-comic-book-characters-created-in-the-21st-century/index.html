The Best Comic Book Characters Created in the 21st Century


The 21st century has been a remarkable time for comic books, marked by a surge of creativity and innovation that has given birth to some of the most memorable characters in the medium’s history. As the comic book industry evolved, so did the representation of diverse voices, complex narratives, and fresh ideas. This blog post delves into some of the best comic book characters created in the 21st century, highlighting their significance, unique traits, and the impact they have made in the world of comics and beyond.

1. Kamala Khan (Ms. Marvel)

Introduced in Ms. Marvel #1 in 2014, Kamala Khan is a Pakistani-American teenager from Jersey City who inherits the mantle of Ms. Marvel. Created by G. Willow Wilson, Adrian Alphona, and Sana Amanat, Kamala’s origin story resonates with many readers, especially those from marginalized backgrounds. As a superhero, she grapples with the challenges of adolescence while navigating her cultural identity and the responsibilities that come with her powers.

Kamala’s shape-shifting abilities allow her to stretch and morph her body, making her a unique addition to the Marvel Universe. Her relatable personality, intelligence, and resilience have made her an icon for young readers, inspiring a new generation of fans who see themselves represented in her story.

2. Jessica Jones

First appearing in Alias #1 in 2001, Jessica Jones was created by Brian Michael Bendis and Michael Gaydos. Initially introduced as a former superhero turned private investigator, Jessica’s character broke the mold for female superheroes by tackling serious themes such as trauma, PTSD, and sexual assault. Her gritty narrative and complex personality resonated with readers, solidifying her place in modern comics.

Jessica’s struggle for agency and her journey toward healing provide a nuanced portrayal of a woman reclaiming her life after trauma. Her popularity led to a successful Netflix series that further expanded her character’s reach and impact, making her one of the standout figures in the 21st-century comic landscape.

3. Miles Morales (Spider-Man)

Miles Morales made his debut in Ultimate Fallout #4 in 2011, created by Brian Michael Bendis and Sara Pichelli. As a biracial teenager of African-American and Puerto Rican descent, Miles brought a fresh perspective to the iconic Spider-Man mantle. His character emerged from the Ultimate Universe, where he took on the role of Spider-Man after the death of Peter Parker.

Miles’ unique abilities, including invisibility and a venom strike, set him apart from other Spider-People. His relatable struggles as a young hero balancing school, family, and crime-fighting endeared him to a diverse audience. The success of the animated film Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse further solidified Miles Morales as a cultural phenomenon, celebrating diversity and representation in superhero narratives.

4. Harley Quinn

While Harley Quinn was originally introduced in Batman: The Animated Series in the 1990s, her character truly flourished in the 21st century, particularly through her solo title launched in 2013 by Amanda Conner and Jimmy Palmiotti. The reimagining of Harley as a complex anti-heroine shifted her narrative from Joker’s sidekick to a fiercely independent character with her own agency.

Harley’s journey of self-discovery, mental health struggles, and her quest for autonomy have resonated with fans. Her unpredictable nature, humor, and charisma make her one of the most dynamic characters in the DC Universe. The portrayal of Harley Quinn in various media, including the Harley Quinn animated series and Birds of Prey, showcases her evolving identity and solidifies her status as a beloved icon.

5. Doctor Aphra

Doctor Aphra, created by Kieron Gillen and Salvador Larroca, first appeared in Darth Vader #3 in 2015. As a rogue archaeologist in the Star Wars universe, Aphra has become a standout character in the expanded lore. Her mix of intelligence, charm, and moral ambiguity sets her apart in a galaxy dominated by heroes and villains.

Aphra’s adventures often involve dangerous artifacts and morally questionable choices, reflecting the complexities of her character. Her popularity led to her own comic series, where she continued to challenge the conventions of the Star Wars narrative. The character’s appeal lies in her relatable flaws and her journey of self-discovery amidst a backdrop of intergalactic conflict.

6. Squirrel Girl

Squirrel Girl, also known as Doreen Green, was created by Will Murray and Steve Ditko in 1991 but saw a resurgence in popularity thanks to her 2015 comic series, The Unbeatable Squirrel Girl, by Ryan North and Erica Henderson. This quirky character possesses the unique ability to communicate with squirrels and has superhuman agility and strength.

What makes Squirrel Girl stand out is her optimistic outlook and unconventional approach to heroism. Rather than relying on brute force, she often defeats her foes through intelligence and empathy. Her humorous adventures and relatable struggles in navigating college life resonate with readers, making her a refreshing and beloved addition to the Marvel Universe.

7. Gwen Stacy (Spider-Gwen)

In 2014, the character of Gwen Stacy as Spider-Woman, or Spider-Gwen, was introduced in Edge of Spider-Verse #2, created by Jason Latour and Robbi Rodriguez. In this alternate universe, Gwen is bitten by the radioactive spider instead of Peter Parker, leading her to become a vigilante in her own right.

Spider-Gwen’s character design, compelling narrative, and exploration of identity have garnered a strong following. Her journey as a superhero grappling with the consequences of her choices while maintaining her friendships and family relationships adds layers to her character. The series has become a symbol of empowerment and has inspired fans with its unique take on the Spider-Man mythos.

8. Black Panther (Shuri)

While Black Panther has been a staple of Marvel Comics since the 1960s, Shuri, T’Challa’s sister, emerged as a prominent character in the 21st century. Introduced as a fully realized character in the Black Panther series by Reginald Hudlin in 2005, Shuri’s intelligence, warrior skills, and leadership qualities have made her a fan favorite.

In recent years, Shuri has taken on a more significant role, even assuming the mantle of Black Panther in various story arcs. Her character not only showcases strength and resilience but also embodies the importance of representation and empowerment, inspiring readers to see the value of intellect and innovation in heroism.

9. Riri Williams (Ironheart)

Riri Williams, created by Brian Michael Bendis and Mike Deodato, debuted in Invincible Iron Man #7 in 2016. A teenage genius and inventor, Riri builds her own suit of armor to become the superhero Ironheart. Her character embodies the spirit of innovation and determination, showcasing the potential of young minds in a world dominated by established heroes.

Riri’s journey is marked by her desire to honor Tony Stark’s legacy while forging her own path as a hero. Her intelligence, resourcefulness, and commitment to making the world a better place resonate with readers, particularly those who aspire to challenge the status quo through creativity and ingenuity.

10. The Green Lantern Corps (Jessica Cruz)

Jessica Cruz, introduced in Green Lantern #20 in 2014, is part of the Green Lantern Corps and has become a significant character in the DC Universe. As a Latina hero struggling with anxiety and fear, Jessica’s character provides a relatable representation of mental health challenges.

Her journey to overcome her fears and embrace her role as a Green Lantern resonates with many readers. Jessica’s story highlights the importance of courage, resilience, and the power of community, making her an inspiring figure in the realm of superhero comics.

Conclusion

The 21st century has ushered in a new era of comic book characters that reflect the diversity, complexity, and richness of modern storytelling. From Kamala Khan’s relatable struggles to Miles Morales’ groundbreaking representation, these characters have not only entertained but also inspired readers around the world. Each of these heroes and heroines has carved out a unique space in the comic book landscape, challenging conventions and pushing boundaries.

As we continue to explore the ever-evolving world of comics, it is essential to celebrate the voices and narratives that have emerged in the 21st century. These characters are more than just superheroes; they are symbols of hope, resilience, and the power of representation in a medium that continues to shape our cultural landscape.
