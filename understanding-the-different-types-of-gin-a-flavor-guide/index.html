Understanding the Different Types of Gin: A Flavor Guide


Gin is one of the most beloved and versatile spirits in the world, enjoyed by cocktail enthusiasts and connoisseurs alike. From its historical roots in Europe to its modern-day popularity in craft cocktail bars, gin offers a range of flavors that can be tailored to different palates. But with so many varieties available, it can be overwhelming for even the most seasoned drinkers to know where to start.

In this flavor guide, we will take you on a journey through the different types of gin, explaining the key characteristics of each and providing suggestions on how to enjoy them. Whether you’re a gin aficionado or new to the spirit, this guide will help you understand what makes each type of gin unique and how to select the right one for your taste preferences.

What is Gin?

Before diving into the various types of gin, it’s important to understand what makes gin a unique spirit. Gin is distilled from grain (such as barley, wheat, or corn) and flavored primarily with juniper berries, which give it its signature piney aroma. The flavor profile of gin is further enriched by additional botanicals, which can range from citrus peel to herbs, spices, and even floral elements. These botanicals are what differentiate one gin from another and create the diverse range of styles available on the market today.

The Basic Types of Gin

At its core, gin can be categorized into several key types. These categories are often based on the distillation process, the botanicals used, and the overall flavor profile of the spirit. Let’s take a look at the main categories:

1. London Dry Gin

London Dry Gin is perhaps the most recognizable and widely consumed style of gin. Despite the name, this style can be produced anywhere, not just in London. The defining characteristic of London Dry Gin is its dry, crisp flavor, with juniper berries as the dominant botanical. It is distilled in a way that ensures no added sweeteners or flavorings are introduced after the distillation process. As a result, London Dry Gin has a clean, refreshing taste that is ideal for classic cocktails like the Gin and Tonic or a Negroni.

Flavor Profile: The flavor is dominated by the sharp, piney notes of juniper, with subtle hints of citrus, herbs, and spices. There is little to no sweetness in London Dry Gin, making it an excellent choice for those who prefer a more savory, refreshing gin experience.

Best Served: London Dry Gin is incredibly versatile and works well in a variety of cocktails. It is particularly popular in gin and tonics, where its dry nature can be highlighted with the bitterness of tonic water and a garnish of lime or lemon.

2. Old Tom Gin

Old Tom Gin is a style that dates back to the 18th century and was once the most common type of gin consumed in England. It’s known for being slightly sweeter than London Dry Gin, making it a good option for those who prefer a smoother, more rounded taste. Old Tom Gin is typically made with the same botanicals as London Dry, but it often includes a small amount of sugar or syrup to soften the bite.

Flavor Profile: Old Tom Gin has a balanced flavor, with prominent juniper notes, but a smoother, sweeter finish. The sweetness is subtle, but noticeable, often complemented by hints of citrus and herbal undertones.

Best Served: Old Tom Gin is perfect for classic cocktails that require a bit of sweetness, such as the Tom Collins or the Martinez. It also works wonderfully in mixed drinks that need a touch of smoothness to balance the sharper flavors of other ingredients.

3. Plymouth Gin

Plymouth Gin is a geographically protected gin that can only be made in the city of Plymouth, England. Known for its smooth, earthy flavor, Plymouth Gin has a unique profile compared to other gins. It is often considered a middle ground between London Dry and Old Tom, offering a slightly sweeter taste with an earthy depth.

Flavor Profile: Plymouth Gin is characterized by a rich, full-bodied flavor with prominent juniper and earthy notes. It also has a slightly fruity undertone, with hints of citrus and spices like cardamom and coriander. It is often described as less dry and more rounded than London Dry Gin, with a noticeable fullness.

Best Served: Plymouth Gin is an excellent choice for cocktails that require a bit more depth and complexity. It pairs well with simple mixers like tonic water, but it also shines in cocktails such as the Gin Martini, where its complexity can be highlighted.

4. Genever (Dutch Gin)

Genever, also known as Dutch Gin, is the predecessor to modern gin and has a long history in the Netherlands and Belgium. Unlike most gins, Genever is made with malted barley, which gives it a more robust, malty base. It is typically aged in oak barrels, which imparts additional complexity to its flavor. Genever is often less juniper-forward than other gins and has a more pronounced maltiness.

Flavor Profile: Genever has a distinctive flavor that combines the botanicals of gin with the rich, malty notes of its base spirit. The juniper flavor is still present but less dominant, allowing other botanicals, such as anise, coriander, and citrus peel, to come through more prominently. The aging process gives it a smooth, slightly woody character.

Best Served: Genever is best enjoyed in classic cocktails such as the Dutch Negroni or the Gin Sour, where its fuller, maltier taste can be appreciated. It also pairs well with ginger ale or simple mixers, offering a slightly richer alternative to traditional gin.

5. New Western Gin (or Contemporary Gin)

New Western Gin is a relatively recent development in the world of gin, often referred to as “contemporary gin.” This style emphasizes the use of non-traditional botanicals, such as exotic fruits, spices, and florals, which results in a less juniper-dominant profile. Distillers of New Western Gin often experiment with unusual ingredients, such as lavender, cucumber, or rose, which give these gins a unique and complex flavor.

Flavor Profile: The flavor of New Western Gin varies widely depending on the botanicals used, but it typically has a lighter, more aromatic quality than traditional gins. The juniper notes are often subdued in favor of brighter, more floral or citrus-forward flavors, making these gins ideal for those looking for something refreshing and unconventional.

Best Served: New Western Gin is perfect for modern cocktails that celebrate the uniqueness of the botanicals used. It is commonly served in cocktails like the Gin Fizz, where its fresh, aromatic qualities can shine through, or in a Gin and Tonic, where the added botanicals bring an extra layer of complexity to the drink.

6. Sloe Gin

Sloe Gin is a fruit-infused variation of gin made by steeping sloe berries (a small, tart fruit) in gin, along with sugar. While it’s technically a liqueur, it has strong gin characteristics due to the base spirit. Sloe Gin has a deep, fruity sweetness with a hint of tartness, making it an enjoyable option for those who appreciate a sweeter, more indulgent gin experience.

Flavor Profile: The taste of Sloe Gin is dominated by the fruity, slightly tart flavors of the sloe berries. It has a sweet, syrupy texture with a hint of bitterness from the gin base. The juniper flavor is still present but takes a backseat to the berry notes.

Best Served: Sloe Gin is often enjoyed on its own, served chilled or over ice, but it also makes an excellent base for cocktails like the Sloe Gin Fizz or the Sloe Negroni. Its sweetness makes it a great option for those looking for a gin-based drink with a fruit-forward profile.

Choosing the Right Gin for Your Taste

With so many types of gin to choose from, selecting the right one can feel like a daunting task. The best way to decide is to think about your personal taste preferences and the kind of cocktails you enjoy. Here are a few tips to help you make an informed decision:

  • For a classic, dry taste: London Dry Gin is the way to go. Its crisp, juniper-forward flavor works well in traditional cocktails like the Gin and Tonic or a Martini.
  • For a sweeter, smoother gin: Try Old Tom Gin, which has a subtle sweetness that complements mixed drinks with a bit of richness.
  • For a more complex, full-bodied gin: Plymouth Gin offers a balance of earthiness and fruity complexity, ideal for sipping or sophisticated cocktails.
  • For something different and maltier: Genever is an excellent choice for those looking for a more robust, malt-based gin that has a rich, smooth flavor.
  • For adventurous flavors: New Western Gin is perfect for those who enjoy floral, citrus, or spice-forward gins that offer a more experimental drinking experience.
  • For a fruity, sweet gin: Sloe Gin is ideal for those who prefer a gin with a pronounced berry flavor and a sweeter profile.

Conclusion

Gin is an incredibly versatile spirit, with a variety of types and flavor profiles to explore. Whether you prefer a classic, dry taste or something more experimental and fruity, there is a gin out there for everyone. By understanding the different styles of gin and how their flavors vary, you can confidently select the best one for your next cocktail adventure. Cheers!
