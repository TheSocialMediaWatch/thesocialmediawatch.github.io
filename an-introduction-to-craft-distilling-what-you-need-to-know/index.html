An Introduction to Craft Distilling: What You Need to Know


Craft distilling, a term once whispered in hushed tones in the shadows of the spirits industry, has emerged from the darkness and into the spotlight. As the craft movement continues to gain momentum, enthusiasts and entrepreneurs are flocking to the idea of creating their own unique spirits, whether it's a small-batch vodka or a bold, experimental gin. But before you trade in your day job for a life of fire and fermentation, there's one burning question you need to answer: what is craft distilling, exactly? A Brief History of Craft Distilling Distillation, the art of extracting a spirit from a base ingredient, has been around for thousands of years. The ancient Greeks and Romans were known to distill wine and herbs to create medicinal elixirs, while Arabian alchemists perfected the art of carbon-producing spirits during the Middle Ages. However, the term "craft distilling" didn't emerge until the late 20th century, with the rise of micro-distilleries and the American craft spirits movement. Defining Craft Distilling Craft distilling refers to the production of spirits in small batches, typically under 10,000 cases per year. This niche approach allows for experimentation and creativity, as producers can focus on innovation and unique flavor profiles rather than mass-market appeal. In contrast to industrial distilleries, craft distilleries often produce fewer styles, experimenting with various flavor profiles and techniques to stand out in a crowded market. Key Elements of Craft Distilling Despite its relatively small size, the craft distilling industry is built around specific principles that distinguish it from large-scale commercial production. Some of the essential elements of craft distilling include:

Small batch production: The production of small batches allows for greater control over the distillation process, permitting makers to achieve a distinct character that aligns with their vision. High-quality ingredients: Micro-distilleries focus on sourcing high-quality ingredients, ensuring that each batch meets the desired flavor profile. This attention to detail translates into complex, balanced spirits. Human expertise: Craft distillers often draw on generations of family knowledge, acquired from trial and error, to refine their techniques and perfect their craft. Synthesis of technique and experimentation: While respecting tradition, craft distilleries innovate by combining new and old techniques, carefully blending strategy and expertise.
