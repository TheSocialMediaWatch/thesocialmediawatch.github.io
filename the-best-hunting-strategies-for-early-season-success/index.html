The Best Hunting Strategies for Early Season Success


Hunting during the early season presents a unique set of challenges and opportunities for both seasoned hunters and newcomers alike. With fewer hunters in the field and animals still in their summer patterns, early season hunting can be one of the most rewarding times to pursue your game. Whether you’re hunting big game, small game, or waterfowl, the early season provides advantages such as warmer weather, greater mobility, and the chance to hone your skills.

Understanding how to adjust your strategies during the early season is key to successful hunts. This article will provide a comprehensive breakdown of the best hunting strategies to increase your chances of success during this critical time.

Understanding Early Season Conditions

The early season brings several distinct factors that will affect your hunting strategy. Animals are typically more active during the early morning and late evening when temperatures are cooler. The weather is often more unpredictable, with warm temperatures followed by sudden drops, meaning animals can be less predictable as they adapt to the changes. Early-season hunters must adapt to these environmental factors for success.

Weather Patterns and Their Impact

The first thing to consider when hunting in the early season is the weather. Unlike the colder months, the early season is characterized by more temperate weather that can have a major impact on animal behavior. Deer, for example, are still in their summer feeding patterns, which means they are more likely to be found in open fields or along the edges of forests, particularly early in the morning and late in the evening.

As the season progresses and temperatures begin to fluctuate, animals’ behavior will shift. A sudden cool front, for example, can trigger animals to move more often, making them easier to track. On the other hand, a sustained period of heat might make them more sluggish and less visible.

Adjusting to the Seasonal Shifts

Your approach should adapt as animals begin to transition from their summer to fall habits. While the early season still reflects many of the patterns seen during the summer months, these animals are preparing for the changes that come with the harsher conditions of the later season.

Early season hunting is also an excellent time to get familiar with your hunting area, as there is less foliage and the terrain is often easier to navigate. This is a great opportunity to scout for deer trails, bedding areas, and water sources that might be important later in the season.

The Best Hunting Strategies for Early Season Success

1. Early Morning and Late Evening Hunts

One of the most consistent factors in early-season hunting success is timing. Early morning and late evening hunts tend to be the most productive. Animals are often feeding during the cool hours of the day before the sun rises or after it sets, making these the prime times for spotting them. In the early season, deer and other big game animals will typically be found feeding in fields, along crop edges, or near water sources.

For the most successful hunts, arrive at your location before dawn and wait patiently. Ensure that you have access to good cover to conceal your movements. Likewise, during the evening, try to set up your stand or blind at least an hour before sunset. The key here is to be in position before animals begin to make their appearance.

2. Focus on Food Sources

During the early season, animals are primarily focused on feeding to replenish their energy stores. This presents an excellent opportunity for hunters to take advantage of their predictable feeding habits. Whether it’s a field of corn, an apple orchard, or a lush meadow, animals are drawn to abundant food sources.

Knowing the type of food your target species prefers is essential. For example, deer love foraging for acorns, berries, and other soft mast, so hunting near oak trees or fruit-bearing shrubs can be highly effective. Additionally, food plots specifically planted for game, like clover or alfalfa, can be extremely productive hunting spots early in the season.

3. Utilize Trail Cameras

One of the best tools at your disposal for early-season hunting success is a good trail camera. Setting up cameras in various locations of your hunting area allows you to monitor animal activity and learn their patterns. This can be particularly helpful for determining feeding times, travel routes, and preferred areas for bedding. Early season is the ideal time to scout without pressuring the animals too much, allowing you to gather crucial data for later in the season.

Positioning your trail cameras along common deer trails, near watering holes, or around food sources gives you insights into when the best times to hunt are. Trail cameras are also useful for monitoring the activity of other wildlife, such as predators that may pose a threat to the game you’re pursuing.

4. Minimal Disturbance and Stealth

Because early-season hunts are typically quieter, it’s essential to maintain as little disturbance as possible in the hunting area. Animals will still be in their summer patterns, which means they haven’t yet developed the heightened wariness that comes with the hunting pressure of the later season.

That said, it’s still important to be as stealthy as possible when entering and exiting the area. Use scent-blocking sprays or wash your clothing in scent-free detergent to avoid detection. When setting up blinds or tree stands, do so well in advance of your hunt to ensure the area is undisturbed. This will increase your chances of not alerting nearby animals.

5. Understanding Animal Behavior and Movement

In the early season, animals tend to follow more predictable patterns compared to later in the year. For example, deer often stay close to their summer bedding areas during the early season and make their rounds in predictable locations throughout the day. Knowing how to read animal tracks, droppings, and signs of activity is an essential skill in the early season.

Pay close attention to the wind direction when setting up your hunting spots. Animals have an excellent sense of smell, and if they catch wind of you, they’ll likely bolt. Understanding how animals move through their territory based on weather, terrain, and food availability can help you get into position early in the day.

6. Adapt to Local Wildlife Conditions

Every hunting area is unique, and early-season hunting success is largely determined by your understanding of the local environment. Whether you’re hunting in forests, grasslands, or marshes, each ecosystem comes with its own challenges and opportunities.

In forested areas, for example, trees and underbrush can be dense, making it difficult to spot animals. Using open areas like clearings or well-worn trails will give you a better chance of finding game. In marshy or wetland areas, pay attention to where animals may be bedding or seeking refuge from predators.

Understanding local wildlife patterns, migration routes, and seasonal shifts can significantly improve your chances of success. Always take time to explore your hunting area before the season opens, paying attention to water sources, animal tracks, and areas where game animals congregate.

7. Be Ready for a Long Sit

Unlike the colder months, when animals may be more active due to the harsh weather, early-season hunts may require longer waiting times. If you are set up in a location where you expect deer or other big game to pass through, be prepared for a long sit with little action at times.

Consider bringing essential supplies with you to make your time in the stand more comfortable. A good thermos with warm tea or coffee, high-protein snacks, and perhaps a good book can help pass the time and ensure you’re in a good mood when the animals do make their appearance.

Conclusion

Early-season hunting offers unique advantages for hunters who are prepared to take full advantage of the opportunities it presents. By focusing on the right times of day, understanding animal behavior, scouting effectively, and adjusting your strategies to local conditions, you can increase your chances of success.

Remember that early-season hunting is not just about putting in time; it’s about being strategic, patient, and adaptable. Whether you’re in the woods pursuing big game or along the water looking for smaller prey, using the right tactics and staying aware of your environment will help you make the most of the season. Happy hunting!
