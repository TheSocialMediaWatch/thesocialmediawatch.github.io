Why Learning by Teaching Is One of the Best Methods


When it comes to mastering any subject or skill, we often think that the best way to learn is through traditional methods like reading textbooks, attending lectures, or watching instructional videos. However, there is another powerful approach that has stood the test of time—learning by teaching. This method has gained traction among educators, students, and professionals alike for its ability to deepen understanding, enhance retention, and improve critical thinking. But why exactly is teaching considered one of the best ways to learn?

The Learning Pyramid: Understanding the Science Behind Teaching

To understand why learning by teaching is so effective, we need to look at the learning pyramid, a well-known model that suggests the best retention rates come from active participation in learning activities. According to the pyramid, teaching others offers one of the highest retention rates—up to 90% of the information is retained when students are teaching the material to others. This is significantly higher compared to methods like reading (10% retention) or watching demonstrations (30% retention).

The Core of Learning by Teaching

At its heart, the concept of learning by teaching is built on the principle of active engagement. When we teach someone else, we are forced to process the information at a deeper level. It requires us to break down complex concepts, explain them clearly, and anticipate potential questions. This process forces us to understand the material more thoroughly and from different angles, which solidifies the knowledge in our own minds.

Unlike passive learning methods, where information simply enters the mind and is quickly forgotten, teaching demands active mental participation. We have to internalize the concepts before we can pass them on to someone else. This constant interaction with the material not only reinforces learning but also boosts our confidence in the subject.

Enhancing Retention Through Explanation

One of the most significant benefits of teaching is the impact it has on long-term retention. When we explain a concept to others, we are essentially organizing the information in a coherent manner, which requires a deeper understanding. It’s one thing to read through a topic and understand it on a surface level, but to teach that same concept requires us to be able to recall and explain every nuance and detail in a logical flow.

For instance, if you were to teach someone the fundamentals of computer programming, you would not only need to know the syntax and rules but also understand the logic behind how and why the code works. This deeper understanding strengthens the neural connections in your brain, making the knowledge stick.

Moreover, teaching involves clarifying misconceptions and answering questions. Through this process, you are forced to confront gaps in your own knowledge. This self-awareness can lead to a more targeted and efficient learning process, as you identify areas where you need to deepen your understanding.

The Power of Active Recall

Active recall is a powerful technique in the world of learning, and teaching others inherently incorporates this method. Instead of passively reviewing notes or reading material, active recall involves actively trying to remember information without looking at notes. When teaching someone else, you are essentially performing active recall throughout the entire process.

Every time you explain a concept, you are challenging your brain to retrieve the information, which reinforces the memory. This technique has been shown to be one of the most effective ways to retain information. It’s not just about reviewing the material; it’s about forcing your brain to work harder to access the information, which strengthens memory retention over time.

Building Confidence and Mastery

Learning by teaching also helps build confidence. As you explain complex ideas and see the comprehension on your student’s face, you gain validation of your own understanding. There’s something uniquely empowering about being able to convey your knowledge in a way that others can understand. This builds your self-esteem and reinforces your confidence in the subject.

Additionally, teaching promotes mastery. When we teach, we don’t simply learn at a basic level—we achieve mastery. Teaching challenges us to think critically, break down information in ways we might not have previously considered, and make connections across different areas of knowledge. This deeper level of mastery is not only beneficial for the act of teaching itself but also translates into other areas of life where you need to apply that knowledge.

Helping Others Helps You

Teaching also benefits the learner in ways that help solidify your understanding. When you explain a concept to someone, it’s likely they will have questions or provide feedback that you hadn’t anticipated. This interaction forces you to rethink the material and approach it from a different perspective, helping you learn even more in the process.

In fact, studies have shown that students who teach their peers often outperform those who simply study alone. This is because teaching requires a more active form of engagement, and the need to explain material to others forces you to actively revisit the concepts, thereby reinforcing your understanding.

Teaching Fosters a Growth Mindset

The act of teaching encourages a growth mindset, which is the belief that abilities and intelligence can be developed through hard work, dedication, and continuous learning. When we teach, we are constantly learning from our mistakes, refining our explanations, and adjusting our approach. Each time we teach, we encounter new challenges, which fosters the idea that intelligence is not fixed but can be cultivated through effort.

This mindset can be incredibly motivating, especially when tackling challenging subjects. Instead of viewing obstacles as insurmountable, you begin to see them as opportunities to learn and grow.

How Teaching Encourages Critical Thinking

Critical thinking is the ability to analyze and evaluate an issue or concept objectively and from various perspectives. Teaching is a fantastic way to hone this skill. When you explain a concept to someone else, you must think about it from different angles. You need to consider alternative explanations, anticipate misunderstandings, and provide examples that can help clarify complex ideas.

This constant need to evaluate, analyze, and present information from different viewpoints encourages deeper cognitive processing, which strengthens critical thinking. As you teach, you learn to see the material from multiple perspectives, which broadens your own understanding and improves your ability to think critically.

How to Incorporate Learning by Teaching Into Your Routine

If you’re wondering how you can incorporate learning by teaching into your routine, the good news is that there are plenty of opportunities. You don’t need to wait until you’re in a formal teaching role to reap the benefits. Here are a few practical ways to integrate teaching into your learning process:

  1. Teach a Friend or Family Member: One of the simplest ways to start teaching is to explain concepts to someone else. This could be a friend, family member, or even a colleague. Choose a subject you’ve recently learned and explain it to them as if you were the teacher.

  2. Create Tutorials or Blog Posts: If you have a blog or social media presence, consider creating tutorials or instructional posts on topics you’re learning. Teaching through writing or videos can be just as effective as verbal explanations.

  3. Join Study Groups: Study groups offer an opportunity for everyone to teach each other. You can take turns explaining different concepts to the group, and in the process, you’ll reinforce your own learning.

  4. Volunteer as a Tutor: If you have the opportunity, consider volunteering as a tutor. Tutoring someone else not only helps them but also allows you to solidify your understanding of the material.

  5. Teach Online: Platforms like YouTube, Udemy, or Coursera allow you to create educational content and teach a global audience. This not only helps you learn but also allows you to contribute to the broader community.

Conclusion

Learning by teaching is one of the most powerful methods to deepen understanding, retain information, and build mastery. By engaging with the material at a deeper level, explaining complex concepts to others, and embracing the process of active recall, we solidify our own knowledge. Teaching also promotes critical thinking, boosts confidence, and encourages a growth mindset—qualities that are essential for continuous learning.

Whether you’re teaching a friend, creating content, or volunteering as a tutor, the act of teaching others not only helps them but also transforms you into a more capable, confident, and knowledgeable learner. The next time you tackle a new subject or skill, consider teaching someone else as part of your learning process. It’s a surefire way to elevate your understanding and achieve true mastery.
