Exploring the Difference Between Gin and Genever


When it comes to spirits, few are as intriguing and versatile as gin and genever. Both have roots steeped in history, yet they embody distinct characteristics that set them apart. For enthusiasts and newcomers alike, understanding these differences can enhance your appreciation for these beloved beverages.

A Brief History

Genever: The Dutch Heritage

Genever, often referred to as Holland gin, has its origins in the Netherlands during the 16th century. The name “genever” is derived from the Dutch word for juniper, “jeneverbes,” which reflects its primary botanical. Originally, genever was created as a medicinal tonic, believed to have health benefits. The spirit quickly gained popularity among the Dutch, evolving from a medicinal remedy to a drink enjoyed by the masses.

By the 19th century, genever had crossed borders, influencing the development of gin in Britain. British soldiers stationed in the Netherlands during the Thirty Years’ War discovered the spirit and brought it back home, sparking a gin craze that transformed the landscape of spirits in England.

Gin: The British Evolution

Gin as we know it today emerged in England in the early 18th century, evolving from the Dutch genever. The spirit became wildly popular, particularly during the Gin Craze of the 1700s, when it was consumed by all social classes. Unlike genever, which maintains a malt base, gin is typically made from neutral grain spirits. The defining characteristic of gin is the use of botanicals, primarily juniper, which gives it its distinctive flavor profile.

Over the years, gin has undergone a renaissance, with craft distilleries experimenting with various botanicals and production techniques, leading to a diverse range of styles and flavors.

The Production Process

Genever Production

The production of genever begins with a base of malt wine, which is a fermented mash of grains such as barley, corn, and rye. This malt wine is typically distilled to a lower alcohol content, retaining more of the grain’s flavor. Once the malt wine is ready, it is re-distilled with botanicals, primarily juniper berries, along with other herbs, spices, and fruits.

Genever is categorized into two main types: oude (old) and jonge (young). Oude genever contains a higher proportion of malt wine, imparting a rich, malty flavor. Conversely, jonge genever is lighter and has a more neutral taste, with a higher alcohol content and less malt flavor.

Gin Production

The production of gin typically starts with a neutral grain spirit, which undergoes a distillation process to remove impurities. The defining step in gin production is the infusion of botanicals. Distillers can use a variety of ingredients, including herbs, spices, fruits, and flowers, but juniper must always be the dominant flavor.

There are several styles of gin, including London Dry, Plymouth, and Old Tom, each with unique characteristics. London Dry gin is known for its crisp, juniper-forward flavor and is often used in cocktails. Plymouth gin has a slightly sweeter profile, while Old Tom gin is sweeter and more aromatic, making it a favorite in vintage cocktails.

Flavor Profiles

Genever: A Complex Taste

Genever’s flavor profile is complex, with a rich, malty backbone complemented by the herbal and piney notes of juniper. The maltiness can impart a slightly sweet and creamy texture, making it a unique spirit that stands apart from traditional gins. The balance of flavors can vary significantly between brands and styles, with some genevers leaning more towards the malty characteristics and others emphasizing the botanical elements.

When tasting genever, you may notice hints of spices such as coriander, anise, and citrus, which add depth to the overall experience. The mouthfeel can be smooth and velvety, making it enjoyable on its own or in cocktails.

Gin: A Botanical Symphony

Gin’s flavor profile is predominantly defined by juniper, but the array of botanicals used can create a wide spectrum of tastes and aromas. Depending on the type of gin, you may encounter floral notes from ingredients like lavender or rose petals, citrus zest from lemon or orange peel, or herbal elements from rosemary or thyme.

The level of sweetness can vary as well, especially in styles like Old Tom gin, which can have a sugary finish. The clean, crisp nature of most gins makes them incredibly versatile in cocktails, allowing them to pair well with mixers like tonic water, soda, or citrus juices.

Cocktails: How They Are Used

Genever in Cocktails

Genever may not be as widely recognized as gin, but it has carved its niche in the cocktail world. Some classic cocktails featuring genever include the Hanky Panky, which combines genever with sweet vermouth and a dash of Fernet, and the Dutch Mule, a refreshing mix of genever, ginger beer, and lime juice.

Genever can also stand alone, served neat or on the rocks, allowing the drinker to savor its rich flavors. Bartenders often experiment with genever in place of gin, finding that its malty qualities can add an interesting twist to classic gin cocktails.

Gin: The Cocktail Star

Gin is often regarded as the backbone of many classic cocktails. Iconic drinks like the Martini, Negroni, and Gin and Tonic showcase gin’s versatility. The Martini, for instance, highlights the spirit’s crispness, while the Negroni balances gin’s botanicals with the bitter notes of Campari and the sweetness of vermouth.

The resurgence of craft cocktails has led to an explosion of gin-based drinks, with bartenders pushing the boundaries of flavor combinations. By using various gins, mixologists can create complex and layered cocktails that appeal to a wide range of palates.

Popular Brands

Genever Brands

Several brands have gained recognition for producing high-quality genever. Some notable ones include:

  • Bols Genever: One of the oldest brands, Bols has been producing genever since 1575. Their genever is known for its balanced flavor profile and versatility in cocktails.
  • Dikke Molen: This brand offers a traditional oude genever, characterized by a rich maltiness and smooth finish.
  • Rutte: Rutte is known for its artisanal approach to genever production, crafting small batches that highlight unique botanical blends.

Gin Brands

The gin market is filled with a plethora of brands, each offering its unique take on the spirit:

  • Tanqueray: A classic London Dry gin, Tanqueray is known for its distinctive juniper-forward flavor and is a staple in many bars.
  • Hendrick’s: This gin stands out with its infusion of cucumber and rose, creating a unique and refreshing profile.
  • Bombay Sapphire: Known for its floral notes and smoothness, Bombay Sapphire is a popular choice for cocktails.

Conclusion

In conclusion, while gin and genever share a common heritage and some similarities, they are distinct spirits with unique characteristics. Genever’s rich, malty flavor and complex profile set it apart from the more botanical-forward nature of gin. Both spirits have their rightful place in the world of cocktails, offering endless possibilities for exploration and enjoyment.

Whether you prefer the smoothness of genever or the crispness of gin, there is no denying the allure of these remarkable spirits. By understanding their differences, you can appreciate the craftsmanship and history behind each, enhancing your drinking experience. So the next time you’re sipping a refreshing cocktail, take a moment to reflect on the rich tapestry of flavors and stories that these two spirits bring to the table. Cheers!
