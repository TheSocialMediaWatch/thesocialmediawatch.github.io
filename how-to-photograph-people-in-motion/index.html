How to Photograph People in Motion


Capturing people in motion can be one of the most exhilarating challenges for any photographer. Whether you’re photographing athletes in action, dancers mid-performance, or simply someone walking through the streets, motion photography offers the opportunity to create dynamic and lively images. The right combination of technique, equipment, and timing can transform ordinary moments into extraordinary shots.

In this guide, we’ll explore how to effectively photograph people in motion, covering essential techniques, camera settings, and tips to help you master the art of motion photography.

Understanding Motion in Photography

Before diving into the technical aspects, it’s important to understand the concept of motion in photography. Motion involves the movement of a subject, which creates a sense of energy in the final image. The goal is not only to capture the motion itself but also to convey that sense of energy and movement.

Motion can be captured in two primary ways:

  1. Freezing the Motion – This technique involves capturing a sharp, clear image of a moving subject without blur. It’s typically used for fast action scenes, such as sports or wildlife photography.
  2. Showing Motion – This technique uses motion blur to convey the subject’s movement. It’s useful for more artistic, dynamic shots, such as dancers or vehicles in motion.

Choosing the right approach depends on the story you want to tell with your image. Both freezing and showing motion require different techniques, so understanding how to achieve both effects will improve your ability to capture compelling images.

Gear Considerations for Motion Photography

When photographing people in motion, the right equipment can make a significant difference. While you don’t necessarily need the most expensive gear, investing in the right tools will improve your chances of getting a great shot.

1. Camera

A camera with a fast autofocus system is essential for capturing moving subjects. Look for a camera that can shoot at a high frame rate (frames per second, or FPS) to help you capture multiple frames in quick succession. Many DSLR and mirrorless cameras today offer excellent autofocus and continuous shooting modes that are perfect for motion photography.

2. Lens

The choice of lens is crucial. A fast prime lens, such as a 50mm f/1.4 or 85mm f/1.8, allows you to capture sharp images in low light and with a shallow depth of field, which helps isolate the subject from the background. A zoom lens (such as a 24-70mm) is ideal if you need flexibility in your composition, allowing you to adjust the framing as the subject moves.

3. Tripod or Gimbal

For some types of motion photography, particularly when you want to track moving subjects smoothly, a tripod or gimbal can be very helpful. A tripod is essential if you’re using slower shutter speeds to blur the background while keeping the subject sharp. A gimbal, on the other hand, helps with stabilizing your camera when panning or following a moving subject.

4. External Flash or Strobe

While most photographers rely on natural light, adding an external flash or strobe can help freeze fast-moving subjects in low light situations. The flash helps illuminate the subject in a way that complements the exposure of the background.

Choosing the Right Camera Settings

The key to successful motion photography lies in selecting the right camera settings. Whether you’re trying to freeze the action or convey motion, getting your settings right is crucial.

1. Shutter Speed

Shutter speed is the most important setting to consider when photographing moving subjects. The speed at which the shutter opens and closes directly affects how motion is captured.

  • Fast Shutter Speeds (1/500s or faster): To freeze motion, you’ll need a fast shutter speed. Sports photographers often use speeds as fast as 1/1000s or even 1/2000s to capture fast action like athletes running or a cyclist speeding by.
  • Slow Shutter Speeds (1/60s or slower): If you want to show motion blur and capture the sense of movement, you can use a slower shutter speed. For example, photographing a runner with a shutter speed of 1/125s might result in some motion blur in the feet and arms, showing the speed of the movement while keeping the body in focus.

2. Aperture

Aperture affects the depth of field in your image. When photographing people in motion, a wide aperture (f/1.4 to f/5.6) can help isolate your subject from the background and create a sense of focus. A shallow depth of field is especially useful when you’re working with fast-moving subjects in busy environments, as it helps to minimize distractions in the background.

If you want more of the scene in focus, such as in wide landscape shots with moving people, you’ll want to use a smaller aperture (f/8 or higher) to increase the depth of field.

3. ISO

ISO determines how sensitive your camera is to light. In motion photography, you’ll need to adjust the ISO based on the available light and the shutter speed you’re using. In low-light situations, you might need to raise your ISO to maintain a fast shutter speed, but be cautious, as higher ISO values can introduce noise into your image.

A general rule of thumb is to keep your ISO as low as possible while ensuring your shutter speed is fast enough to freeze motion. In daylight, ISO 100 or 200 is usually sufficient. In darker environments, you may need to raise the ISO to 800 or higher.

4. Autofocus Mode

The continuous autofocus mode (often labeled as “AI Servo” or “Continuous AF”) is ideal for motion photography. This mode allows the camera to continuously adjust focus on a moving subject, ensuring that the subject remains sharp even as they move through the frame.

If your camera supports it, consider using tracking autofocus, which allows the camera to follow the movement of the subject across the frame, adjusting focus automatically.

Techniques for Capturing People in Motion

Mastering the technical aspects of your camera settings is only half the battle when it comes to motion photography. The other half involves understanding the different techniques that can help you capture motion effectively.

1. Panning

Panning is a technique where you move your camera along with a moving subject, creating a motion blur effect in the background while keeping the subject relatively sharp. To achieve this, follow these steps:

  • Set a slower shutter speed (1/60s to 1/125s): This allows enough time for the background to blur while still capturing the motion.
  • Keep your camera steady and follow the subject: Use a smooth, fluid motion to track the subject’s movement across the frame. Keep your camera in sync with the subject’s speed.
  • Focus on the subject: While panning, make sure to maintain focus on the subject as much as possible. This ensures that the moving person remains sharp.

Panning works best with subjects moving at a consistent speed, like cyclists or cars. It can add a dynamic feel to the image, conveying a sense of speed and motion.

2. Freeze the Motion

When you want to freeze a fast-moving subject in action, you’ll need a very fast shutter speed. This technique works best for capturing moments in sports, dance, or action-packed scenes. For example, capturing a basketball player mid-jump requires a shutter speed of at least 1/1000s to ensure a crisp shot without any motion blur.

To ensure sharpness in these images, be mindful of your focus. Use continuous autofocus to keep the moving subject sharp, and try to shoot in burst mode to increase your chances of capturing the perfect moment.

3. Use the Environment to Your Advantage

In motion photography, the environment can play a big role in how the motion is perceived. For example, photographing a dancer in a studio with clean, minimalist backgrounds can help draw attention to the movement, while photographing a runner against a bustling city backdrop can emphasize the contrast between the person and the surroundings.

Take advantage of different lighting conditions, reflections, and environmental elements that can enhance the story of your photograph.

4. Timing and Anticipation

One of the most crucial aspects of photographing people in motion is timing. It’s not always about waiting for the perfect moment to happen—it’s about anticipating what will happen next. For example, when photographing a dancer, anticipate the peak of a jump or a twist in their movement. For athletes, predict the moment of impact or the height of a jump.

By knowing when to press the shutter, you can capture moments that tell a story, even if those moments are brief.

Conclusion

Photographing people in motion is a rewarding yet challenging skill that requires practice, patience, and understanding of both technical and creative aspects. Whether you’re freezing action or conveying motion, mastering the art of motion photography involves a combination of gear, settings, and technique.

By selecting the right equipment, experimenting with different settings, and employing various techniques, you can capture powerful, dynamic images that bring the subject’s movement to life. Don’t forget to practice and observe how different elements like lighting, timing, and framing contribute to your final image. With time and experience, you’ll become more adept at capturing people in motion with stunning clarity and artistry.
