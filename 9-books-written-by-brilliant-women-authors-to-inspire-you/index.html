9 Books Written by Brilliant Women Authors to Inspire You


Throughout history and across the civilizations, women have played an instrumental role in shaping our world, though sometimes their voices have gone unrecognized or ignored. Today, we pay homage to these remarkable women not just by celebrating their accomplishments but also by reading and engaging with their writings, which continue to inspire millions. To inspire your reading journey this year, here are nine books written by brilliant women authors that deserve a place on every bookshelf.

1. The Alchemist by Paulo Coelho

Many people may not be aware that this exquisite novella was authored by a woman, Lygia Fagundes Telles. Chilean-Brazilian author Fagundes Telles explores the human psyche and the profound impact that dreams have on our overall being. Set in Brazil, The Alchemist is a timeless piece that brings to light the mysterious opulence of dreams and imaginations.

2. To the Lighthouse by Virginia Woolf

One of the most prominent figures of the modernist literary movement, Virginia Woolf, published To the Lighthouse in 1927. It is an extraordinary piece of literature, setting the backdrop of the Ramsay family’s summer vacation in the Scottish Hebrides, interweaving the thoughts and feelings of men, women, and children. This profound novel masterfully captures the nature of memory, perception, and human connection.

3. The Color Purple by Alice Walker

Alice Walker’s The Color Purple writes an evocative story of Celie, an African-American woman living in rural Georgia during the 20th century. Celie’s journey includes pain, love, loss, and discovery, showing the resilience of the human spirit and the transformative power of sisterhood and love. This Pulitzer Prize-winning novel remains a must-read to this day for its representation of an underheard voice.

4. Beloved by Toni Morrison

Another treasure from Nobel laureate Toni Morrison, Beloved chronicles the life of Sethe, a former slave trying to cope with her past trauma in Ohio.ibelovedWeaving history, allegory, and magical realism, this emotional story become an instant classic with its profound exploration of race, slavery, and their deep-reaching consequences.

5. Wild Swans: Three Daughters of China by Jung Chang

Award-winning author Jung Chang’s Wild Swans is a poignant memoir that spans four generations, presenting an indelible portrait of twentieth-century China. Ranging from colorful emperors and concubines to peasants and intellectuals, this captivating narrative captures the immense resilience of her family and her people.

6. The Secret Life of Bees by Sue Monk Kidd

In Sue Monk Kidd’s enchanting novel, a ten-year-old girl sets out to find her father, guided by a mysterious symbol and leading her to the honey-producing oasis of South Carolina. Along the way, she encounters the strong women of a bee-honey collective who change her life forever. A splendid tale of innocence, secrets, race, and the divine feminine, The Secret Life of Bees is an unforgettable work of fiction.

7. A Thousand Splendid Suns by Khaled Hosseini

Though written by a male author, Khaled Hosseini said, “I would have written a different book were Mariam not a woman.” This beautifully written story set in Afghanistan delves into the fears and hopes of women within a society bound by tradition and prejudice. A Thousand Splendid Suns will tug at your heartstrings while remaining true to the tale of female resilience.

8. The Handmaid’s Tale by Margaret Atwood

Written by Canadian author Margaret Atwood, The Handmaid’s Tale is a chilling novel that revolves around a woman named Offred living in a dystopian future society where women are stripped of their independence and individuality. Her story powerfully expresses female experiences, with themes of resistance, identity, and power concerning the reader long after the book has ended.

9. Wolf Hall by Hilary Mantel

In her novel Wolf Hall, British author Hilary Mantel puts forth a unique perspective on the rise of King Henry VIII’s right-hand man, Thomas Cromwell. The story, which spans over three decades of British history, brings complexity and humanity to her male protagonist, making it a gripping read that explores gender dynamics, power, and identity.

From triumphant tales of heroism and resilience to heart-wrenching stories of love, loss, and longing, these books by women authors invoke the raw depth of human emotion, reminding us of the vital and transformative voices carried by women in literature. As we immerse ourselves in these extraordinary works, we draw closer to understanding our own lives and, perhaps, even help shape a more inclusive and appreciative world.
