Liquor and Literature: Famous Writers and Their Favorite Spirits


When it comes to the world of literature, it’s no secret that many famous writers have had a fondness for the occasional drink. Whether it’s a glass of whiskey to help with the creative process or a sip of absinthe to find inspiration, these authors have turned to spirits to fuel their writing. In this blog post, we’ll explore the favorite liquors of some of the most renowned writers in history.

Ernest Hemingway: Mojito

Ernest Hemingway, known for his straightforward writing style and adventurous spirit, had a strong affinity for the mojito. This classic Cuban cocktail, made with rum, lime juice, sugar, mint, and soda water, was a favorite of Hemingway during his time spent in Havana. It’s said that he enjoyed the refreshing combination of flavors, which provided a much-needed respite from the sweltering heat of Cuba.

F. Scott Fitzgerald: Gin Rickey

F. Scott Fitzgerald, the author of “The Great Gatsby,” had a taste for the glamorous and decadent. His drink of choice was the gin rickey, a simple yet sophisticated cocktail made with gin, lime juice, and soda water. Fitzgerald often found inspiration in the extravagant parties and social scenes of the Jazz Age, and the gin rickey perfectly embodied the spirit of that era.

Virginia Woolf: Sherry

Virginia Woolf, one of the most influential modernist writers, had a preference for sherry. This fortified wine, typically enjoyed as an aperitif, was a staple in Woolf’s writing routine. It’s said that she would indulge in a small glass of sherry before sitting down to write, as it helped her relax and find her creative flow. The delicate flavors of sherry undoubtedly contributed to Woolf’s introspective and poetic prose.

Charles Bukowski: Whiskey

Known for his raw and gritty writing style, Charles Bukowski was no stranger to the allure of whiskey. This strong and aromatic spirit was often a companion to Bukowski as he poured his emotions onto the page. Whiskey, with its bold flavors and warming effect, seemed to fuel Bukowski’s unfiltered and unapologetic storytelling. It’s no wonder that this spirit became synonymous with his writing persona.

Dorothy Parker: Martini

Dorothy Parker, a prominent figure in the literary world during the early 20th century, was celebrated for her wit and sharp-tongued humor. Her drink of choice was the classic martini, a sophisticated cocktail made with gin and vermouth. Parker’s love for the martini mirrored her elegant and sardonic writing style, and it’s said that she enjoyed a dry martini with a twist of lemon, perfectly balancing the bitter and the tart.

Conclusion

From mojitos to martinis, the favorite spirits of famous writers provide a glimpse into their creative processes and preferences. These liquors, each with their distinct flavors and characteristics, seem to have played a role in shaping the writing styles and voices of these literary icons. So, the next time you pour yourself a drink, raise a glass to the great writers who found inspiration in the bottom of a glass. Cheers!
