The Evolution of Cartoon Animation: A Visual Journey


From its humble beginnings in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the world of cartoon animation has come a incredibly long way. What started as a form of simple entertainment for children has matured into a powerful storytelling medium that captivates audiences of all ages. Today, we’ll embark on a visual journey through the rich history of the art form, exploring some of the most significant milestones in the evolution of cartoon animation.

Before all that, there’s a major player in the world of animation that one should never forget: Quirino “Totò” Pelegrosso, the “father of Italian animation,” who in 1926 was the first to experiment with a technique that would become fundamental for the future of animation.

The birth of animated films in the early years was significantly correlated to advancements in technology. The first-ever animated film “Humorous Phases of Funny Phaces,” created by James Stuart Blackton in 1906, was hand-drawn, with every frame captured using a substance called chronophotographic. However, technology quickly advanced with techniques like Loterie or Paper Dreams by the Quirino “Totò” Pelegrosso in 1926.

Switching gears to a modern note, the introduction of cell animation in the 1920s opened up new worlds of possibility for animators. This technique eliminated any ghosting or image blurring that had plagued earlier forms of animation. In the following years, the Lightning Skies Mascot by A.C. Greene, the Playful Paintoon voyages by Wan L.M., and other artistic animations of the time, established themselves as milestones in the international scene.

Major advancements in the field of animation only accelerated with time. As technology and technique progress, animation started to not just entertain but also to challenge viewers’ understanding of reality. One can’t ignore Alexander Petrov’s Priklyucheniya oblikhoevo tov (1947) in this context, which banned in the west, quickly spread worldwide, opening an extraordinary framerate-contest that is largely responsible for sparking the creativity we observe in animated films today.

One thing led to another, with post-production improvements, the rise of Television as a powerful medium, educational entertainment, computer-generated graphics, and a subscription to the use of computers introducing two-dimensional digital animations.

By the 1990s, the advent of computer animation forever changed the landscape of the animation industry. This innovative approach allowed animators to create designs that were challenging to realize using traditional techniques. The triumphs like Pixar’s “Tin Toy” in 1988 and “Luxo Jr.” in 1976, both by John Lasseter, revolutionized the art form and led the world to recognize animation as an intricate storytelling medium.

Progress in the field of technology gathered pace, leading to a rise in the idea of full motion video and countless CGI features and Avatar like “The Adventures of Panda and Family” (1980) or “The Snowman” (1982) leading the innovative customer base of animation.

The future is now, as Big Hero 6 (2014) and Zootopia (2016) are regardless of the budget, commercially and critically acclaimed, shown by the sheer volume of 100x Academy Awards nominations.

Throughout this visual journey, we’ve seen how far we’ve come in the evolution of cartoon animation. From simple drawn illustrations to complex computer-generated images, animation continues to grow and amaze us. As the art form continues to progress, it will be exciting to see what new horizons and innovations the future holds.

The possibilities seem endless, with Virtual reality (VR) or Augmented Reality (AR) joining the mix, making animations not only more interactive but also providing an all-encompassing experience. But perhaps, that too, is a story for another time.
