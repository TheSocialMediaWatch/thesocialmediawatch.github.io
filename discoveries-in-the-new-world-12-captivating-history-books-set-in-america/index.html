Discoveries in the New World: 12 Captivating History Books Set in America


From the early explorers who set foot on American soil to the leaders who turned it into one of the greatest superpowers, America’s history is teeming with awe-inspiring stories. With numerous history books set in the New World, one wonders: which ones stand out among the rest? In this post, we feature 12 compelling history books that bring out the best of America’s remarkable past. Grab your reading glasses and dive into the vibrant and engrossing world of historical discoveries that shaped the New World as we know it today.

  1. 1491: New Revelations of the Americas Before Columbus by Charles C. Mann

In the midst of Columbus’s famous voyages and the age of discovery, “1491” sheds light on an altogether different narrative of America before the arrival of Europeans. Written by science journalist Charles C. Mann, this book unravels a multitude of American histories, resisting the overgeneralization of the past. Mann bases the content on contemporary scholarship, giving readers a unique perspective on the continent before Columbus, scripting an enthralling tale of human achievement in the heart of the New World.

  1. The Journey of Christopher Columbus by Laurence Bergreen

A fantastical journey echoing with adventure, mystery, and strife, this biography gives readers a front-row seat into Christopher Columbus’s life and explorations. Author Laurence Bergreen uncovers Columbus’s inner world, weaving together his dreams and voyages that heralded a new age in history. This book provides invaluable insights into the celebrated explorer’s motivations, fears, desires, and flaws, painting a larger-than-life character who inadvertently set course for great cardiac and cultural encounters in the Spanish main.

  1. The First Thanksgiving: What the Real Story Tells Us About Loving Others Despite Our Differences by Robert J. McCain

Patriotic themes often overshadow the intricacies of Thanksgiving, distorting the narrative into a fairy tale where everything aligns with neatly wrapping love and harmony. “The First Thanksgiving” dives deep into the complex realities of America’s past, taking readers into the specifics of its historic event and its significance in the long trajectory of tolerance, diplomacy, and faith.

  1. The Library Book by Susan Orlean

Diving into the unlimited potential and immense struggle of libraries, Susan Orlean masterfully crafts this celebration of the undoubtedly humanizing institution of libraries in her book “The Library Book”. While its narrative has no direct connection to America’s past, it reflects the renowned role libraries played in the fostering of knowledge and the pursuit of progress in America.

  1. The Terrible Two by Mac Barnett and Jefapper

Children will find themselves enthralled by the playful account of the infamous duo, Etienne and Carl, who set foot on King Louie’s palace grounds with acts of mischief and mayhem. While it’s neither a history book nor set uniquely in America, “The Terrible Two” still pays tribute to a time when pranksters and troublemakers turned this New World’s capital into a playground of chaos and uproar.

  1. A Peacock in the Land of Penguins by Maira Kalman

Although not strictly a historical book, “A Peacock in the Land of Penguins” by Maira Kalman provides an intimate examination of America’s past by deftly navigating through history’s highlights. Chronicles of the land’s ebbs and flows through text and watercolor paint a remarkable image of a forceful personality that refuses to fit in.

  1. The–and-exp Directory of American History Pages compiled by Anne Haubenstricker and Marilyn Bennett

This comprehensive directory shines a light on pages by and about important personalities from American history, resonating with crucial historical events, experiences, and ground-breaking moments. A must-read for history aficionados, it binds America’s long and eventful journey into an understandable and captivating remembrance of its illustrious past.

  1. History of the American People by Paul Johnson

Featuring a broad canvas of American history, Johnson’s work covers significant developments in America’s journey from colonial times to the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics. With a masterful narrative that guides the reader through the tumultuous events shaping this land, Johnson’s work remains a surefire read to satiate a thirst for American history.

  1. The Time Traveler’s Guide to Medieval England by Ian Mortimer

Looking at England’s past, this book provides a sense of realistically experiencing life in medieval times. Though it’s not set in America, its immersive content gives us a deeper understanding of Western civilization’s evolution, pushing the limits of possibility and exposing the New World’s roots in olden times.

  1. Cities of the Plain by Cormac McCarthy

Exploring several pertinent themes of violence, morality, and human endurance, McCarthy’s novel provides a gripping insight into the forces that shaped and continue to shape America. Set in the heart of Texas, this book reveals the land’s twin nature of promise and brutality, setting the pulse of the New World’s varying landscapes and souls.

  1. American Mastodon by Carol Buckley

Mesmerizing in its revelation and intriguing in its narrative, Carol Buckley’s “American Mastodon” takes its readers on a thrill ride through history while exploring the complex story of mammoth excavations. With insights into hitherto unexplored Americana, this book leaves readers with a newfound appreciation for the forces in nature that have shaped history.

  1. Full Body Burden: Growing Up in the Nuclear Shadow of Rocky Flats by Kristen Iversen

In a riveting narrative of family and environment, Iversen elucidates her childhood memories of growing up in the shadow of Rocky Plant, exploring the intimate connection between environment, the human body, and the enduring impact of nuclear weapons in America. “Full Body Burden” brings forth untold stories of the land of America’s past and the surreptitious humanzees that still inhabit it today.

In closing

America’s history encompasses an immaculately diverse and rich narrative of madness, perseverance, creativity, and determination. These twelve books not only capture the essence of the diverse history of the New World but also bring forth its life and creativity over time. Carefully unraveling the interwoven threads of American history and its delightful oddities, these books offer a portrait of the New World as vibrant, complex, and fascinating as the land itself.
