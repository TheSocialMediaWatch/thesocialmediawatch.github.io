How to Make Your Own Hunting Calls: A DIY Guide


Hunting calls have long been an essential tool for hunters seeking to mimic the sounds of animals in their natural environment. Whether trying to attract game, communicate with nearby animals, or simply enhance the hunting experience, creating your own hunting calls can be not only rewarding but also cost-effective and customizable. This comprehensive guide will walk you through the various types of hunting calls, materials needed, step-by-step instructions, tuning methods, and tips to maximize effectiveness.

Understanding Different Types of Hunting Calls

Before diving into crafting your own hunting calls, it’s crucial to understand the main varieties and their specific purposes:

1. Mouth Calls (Spectator Calls, Diaphragm Calls)

These calls use a latex or plastic reed that vibrates when air is pushed through the mouth. Mouth calls are typically silent to others and highly portable.

2. Pot Calls (Friction Calls)

Pot calls consist of a surface (often slate, glass, or plastic) on which a striker is rubbed to produce sound. These mimic the clucks, yelps, and purrs of animals like turkeys.

3. Box Calls

Made of wood, these calls generate sound through a lid or paddle sliding against the box surface. Box calls produce a loud, realistic call suitable for long distances.

4. Tube Calls

Primarily used for waterfowl, tube calls use reeds inside a tube to replicate duck or goose calls.

5. Electronic Calls

Though not DIY-friendly traditionally, some hobbyists modify or create basic electronic calls using prerecorded sounds, amplifying these for their hunting needs.

Each type requires different techniques and materials, so choose the one best fitting your game and skill level before you begin your project.

Essential Tools and Materials for DIY Hunting Calls

Crafting hunting calls at home doesn’t require a professional workshop but having the right tools and materials helps ensure quality.

  • Wood: Hardwoods like maple, cherry, or walnut work best for box calls and pot call bodies.
  • Slate, Glass, or Acrylic: Smooth surfaces ideal for pot calls.
  • Latex Sheets: For making the reeds in mouth calls.
  • Striker/Wooden Paddle: Used on box and pot calls.
  • Sandpaper: Multiple grits for smoothing wood and fine-tuning call surfaces.
  • Saw and Knife: For shaping wood parts.
  • Drill and Small Files: For precise joining and reed shaping.
  • Glue (wood and super glue): To secure parts.
  • Varnish or Wood Finish: Protecting surfaces from moisture.
  • Reeds (ready-made or homemade latex reeds): Crucial for diaphragm or mouth calls.
  • Measuring Tools: Ruler, caliper, for accuracy.
  • Voice and Practice: Yes, your own practice enhances the call’s effectiveness.

Gathering these basics ensures the crafting process flows smoothly.

Making a Simple Box Call: Step-by-Step

One of the best beginner projects is a wooden box call. The box call replicates turkey sounds through a simple wooden mechanism and is highly effective.

Materials Needed:

  • Two pieces of hardwood (e.g., cherry or maple)
  • Wood glue
  • Small brad nails or screws
  • Sandpaper (various grits)
  • Varnish or oil finish

Instructions:

Step 1: Cut Wood Pieces

  • Cut a rectangular base piece roughly 6" x 3" x 0.75" thick.
  • Cut a paddle piece about 3" x 1.5" x 0.5".

Step 2: Shape the Paddle

  • Sand the edges and face of the paddle to a smooth finish.
  • Taper one edge slightly so it will glide smoothly over the base.

Step 3: Build the Box Frame

  • Use the base piece as the bottom.
  • Cut and attach side wooden strips on the longer edges to form a shallow box, about 1" tall.
  • Secure with glue and nails or screws.

Step 4: Attach the Paddle

  • Attach the paddle via a small hinge or flexible cord at one end of the box.
  • Ensure it can rock back and forth smoothly to create friction sounds.

Step 5: Fine Sanding and Finishing

  • Sand all surfaces smoothly, increasing with finer grits.
  • Apply a durable finish like oil or varnish protecting the wood.

How to Use Your Box Call:

Hold the handle firmly, and move the paddle back and forth across the edge of the box with a rhythmic motion. This friction produces the characteristic turkey yelps and clucks that attract birds.

Crafting a Pot Call with Simple Tools

Pot calls can be made by attaching a friction surface to a wooden frame. The striking tool rubbed against the surface creates realistic calls.

Materials:

  • Hardwood for frame (e.g., cherry or maple)
  • Smooth friction surface (slate, glass, or acrylic)
  • Striker (a hardwood dowel rounded at one end)
  • Wood glue
  • Sandpaper and finish

Instructions:

  1. Frame Construction
    • Cut the hardwood into a circular or square frame with a shallow cavity to fit the friction surface.
  2. Attach Friction Surface
    • Glue the slate, glass, or acrylic into the cavity flush with the frame edges.
  3. Prepare Striker
    • Round one end of a hardwood dowel using sandpaper to create a comfortable handle.
  4. Finishing Touches
    • Sand and varnish the frame to protect the wood.

Using the Pot Call:

Hold the striker perpendicular to the friction surface and rub with a tapping, dragging motion. Practice different rhythms to imitate various animal sounds, from soft clucks to excited yelps.

Making a Mouth Call Using Latex Reeds

Mouth calls offer discreet calling capabilities. They require a bit more delicacy, particularly in making latex reeds that vibrate when air passes through.

Materials:

  • Thin latex sheets (available online or outdoors stores)
  • Thin wire or small cutting tools to shape reeds
  • Mold or frame to hold reeds (can be crafted from plastic or wood)
  • Scissors and glue

Instructions:

  1. Cut Latex into Strips
    • Cut the latex into thin rectangular strips about 2-3 cm wide and 5-7 cm long.
  2. Shape Reeds
    • Trim the edges to taper slightly.
    • Use small wire or clamps to create tension points where reeds will vibrate.
  3. Build Frame
    • Construct the frame to hold the reeds flat and in position inside the mouth.
  4. Assemble
    • Attach latex reeds to the frame using glue or by pinching in place.
  5. Test and Adjust
    • Blow air through the call adjusting tension on reeds to alter pitch and tone.

Mouth calls may require patience and repeated tuning, but mastering this method grants silent, versatile calling.

Tuning and Perfecting Your DIY Call

No hunting call is perfect out of the box (or hand). Fine tuning is critical to maximize effectiveness.

  • Sound Testing: Try your call outdoors at various distances.
  • Adjust Tension: For reeds, small changes in tension can raise or lower pitch.
  • Surface Texture: For pot and box calls, sanding changes friction and tone.
  • Moisture: Many calls sound better slightly moist, especially mouth calls.
  • Practice Calls: Mimic the rhythms of animal sounds by listening to recordings and experimenting.

Regular maintenance is also important; keep wooden calls dry and periodically sand or reapply finish.

Tips for Crafting High-Quality Hunting Calls

  • Experiment with Materials: Different woods, latex thickness, and friction surfaces yield diverse sounds.
  • Practice Makes Perfect: Skillful calling is as much technique as equipment.
  • Record and Compare: Record your calls to evaluate and adjust tone.
  • Watch Tutorials and Join Forums: The hunting community is a great resource.
  • Customize for Local Wildlife: Adjust calls based on species and regional animal behaviors.

Conclusion

Making your own hunting calls is both a craft and an art that connects you closer to the natural environment. By understanding the types of calls, sourcing the right materials, and following these step-by-step instructions, you can create effective customized calls tailored to your hunting needs. Whether it’s the rustic crack of a box call or the subtle vibrations of a mouth call, the satisfaction derived from your handmade tool is unmatched. So gather your tools, embrace some trial and error, and get ready to bring your hunt to life with authentic sounds crafted by your own hands. Happy hunting!
