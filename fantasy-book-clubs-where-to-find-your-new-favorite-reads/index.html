Fantasy Book Clubs: Where to Find Your New Favorite Reads


There’s something undeniably magical about discovering a book that whisks you away to new worlds. For fans of fantasy, this feeling is addictively familiar. The genre’s sprawling universes, intricate magic systems, and epic adventures are only half the fun. The real magic often happens beyond the pages—when passionate readers gather to discuss, debate, and bond over their favorite stories. If you’re looking to join a vibrant community of bibliophiles, or just craving recommendations for your next great read, fantasy book clubs might just be your next adventure.

Why Join a Fantasy Book Club?

Reading is often a solitary pursuit, but books are meant to be shared. Joining a book club, especially one focused on fantasy, transforms reading from a private delight into a communal experience. Here’s what draws people into these groups:

  • Shared Excitement: There’s nothing like collectively gasping at a plot twist, debating world-building choices, or theorizing about prophecy-laden destinies.
  • Recommendations You’ll Actually Love: Fellow members can suggest hidden gems or backlist titles you’d never stumble upon alone.
  • Accountability: Want to read more or expand your horizons? Book clubs give your reading habits structure and momentum.
  • Deeper Engagement: Discussing books draws out new insights and perspectives—sometimes challenging your own take and making the story richer.

Whether you’re a diehard Sanderson fan, obsessed with “A Song of Ice and Fire,” or eager to explore beyond the classics, there’s a fantasy book club for you.

Popular Online Fantasy Book Clubs

With the internet bridging distances, fantasy lovers worldwide can now connect in vibrant online communities. Here are some standout spaces to consider:

r/Fantasy Book Club on Reddit

Reddit’s r/Fantasy is a behemoth in the fantasy community. Each month, the subreddit hosts a group read, often featuring author AMAs (Ask Me Anything sessions) or lively discussion threads. Their reading selections span new releases, classic sagas, underrated indie tales, and thematic deep dives (urban fantasy, epic high fantasy, dark academia—you name it). Engagement is as easy as dropping in on a comment thread and sharing your thoughts.

Goodreads Fantasy Buddy Reads

Goodreads, the go-to social catalog for book lovers, houses numerous fantasy-focused groups. “Fantasy Buddy Reads” and “The Fantasy Book Club” coordinate monthly or bi-monthly reads, posting schedules, discussion forums, and polls to select future books. The digital bookshelf functionality makes tracking progress and recalling favorite quotes effortless.

Instagram Fantasy Book Clubs

Instagram’s #FantasyBookClub, #Bookstagram, and #SFFBookClub hashtags connect thousands of readers. Book clubs like @fairylootbookclub and @enchantedbookclub organize live chats, Instagram Lives, and themed photo challenges. The platform’s visual flair means you get stunning fan art, cosplay, and behind-the-scenes peeks from both authors and fellow readers.

Facebook Groups: Fantasy Books & Book Clubs

Facebook hosts countless private and public groups dedicated to fantasy literature. “Fantasy Book Club” and “Fantasy and Science Fiction Book Club” offer monthly reads, virtual meetups, and a steady stream of recommendations. These groups are ideal for readers who like regular prompts and interactive discussion threads.

Discord Fantasy Book Clubs

Discord has emerged as an active hub for genre communities. Servers like “Mythic Minds” and “The SFF Oasis” offer real-time discussions, buddy reading opportunities, themed readalongs, and after-hours voice chats. Many servers also have dedicated channels for favorite series (“Mistborn Crew,” “Wheel of Time Tavern”) or topics (writing craft, fantasy TV, movie adaptations).

Local Fantasy Book Clubs

Sometimes, nothing beats cozying up in a real-world bookshop, library, or coffeehouse, surrounded by fellow aficionados. Local book clubs offer a change of pace from digital interactions, making arguments over magic systems and character arcs that much livelier.

Indie Bookstores & Libraries

Independent bookstores often hold specialty book clubs for genre fiction, including fantasy. These clubs typically host monthly in-person sessions, sometimes with local authors stopping by for readings or Q&A. To find options near you, check your local store’s website, event calendar, or ask booksellers directly.

Public libraries are another overlooked resource. Many libraries offer genre-specific book clubs that meet in plush reading rooms or community centers. These organizations often provide complimentary copies of each month’s selection, making participation extra accessible.

Meetup.com

Meetup is a goldmine for local fantasy book clubs. A quick search for “fantasy book club” in your city will likely reveal groups meeting in parks, pubs, coffee shops, or cultural centers. These gatherings can be especially welcoming for newcomers, since they often attract a mix of ages and experience levels. Some Meetup groups even mix themed trivia, movie nights, or storytelling workshops with their reading schedule.

University & College Clubs

If you’re a student, campus organizations often include literary societies with a soft spot for fantasy. These clubs might focus on genre fiction broadly, or narrow in on specific interests (think “Tolkien Society” or “Harry Potter Alliance”). These settings encourage lively academic analysis along with plain old fandom.

How to Start Your Own Fantasy Book Club

Sometimes, you search all corners of the internet (and your hometown), and still don’t find the right book club vibe. If so, why not start your own? Here’s a quick roadmap:

1. Define Your Focus

Think about what excites you most: classic high fantasy, indie and self-published authors, specific subgenres (grimdark, sword and sorcery, magical realism), or maybe diverse and international voices. Narrowing your scope initially can help draw in like-minded readers.

2. Choose a Format

Decide whether you want to meet online (via Zoom, Discord, or social media) or in person (at a venue or rotating homes). Consider if you want to select one book per month, hold themed readalongs, or host open discussions around specific topics.

3. Recruit Members

Promote your club on social media, bookstore bulletin boards, library announcements, or platforms like Meetup. Invite friends, coworkers, or classmates who might be interested.

4. Set a Schedule

Consistency breeds momentum. Choose a recurring date and time—the last Friday evening of the month, every second Wednesday, etc.—and stick with it as much as possible. If you’re online, set up RSVP or poll systems to help everyone track meetings.

5. Pick Your First Book

Democracy can help: host a poll for your first selection, or take turns nominating favorites. Icebreakers—like sharing your favorite fantasy creature or world—help lighten the intro session.

6. Keep the Magic Alive

Balance structure with fun. Mix up your reading list with classics and curveballs. Invite guest speakers (indie authors are often happy to chat!) or host themed events, such as fantasy trivia or cosplay nights.

What Makes a Fantasy Book Club Thriving

Picture this: everyone finishes the book, opinions are flying, someone’s yelling about a plot twist, and another’s frantically googling “hard magic system vs. soft magic system.” That’s the sign of a club that works. But what helps drives that energy?

  • Inclusive Atmosphere: Members feel safe voicing hot takes or unpopular opinions (hello, “The Name of the Wind” hyperfans and haters!).
  • Active Moderation: Organizers or moderators keep things flowing, ask probing questions, and mediate (gently) when conversations get too rowdy or off-topic.
  • Forgiveness for Spoilers (Within Reason): A clear policy on where each meeting cuts off in the book, to avoid accidental reveals.
  • Room for Tangents: Some of the best discussions spiral from the text—into gaming, movie adaptations, or wild world-building speculation.
  • Flexibility: Life happens. Clubs that offer asynchronous participation (forums, chat threads, recorded sessions) keep more members engaged.

Choosing What to Read: Crowdsourced Fantasy Favorites

Struggling with too many choices? Here’s a sample mix of what’s popular—pulled from fantasy book clubs’ top picks in recent years:

  • The Priory of the Orange Tree by Samantha Shannon – Epic, dragon-filled, and with a feminist twist.
  • The Lies of Locke Lamora by Scott Lynch – Oceans 11, but with thieves in a Venice-like fantasy city.
  • Black Sun by Rebecca Roanhorse – Inspired by the pre-Columbian Americas and packed with prophecy and intrigue.
  • The Bone Ships by RJ Barker – Nautical fantasy that’ll convert even non-pirate readers.
  • The Fifth Season by N.K. Jemisin – Deep world-building, unconventional storytelling, and genre-defying magic.
  • City of Stairs by Robert Jackson Bennett – Murder mystery meets dead gods and crumbling empire.
  • Nettle & Bone by T. Kingfisher – Fairy tales turned inside out, with grit and humor in equal measure.
  • A Marvellous Light by Freya Marske – Edwardian magical intrigue with a touch of romance.

Of course, every club will have its own flavor—some love chunky trilogies, others prefer standalone adventures or novellas for lighter months.

Making Friends (and Rivals) in the Fantasy Community

Book clubs aren’t just about the stories. They’re a place to build friendships with people who clean out bookstore fantasy shelves just as quickly as you do. Whether you agree, disagree, or commiserate over that one series that kept you up all night, the bonds formed over shared books often last years beyond a single read.

Don’t be surprised if your club starts swapping recommendations for fantasy board games, podcasts, or D&D campaigns, too. The lines between fantasy book fan and all-around geek are blurry, and that’s part of the fun.

Tips for First-Time Fantasy Book Clubbers

New to the book club scene? Here’s a bit of guidance for a smooth experience:

  • Don’t Stress If You Don’t Finish – Life interferes; most clubs understand if you haven’t read every page.
  • Share Your Take, Even If It’s Polarizing – Unique outlooks keep the conversation lively.
  • Listen as Much as You Talk – Sometimes, the best revelations come from someone else’s offhand comment.
  • Be Open to New Subgenres – Fantasy is vast. Trying grimdark or cozy fantasy may surprise you.
  • Have Fun with It – Dress up as your favorite character for a themed night, crack in-jokes, or bring your dragon-shaped cookies.

Final Thoughts

Fantasy book clubs are more than a reading commitment—they’re portals to worlds within worlds. Whether online or in a local cafe, you’ll find people as passionate about fantasy as you. They’ll keep you reading, push you out of your comfort zone, and help make the experience ten times more fun.

Swipe right on your next fantasy book club adventure. A new favorite story (or friend) is waiting.
