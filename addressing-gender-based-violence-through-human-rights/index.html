Addressing Gender-Based Violence Through Human Rights


Gender-based violence (GBV) is a pressing global issue deeply rooted in social and cultural norms that undermine human rights. GBV encompasses a wide range of violating actions directed towards individuals based on their gender, most often targeting marginalized communities, such as women, trans, and non-binary individuals. It’s crucial to validate these experiences and address the barriers that perpetuate such violations. This blog will explore ways of confronting GBV through human rights, focusing on prevention strategies and the critical role of intersectionality.

The centrality of human rights in addressing GBV is the acknowledgment that every person has inherent value and the right to fairness, equality, and dignity. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights unequivocally condemns all forms of violence, asserting that everyone “is entitled in full equality to a fair and equal hearing in all matters.” However, pursuing a gender-just world requires moving beyond declarations to concrete actions and sustainable transformations.

Prevention is paramount in addressing and uprooting GBV. Strategies can be divided into three main areas: legal, educational, and socio-cultural. Firstly, legal interventions should focus on developing and enforcing robust and effective laws that guarantee justice and protection for survivors of GBV. This includes conducting comprehensive analysis and revisions of national legislations and ratifying international instruments like the ones established by the United Nations.

Secondly, education entails creating awareness and fostering dialogues through targeted and inclusive campaigns. This involves addressing power dynamics within the context of GBV, challenging cultural norms and practices perpetuating inequalities, and promoting a collective understanding of consent. Educational efforts should address the intersections of GBV with various discriminatory grounds, such as age, race, ethnicity, and disability.

Thirdly, socio-cultural prevention must engage all sectors of society, including government agencies, religious institutions, media, private sector, and community leaders. This approach encompassesnormative changes, promoting gender-sensitive culture and fostering positive masculinities. Ultimately, this provides an opportunity to change the dominant culture, enabling a collective, intergenerational responsibility to end GBV.

Intersectionality, as defined by Kimberlé Crenshaw, emphasizes that marginalized people experience compound forms of disadvantage and oppression, introducing a nuanced understanding of identity, power, and inequality. The human rights framework must recognize the interlocking nature of oppressive systems and address GBV within the context of intersectionality. This means adopting diverse and inclusive approaches that recognize the specificities of different groups. Reality informs that the same ways that constructs of gender, race, class, sexuality, and age, for example, interact in the issue of GBV.

To conclude, addressing GBV through human rights is a long-term and complex process that requires active and sustained engagement from all stakeholders. It is a matter of guaranteeing and fulfilling the human rights of all, regardless of their gender identity or expression. Adopting such measures to address GBV will not only transform survivors’ lives but also foster a more equitable and just society for all. Moreover, by confronting GBV through human rights, societies encourage individuals to see themselves as agents of change and embodiment of human rights principles, creating a world that acknowledges and values the inherent dignity in every person.
