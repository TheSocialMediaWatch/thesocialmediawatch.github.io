The Problem of Moral Luck: A Philosophical Dilemma


Moral luck is a perplexing and controversial concept in philosophy, one that challenges our basic intuitions about moral responsibility, fairness, and justice. At its core, moral luck refers to the idea that factors beyond an individual’s control—factors that they did not choose or influence—can significantly affect the moral judgment of their actions. This creates a dilemma, as it suggests that people may be judged morally for outcomes they had no control over, which appears to contradict our traditional understanding of moral responsibility.

In this blog post, we will explore the nature of moral luck, its implications for ethical theory, and the challenges it presents to the notion of free will. We will also delve into the main arguments surrounding this concept and examine how different philosophers have responded to the problem of moral luck. By the end of this post, you will have a deeper understanding of the moral luck dilemma and its importance in contemporary moral philosophy.

What is Moral Luck?

Moral luck refers to the idea that an individual’s moral responsibility can be influenced by factors outside their control. Essentially, it questions the fairness of holding someone morally accountable for an outcome that is partly, or entirely, the result of luck. This concept presents a stark contrast to traditional views of moral responsibility, which typically emphasize that people should be held responsible only for the actions or choices they can control.

There are several different types of moral luck that illustrate how outcomes, which individuals did not intentionally bring about, can shape moral judgments:

  1. Resultant Luck: This type of moral luck occurs when an individual’s actions lead to different outcomes, despite their intentions being the same. For example, two drivers may both be driving recklessly, but one accidentally hits a pedestrian while the other arrives safely at their destination. The driver who caused harm is judged more harshly, even though both drivers engaged in similar behavior.

  2. Constitutive Luck: This refers to the luck of having certain character traits, abilities, or dispositions that influence one’s behavior. For example, a person born with a naturally calm temperament may have an easier time avoiding violent outbursts than someone who is more impulsive, even if both individuals make similar moral decisions. The individual with the calm temperament may be judged more favorably, even though they did not have control over their disposition.

  3. Circumstantial Luck: This type of luck concerns the situation or context in which a person finds themselves. Someone who is placed in a morally challenging situation may make a poor decision due to external pressures, even though they might have acted differently under other circumstances. The judgment of their moral responsibility may vary depending on their situation, even if they acted similarly to someone who faced less pressure.

  4. Causal Luck: This type of luck involves the chain of events that leads to a particular outcome. Sometimes, even if an individual acts in good faith and with good intentions, they may still contribute to an undesirable outcome because of a series of events they had no control over. This raises the question of whether they should be held responsible for something that was partly a result of a larger causal chain.

The Challenge to Moral Responsibility

Moral luck challenges the very foundation of moral responsibility. If individuals are judged based on factors outside their control, it seems unjust to hold them morally accountable for things they did not have the power to influence. This undermines the concept of moral agency, which holds that individuals should be responsible for the choices and actions they freely make.

For example, consider a case where two individuals make the same decision, but one person’s choice results in a terrible consequence while the other person’s results are neutral or even beneficial. If we judge the two people based on the outcomes of their actions, then it seems as though their moral responsibility is determined not by the decision itself, but by chance. This introduces a moral inconsistency—people are not being judged for their intentions or actions, but for the randomness of the outcomes.

This poses a significant problem for ethical theories that are built around the idea of moral agency and responsibility. Many ethical theories, such as Kantian deontology and virtue ethics, place a premium on an individual’s ability to act according to reason, moral rules, or virtuous character. However, if luck can influence moral judgments, it seems as though individuals are being judged for factors that they cannot control, which appears unjust from the standpoint of these theories.

Kantian and Utilitarian Perspectives on Moral Luck

Two of the most prominent ethical frameworks—Kantian deontology and utilitarianism—offer contrasting responses to the problem of moral luck. Both theories have implications for how moral luck is addressed and provide insight into the moral dilemmas posed by this concept.

Kantian Ethics

Immanuel Kant’s deontological ethics emphasize the importance of duty, intention, and rational will. According to Kant, an action is morally right if it is performed out of a sense of duty, guided by reason, and in accordance with moral laws. Kantian ethics focuses on the agent’s intention and the principle behind their action, rather than the outcome or consequences.

From a Kantian perspective, moral luck is problematic because it suggests that individuals can be judged for things beyond their control. Since Kantian ethics holds that people are only responsible for their intentions and actions, the idea that outcomes—especially those influenced by luck—could shape moral judgment appears unjust. According to Kant, the morality of an action should be based on the agent’s intention and not on contingent outcomes that may be influenced by luck.

Utilitarianism

In contrast, utilitarianism is a consequentialist theory that judges the morality of an action based on its outcomes. For utilitarians, an action is morally right if it maximizes happiness or well-being for the greatest number of people. Since utilitarianism evaluates actions based on their results, moral luck seems to be a natural part of the theory. After all, if an action produces good outcomes due to factors beyond the agent’s control, the person is still credited with having contributed to that positive result.

However, utilitarians might also face moral dilemmas in cases where bad outcomes are the result of bad luck. In these situations, moral responsibility may appear to be unfairly assigned to the individual, despite their good intentions. The challenge for utilitarianism is determining how much weight should be given to outcomes influenced by luck, versus the agent’s intent and effort.

Philosophers’ Responses to Moral Luck

Many philosophers have grappled with the problem of moral luck, and various responses have emerged to address the issue. Some philosophers argue that moral luck is an inevitable part of the human condition and that we must accept it as a reality of moral judgment. Others seek to develop new frameworks that can account for the influence of luck while still preserving individual responsibility.

Thomas Nagel and the Problem of Moral Luck

Thomas Nagel, one of the leading philosophers to explore the concept of moral luck, argues that moral luck presents a serious challenge to our understanding of moral responsibility. In his essay “Moral Luck,” Nagel suggests that moral luck reveals a paradox in our moral intuitions. On the one hand, we believe that individuals should be held accountable for their actions, but on the other hand, we recognize that external factors beyond an individual’s control can influence the moral outcome of their actions.

Nagel’s solution to the problem of moral luck is to acknowledge that it is a part of the human experience. He suggests that moral responsibility can be reconciled with the reality of luck by adopting a more nuanced understanding of responsibility. Instead of holding individuals entirely responsible for the outcomes of their actions, Nagel argues that we must consider the broader context, including the role that luck plays in shaping the results of those actions.

Bernard Williams and the Concept of Moral Responsibility

Bernard Williams, another influential philosopher, offers a different approach to the problem of moral luck. Williams contends that moral luck challenges our intuitions about moral responsibility but does not require us to abandon those intuitions altogether. In his view, moral luck does not eliminate moral responsibility but forces us to rethink how we understand it.

Williams argues that our moral judgments are influenced by a combination of factors, including both the agent’s intentions and the outcomes of their actions. He suggests that we must take into account the complex interplay between personal agency and external circumstances when making moral evaluations. This approach allows for the acknowledgment of moral luck without completely abandoning the concept of personal responsibility.

Conclusion: The Continuing Dilemma of Moral Luck

The problem of moral luck presents a significant challenge to traditional notions of moral responsibility and free will. It forces us to confront the possibility that factors beyond our control—such as luck, chance, and circumstance—can influence how we are morally judged. Whether or not we accept this idea depends on how we view the nature of moral responsibility and the degree to which we believe individuals are accountable for their actions.

While philosophers like Nagel and Williams have offered insightful responses to the problem of moral luck, the debate is far from settled. The dilemma continues to provoke discussion and reflection, as it touches on fundamental issues about human agency, justice, and fairness. Ultimately, the problem of moral luck reminds us that the complexities of moral judgment go beyond simple rules and rigid frameworks, urging us to consider the full range of factors that shape our actions and their consequences.
