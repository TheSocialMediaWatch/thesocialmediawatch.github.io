From Newton to Neural Nets: A historical perspective on the development of mathematical principles


Mathematics has always been a curiosity-inspiring field, extending its roots as far back as ancient civilizations. With every passing era, innovations in this domain brought about significant intellectual and technological transformations, often aligning with unfolding human experiences. To unravel this historical perspective, let’s embark on a journey that starts with Sir Isaac Newton and concludes with the advent of neural nets.

Newton’s Laws of Motion and Universal Gravitation (1687)

The father of classical mechanics, Sir Isaac Newton, introduced three fundamental laws of motion that explained the behavior of objects in motion or at rest. He also developed the theory of universal gravitation-postulating that any two bodies in the universe attracted each other with a force, proportional to their mass and inversely proportional to the distance squared between them. Newton’s works established a strong foundation for future mathematical developments.

Calculus: A bridge between the discrete and continuous (1665-1669)

While working on his theory of motion, Newton laid the groundwork for calculus, a branch of mathematics addressing issues of continuity and infinity. It would later be refined by German mathematician Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Calculus proved essential for numerous scientific developments; its principles of differentiation and integration helped shape fields from physics to engineering and econometrics.

Probability & Statistics: Rigorous quantitative reasoning (18th Century)

As societies became more complex, it became crucial to reason with uncertainty. Probabilistic ideas and statistical methods emerged in the 18th century, with figures like Abraham de Moivre and Laplace. These mathematicians addressed vital societal questions, from predicting weather patterns to understanding the risk of financial investments.

Abstract Algebra: The marriage of structure and non-structure (19th Century)

The 19th century saw a blossoming of algebraic structures, giving birth to Abstract Algebra. Purely theoretical concepts like groups, rings, and fields were concocted, expanding our understanding of axiomatic systems. Félix Klein’s Erlanger Programm provided deeper insights about geometries based on these abstract constructs.

Geometry: Beyond Euclid’s Elements (16th-19th Century)

The study of geometry witnessed significant advancements from the 16th to 19th centuries. the likes of René Descartes applied algebra to geometry; Carl Friedrich Gauss generalized the concepts of Euclidean geometry; Bernhard Riemann took this " Geometry " notion to extraordinary heights by defining Riemannian geometry capturing the mathematical underpinning of the universe’severe curvature.

Alan Turing’s Machines & AI (1936)

Alan Turing, a British mathematician, revolutionized computing through theoretical devices like Turing Machines. Embodying the concept of Universal Turing Machine, one could say Turing coordinated the marriage between mathematics and computers’ growing functionality.

Neural Networks: The brain-inspired renaissance (1980s-Present)

With the advent of the digital age, scientists have been trying to emulate the incredibly complex human brain. The creation of neural networks drew inspiration from this natural supercomputer. Researchers like Geoffrey Hinton and Yann LeCun pioneered work on multilayer neural nets, leading to elements of modern-day Artificial Intelligence, including natural language processing and computer vision. Deep Learning, a significant subset of Neural Networks, has powered unprecedented leaps in pattern-recognition abilities with applications from self-driving cars to disease diagnosis.

In conclusion, this historical perspective on mathematical principles unraveled the progression from Sir Isaac Newton’s laws to emerging neural networks-influencing profound changes throughout history. While we reflect on these significant milestones and marvel at where mathematics has already taken us, one can reasonably anticipate what lies ahead. Undoubtedly, we stand on the shoulders of giants as we continue to advance the daring, puzzling world of mathematics.
