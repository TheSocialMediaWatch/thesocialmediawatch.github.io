Haunted by History: The 12 Best Historical Fiction Novels That Stir the Soul


Historical fiction novels have an extraordinary ability to transport readers to different times, places, and cultures. By exploring real events, legendary figures and unique time periods, these books allow us to empathize with the human condition and learn from the past. Here we present a carefully curated selection of 12 astonishing historical fiction novels that transcend time, stirring the soul as they captivate our imaginations.

1. The Book Thief by Markus Zusak
Set in Germany during World War II, Hitler’s Rise, The Book Thief tells the story of Liesel Meminger, a young girl who learns to read. She finds solace in stolen books and shared stories with others. The novel delves into the power of human connection during times of war and persecution, weaving WWII history into a timeless coming-of-age tale.

2. Wolf Hall by Hilary Mantel
Winning the Man Booker Prize, Wolf Hall takes us back to Tudor England and given its focus on the life of Thomas Cromwell. This novel from Hilary Mantel reframes the historical landscape of the English Reformation by bringing to life one of history’s most enigmatic figures.

3. All the Light We Cannot See by Anthony Doerr
Set during World War II in France, All the Light We Cannot See deftly weaves together the stories of Marie-Laure and Werner, two individuals whose paths cross by chance. Their intertwined narratives affirm the strength of the human spirit, and underscore the importance of literature in giving meaning to inhuman acts.

4. Gone with the Wind by Margaret Mitchell
An American classic, Gone with the Wind portrays the South before, during, and after the U.S. Civil War. Through the eyes of Scarlett O’Hara and Rhett Butler, we are taken on a captivating journey exploring love, loss, and betrayal.

5. The Thirteenth Tale by Diane Setterfield
In this spellbinding novel, a reclusive author’s final story unravels the secrets of England’s brooding country houses giving us an insight into the hidden world of the English aristocracy. We delve into the secrets of the past, and as the narrative unfolds, we learn the importance of stories in making sense of ourselves.

6. The Pillars of the Earth by Ken Follett
Follett’s ambitious epic The Pillars of the Earth sweeps us through the politics, conflicts, and intricacies of the 12th century in the construction of a cathedral. Central characters interweave the power struggles of this transformative time in architecture, politics, and religion.

7. The Name of the Rose by Umberto Eco

Created by the influential Italian author Umberto Eco, The Name of the Rose is a fictional murder mystery set in a 14th-century monastic library. This novel is a thrilling treat for anyone fascinated by the historical pursuit of hidden truths, religious hierarchies, and the intellectual vigor of the medieval world.

8. The Other Boleyn Girl by Philippa Gregory

Transporting readers to the court of Henry VIII, The Other Boleyn Girl is a masterful tale of courtly intrigue, family betrayal, and romance. Through the eyes of Mary and Anne Boleyn, we witness the tumultuous undercurrents of Henry’s court and the sacrifices required for power.

9. The Last Kingdom by Bernard Cornwell

Bringing to life 9th century England as seen through the eyes of a young man named Uhtred, The Last Kingdom explores the Viking invasions and the rise of Alfred the Great. Bernhard Cornwell’s novel is a rousing tale of battles, leadership, and destiny that will leave readers fascinated by the complexities of history.

10. Pachinko by Min Jin Lee

Traversing time from early 1900s Korea to its diaspora in Japan, Pachinko captures the struggles and aspirations of one Korean family living abroad. The novel is an inspiring story about identity, assimilation and the fight to preserve cultural heritage.

11. The Kite Runner by Khaled Hosseini

Centered around the power of redemption and the enduring bonds of friendship, The Kite Runner dives into the complexities of Afghanistan’s tumultuous history as seen through the eyes of Amir and his best childhood friend Hassan. This gripping narrative offers valuable insight into the effects of political turmoil on personal relationships.

12. Memoirs of a Geisha by Arthur Golden

Notorious for its meticulous research, Memoirs of a Geisha immerses us in the life of a geisha named Chiyo, set in pre-World War II Japan. Golden captures the essence of the geisha culture from the 1920s through the 1960s by weaving the delicate lives of these women into a captivating epic.

These novels are essential reading for anyone who wants not only to entertain themselves with exceptional storytelling but also to understand, appreciate, and reflect upon history’s influence on contemporary society. As we are ensnared by their brilliance, their unforgettable characters, and the history they bring to life, we are reminded that stories are powerful catalysts for change and an integral part of the human experience. To embark on these journeys is to experience history, both in all its triumphs and tragedies, as never before.
