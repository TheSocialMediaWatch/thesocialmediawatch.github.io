Deciphering the Riddle of Stonehenge: Examining Ancient Construction Techniques


When we think of Stonehenge, images of mysterious and monumental circular arrangements of large stones immediately come to mind. The intriguing complexity and antiquity of this site have captivated people for centuries, leaving many questions unanswered. How were these massive stones transported and erected? How were their positions determined? And most importantly, why was this monument constructed in the first place?

Located in Wiltshire, England, Stonehenge is one of the oldest landmarks still standing. Dated back to around 3000 BCE to 2000 BCE, this magnificent arrangement comprises of two types of stones – sarsen and bluestones. The arrangement reveals an intricate alignment with the sun and moon, indicating that the site may have been used as an astronomical observatory. Despite our evolving understanding of Stonehenge, several mysteries still surround it. This blog post will delve into the lesser-discussed ancient construction techniques used to create Stonehenge.

To understand the construction techniques, we must first acknowledge the undeniable accomplishments of ancient engineers and architects. Stonehenge, despite having no technological know-how, is a testament to the incredible skills of Neolithic builders.

Early Planning and Stone Selection

The materials for Stonehenge were carefully chosen and transported over long distances. The sarsen stones, forming the outer circle, came from the Marlborough Downs about 20 miles (32 km) away. These hard, expensive-to-extract sandstone blocks weighed up to 30 tons each. It required a well-planned and strategic operation to move them around and place them where they are now.

The bluestones, distributed in the inner horseshoe, are of a different significance, coming from as far as 140 miles (225 km) away. Bluestones are significantly lighter, weighing up to 5 tons each. Their unique origin led to speculation about their transport, which we will be addressing shortly.

Structural Alignment

The orientation of Stonehenge along the sunrise on the shortest day of the year (Winter Solstice) and the alignment of its entrances point to specific celestial events is a well-documented fact. However, the mystery surrounding the incredible precision of these alignments remains unsolved.

The main axis of Stonehenge lies almost exactly north to south, with a deviation of only half a degree. Moreover, the positions of sarsen stones were planned in such a way that they mark the exact positions of the rise and set of the sun during midsummer and midwinter. This accuracy requires knowledge far beyond the existing understanding of Neolithic people. One theory suggests the use of a complex ritual to determine these celestial alignments.

Stonehenge “Strange Logs”

Robert Phillips, a 21st-century engineer, has provided an interesting perspective by comparing Stonehenge’s mysterious bluestones upon which the miniature stones rest. He states that these stones may not have been carried by conceivably unfit humans on wheels – the “outlandish proposal” that some archaeologists believe. Instead, they were placed using titanic forces like glaciers or cursus-like channels.

He expanded upon this interesting theory further by comparing Stonehenge to similar bluestone monuments like Avebury. Phillips contends that Stonehenge may have been a “Megalithic GNU” (Gee-NEW) structure. An artificial glacier (glacis) created by periodically flooding the area with rainwater would deliver the ‘strange logs’ or bluestones into their precise locations.

This theory offers another layer of mystery and understanding of the incredible feats in engineering that could rival the modern engineering marvels. But more digs, scans, and analyses must be conducted to validate this and other theories.

In conclusion, Stonehenge’s construction is a testament to the ingenuity and determination of the ancient builders. Understandably, transporting massive stones and aligning them with astronomical precision is a remarkable feat, even by today’s standards. The riddle of Stonehenge endures, but we can certainly appreciate that these ancient architects had a profound understanding of their environment and cosmos. They managed to leave us with an enduring place of wonder and a reminder that answers sometimes add to our endless curiosity.
