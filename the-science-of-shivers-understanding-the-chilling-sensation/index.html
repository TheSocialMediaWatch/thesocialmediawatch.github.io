The Physiology of Shivers

The sensation of shivers is a result of intricate interactions between our nervous system, muscles, and skin. When we are exposed to cold temperatures or experience strong emotions such as fear or excitement, our brain signals the muscles at the base of each hair follicle to contract, causing the hair to stand on end. This phenomenon, known as piloerection, is a primitive reflex that dates back to our evolutionary past when our ancestors had more body hair. While it may no longer serve a practical purpose, it continues to manifest in response to various stimuli.

Simultaneously, the sympathetic nervous system, responsible for the body’s fight-or-flight response, springs into action. This triggers the release of adrenaline, which further activates the tiny muscles attached to each hair follicle, intensifying the sensation of shivers. These combined responses are a testament to the intricate and finely tuned nature of our body’s physiological reactions.

The Emotional Connection

Beyond its physiological underpinnings, shivers also have a profound emotional dimension. Have you ever felt shivers down your spine while listening to a particularly moving piece of music or witnessing a powerful performance? This phenomenon is often referred to as “frisson,” a French term that encapsulates the thrilling sensation induced by a poignant moment.

Research suggests that experiencing frisson is linked to the release of dopamine in the brain, the neurotransmitter associated with pleasure and reward. As we immerse ourselves in emotionally charged stimuli, our brain’s reward system is activated, leading to the release of dopamine and the subsequent onset of shivers. This emotional aspect of shivers highlights the intricate interplay between our physiological responses and our deeply ingrained emotional experiences.

The Evolutionary Perspective
