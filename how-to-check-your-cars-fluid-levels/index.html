How to Check Your Car's Fluid Levels


Maintaining your car’s fluid levels is crucial for its proper functioning. It plays an essential role in ensuring that your automobile performs at its best. While getting this done professionally is crucial, understanding the process and keeping an eye on these vital liquids is part of being an informed car owner. Let’s dive into the step-by-step process for checking some of the primary fluids in your car.

Step 1: Locate the Different Fluid Reservoirs

Firstly, it is essential to find where the various fluids are stored in your car. Typically, they can be classified into five (5) primary categories:

  1. Engine Oil
  2. Engine Coolant (antifreeze)
  3. Brake Fluid
  4. Power Steering Fluid
  5. Transmission Fluid (manual or automatic)

For most vehicles, these fluids have their designated reservoirs, and they usually have caps with identifying logos or wording.

TIP: If unsure of the reservoir location or if you see a fluid that seems out of the ordinary, refer to your owner’s manual.

Step 2: Check the Fluid Levels

After locating the reservoirs, you should inspect the fluid levels.

Engine oil

Locating the Oil Reservoir: Usually under the bonnet, close to the engine.

To check the engine oil level, make sure your engine is off and has been running for some time to reach operating temperature. Then, refer to the dipstick, which is usually accessible near the engine. Pull out the dipstick, wipe it clean with a cloth, reinsert it, turn the engine over, pull it back out, and see where it falls on the stick. The recommended level varies by vehicle - refer to the owner’s manual for correct oil levels.

Signs of low oil: If the oil is below the recommended level, schedule an oil change or top-up.

Engine Coolant/Antifreeze

Locating the Coolant Reservoir: Under the hood, typically orange or green colored container.

Like checking the oil, turn off the engine and make sure it’s warm by running it for a while. Find your coolant reservoir, often under the hood. Check its level carefully. When the coolant is warm, it should fall somewhere between the minimum and maximum markers on the reservoir. If it’s lower than the minimum level, it’s time to get it refilled.

Signs of low coolant: If your vehicle is overheating, steam creeping from the hood, or the coolant is low, get it checked immediately.

Brake Fluid

Locating the Brake Fluid Reservoir: Near the brake booster usually, with a bright-colored reservoir.

Stop the engine and locate the brake fluid reservoir. It’s typically transparent but may be colored or opaque, depending on the vehicle - refer to your owner’s manual. Remove the cap and observe the fluid. Ideally, it should be close to the “Full” or “MAX” level. If you can’t see any fluid inside, it might indicate a leak, and you should have the brakes system examined.

Signs of low brake fluid: If you see the brake fluid warning light or experience slow or spongy brakes, it’s an indication that it needs topping up.

Power Steering Fluid

Locating the Power Steering Fluid: This is usually a dark fluid reservoir close to the steering.

Just like the others, shut down the vehicle. Locate and loosen your steering fluid reservoir cap. Check the level you find. The fluid should be slightly above the “MIN” line, ensuring you have sufficient power steering.

Signs of low power steering fluid: The “EPS” or power steering warning light will illuminate, and you may hear a loud clicking sound when turning the steering wheel.

Transmission Fluid (manual or automatic)

Locating the Transmission Fluid Reservoir*: This depends upon your car, it can either be near the radiator or transmission.

Even though it may look similar to engine oil, transmission fluid is another crucial fluid under the bonnet. To check it, turn off the engine and locate the transmission fluid reservoir (refer to your owner’s manual). Check the fluid level to be somewhere close to the “Full” or “MAX” line - different types of transmissions have different fluid types, so reaching out to your dealership or mechanic is essential.

Signs of low transmission fluid: If your vehicle is slipping gears or the gear selector feels hard to move, it’s crucial to top up the fluid or inspect for a leak.

Step 3: Regular Routine Maintenance Saves You

Regularly monitoring and maintaining your car’s fluid levels is crucial for optimal performance, safety, and smooth driving. Remember that detecting problems early can save you significant repair costs down the road.

You don’t need to be an expert to ensure your car is performing its best. Taking preventative measures and keeping an eye on your car’s fluids will extend its lifespan and give you peace of mind on the road.

The information provided in this post is general information about checking your car’s fluid levels. If you aren’t confident about inspecting the fluid levels, it’s best to consult your dealership or mechanics who will be pleased to guide you or perform the check-up.
