The Great Gatsby's Lost Tech: The Enigma of Productivity through Excess


In F. Scott Fitzgerald’s emblematic novel, The Great Gatsby, we are thrust into a world of conspicuous consumption and lavish extravagance. A time when wealth was not just an indicator of status but a means of attainability - no matter how illusory. The social milieu depicted in the novel is a fascinating case study of how excess can become an enigmatic mechanism for achieving seemingly unattainable feats of productivity.

It is no secret that human beings have a penchant for novelty and astonishment. In the Roaring Twenties of the 1920s, the wealthy indulged in this human desire by throwing extravagant parties, hosting grandiose buildings, and flaunting luxury automobiles. The Gatsby era displays a sort of lost tech of productivity, where the extravagant display of wealth became the means through which the impossible was made possible. And perhaps there’s a rich lesson to be learned here.

At face value, it might seem paradoxical to associate excess with productivity. Isn’t productivity all about efficiency, doing more with less, and saving resources to maximize output? Well, the story of The Great Gatsby and his creation of wealth through ostentatious partying and lavish spending could imply that the act of extravagance itself was a means to rake in an even greater yield.

Consider the character of Jay Gatsby, whose proclamation of wealth was only second to the actual possession of it. He built an empire out of almost nothing through his inciting of lavish parties that attracted the who’s who of the 1920s. By outdoing everyone else in terms of extravagance, he amassed limitless quantities of wealth and social-siteleri cachet. Within this context, Gatsby’s productivity isn’t merely about the economic gains from his lavish parties, but also about how such events and display created a network of influential social connections. Gatsby used this network to boost his business ventures, thereby showing his true productivity was rooted in his ability to create wealth quicker than he could spend it.

Some intriguing anthropological research supports the idea that display and excess can fuel productivity in unexpected ways. For instance, a study on the cattle-keeping herdsmen of East Africa showed that they considered goats to be less valuable than cattle, as the latter enabled more ostentatious displays of wealth and social prestige within their community. As such, the herdsmen often engaged in cattle raids simply to outdo their neighbors and gain superior social status.

While the herdsmen of East Africa might not boast the tales of opulence comparable to the social climbers of the Roaring Twenties, the general functional relationship between display, excess, and productivity is undeniable.

The historical polarity of the sublime excess of the Roaring Twenties and the puritanical work ethic espoused by earlier eras might be echoing a forgotten wisdom. One that transcends superficial concerns of efficiency, embracing the importance of visual, audial, and psychological cues that propel the human spirit onward in pursuit of the impossible. After all, who says productivity is only about getting more done, in less time, and spending less in the process?

F. Scott Fitzgerald didn’t bring this to the forefront, but his portrayal of the newly-rich in The Great Gatsby tantalisingly suggests that sometimes, excess serves a purer, more ancient function: to inspire human ambition and encapsulate productivity in the most splendid of garbs.

The lost tech of The Great Gatsby reminds us of a vital lesson. That excess and productivity aren’t mutually exclusive. Rather, they can reinforce each other in the fulfilment of exceptional human achievements and social status reach. Plus, who can deny the allure of a little extravagance, from time to time, in a world driven by continuous demands for resourcefulness?
