How to Pair Wine with Food Like a Pro


Imagine the perfect meal - the perfectly cooked steak, the crunchy yet tender roasted vegetables, and the delicious wine pairing. Today, I’ll be unveiling the secrets of expertly matching wines with your favorite dishes. Follow these tips and guidelines, and you’ll feel like a pro in no time!

Understanding Wine Basics

Wine can be intimidating, but it’s essential to comprehend its basics for an enjoyable pairing experience. There are six main types of wine: red, white, rosé, sparkling, fortified, and dessert. Knowing the characteristics of each type will guide your pairing choices:

  1. Red Wines - full-bodied and robust, they complement hearty dishes. Examples include Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah, and Pinot Noir.
  2. White Wines - lighter and crisp, perfect for delicate meals. Chardonnay, Pinot Grigio, and Sauvignon Blanc are popular white options.
  3. Rosé Wines - slightly sweet with a fruity taste. They suit summer salads, grilled fish, and light appetizers.
  4. Sparkling Wines - exciting and effervescent. We recommend pairing them with appetizers and poultry dishes.
  5. Fortified Wines - sweet and strong, great with desserts or as a digestif. Port and Sherry are well-known fortified wines.
  6. Dessert Wines - made for pairing with sweet treats. Try a glass of Sauternes with chocolate cake or Tawny Port with Christmas pudding.

Matching Wine with Food - The Holy Grail

Matching wine and food is more than simply choosing a red wine with steak. It’s an art that considers flavors, textures, and regions. To be a pro, start by focusing on the following:

  1. Flavors and Tannins

Wines have flavors, and so do your dishes. When you match similar flavors, you create a symphony of taste. For instance, full-flavored wines like Malbec match well with spicy, bold foods. In contrast, delicate wines, such as Pinot Noir, need milder dishes.

Tannins, found in red wines, pair wonderfully with fatty meats. That’s why the famous ’trophy’ wine and food pair - Cabernet Sauvignon with a juicy steak - is a home run.

  1. Texture

Texture is an overlooked aspect of pairing. The idea is to match hard textures with light wines (risotto with a crisp Sauvignon Blanc) and soft textures with heavier wines (cheesy risotto with a full-bodied Chardonnay).

  1. Regional Pairings

Certain regions are known for specific wine and food pairings, owing to their local produce and cooking styles. Think Italian Sangiovese with tomato-based pasta dishes or Spanish Rioja with their signature tapas.

  1. Temperature and Aging

Don’t forget to consider the aging factor. Pair old wines with complex dishes for a harmonious blend of flavors. Younger wines work well with simple, light dishes.

Tips for Successful Pairing

Here are some handy tips for successful wine and food pairing:

  • Try subtle wines with complex dishes and robust wines with simple dishes.
  • Avoid pairing fish with oaky wines or sweet wines with spicy foods.
  • Remember that acidity in wine can cut through richness or saltiness.
  • Don’t shy away from experimenting!
  • Lastly, if a pairing doesn’t work, switch up the wine, not the food!

In conclusion, wine and food pairing requires practice and curiosity. The more you experiment, the better you’ll get. So uncork a bottle, fire up the grill, and enjoy the art of pairing to its fullest!
