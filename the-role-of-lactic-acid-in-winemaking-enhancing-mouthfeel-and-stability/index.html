The Role of Lactic Acid in Winemaking: Enhancing Mouthfeel and Stability


Lactic acid, a naturally occurring organic acid, plays a significant role in the winemaking process. While it may not be as well-known as other components such as sugars and tannins, lactic acid contributes to the overall quality and characteristics of wine. In this blog post, we will explore how lactic acid enhances the mouthfeel and stability of wine, providing a deeper understanding of its importance in winemaking.

What is Lactic Acid?

Lactic acid is a type of acid produced during a secondary fermentation process known as malolactic fermentation (MLF). Unlike primary fermentation, which converts sugars into alcohol, MLF involves the conversion of harsher malic acid into softer lactic acid. This process is primarily carried out by lactic acid bacteria naturally present in the wine or added intentionally.

Enhancing Mouthfeel

One of the key contributions of lactic acid to wine is its impact on mouthfeel. Mouthfeel refers to the tactile sensations experienced in the mouth when consuming wine, including texture, body, and weight. Lactic acid helps to enhance the overall smoothness and roundness of wine, providing a more enjoyable drinking experience.

When lactic acid is produced through MLF, it reduces the overall acidity of the wine. This reduction in acidity helps to balance the flavors and makes the wine less sharp or tart. The softer and creamier mouthfeel resulting from MLF can be particularly beneficial in the case of high-acidity wines, such as cool-climate white wines or some red wines.

Improving Stability

In addition to enhancing mouthfeel, lactic acid also plays a crucial role in improving the stability of wine. Stability refers to the ability of wine to maintain its sensory characteristics over time, without undergoing undesirable changes. Lactic acid contributes to the stability of wine in several ways.

Firstly, lactic acid supports microbial stability by inhibiting the growth of harmful microorganisms that could spoil the wine. The presence of lactic acid bacteria during MLF creates an environment that is unfavorable for spoilage bacteria, thus reducing the risk of wine spoilage.

Secondly, lactic acid can help mitigate the risk of volatile acidity. Volatile acidity refers to the presence of volatile acids, such as acetic acid, which can give wine a sharp vinegar-like smell and taste. Through MLF, lactic acid production helps to reduce volatile acidity levels and improve the overall aroma and flavor profile of the wine.

Conclusion

Lactic acid, a product of malolactic fermentation, plays a vital role in winemaking by enhancing mouthfeel and improving stability. Its ability to soften acidity and create a smoother mouthfeel contributes to the overall quality and enjoyment of wine. Additionally, lactic acid contributes to the microbial stability and reduction of volatile acidity, ensuring that the wine maintains its desired sensory characteristics over time.

Understanding the role of lactic acid in winemaking allows us to appreciate the complex science behind crafting exceptional wines. The next time you savor a glass of wine, take a moment to appreciate the subtle influence of lactic acid, working behind the scenes to enhance your tasting experience.

Remember to always drink responsibly and in moderation.
