The Best Comic Book Series of the 21st Century


The 21st century has witnessed a remarkable evolution in the world of comic books. With an explosion of creativity, innovative storytelling, and the rise of diverse characters, this era has produced some of the most compelling series that have captivated readers worldwide. In this post, we’ll explore the best comic book series of the 21st century, highlighting their unique contributions to the medium and why they deserve a spot on your reading list.

1. “Saga” by Brian K. Vaughan and Fiona Staples

One of the most groundbreaking series of the 21st century is “Saga,” created by the dynamic duo of writer Brian K. Vaughan and artist Fiona Staples. First launched in 2012 by Image Comics, “Saga” is an epic space opera that blends fantasy and science fiction elements, following the story of Alana and Marko, two lovers from warring extraterrestrial races.

Why It Stands Out

“Saga” stands out for its rich world-building, complex characters, and bold themes. The series explores love, family, and the struggles of parenthood against a backdrop of intergalactic war. Vaughan’s writing is sharp and poignant, often blending humor with heart-wrenching moments, while Staples’ art is a stunning visual feast that brings the vibrant universe to life. The series has received multiple Eisner Awards and is celebrated for its progressive portrayal of gender and sexuality.

2. “The Walking Dead” by Robert Kirkman, Tony Moore, and Charlie Adlard

Launched in 2003, “The Walking Dead” is another monumental series that has had a profound impact on the comic book landscape. Created by Robert Kirkman, with art initially by Tony Moore and later by Charlie Adlard, this post-apocalyptic horror series follows a group of survivors navigating a world overrun by zombies.

Cultural Impact

“The Walking Dead” transcended the comic book realm, spawning a successful television series and a vast media franchise. Its exploration of human nature, morality, and survival in dire circumstances has resonated with audiences. Kirkman’s intricate storytelling and character development ensure that readers remain invested in the fate of each character, making it a compelling read that goes beyond mere horror.

3. “Ms. Marvel” by G. Willow Wilson and Adrian Alphona

Debuting in 2014, “Ms. Marvel” introduces Kamala Khan, a Pakistani-American teenager from Jersey City who discovers her superpowers. Written by G. Willow Wilson and illustrated by Adrian Alphona, this series has become a cultural touchstone for representation in superhero comics.

Breaking Barriers

“Ms. Marvel” stands out for its authentic representation of a Muslim-American teenage girl navigating the challenges of her heritage and identity. The series balances the excitement of superhero antics with relatable teenage dilemmas, making it accessible to a wide audience. Kamala’s journey resonates with readers, and the series has received critical acclaim for its positive portrayal of diversity in comics.

4. “Sandman Overture” by Neil Gaiman

Returning to the world of dream and myth, “Sandman Overture,” a prequel to Neil Gaiman’s seminal “Sandman” series, was published in 2013. This beautifully illustrated series reunites Gaiman with artist J.H. Williams III to explore the origins of Dream, one of the Endless.

A Masterclass in Storytelling

“Sandman Overture” is a visual and narrative masterpiece, showcasing Gaiman’s lyrical writing and Williams’ stunning artwork. The series delves into themes of creation, destiny, and the nature of dreams, providing a rich backstory to one of the most beloved characters in comic book history. For fans of “Sandman,” this series is a must-read that adds depth to the existing lore.

5. “Black Hole” by Charles Burns

Originally serialized from 1995 to 1996, “Black Hole” gained popularity in the 21st century due to its collected edition released in 2005. This graphic novel tells the story of a group of teenagers in the 1970s who contract a mysterious sexually transmitted disease that causes grotesque mutations.

A Dark Coming-of-Age Tale

“Black Hole” is renowned for its haunting art and exploration of adolescence, sexuality, and societal alienation. Burns’ detailed black-and-white illustrations create a surreal atmosphere that complements the unsettling narrative. The graphic novel has been praised for its psychological depth and its commentary on the fears and insecurities of youth.

6. “The Wicked + The Divine” by Kieron Gillen and Jamie McKelvie

Launched in 2014, “The Wicked + The Divine” mixes mythology with contemporary culture, telling the story of a group of deities reincarnated as pop stars, who have only two years to live. Written by Kieron Gillen and illustrated by Jamie McKelvie, this series is a celebration of art, fame, and the ephemeral nature of life.

A Unique Blend of Themes

The series explores themes of worship, fandom, and the impact of celebrity culture. Gillen’s sharp dialogue and McKelvie’s vibrant art create an engaging narrative that keeps readers on their toes. Each issue is a visual spectacle, and the series has garnered a dedicated following, thanks in part to its innovative storytelling and diverse cast of characters.

7. “Y: The Last Man” by Brian K. Vaughan and Pia Guerra

Running from 2002 to 2008, “Y: The Last Man” follows Yorick Brown, the last surviving male human after a mysterious plague wipes out all male mammals on Earth. Written by Brian K. Vaughan and illustrated by Pia Guerra, this series presents a thought-provoking exploration of gender, society, and survival.

Thought-Provoking Exploration

“Y: The Last Man” delves into complex themes of masculinity and gender roles, posing questions about society’s structure in the absence of men. Vaughan’s storytelling is both engaging and insightful, with Guerra’s art enhancing the narrative’s emotional depth. The series has received numerous accolades and remains a relevant commentary on gender dynamics.

8. “Hellboy” (Hellboy in Hell) by Mike Mignola

While “Hellboy” began in the 1990s, the “Hellboy in Hell” series, which started in 2012, continues to captivate audiences. Created by Mike Mignola, Hellboy is a half-demon who works for the Bureau for Paranormal Research and Defense.

A Darkly Enigmatic World

Mignola’s unique art style and storytelling create a dark, atmospheric world filled with folklore, mythology, and horror. “Hellboy in Hell” explores themes of redemption, destiny, and the struggle between good and evil. Mignola’s ability to weave intricate narratives with rich visual storytelling has solidified Hellboy’s status as a modern classic.

9. “Locke & Key” by Joe Hill and Gabriel Rodríguez

Debuting in 2008, “Locke & Key” is a horror-fantasy series written by Joe Hill and illustrated by Gabriel Rodríguez. The story follows the Locke family as they navigate the mysterious and magical keys hidden in their ancestral home, Keyhouse.

A Captivating Blend of Horror and Fantasy

The series combines elements of horror and fantasy, creating an engaging narrative filled with twists and turns. Hill’s ability to craft relatable characters facing supernatural challenges adds depth to the story, while Rodríguez’s artwork enhances the eerie atmosphere. “Locke & Key” has received critical acclaim and was adapted into a successful Netflix series, further expanding its audience.

10. “Paper Girls” by Brian K. Vaughan and Cliff Chiang

Another creation by Brian K. Vaughan, “Paper Girls” debuted in 2015 and quickly garnered a fanbase. Set in the 1980s, the series follows four newspaper delivery girls who stumble upon a time-traveling mystery.

Nostalgic Yet Innovative

“Paper Girls” blends nostalgia with a fresh take on science fiction. Vaughan’s character-driven storytelling captures the essence of childhood friendship and adventure, while Chiang’s art evokes a vibrant and nostalgic feel. The series has been praised for its unique premise and strong character development, making it a standout in contemporary comics.

Conclusion

The 21st century has been a golden age for comic books, marked by innovative storytelling and diverse representation. The series highlighted in this post have pushed the boundaries of the medium, exploring complex themes and captivating readers with their unique narratives. Whether you’re a lifelong comic book fan or a newcomer to the genre, these series offer something for everyone. Dive into these exceptional works and discover the magic of comic books in the 21st century!
