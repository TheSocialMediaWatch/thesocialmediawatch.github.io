How to Survive a Storm in the Wilderness


When venturing into the wilderness, whether it’s a hiking, camping, or backpacking trip, one of the most unpredictable and dangerous elements you can face is a sudden storm. Storms in the wild are not just inconvenient, but they can be life-threatening if you don’t know how to handle them. Being prepared for such emergencies is not just about having the right gear but also understanding the essential survival skills that could make all the difference between life and death.

In this guide, we’ll cover everything you need to know about surviving a storm in the wilderness, from how to prepare for one in advance to what to do if you’re caught off guard.

1. Understanding Storms in the Wilderness

Before we jump into the specifics of surviving a storm, it’s crucial to understand the kinds of storms you might encounter in the wilderness. Storms vary in intensity, from light rain showers to full-blown thunderstorms, and even severe snowstorms or hurricanes.

  • Thunderstorms: These are among the most common types of storms you might face while out in nature. Thunderstorms are characterized by heavy rain, lightning, and strong winds. Lightning is the primary danger, especially when you’re exposed on a high ground or in an open area.

  • Snowstorms: If you’re in colder climates, snowstorms pose unique challenges. Visibility can drop to zero, temperatures can plummet rapidly, and hypothermia becomes a real threat. Snowstorms also create the risk of avalanches in mountainous areas.

  • Hurricanes and Tornadoes: Although rare, particularly in certain regions, hurricanes and tornadoes are extremely dangerous and can uproot trees, cause flash flooding, and destroy shelters.

Regardless of the type, the fundamental strategies for survival remain the same: seeking shelter, staying dry, and maintaining warmth.

2. Preparation Is Key

The first rule of surviving a storm is preparation. It’s essential to be ready before the storm hits.

Gear and Tools to Bring

While you can’t predict when a storm will happen, you can certainly be prepared for it. Here’s a list of essential gear that will help you survive if the unexpected happens:

  • Waterproof Shelter: A durable, weather-resistant tent or tarp is one of the most important pieces of equipment. Ensure your shelter is large enough to fit your gear, as getting your equipment wet in a storm can complicate your survival efforts.

  • Insulating Layers: In cold conditions, layering up with moisture-wicking, insulating clothing is critical. Avoid cotton, as it retains moisture, which can lead to hypothermia. Instead, choose materials like wool or synthetic fibers that dry faster.

  • First Aid Kit: Always carry a first aid kit that includes bandages, antiseptic, pain relievers, and medications for any chronic conditions. Don’t forget items like tweezers and scissors.

  • Fire-starting Kit: In wet conditions, starting a fire can be a challenge. A waterproof fire starter or flint will be essential for warmth and cooking. It’s also a signal for rescuers if you’re lost.

  • Headlamp and Extra Batteries: A reliable headlamp can help you navigate in the dark, particularly during a storm when visibility drops dramatically.

  • Food and Water: Keep high-calorie, non-perishable food items like energy bars or freeze-dried meals. You’ll need to stay nourished to keep your energy up while you wait out the storm.

Skills to Learn Before Heading Out

Knowing how to handle certain situations before you face them will increase your chances of survival. Here are some vital skills to practice ahead of time:

  • Building a Shelter: Practice setting up a shelter in various weather conditions. Learn how to create a lean-to with a tarp or build a shelter using natural materials.

  • Fire Making: Before you go into the wilderness, practice starting a fire in wet conditions. Learn how to use a firestarter, flint, or other methods for creating fire in damp environments.

  • Navigation Skills: Understanding how to use a map and compass (or GPS device) is vital if you need to find shelter or evacuate to a safer location during a storm.

  • First Aid Skills: Know how to handle injuries, especially those that might occur during a storm, such as cuts from falling debris or frostbite in cold weather.

3. When the Storm Hits: Immediate Actions

Sometimes, despite all the preparation, a storm will catch you off guard. Here’s how to respond when you’re suddenly in the midst of one.

Find Shelter Immediately

The first priority when a storm strikes is to find shelter. If you’re caught in a thunderstorm, avoid high ground, as lightning tends to strike tall objects. If you’re in a forested area, seek shelter under thick trees. However, do not stand directly beneath a tree as lightning can strike it and spread to the ground.

For snowstorms, look for a place that offers protection from the wind and cold, such as a cave or rock outcrop. If there’s no natural shelter available, set up your own using a tarp or tent.

In extremely strong winds or tornado-like conditions, it’s critical to find shelter away from trees, cliffs, and unstable structures. Ideally, you should try to find a low-lying area, like a ravine, or even dig a shallow pit for additional protection from flying debris.

Stay Dry and Keep Warm

Wet clothing and exposure to cold temperatures are two of the biggest threats to your survival in a storm. Hypothermia can set in quickly, especially if you’re wet and exposed to wind. This is why it’s essential to keep your shelter waterproof and to layer your clothing.

If you find yourself in a storm with no shelter, your priority should be to build one. In the meantime, wrap yourself in any emergency blankets or extra clothing you have available. If possible, create a fire as soon as you can to dry your clothes and provide warmth.

Stay Hydrated and Nourished

You’ll need energy to stay alert and manage your survival situation. Drink water regularly to stay hydrated, even if you don’t feel thirsty. You can collect rainwater using a tarp or other waterproof material, or you can melt snow if it’s available. However, never drink water from rivers or lakes during a storm, as flooding can contaminate water sources.

Eat high-energy food to keep your strength up, such as granola bars, nuts, or dried fruit. Avoid expending energy unless necessary to conserve your resources.

4. Staying Safe During Specific Types of Storms

Thunderstorms and Lightning

If you’re caught in a thunderstorm, lightning is the most dangerous element. Your shelter is key. Stay away from metal objects and water, as both conduct electricity. If you’re in a tent, don’t touch the walls, and avoid contact with the ground if possible.

If the storm is severe, you may need to stay low to the ground. Crouch down in a crouching position with your feet together, minimizing your exposure to the storm.

Snowstorms and Extreme Cold

Snowstorms present unique challenges, especially in terms of visibility and temperature. A lack of visibility can disorient you, making it difficult to find shelter or navigate. Additionally, snow can accumulate rapidly, potentially causing hypothermia if you’re caught outside.

Always keep your shelter insulated and ensure it has adequate ventilation to prevent the buildup of moisture. If you’re inside a tent, be sure to keep it securely closed to prevent snow from entering.

Hurricanes and Tornadoes

In the rare but serious event of a hurricane or tornado, it’s critical to seek the lowest, most stable shelter possible. If there is a storm shelter nearby, take refuge there immediately. If not, find a low-lying area such as a ditch and lie flat to protect yourself from flying debris. Avoid shelters like tents, as they can be easily destroyed by strong winds.

5. After the Storm: Evaluating the Situation

Once the storm subsides, it’s time to assess the situation and take necessary actions. Here’s what you need to do:

  • Check for Injuries: First, check yourself and your companions for any injuries. Tend to any wounds and stabilize anyone who may be injured.

  • Ensure Shelter Stability: If your shelter is damaged, rebuild it or seek a new one. Don’t assume the storm is over just because the sky has cleared—strong winds and additional rainfall can follow.

  • Continue to Stay Hydrated and Nourished: Make sure everyone in your group continues to drink water and eat food, especially if you’ve been stuck in the storm for an extended period.

  • Assess the Need for Evacuation: If the storm caused significant damage, such as fallen trees or flooding, you may need to evacuate the area. If you’re unsure of the storm’s aftermath, wait for better weather and clearer visibility to make decisions.

6. Conclusion

Surviving a storm in the wilderness requires a combination of preparation, knowledge, and quick thinking. By understanding the types of storms you may face, packing the right gear, and knowing how to respond when the storm hits, you greatly increase your chances of making it through safely.

The wilderness is an unpredictable place, but with the right skills and mindset, you can handle whatever nature throws at you. Stay calm, stay prepared, and you’ll be able to ride out any storm that comes your way.
