Wine and Philosophy: The Ancient Greeks' Influence on Winemaking


From the cradle of Western civilization, Ancient Greece, emerged a culture deeply intertwined with the art of winemaking. For the Greeks, wine was more than just a beverage; it was a symbol of culture, social status, and even philosophic inquiry. Let’s delve into how ancient Greek philosophy shaped winemaking practices and, in turn, influenced the world of vine and grapes.

The ancient Greeks revered the concept of balance. They believed that the world was governed by a central principle known as “isonomia,” meaning equal rights or fairness. This worldview extended to the art of winemaking, bringing about ideas for the perfect blend. In what could be called an “عات” or “dialectical” approach, Greeks blended different grape varieties to craft unique wines, each with distinct characteristics.

One of the most significant philosophical schools of the time, the Stoics, emphasized the significance of living in harmony with nature. They argued that one should consider external influences and adapt accordingly. This stoic approach was embraced by winemakers, who studied weather patterns, soil composition, and grapevines’ growth cycles to produce optimal wines. Much like the stoic ideal of resilience, Greek winemakers learned to bounce back from harsh seasons or poor harvests, constantly adapting their practices to the changing environment.

The Greeks’ fascination with the cosmos and universal order is evident in astronomy-based calendars that dictated the correct vinification process. Ancient Greeks were the first to introduce the idea of the zodiac,umeric cycles, and astronomical almanacs, which they consequently applied to grape cultivation. The grape harvests of ancient Greece were tied to celestial events, with winemakers following a lunar calendar to pinpoint the best time for pressing grapes.

Wine, in ancient Greece, was not merely a drink but represented a deeper societal connection. From the philosopher Socrates, who held symposiums debating the meaning of life over a glass of wine, to the custom of creating amphora vessels for trading wine, the Greeks found significance in wine’s role as a cultural unifier. Today, this tradition is perpetuated in wineries that uphold the ancestral roots of the vine.

A consequence of Greek philosophy’s profound influence on wine is the masterpiece of Aristotelian logic. Aristotle studied the properties of different wines based on where they were produced. The philosopher recognized the unique characteristics of wines from different regions, each with different climates, soil types, and grape varieties. His observations led to the identification of the concept of “terroir” - the extreme importance of a wine’s place of origin in defining its character.

In conclusion, the influence of ancient Greek philosophy on winemaking cannot be overstated. As society once again recognizes and values the art of winemaking, we rediscover the importance of balance, adaptability, connection, and place - elements ingrained in the ancient world. The future of vine cultivation and wine appreciation may be found in looking back at the philosophic roots of the past.
