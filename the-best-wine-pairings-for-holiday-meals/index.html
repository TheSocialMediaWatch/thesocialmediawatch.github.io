The Best Wine Pairings for Holiday Meals


Holiday meals are a perfect opportunity to gather with loved ones, celebrate traditions, and indulge in delicious food. Yet, one element that can truly transform these festive feasts from good to unforgettable is the wine pairing. Selecting the right wine to complement your holiday dishes adds layers of flavor and elevates the entire dining experience. Whether you’re serving roast turkey, glazed ham, or a vegetarian spread, understanding how to choose the best wine pairings will impress your guests and delight your palate.

Understanding the Basics of Wine and Food Pairing

At its core, great wine pairing balances the weight, flavor intensity, and texture of both the wine and the food. Holiday meals often feature a wide range of dishes—rich gravies, sweetened vegetables, savory proteins, and aromatic herbs. This complexity calls for wines that either harmonize with or provide contrast to different flavors.

The main elements to consider when pairing wine with holiday food include:

  • Acidity: High-acid wines can cut through rich, fatty dishes and refresh the palate.

  • Tannin: Tannins, found mostly in red wines, complement fatty proteins but can clash with bitter greens or spicy elements.

  • Sweetness: Sweet wines pair well with similarly sweet foods and desserts but should be balanced with acidity to avoid cloying the palate.

  • Body: The body of the wine, whether light, medium, or full, should match the heaviness of the dish to prevent overpowering or underwhelming the pairing.

Keeping these factors in mind, let’s dive into the best pairings for classic holiday fare.

Pairing Wine with Turkey and Other Poultry

Turkey is the quintessential holiday centerpiece, especially during Thanksgiving and Christmas in many households. Its mild flavor and lean texture allow for great flexibility, but the accompanying sides—from buttery mashed potatoes to cranberry sauce—intensify the challenge.

White Wine Options for Turkey

  • Chardonnay: A rich, oak-aged Chardonnay with hints of vanilla and butter can complement the roasted flavors and creamy sides. The wine’s medium to full body stands up to turkey gravy and buttery mashed potatoes.

  • Sauvignon Blanc: With lively acidity and citrus notes, Sauvignon Blanc cuts through the richness of the meal, refreshing the palate between bites.

  • Riesling: A dry or off-dry Riesling offers fruity sweetness and acidity that pairs beautifully with floral herb stuffing and sweet glazed carrots.

Red Wine Options for Turkey

  • Pinot Noir: Often considered the classic red wine for turkey, Pinot Noir’s light tannins and red fruit flavors blend seamlessly with both the turkey and cranberry sauce.

  • Beaujolais: Made from the Gamay grape, Beaujolais has bright acidity and soft tannins that work well with lighter poultry dishes and varied holiday sides.

  • Zinfandel: For those who prefer a bolder red, a fruit-forward Zinfandel with peppery notes can stand up to spiced or smoked turkey.

The Best Wines for Glazed Ham

Ham brings its own unique set of challenges due to its salty, smoky, and occasionally sweet flavors. Pairing with the wrong wine can result in a metallic or overly sweet experience, so balance is key.

Ideal Whites with Ham

  • Gewürztraminer: This aromatic wine with rich floral and spice notes balances the saltiness of ham and complements glazes that include honey or brown sugar.

  • Viognier: With its lush texture and apricot flavors, Viognier enhances the sweetness of a glazed ham without overwhelming the palate.

  • Chenin Blanc: A versatile wine with crisp acidity and fruity character, Chenin Blanc handles salty and smoky notes elegantly.

Reds that Complement Ham

  • Grenache: Grenache’s red fruit and subtle spice match well with ham’s smoky-sweet profile while its medium body ensures balance.

  • Côtes du Rhône: These blends combine Syrah, Grenache, and Mourvèdre grapes to provide fruity, peppery notes with moderate tannins, ideal for holiday ham.

  • Pinot Noir: In addition to turkey, lighter red Pinots can also pair surprisingly well with ham’s saltiness and complexity.

Wine Pairing for Beef and Lamb Holiday Dinners

For many, holiday feasts might include prime rib, roast beef, or lamb chops. These rich meats benefit from structured wines that can complement their bold flavor profiles.

Red Wine Choices

  • Cabernet Sauvignon: Full-bodied with firm tannins and dark fruit, Cabernet Sauvignon is a classic match for juicy prime rib and roast beef.

  • Merlot: Softer tannins and plum notes make Merlot a fantastic option if you prefer a mellow but rich pairing.

  • Syrah/Shiraz: This spicy, peppery wine pairs exceptionally well with herb-crusted lamb or dishes with a smoky finish.

Whites to Consider

  • Oaked White Burgundy: The creamy texture and subtle minerality of white Burgundy can stand up to roasted beef dishes, especially if the meal includes rich sauces.

  • Champagne or Sparkling Wine: Don’t overlook sparkling wines, which can cut through the richness of red meat and clean the palate with crisp bubbles.

Vegetarian and Side Dish Wine Pairings

Holiday meals often include a variety of vegetable sides—Brussels sprouts, roasted root vegetables, creamy gratins, and hearty stuffing. These dishes might seem secondary but deserve thoughtful wine pairing to enhance the overall meal.

Versatile Whites for Vegetables

  • Albariño: This bright, citrusy white offers excellent acidity and minerality, complementing roasted vegetables and creamy sides.

  • Pinot Grigio: With its light body and crisp finish, Pinot Grigio pairs nicely with lighter vegetarian dishes.

  • Chenin Blanc: Its tailored sweetness and acidity balance caramelized root vegetables and sweet potatoes.

Red Wines that Work with Veggies

  • Barbera: Italy’s Barbera grape features high acidity and red fruit flavors that bring out the earthiness of dishes like mushroom or lentil stuffing.

  • Dolcetto: Another Italian grape, Dolcetto’s soft tannins and fruity notes work well with vegetable gratins and squash dishes.

  • Grenache: Its light to medium body adapts well to dishes with herbs and roasted flavors.

Dessert and Wine: An Artful Finale

Dessert wines should be at least as sweet as the dessert itself to avoid a clashing bitter taste. Holiday sweets range from pumpkin pie and apple crisp to chocolate yule logs, dictating different kinds of dessert wines.

Sweet and Fortified Wines

  • Port: Classic for chocolatey desserts, a rich, ruby or tawny port enhances deep cocoa flavors.

  • Sauternes: This luscious wine with honeyed apricot notes pairs beautifully with fruit-based desserts and cream sauces.

  • Moscato d’Asti: Lightly sparkling and sweet, Moscato d’Asti complements desserts with citrus or floral notes without overwhelming them.

Lighter Fortified Options for Variety

  • Madeira: With its nutty, caramel flavors, Madeira works well with spiced cakes and nut desserts commonly served at holidays.

  • Ice Wine: An indulgent, intensely sweet wine that pairs ideally with fruit tarts and pies.

Tips for Serving Wine During Holiday Meals

  • Serve at the Right Temperature: Whites should be chilled but not too cold, typically between 45-55°F. Reds breathe best around 60-65°F. Bring colder reds out of the fridge early and avoid serving whites that are too icy.

  • Have Variety Available: Given the diversity of dishes at holiday meals, providing at least one red, one white, and one sparkling or dessert wine ensures there’s something for everyone.

  • Use Appropriate Glassware: Red wines benefit from wider bowls, while whites and sparkling wines taste best in narrower glasses.

  • Consider Wine Decanting: Full-bodied reds gain complexity when allowed to breathe. Decanting 30 minutes before serving can soften tannins and release aromas.

Final Thoughts: Crafting Memorable Holiday Wine Experiences

The best wine pairings for holiday meals enhance flavors without overpowering the rich diversity of dishes. Whether you prefer bold reds, crisp whites, or sweet dessert wines, understanding the flavor profiles and characteristics of your meal is key.

Approach wine pairing as a fun adventure rather than a strict science. Experiment with familiar favorites and unexpected matches. Above all, the best pairing is the one that brings joy to your table and complements the spirit of celebration.

Raise your glass, savor each bite, and make your holiday feast a multisensory experience to remember. Cheers to good food, good wine, and great company!
