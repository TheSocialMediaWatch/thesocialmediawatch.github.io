How to Improve Your Backhand in Tennis


When it comes to improving your tennis performance, the backhand can often be the difference between winning and losing points. Whether you’re a beginner looking to build solid fundamentals or an advanced player striving for more consistency, power, and control, refining your backhand stroke is essential. This guide will take you through the step-by-step process to upgrade your backhand, covering technique, drills, footwork, mental approach, and common mistakes to avoid.

Understanding the Importance of the Backhand

The backhand is one of the two primary groundstrokes in tennis, alongside the forehand. It’s typically played on the non-dominant side and can be hit as a one-handed or two-handed stroke. While the forehand often gets more attention due to its natural power and reach, a reliable backhand gives you balance on the court, enabling you to cover more ground and return shots effectively under pressure.

Many players struggle with backhand consistency or power, leading to weaker returns and lost rallies. Improving your backhand not only enhances your baseline game but also boosts your confidence when facing opponents who target your “weaker side”.

One-Handed vs. Two-Handed Backhand: Which Is Right for You?

Before jumping into technique, it’s important to determine which backhand style suits you:

  • One-Handed Backhand: Offers greater reach and fluidity, often favored for its ability to generate topspin and slice shots. It requires solid timing and strength.
  • Two-Handed Backhand: Provides extra stability and power, making it a popular choice, especially among beginners and intermediate players. It allows better control when dealing with heavy topspin from opponents.

Many modern pros use two-handed backhands due to the advantages in power and consistency, but some elite players still showcase masterful one-handed strokes. Choose the grip and style that feels comfortable, complements your strengths, and allows you room to grow.

Step-by-Step Technique for a Flawless Backhand

1. The Grip

The foundation of a great backhand starts at the grip. For a one-handed backhand, the Eastern backhand grip is most common. Place the base knuckle of your index finger on bevel 1 of the racket handle (if the racket is vertical, bevels are numbered clockwise for right-handers).

For a two-handed backhand, the dominant hand usually uses a Continental grip while the non-dominant hand is positioned above with an Eastern forehand grip. This combination supports power and control.

2. Ready Position and Stance

Stay alert and maintain a low, balanced stance with knees slightly bent. When you anticipate the ball coming to your backhand side:

  • Rotate your shoulders and hips so your non-dominant shoulder points toward the net.
  • Shift your weight slightly onto your back foot.
  • Hold the racket with both hands (for two-handed) or one hand (for one-handed) out in front, ready to swing.

3. Backswing

The backswing should be smooth and compact:

  • For one-handed, coiling your upper body while taking the racket back is key. Your racket head should point downward or slightly upward.
  • For two-handed, the non-dominant hand helps pull the racket back, creating tension and storing energy.

Avoid over-swinging; a controlled backswing allows quicker preparation for the shot.

4. Forward Swing and Contact

Transfer your body weight from the back foot to the front foot while uncoiling your torso.

  • Swing the racket forward with a low-to-high motion to generate topspin.
  • Make contact with the ball in front of your body and at waist height.
  • Keep your eyes on the ball to ensure precise timing.

5. Follow-Through

A complete follow-through is fundamental for power and accuracy:

  • For one-handed backhands, the racket should finish over your shoulder.
  • For two-handed backhands, the racket often finishes on the opposite side of your body.
  • Your body should be facing slightly forward with hips open to the net.

A fluid follow-through helps avoid injury and adds control.

Essential Drills to Strengthen Your Backhand

Improvement comes with focused practice. Here are some effective drills that emphasize different aspects of the backhand:

Cross-Court Backhand Rally

Find a partner or ball machine and rally cross-court using only your backhand side. This drill builds consistency, control, and footwork by forcing you to focus on placement.

Backhand Approach Shots

Practice coming to the net using your backhand to execute approach shots. This improves your ability to transition from baseline to net, crucial in singles and doubles.

Backhand Slice Practice

Incorporate slice shots to your backhand training. Slice adds variety to your game and helps you neutralize powerful shots. Hit low, skidding backhands to the service box or baseline.

Shadow Swings

Without a ball, practice your backhand motion focusing on footwork, grip, and swing path. Visualizing perfect technique can reinforce muscle memory.

Resistance Band Exercises

Boost the strength needed for your backhand by performing resistance band exercises targeting the forearms, wrist, and shoulders. This helps increase racket head speed and stability.

Footwork and Positioning: The Unsung Heroes

Often underestimated, footwork is a game changer for backhand success. A good backhand isn’t just about the arm; it’s about positioning your body correctly to hit the ball.

  • Split Step: When your opponent hits, perform a small jump to prepare for lateral movement.
  • Side Shuffle and Crossover Steps: These help you quickly get into the ideal hitting zone.
  • Balance: Maintain a low center of gravity and avoid leaning too far forward or backward.

Fine-tuning footwork drastically reduces errors and reactive times.

Common Backhand Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Identifying weak points is necessary for rapid improvement. Watch out for these pitfalls:

  • Improper grip: Using the wrong grip can hamper shot control and power. Experiment until your grip feels natural.
  • Off-balance swings: Swinging while off-balance often leads to mishits.
  • Hitting too late: The ball should be struck in front, not beside or behind your body.
  • Poor follow-through: Cutting the follow-through short limits spin and speed.
  • Over-swinging: Big swings may reduce control and increase fatigue.

Video recording your strokes during practice and reviewing technique can help spot and correct these issues.

Mental Tactics to Boost Your Backhand Confidence

Confidence plays a big role in executing backhands under pressure situations. Here are mental tips:

  • Visualization: Imagine successful backhand rallies to build positive mental conditioning.
  • Routine: Develop a consistent pre-shot routine to calm nerves.
  • Stay patient: Don’t rush your backhand even if you feel pressured. Focus on clean technique.
  • Positive reinforcement: Celebrate small improvements to stay motivated.

Mental toughness can turn your backhand into a weapon during tough matches.

How to Incorporate Backhand Training Into Your Practice Schedule

A targeted training plan makes improvement measurable:

  • Warm-up Session: Spend 10-15 minutes on light backhand drills and stretching.
  • Focused Drills: Dedicate 30-45 minutes daily (or every other day) to specific backhand exercises.
  • Physical Conditioning: Add strength and flexibility training 2-3 times per week.
  • Match Play: Simulate real game scenarios at least once weekly.
  • Feedback Loop: Regularly seek input from coaches or film analysis.

Consistency is key — small daily improvements accumulate quickly.

Equipment Tips for Optimizing Your Backhand

Your tennis gear can influence your backhand technique and comfort:

  • Racket: Choose a racket with appropriate weight and balance; heavier rackets offer stability, lighter ones can enhance maneuverability.
  • String Tension: Lower tension offers more power but less control, while higher tension provides accuracy.
  • Grip Size: Ensure your grip size fits comfortably; too small or too large can cause injury or reduced performance.
  • Shoes: Use tennis-specific shoes with proper lateral support for quick side-to-side movement.

Trying different setups during practice sessions can highlight what benefits your backhand stroke the most.

Advanced Backhand Variations to Expand Your Arsenal

Once your fundamental backhand is solid, consider adding these shots:

  • Backhand drop shot: A surprise move that requires delicate touch.
  • Backhand topspin lob: Useful to counter aggressive net players.
  • Inside-out backhand: Hitting sharp angled shots to destabilize opponents.

Experimenting with these will make your backhand unpredictable and more effective.

Improving your backhand in tennis is a rewarding journey that requires a blend of technical knowledge, practice, and mental readiness. By following the steps outlined here, you’ll see enhanced consistency, power, and variety in your backhand, ultimately elevating your overall game. Every court session is an opportunity—embrace it, and your backhand will soon become one of your most formidable weapons on the tennis court.
