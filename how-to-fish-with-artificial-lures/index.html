How to Fish with Artificial Lures


Fishing with artificial lures is one of the most exciting and rewarding techniques for both novice and experienced anglers. Whether you’re targeting freshwater or saltwater species, artificial lures offer versatility and effectiveness that can mimic the behavior of live prey. This guide will take you through everything you need to know about fishing with artificial lures, from understanding the different types of lures to choosing the right one for specific fish species and conditions.

Understanding Artificial Lures

Artificial lures are man-made baits designed to resemble the appearance and movement of real prey. These lures can be crafted from various materials such as plastic, wood, metal, or rubber. They come in numerous shapes, sizes, and colors, each serving a unique purpose to attract different types of fish.

The primary goal of using artificial lures is to trigger a predatory response from fish. By mimicking the movement, color, and shape of prey species, artificial lures can entice fish to strike, even when live bait is unavailable or less effective. The key to successful artificial lure fishing is understanding how these lures work and how to present them in a way that mimics natural prey.

Types of Artificial Lures

There are several types of artificial lures, each designed for different fishing conditions and species. Let’s explore the most common types of artificial lures and their uses:

1. Crankbaits

Crankbaits are hard-bodied lures that are designed to dive to a specific depth and imitate a wounded or fleeing fish. These lures have a lip that helps them dive when retrieved, making them ideal for targeting fish that are near the bottom or in deeper waters. Crankbaits come in various shapes and sizes, and their action varies depending on their design. Some produce a subtle wiggle, while others create a more aggressive action that can trigger predatory fish.

When to Use:

  • Ideal for fishing in deeper waters or around structures like rocks or submerged vegetation.
  • Effective for species such as bass, pike, and walleye.

2. Jerkbaits

Jerkbaits are slender, elongated lures that are designed to mimic the erratic movement of an injured baitfish. They can be either hard-bodied or soft, and they are typically fished with a “jerk-pause” retrieve, where you jerk the rod tip to create sudden, sharp movements followed by short pauses. This erratic action is highly effective in triggering strikes from fish.

When to Use:

  • Best for shallow water or areas with low visibility, such as murky lakes or rivers.
  • Great for targeting predatory species like bass, trout, and pike.

3. Spinnerbaits

Spinnerbaits are unique lures that feature one or more spinning blades attached to a wire frame. The spinning blades create flash and vibrations in the water, which attract fish from a distance. Spinnerbaits often have a skirt made of rubber or silicone that mimics the movement of a small fish or insect. These lures are versatile and can be used in a variety of fishing conditions.

When to Use:

  • Effective in both clear and murky water, especially around structures like submerged rocks, weeds, or fallen trees.
  • Excellent for targeting species such as bass, pike, and muskellunge.

4. Soft Plastics

Soft plastic lures are highly versatile and come in many different shapes, including worms, crawfish, minnows, and grubs. They are made from flexible materials that allow them to imitate the movement of live prey. Soft plastics can be rigged in various ways, including Texas rigs, Carolina rigs, or jig heads, depending on the fishing situation.

When to Use:

  • Ideal for bottom fishing or fishing in areas with heavy cover.
  • Popular for bass fishing, but also effective for other species like walleye, trout, and panfish.

5. Topwater Lures

Topwater lures are designed to float on the surface of the water and create a disturbance that simulates the movement of injured or struggling prey. These lures can range from popping lures that create a “popping” sound when retrieved to walking baits that glide side-to-side on the surface. Topwater fishing is thrilling because it often results in explosive strikes from fish as they break the surface to attack the lure.

When to Use:

  • Best for fishing in warm water conditions, particularly during early mornings or late evenings when fish are more active.
  • Highly effective for targeting species like bass, trout, and panfish in shallow waters.

Choosing the Right Lure for the Job

The key to successful artificial lure fishing is selecting the right lure for the fish species, fishing environment, and conditions. Here are a few tips on how to choose the best lure for the job:

Consider the Water Conditions

Fish behavior is often influenced by the clarity of the water. In clear water, fish may be more cautious, so it’s important to use lures that offer a more subtle presentation, such as jerkbaits or soft plastics. In murky or stained water, lures that create vibrations or flash, such as spinnerbaits or crankbaits, can be more effective at attracting fish.

Match the Lure to the Fish Species

Different species of fish are attracted to different types of prey, so it’s important to choose a lure that closely resembles their natural food source. For example, bass are often attracted to lures that mimic minnows or crawfish, while pike may be more attracted to larger lures that resemble small fish. Research the target species in your area to better understand what type of prey they typically feed on.

Pay Attention to the Water Depth

Fish are often found at different depths depending on the time of day and the water temperature. Crankbaits are great for deeper waters, while topwater lures are better for fishing in shallow areas. If you’re unsure about the water depth, it’s a good idea to experiment with different lures that can cover a range of depths.

Time of Day and Seasonal Changes

Fish behavior can also change depending on the time of day and the season. During early mornings and late evenings, fish are often more active and may be more likely to strike topwater lures. In cooler months, fish may move deeper into the water column, so you might need to use deeper-diving lures such as crankbaits. Understanding the seasonal patterns of fish will help you choose the right lure and fishing technique.

How to Fish with Artificial Lures

Now that you have a better understanding of the different types of artificial lures and how to choose the right one, let’s look at how to fish effectively with these lures. The key to success is mastering the retrieve technique and presenting the lure in a way that mimics natural prey.

The Retrieve Techniques

The way you retrieve your artificial lure can make a huge difference in your success rate. Here are some common retrieval techniques for different types of lures:

1. Steady Retrieve

The steady retrieve is the most basic and simple way to retrieve an artificial lure. With this technique, you reel in the lure at a consistent speed, keeping the action smooth and steady. This retrieve is particularly effective with crankbaits and spinnerbaits, as it allows them to swim or spin through the water in a natural way.

2. Jerk-Pause Retrieve

The jerk-pause retrieve is a technique often used with jerkbaits and soft plastics. To perform this retrieve, cast the lure and then jerk the rod sharply to make the lure dart through the water. After each jerk, pause briefly to let the lure “rest” before jerking it again. This technique creates an erratic action that imitates an injured or fleeing baitfish, making it irresistible to predatory fish.

3. Pop-and-Retrieve

For topwater lures, particularly poppers, the pop-and-retrieve technique is commonly used. To do this, you cast the lure out and then give it a sharp “pop” by twitching the rod. This creates a splash on the surface of the water, simulating the movement of a struggling fish. After each pop, pause briefly before repeating the action.

Pay Attention to Your Line

When fishing with artificial lures, your fishing line plays a critical role in how effectively you present your lure. Monofilament, fluorocarbon, and braided lines each have their unique characteristics. Monofilament lines are more flexible and have some stretch, which can be beneficial when using soft plastics. Fluorocarbon lines are less visible underwater and are ideal for clear water conditions. Braided lines are stronger and more sensitive, making them great for deep fishing and using heavier lures.

Make sure to use the appropriate line for the lure and fishing conditions. A line that is too thick or too thin can affect how the lure behaves in the water, leading to fewer strikes.

Be Patient and Persistent

Fishing with artificial lures requires patience and persistence. Unlike live bait, which may naturally attract fish through scent, artificial lures rely on your technique and the fish’s predatory instincts. If you’re not getting bites right away, don’t be discouraged. Experiment with different lures, retrieval techniques, and fishing spots until you find the right combination.

Conclusion

Fishing with artificial lures is a rewarding and dynamic way to target a variety of fish species. By understanding the different types of lures, matching them to the right conditions, and mastering retrieval techniques, you can significantly increase your chances of landing a big catch. Whether you’re fishing for bass in a local lake or targeting saltwater species, artificial lures provide endless opportunities for success. Happy fishing!
