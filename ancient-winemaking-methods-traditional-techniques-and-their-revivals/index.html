Ancient Winemaking Methods: Traditional Techniques and Their Revivals


For centuries, mankind has been cultivating grapes and producing wine. Wine, an ancient tradition, has come a long way from the earliest winemaking techniques to the modern-day advancements. As we delve into the past, we can appreciate the traditions, wisdom, and rhythms of history embodied in ancient winemaking methods.

The Birth of Wine: Ancient Winemaking Techniques

Winemaking traces its roots to 6000 BCE in the foothills of modern-day Iran. The process then was relatively simple: indigenous grapes were crushed and left to ferment in large jars. Fast forward a few thousand years, and winemaking techniques have advanced significantly. Yet, ancient practices have been gradually rediscovered, embraced, and rebooted by winemakers worldwide.

Ancient Winemaking Tools

Early winemakers utilized limited tools and equipment. Grapes were crushed by foot or with simple wooden mallets. Vessels of various sizes - from large jars to small amphorae - were fashioned from clay, wood, or stone. The containers provided protection against exterior elements, favoring controlled fermentation.

Modern Winemaking Techniques vs. Ancient Methods

The modern winemaking process typically involves the use of temperature-controlled stainless-steel tanks, filtration, and sophisticated machinery. In contrast, ancient methods prioritize natural fermentation processes and minimal intervention.

Reviving Ancient Winemaking Methods

In recent years, there has been a resurgence of interest in restoring these age-old traditions. Examples include:

1. Amphora Vessel Winemaking:

The amphora, popular in ancient Greece and Rome, is making a comeback. These earthenware pots provide a unique, slow, and oxygen-controlled environment for fermentation. Recent experiments have produced distinctive, high-quality wines with surprising charm.

2. Carbonic Maceration:

Known as Ancestral Method or Carbonique in France, this ancient Italian technique involves fermenting whole cluster grapes in a sealed container, similar to the natural carbonation process of producing soda. The Wölffendorf winery in California has gained significant attention for its carbonic-matured Pinot Noir, ‘Thiev’ among wine aficionados.

3. Naked Winemaking:

“Naked” or “Skin-contact” wines come from hanging or pressed grapes soaking in their own juice, leading to a more complex wine with increased tannins and color. Sans Lie Baghdad, a small California winery, crafts popular naked wines.

4. Biodynamics and Natural Winemaking:

Wine production with minimal human intervention is seeing a resurgence. Biodynamic farming, inspired by Rudolf Steiner’s ideas, uses cosmic and astrological influences to organically enhance wine quality. Biodynamic and natural wine, often called “terroir-driven,” pays utmost respect to the land from which the grapes are harvested.

Why Bring Back Old Techniques?

These ancient methods offer unique perspectives on winemaking, showcasing varietal characteristics and emphasizing the role of terroir - the land, climate, and other location-specific factors affecting a wine’s quality. Winemakers recognize the value of incorporating ancient wisdom into contemporary winemaking, creating a harmonious blend of old and new.

To Wrap Up…

We have explored the elegant and captivating world of ancient winemaking techniques, which modern winemakers are embracing. This unearthing of past practices offers exciting opportunities to enhance and diversify the world of wine we know today.
