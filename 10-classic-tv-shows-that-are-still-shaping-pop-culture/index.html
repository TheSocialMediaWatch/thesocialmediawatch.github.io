10 Classic TV Shows That Are Still Shaping Pop Culture


Television, more than just a box in the living room, has played a massive role in defining trends, flipping societal norms, and seeding catchphrases we still echo today. Some shows, despite decades off air, have stayed alive in parodies, conversations, and even the ways modern series are written or shot. This isn’t mere nostalgia: From modern streaming libraries to memes flooding your feed, these classic TV shows continue to shape pop culture in ways both subtle and bold.

Let’s take the channel-changer back in time and see how ten iconic television series are still influencing everything from style to storytelling.

1. I Love Lucy: The Blueprint for Sitcoms

Lucille Ball’s antics remain a comedic benchmark. Premiering in 1951, “I Love Lucy” broke ground with its innovative three-camera filming technique and live studio audience, literally inventing the sitcom format most sitcoms use today. But beyond the technical, the show’s physical comedy, relationship dynamics, and character archetypes keep inspiring writers and actors. Modern sitcoms, be it “Friends,” “The Big Bang Theory,” or “Brooklyn Nine-Nine,” routinely echo Lucy’s wit, pacing, and slapstick charm.

Fashion, too, owes Lucy a nod—her polka-dotted dresses and vibrant lip color routinely circle back as vintage-chic must-haves. Even more, gifs of Lucy’s wide-eyed expressions are meme staples. Parodies and references to Ricky’s “You’ve got some ‘splainin’ to do!” pop up everywhere, cementing the show’s catchphrases in social media lingo.

2. The Twilight Zone: Surrealism and Social Critique

Rod Serling’s “The Twilight Zone,” debuting in 1959, offered more than eerie music and twist endings. Each episode served up a cocktail of philosophical speculation, social commentary, and genre-bending storytelling that still inspires genre TV. Anthology series like “Black Mirror” and “American Horror Story” owe a huge creative debt—mirroring the show’s willingness to blend horror, science fiction, and drama to comment on contemporary anxieties.

Phrases like “You’ve just crossed over into the Twilight Zone” are retooled in memes and pop references. Numerous shows, from “The Simpsons” to “Community,” have directly spoofed classic episodes, and pop culture often uses the term “Twilight Zone moment” for anything surreal or bizarre. Its legacy runs deep in the way we talk about technology and morality in entertainment.

3. Star Trek: The Original Series: Optimism and Technology

First airing in 1966, “Star Trek: The Original Series” boldly went where no TV show had gone before, envisioning a utopian and diverse future. Its storytelling introduced concepts such as teleportation, communicators (precursors to cell phones), and AI companions—ideas that have shaped the aesthetics and themes of countless science fiction films and series since.

Gene Roddenberry’s vision directly inspired scientists, astronauts, and tech creators. But in pop culture, “Star Trek” stands for more than just gadgets. Its fandom practically invented the modern concept of a ‘fandom’—conventions, cosplay, and even fan fiction. The show’s inclusive worldview, forward-thinking technology, and moral dilemmas echo today, influencing everything from “The Orville” to “Stranger Things.” And let’s not forget, “Beam me up, Scotty” remains one of the most quoted lines, period.

4. The Brady Bunch: The Blended Family Model

“The Brady Bunch,” airing from 1969 to 1974, may seem campy now, but its depiction of a blended family was a seismic shift for prime-time audiences. Carol and Mike Brady’s six kids navigated sibling squabbles, school drama, and wild hairdos—planting the roots for the modern ensemble family sitcom.

Everything from style to slang (“Marcia, Marcia, Marcia!”) keeps boomeranging back in pop shorthand. You see echoes of “The Brady Bunch” in everything from “Modern Family” to “The Fosters.” Even the show’s catchy theme song and the famous opening grid reappear in parodies, Super Bowl commercials, and sitcom spoofs. The Brady house now stands as a cultural icon, regularly popping up on renovations shows and 1970s nostalgia lists.

5. MAS*H: Blending Laughter and Trauma

Set against the backdrop of the Korean War, “MAS*H” (1972-1983) walked a fine line—making viewers laugh even as it explored the traumas of conflict. This mixture of heartfelt drama and black comedy set the tone for future dramedies. Its influence can be tracked to medical dramas like “Grey’s Anatomy,” and even shows such as “Scrubs” adopt its blend of humor and pathos.

The show’s series finale remains the most-watched TV episode in history. Pop culture has kept “MAS*H” alive through allusions, parody episodes, and constant reruns. It taught future creators that sitcoms didn’t have to dodge tough issues—meaningful stories and laughs could coexist, deeply affecting both TV storytelling and audience expectations.

6. Saturday Night Live: The Comedy Launchpad

Since 1975, “Saturday Night Live” has been synonymous with American humor. More than a sketch show, SNL created a launchpad for comedians and actors who dominated every other entertainment platform—Eddie Murphy, Tina Fey, Will Ferrell, to name a few. Its comedy sketches keep spinning into movies, viral clips, and water-cooler conversations.

The structure—a rotating host, live musical acts, and topical satire—shapes how late-night and sketch comedy are produced. SNL’s “Weekend Update” turned parody news into an art form, directly influencing “The Daily Show” and “Last Week Tonight.” Despite changes in taste and tone, SNL’s fingerprints are on almost every aspect of American comedic culture. Catchphrases like “Live from New York, it’s Saturday Night!” and meme-worthy sketches (“More cowbell,” anyone?) circulate endlessly.

7. The Mary Tyler Moore Show: The Working Woman’s Anthem

Aired from 1970 to 1977, “The Mary Tyler Moore Show” gave TV a new female role model—a single woman succeeding in her career, with no need for a husband to save her. This was something revolutionary for mainstream television at the time, setting a new standard for how women were written and portrayed.

Beyond inspiring writers and creators of “30 Rock,” “Murphy Brown,” and “Parks and Recreation,” the show’s structure became the template for workplace comedies. Mary’s quirky, lovable newsroom family laid the foundation for the ensemble-driven plots that would dominate decades of sitcoms. Even the iconic hat toss from the opening credits proves impossible to leave out of pop culture references—see parodies in everything from “Sex and the City” to “The Simpsons.”

8. Cheers: The Bar Where Everybody Knows Your Name

“Cheers” (1982-1993) turned the Boston bar setting into one of television’s most famous hangouts. The chemistry between Sam, Diane, and the rest of the bar’s lovable regulars created a familiar, cozy vibe that would inspire countless “hangout sitcoms.”

From “Friends” to “How I Met Your Mother,” the concept of a local public space as emotional core owes a debt to “Cheers.” The series also pioneered the slow-burning romance story arc—think Ross and Rachel. Sam and Diane’s “will they, won’t they?” dynamic became TV gospel. The show’s theme song alone (“Where Everybody Knows Your Name”) is still referenced in movies, commercials, and memes.

9. The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air: Hip-Hop and Heart

In 1990, “The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air” exploded onto screens, forever linking Will Smith’s charisma to the transformation of ‘90s TV comedy. The show mixed fish-out-of-water laughs with poignant moments tackling race, class, and family. Its cool blend of music, fashion, and dance—think Carlton’s infamous dance—fused hip-hop style with wider pop style. Tracksuits, crisp sneakers, and high-top fades? That’s Fresh Prince enduring style.

The show’s catchy theme song is a rite of passage for karaoke enthusiasts. Its emotional moments, especially “Why don’t he want me, man?” (Will’s breakdown scene), have become go-to GIFs and reference points. Modern reboots and dramatic reinterpretations, like the “Bel-Air” series, showcase the long shadow “The Fresh Prince” continues to cast over TV and internet culture.

10. The Simpsons: Cartoons for Grown-Ups

Since its 1989 debut, “The Simpsons” has thrived longer than most of us have held jobs. Matt Groening’s yellow-skinned, dysfunctional family made “cartoons for adults” an industry norm, opening the floodgates for edgy animated series such as “Family Guy,” “Bob’s Burgers,” and “Rick and Morty.”

The show’s cultural references, lampooning of everything from politics to pop stars, and endless parade of guest stars make it a never-ending pop commentary. Phrases like “D’oh!” and “Eat my shorts!” are woven into daily conversation. Meme culture is absolutely saturated with Homer, Bart, and the gang—if there’s a pop culture moment, odds are there’s a “Simpsons” gif for it.

Ripples Across Generations

These ten shows are more than just reruns or Trivial Pursuit answers; they are the foundational DNA of modern entertainment. From the physical comedy of Lucy Ricardo to the political barbs hurled on Saturday nights, their influence stretches far beyond their original airtime.

Syndication and streaming have only broadened their audience. Millennials and Gen Z are just as likely to quote Simpsons jokes, binge “Star Trek” on a sci-fi kick, or reference “Fresh Prince” when explaining family drama. Even the way modern series are pitched or shot—ensemble casts, running gags, single-camera setups—trace back to choices these shows first made.

And perhaps the deepest influence is in language itself. So many catchphrases, punchlines, and sneaky references stem from these classic shows. From Buzzfeed listicles to TikTok sounds, their echoes ripple through every slice of internet culture.

The Enduring Power of Classic TV

Why do these shows last? Partly, they nailed universal themes—family, ambition, absurdity, love. And every show on this list, despite being products of their era, offers a blueprint for succeeding in TV’s constantly changing landscape. Whether it’s representation, risk-taking, witty writing, or simply unforgettable characters, these shows laid the groundwork that others are still building on.

When you next laugh at a meme, pick an outfit “inspired by the ’90s,” or find yourself humming a familiar TV theme song, you’re part of the ongoing conversation between generations. Classic television isn’t just something we used to watch—it’s something we still live, quote, remix, and build upon every day. And as entertainment evolves, those old reruns might just have a few more surprises for us yet.
