The Mythology of Time: Gods and Goddesses of Past, Present, and Future


Time is a concept so vast and profound that it has inspired numerous myths and legends across different cultures. In many ancient societies, time was perceived as a deity, a force beyond human control. We often look to these myths to better understand our own place in the vast continuum of time. This exploration focuses on three key aspects of time: past, present, and future. We’ll examine the gods and goddesses of these three realms, delving into their stories, attributes, and significance.

Chronos: God of Time

In Greek mythology, Chronos, the personification of time, was the father of the universe. He was often depicted as an old man with winged sandals, symbolizing the steady passage of time. Chronos created the world and ruled over its cycles. Sadly, he became a symbol of hunger for power, devouring his children to prevent them from surpassing him. This myth might enforce the humanistic perspective that life has a predetermined course and that we’re destined to follow it.

Kali: Goddess of Time

In Hindu mythology, time and creation are intertwined with Kali, a formidable goddess. She represents the cyclic nature of time, symbolizing change, transformation, and infinity. Painted with a dark complexion, Kali embodies the idea that time erases and alters all things, offering a fresh start. Kali’s slaying of demons tied to creation’s cyclic purifying progress via regeneration.

Anansi: God of Storytelling Time

African mythology offers Anansi the Spider, a God of stories and wisdom. Anansi’s roles as both the weaver and the trickster highlight time’s slippery nature. Anansi’s tales explore humanity’s cunning, embellish its tragedies and joys, while teaching listeners lessons about life, times, and consequences. Anansi’s memory-filled web symbolizes the archiving function of storytelling, adding depth and richness to the passing of time.

Eternity: Goddess of Present Time

In Egyptian mythology, eternity exists in the present, symbolized by Ma’at, the goddess of balance. She personifies order in chaos and is often depicted as a woman with a feather headdress. Ma’at not only embodies genuine sobriety but also practices fairness, justice, and reason. Her continuation sanctifies the art of treading time’s present moment wisely.

Nostradamus: Prophet of Future Time

Though not a goddess or god, Michel de Nostredame, known more simply as Nostradamus, proves instrumental in the study of future time. A renowned astrologer, Nostradamus unveiled ‘The Centuries,’ a collection of 942 poetic quatrains, offering potential clues to future events. His theories shaped our understanding of time’s predictive value, urging humans to prepare for upcoming tests and challenges.

In conclusion, whether we gird ourselves with wings or webs, revel in the dance of cosmic cycles, draw from a wellspring of ancient wisdom, or perceive steady harmony, our myths enrich our understanding of time and our place within it. These gods and goddesses, stories, and concepts offer a profound reflection on how to relate to chronology and eons past, present, and future. By embracing the wisdom found within these myths, we can start to grasp the elusive and enigmatic nature of time itself. So, how will you choose to spend your time wisely?
