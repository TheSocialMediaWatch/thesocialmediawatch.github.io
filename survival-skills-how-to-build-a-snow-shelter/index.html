Survival Skills: How to Build a Snow Shelter


When winter weather strikes, and you find yourself in a cold and snowy environment, knowing how to build a snow shelter could make the difference between life and death. Snow shelters are crucial for staying warm, dry, and safe in extreme conditions. In this guide, we will explore the steps involved in building a snow shelter, including the necessary tools, techniques, and safety precautions to ensure your survival in harsh winter conditions.

Understanding the Importance of a Snow Shelter

A snow shelter is an essential tool for any winter survival situation, especially if you are caught in the wilderness without proper shelter. Snow is a remarkable material that can provide insulation from the cold, but it must be used effectively. A well-built snow shelter can protect you from the harsh winds, freezing temperatures, and the dangers of hypothermia.

Without proper shelter, exposure to the cold can rapidly lead to frostbite and hypothermia. These conditions occur when the body loses heat faster than it can produce it, causing the core temperature to drop. Hypothermia, in particular, is a serious threat to survival in freezing environments. A snow shelter provides an insulated space where your body heat can accumulate, keeping you safe until help arrives or you can continue your journey.

Types of Snow Shelters

Before you begin constructing a snow shelter, it’s important to understand the different types available. Each type has its own advantages and considerations depending on the situation, environment, and the amount of snow available.

1. Quinzhee

A quinzhee is a traditional snow shelter built by hollowing out a large pile of snow. It is simple to build and requires minimal tools. The quinzhee is often used by winter campers and survivalists due to its effectiveness and the fact that it can be constructed quickly.

Advantages of a Quinzhee:

  • Easy to build with minimal tools.
  • Good insulation properties.
  • Can be built in deep snow.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires a large amount of snow.
  • Takes time to hollow out.

2. Igloo

An igloo is a more complex structure built from blocks of packed snow. It is traditionally associated with the Inuit people, who developed the technique in the Arctic regions. Igloos are highly insulated, and their domed shape helps them shed wind and retain heat efficiently.

Advantages of an Igloo:

  • Excellent insulation properties.
  • Strong structure capable of withstanding heavy snow and wind.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires precise planning and more advanced snow building skills.
  • Takes longer to build than a quinzhee.

3. Snow Cave

A snow cave is another popular snow shelter, built by digging into a snowbank or hillside. It provides a natural, windproof environment and is highly effective in protecting against cold temperatures.

Advantages of a Snow Cave:

  • Provides great protection from wind.
  • Requires less snow than an igloo.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires access to a snowbank or slope.
  • Can be difficult to ventilate if not done properly.

Tools You’ll Need

While it’s possible to build a snow shelter with just your hands, having the right tools will make the process much easier and faster. Below are some tools that will help in constructing a snow shelter:

  • Shovel: A shovel is essential for digging and shaping the snow. A collapsible or small shovel is a good option if you’re traveling light.
  • Saw or Knife: If you’re building an igloo, you’ll need a saw or knife to cut snow blocks. These tools help ensure the blocks are evenly sized and fit tightly together.
  • Rope or String: Rope can be used to measure the size of your shelter or to help with guiding the shape of your snow shelter.

Step-by-Step Guide to Building a Snow Shelter

Building a snow shelter requires patience and planning. Below is a step-by-step guide to help you build a basic snow shelter. Depending on the type of shelter you’re constructing (quinzhee, igloo, or snow cave), the steps may vary slightly.

Step 1: Choose the Right Location

The first step in building a snow shelter is to choose a suitable location. Consider the following factors when selecting your site:

  • Snow Quality: Look for packed, stable snow that is not too loose or powdery. Powdery snow doesn’t provide much insulation and may collapse easily.
  • Wind Protection: Choose a location that is sheltered from the wind, such as near a snowbank, a tree line, or on the leeward side of a ridge.
  • Safety: Avoid areas that could be prone to avalanches or where the snow might be unstable. Avoid valleys or areas where snow may slide downhill.

Step 2: Gather Snow

Once you’ve selected your location, it’s time to gather snow. If you are building a quinzhee or snow cave, you can use the snow in your immediate area. For an igloo, you will need to cut blocks of snow from the surrounding area.

  • For a Quinzhee: Pile snow into a mound about 4-5 feet high and at least 6-8 feet wide. This pile will later be hollowed out to create your shelter.
  • For an Igloo: Cut large blocks of snow from a snowbank or hillside. Each block should be about 12 inches long and 6 inches high. These blocks will be stacked to form the walls of your igloo.
  • For a Snow Cave: Dig into a snowbank or hillside to create a small cave. The opening should be low to the ground to allow warm air to collect inside.

Step 3: Shape the Shelter

  • For a Quinzhee: Once your mound of snow is large enough, wait for the snow to settle and compact naturally. After several hours, or the next day, begin hollowing out the mound with a shovel. Start by digging a small entrance and then continue hollowing out the interior. Make sure to leave a thick layer of snow around the walls to ensure stability and insulation. The interior should be large enough for you to lie down or sit comfortably.

  • For an Igloo: Start by laying the first layer of snow blocks in a circle. As you build, tilt the blocks inward slightly to form the dome shape. Keep the blocks tightly packed to prevent gaps. After completing the walls, create a small hole at the top of the igloo for ventilation. The entrance should be dug at a lower level than the shelter to allow cold air to escape and warm air to stay inside.

  • For a Snow Cave: Begin by digging a horizontal tunnel into the snowbank or hillside. The tunnel should slope downward slightly, allowing cold air to flow out and warm air to rise inside. Dig the tunnel wide enough to crawl through, and then hollow out a small sleeping area inside the cave. Make sure the ceiling is high enough for you to sit or lie comfortably.

Step 4: Insulate and Improve the Shelter

To ensure your shelter is properly insulated, take a few extra steps to add additional protection from the cold:

  • Add Snow Insulation: Cover the walls and floor with a thick layer of snow. This will help retain the heat inside your shelter.
  • Make an Insulated Sleeping Pad: If possible, gather extra snow to create a soft layer on the ground for sleeping. This will help insulate your body from the cold snow beneath.
  • Create Ventilation: Ensure your shelter has proper ventilation to avoid carbon dioxide buildup. A small hole near the top of the shelter (for igloos) or a small vent hole in a snow cave will allow for air circulation.

Step 5: Stay Safe Inside the Shelter

Once your shelter is complete, it’s time to focus on safety and staying warm inside.

  • Dress Warmly: Wear multiple layers of clothing to keep your body insulated. Make sure to keep your feet and hands warm by wearing appropriate gloves and boots.
  • Preserve Body Heat: Once inside your shelter, try to remain as still as possible to preserve body heat. Avoid exposing your body to the cold air outside.
  • Stay Dry: Wet clothing can dramatically lower your body temperature. Make sure to stay dry by using waterproof layers and avoiding moisture from snow or sweat.

Conclusion

Building a snow shelter is a crucial survival skill when you find yourself in a winter wilderness. Whether you opt for a quinzhee, igloo, or snow cave, understanding how to select the right location, gather snow, and build an effective shelter can keep you safe and warm in even the harshest conditions. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you will be prepared to survive the cold and ensure your safety until help arrives or you are able to continue your journey.

Survival in snowy conditions is all about preparation and understanding the environment around you. With the right skills, tools, and techniques, you can create a shelter that will protect you from the deadly effects of cold exposure. So, the next time you venture into the winter wilderness, make sure you are equipped with the knowledge to build a snow shelter and ensure your survival.
