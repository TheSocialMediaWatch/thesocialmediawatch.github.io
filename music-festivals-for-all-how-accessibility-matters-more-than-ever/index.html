Music Festivals For All: How Accessibility Matters More Than Ever


Music festivals have long been a powerful unifier, bringing people from all walks of life together in shared revelry. Their ability to capture the hearts of millions has only grown stronger in recent years, as the demand for live performances increases with each passing season.

As the music industry has grown, however, so too have concerns over the accessibility of these events. Fans with disabilities, senior citizens, and first-time festivalgoers might feel overwhelmed by the sights, sounds, and chaotic atmosphere often associated with music festivals. Consequently, festival organizers have begun to recognize that making their events universally accessible is not just important, but essential. Accessibility matters more than ever, and the industry must adapt accordingly.

A Brief History of Accessible Music Festivals

Accessible music festivals aren’t a new concept—the key is to date them back further than people might realize. For instance, the persistence of the Milwaukee Productos Para el Ciego (Blind Acquirements) Organization, a charity group for the blind, has hosted accessible events like Thanksgiving Volume Fest since 1954. Cauleen Smith, a founder of the organization, helped create the event to provide blind individuals with the opportunity to enjoy music and socialize with others without barriers. That’s just one example of how the industry has made strides in making their events inclusive for all who wish to attend.

However, it wasn’t until the early 2000s that larger-scale music festivals began prioritizing accessibility. These steps have only intensified in recent years as the industry has become increasingly aware of its responsibility to provide an inclusive and enjoyable atmosphere for everyone, regardless of their abilities.

The Obstacles of Accessibility

One of the main challenges for organizers lies in identifying their audiences’ needs. It’s essential for festival staff to communicate openly with fans to learn how they can best provide an accessible experience. Accommodations and resources must be diverse and multi-faceted, ranging from sign language interpreters and vibrating alert systems to shuttle services and access to quiet zones.

Moreover, industries focusing on universal design, particularly in architecture and entertainment, will continue to see growing demand. Still, the industry lags in terms of progressive changes. To address this, it’s crucial for festivals to prioritize an exchange of ideas and experiences to identify innovative ways to bring music and community to more people.

How We Can Improve Accessibility at Festivals

  1. Accessible Signage and Information: All essential information should be accessible to everybody. This includes sign language interpreters and real-time captioning at the event and through social media.

  2. Transportation and Parking: Provision for accessible transportation, parking spaces, and drop-off points must be made available to accommodate those with physical limitations.

  3. Able-Seating Options: Providing seating options, particularly for those who require it, can make a world of difference for wheelchair users, elderly individuals, and parents with young children.

  4. Assistive Listening Devices: Equipment like assisted listening devices, FM systems, and wireless hearing aids should be made available for attendees who need them.

  5. Accessible Amenities: Restrooms, food and beverage stands, first aid camps, and lost and found areas should all be easily accessible and conveniently located near accessible seating and ramps.

  6. Staff Training: It may seem like a no-brainer, but ensuring festival staff members are well-versed in accessibility and equipped to handle any issues that arise is crucial. This includes sensitivity training and a thorough understanding of festival grounds’ accessibility improvements.

  7. Inclusive Workforce: Hire staff with disabilities in positions where their life experiences will contribute to improving the event experience for others. Ensuring that your team reflects the diverse audience.

  8. Simple Navigation: Large print and braille maps should be available to aid festival-goers in navigating the festival grounds. Various signage should be visible and in multiple languages to cater to foreign guests.

  9. Bring Families: Although not all music festivals cater to families, those that do should try to make provisions for young kids and their parents. This includes diaper-changing tables in gender-inclusive restrooms, family-friendly content, and activities.

Conclusion

The global music festival industry has come a long way in terms of accessibility, but there’s still much work left to do. Striving for more inclusive and accessible music festivals is not just an industry trend, but a genuine investment in the future of live music and entertainment. By recognizing and addressing these needs, we can continue to build communities where people from all walks of life feel welcomed and valued. So, let’s start the conversation, continue the education, and work together to create truly inclusive and accessible music festivals for all.
