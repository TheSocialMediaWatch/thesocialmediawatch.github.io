The Mythological Symbolism of Colors and Numbers


In mythologies and ancient belief systems, colors and numbers hold significant symbolic meanings. These symbolic associations have been passed down through generations, shaping the way we perceive and interpret the world around us. In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating world of mythological symbolism of colors and numbers, delving into their deeper meanings and the stories behind them.

Colors

Red

The color red is often associated with passion, power, and vitality. In many mythologies, red is the color of fire and blood, symbolizing both destruction and creation. In ancient Greek mythology, the god of war, Ares, was often depicted wearing red armor, representing the chaos and violence of battle. Red is also associated with love and desire, as seen in the story of Cupid and Psyche in Roman mythology.

Blue

Blue is often associated with calmness, spirituality, and wisdom. In many cultures, blue is the color of the sky and the sea, connecting it to the divine and the unknown. In Hinduism, the god Krishna is often depicted with blue skin, symbolizing his divine nature and his ability to bring peace and enlightenment. Blue is also associated with loyalty and trust, as seen in the symbolism of the “blue blood” in European aristocracy.

Yellow

Yellow is often associated with happiness, enlightenment, and creativity. In many mythologies, yellow is the color of the sun, representing warmth and vitality. In ancient Egyptian mythology, the god Ra, the sun god, was often depicted with a golden yellow disk on his head. Yellow is also associated with knowledge and intelligence, as seen in the symbolism of the “yellow brick road” in L. Frank Baum’s “The Wizard of Oz.”

Green

Green is often associated with growth, fertility, and rebirth. In many mythologies, green is the color of nature and the spring season. In Celtic mythology, the Green Man is a symbol of the cycle of life, death, and rebirth. Green is also associated with envy and jealousy, as seen in the story of Shakespeare’s “Othello.”

Numbers

One

The number one represents unity, singularity, and the beginning. In many mythologies, the number one is associated with the creation of the universe and the divine. In ancient Egyptian mythology, the sun god Ra is often referred to as the “one who became many,” symbolizing the creation of life from a single source. The number one also represents the self and individuality.

Three

The number three represents balance, harmony, and completion. In many mythologies, the number three is associated with the three realms of existence - the heavens, the earth, and the underworld. In Norse mythology, the three Norns, representing past, present, and future, weave the threads of fate. The number three also represents the Trinity in Christianity - the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Seven

The number seven is often associated with mystery, spirituality, and perfection. In many mythologies, the number seven is considered sacred and divine. In Hindu mythology, there are seven chakras, representing the energy centers of the body. In Judaism, the menorah has seven branches, symbolizing the seven days of creation. The number seven also appears frequently in fairy tales and folklore, representing a magical and mystical number.

Nine

The number nine represents completion, transformation, and enlightenment. In many mythologies, the number nine is associated with the end of a cycle and the beginning of a new one. In Norse mythology, Odin hung himself from the world tree Yggdrasil for nine days and nights to gain wisdom and knowledge. The number nine also appears in the Enneagram, a symbol of personality types and self-discovery.

Conclusion

Colors and numbers have played significant roles in mythologies throughout history, shaping our perception of the world and our understanding of the divine. Understanding the symbolic meanings behind these colors and numbers allows us to delve deeper into the stories and beliefs of ancient cultures. So, the next time you see a red rose or hear about the number seven, remember the rich mythological symbolism that lies behind them.
