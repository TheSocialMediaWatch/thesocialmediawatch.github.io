Make Money by Testing Websites and Sharing Your Opinion


In the digital age, where websites are the front door to businesses, apps, and services, user experience matters more than ever before. Companies worldwide invest significant resources to ensure their websites are functional, intuitive, and enjoyable to navigate. One increasingly popular way for these companies to refine their online presence is by engaging everyday people like you to test their websites and provide honest feedback. Not only does this help businesses improve, but it also opens a unique and accessible stream of income for users interested in making money from home or supplementing their earnings with flexible digital gigs.

This comprehensive guide explores how you can turn website testing and opinion sharing into a profitable venture. From understanding the basics of the job, navigating popular platforms, to maximizing your earning potential, this post covers everything you need to know.

What Does Website Testing Involve?

Website testing, also known as user testing or usability testing, involves interacting with websites or apps and offering feedback about your experience. Testers are asked to perform specific tasks, such as navigating through a site, completing a purchase, or using a search feature, while talking through their thoughts. Testers might be prompted to identify confusing layouts, broken links, slow loading times, or areas that could be improved for a better user experience.

The primary goal is to provide insights into how real users interact with digital products, which helps developers and companies create more efficient and enjoyable online environments.

Common Tasks for Website Testers

  • Navigating to specific pages and locating information.
  • Testing mobile responsiveness by using websites on various devices.
  • Completing transactions or form submissions.
  • Exploring new features, buttons, or UI elements.
  • Reporting bugs or errors encountered during interaction.
  • Sharing overall impressions regarding design, wording, and usability.

Why Companies Outsource User Testing

Most companies lack the internal capabilities or, more importantly, the diverse perspectives essential for thorough user testing. Involving real users from varying demographics, tech skill levels, and interests means companies receive a wealth of valuable data that a limited in-house team cannot provide.

Furthermore, outsourcing website testing is cost-effective. Hiring freelancers or utilizing crowdsourced platforms eliminates the need for expensive in-house usability labs, specialized equipment, or corporate focus groups. The flexibility and speed with which testers can be engaged accelerate product iterations.

How to Get Started as a Website Tester

Getting started with website testing requires minimal upfront investment, no specialized skills or certifications. What you need is curiosity, a reliable internet connection, and the ability to communicate clearly your thoughts and observations.

Step 1: Identify Reputable Testing Platforms

Several user testing websites act as intermediaries between companies needing feedback and testers eager to earn money. Popular platforms include:

  • UserTesting: One of the most recognized platforms, offering tests on websites and apps from various industries.
  • TryMyUI: Allows testers to provide video feedback on different usability aspects.
  • Userlytics: Focuses on remote usability testing, providing testers with opportunities to test websites, prototypes, and mobile apps.
  • TestingTime: Connects testers with a broad spectrum of projects, some requiring live interaction.
  • Enroll: Offers micro-testing and poll tasks that are quick to complete.

Registering on multiple platforms increases your chances of receiving more frequent testing invitations.

Step 2: Sign Up and Complete Qualification Tests

Most platforms require potential testers to create accounts and complete preliminary qualification tests designed to assess your ability to articulate feedback and follow instructions. These tests often involve recording a sample review of a simple website or completing brief tasks to demonstrate your usability insight and communication clarity.

Step 3: Familiarize Yourself with Testing Guidelines

Once approved, itâ€™s important to understand how to perform the tests properly. Each platform provides tutorials or guidelines demonstrating how to record your screen, use microphones for verbal comments, and submit effective reviews.

Step 4: Start Taking Tests and Providing Feedback

Assignments may vary from short surveys to detailed explorations of complex websites. Always read test instructions carefully, ensure your recording setup works flawlessly, and, importantly, be honest and thorough in your feedback.

How Much Can You Earn Testing Websites?

Compensation varies depending on factors such as the length and complexity of the test, the platform used, and the specific clientâ€™s budget. On average:

  • Per Test Earnings: Range from $10 to $60.
  • Time Commitment: Typically 10 to 30 minutes per test.
  • Monthly Income: Active testers can make anywhere from $100 to $500 or more monthly by consistently engaging with testing opportunities.

Specialized or live moderated tests may offer higher pay but might require scheduling availability.

Tips to Maximize Your Earnings

  1. Increase Acceptance Rates: Practice delivering clear, concise, and constructive feedback in practice tests to boost approval chances.
  2. Diversify Your Platforms: Sign up for multiple testing sites to increase the volume of available tests.
  3. Respond Quickly: Tests can have limited slots; responding quickly improves your participation rate.
  4. Enhance Equipment: Invest in a good microphone and webcam to ensure your recordings are clear and professional.
  5. Stay Consistent: Block out dedicated times during your week solely for testing assignments.
  6. Provide Detailed Feedback: Clients value actionable insights; thoughtful comments are often rewarded with better ratings and more test invitations.

Occasionally, technical glitches (poor internet, incompatible devices) can interfere with test submissions. Prepare by testing your setup beforehand and maintaining a reliable internet connection.
