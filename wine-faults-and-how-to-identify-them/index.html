Wine Faults and How to Identify Them


Wine is a complex and fascinating beverage that has been enjoyed by people for centuries. It can be a source of great pleasure and enjoyment, but sometimes, it can also disappoint. One of the reasons for this is the presence of wine faults. Wine faults are undesirable characteristics that can affect the quality and enjoyment of a wine. In this blog post, we will explore some common wine faults and learn how to identify them.

Oxidation

Oxidation is one of the most common wine faults, and it occurs when wine is exposed to too much oxygen. This can happen during the winemaking process or after the bottle has been opened. Oxidized wine often has a brownish color and a flat taste. It may also have a nutty or sherry-like aroma. To identify oxidation, pay attention to the color, smell, and taste of the wine. If it looks and tastes dull, it may be oxidized.

Cork Taint

Cork taint is another common wine fault that is caused by a compound called TCA (2,4,6-trichloroanisole). TCA can contaminate the wine if the cork used to seal the bottle is infected. Cork taint is characterized by a musty or wet cardboard smell. It can significantly affect the aroma and flavor of the wine, making it unpleasant to drink. To identify cork taint, give the wine a good sniff. If you detect a musty or damp aroma, it may be affected by cork taint.

Brettanomyces

Brettanomyces, often referred to as “Brett,” is a type of yeast that can cause wine faults. It can produce compounds that create barnyard or horse stable-like aromas in the wine. While some wine enthusiasts appreciate these earthy aromas in small amounts, excessive Brettanomyces can overpower the wine and make it unpalatable. To identify Brettanomyces, take a deep sniff of the wine. If you detect strong barnyard or sweaty saddle-like aromas, it may be affected by Brett.

Volatile Acidity

Volatile acidity refers to the presence of acetic acid in wine. In small amounts, volatile acidity can contribute to the wine’s complexity, but excessive amounts can lead to a vinegar-like smell and taste. High levels of volatile acidity can make the wine unpleasant and difficult to drink. To identify volatile acidity, pay attention to the aroma of the wine. If it smells vinegary or sour, it may have high levels of volatile acidity.

Sulfur Compounds

Sulfur compounds are commonly used in winemaking to prevent spoilage and oxidation. However, if used in excessive amounts, these compounds can create off-putting aromas and flavors in the wine. Sulfur faults can manifest as a burnt match or rotten egg smell. In some cases, the wine may even taste like burnt rubber. To identify sulfur faults, give the wine a good sniff. If you detect any unpleasant, pungent aromas, it may be affected by sulfur compounds.

Conclusion

Being able to identify wine faults is an essential skill for any wine enthusiast. By understanding the common wine faults and their characteristics, you can make informed decisions about the wines you choose to drink and enjoy. Remember to trust your senses and pay attention to the color, smell, and taste of the wine. If something seems off, it’s worth investigating further. Cheers to a better understanding of wine faults and the enjoyment of quality wines!
