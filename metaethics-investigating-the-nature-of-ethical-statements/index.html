Metaethics: Investigating the Nature of Ethical Statements


Metaethics, often regarded as the foundation of all moral reasoning, attempts to understand the nature of moral judgments and their implications. It is divided into various sub-disciplines, each with unique perspectives and methodologies. By examining these divergent views, we might better explore the essence of moral discourse.

At the heart of metaethical inquiry lies one crucial question: Are moral judgments true or false? Are they factual claims or expressions of personal feelings? This Melian scholar, echoing the words of ancient Greek historian Herodotus, asserts that “[we] bow our heads to perplexity” when examining the underpinnings of morality (Herodotus, 5.87). Yet, a closer look at the answers suggested by various metaethical perspectives provides an intriguing and intellectually stimulating journey.

  1. Descriptivism

Descriptivists argue that moral judgments are purely descriptive and devoid of any objective truth. Ethical statements merely describe what someone believes, feels, or intends to do. For example, when a vegetarian says “killing animals for food is wrong,” they are articulating their moral perspective and expecting others to agree.

  1. Coherentism

Coherentists assert that moral judgments are justifiable based on a consistent set of beliefs. They reject the idea of a comprehensive, objective moral truth. Instead, moral reasoning becomes a web of interconnected, yet subjective, beliefs. Each individual constructs their moral fabric, and it remains valid so long as it holds together coherently.

  1. Cognitivism

Cognitivists argue that moral judgments have truth-value, either as objectively true or false. They dispute the notion of morality as mere sentiment or subjective preference. It posits that moral judgments can be debated, and consensus can sometimes be reached.

  1. Relativism

Relativists emphasize the situational and contextual nature of morality. They argue that no singular moral truth exists; instead, right and wrong are contingent upon the cultural, social, or personal context in which they are evaluated. For example, in some communities, it might be acceptable to partake in alcohol; however, other cultures might view it as a vice.

  1. Intuitionism

Intuitionists contend that moral judgments stem from individuals’ innate intuition or moral sense. These intuitions are independent of logical reasoning and transcend rational or empirical justification. This perspective maintains a strong sense of moral subjectivity and individual autonomy.

  1. Error Theory

Contrary to cognitivism, error theorists argue that moral judgments are never true. Regardless of the moral standpoint, every moral claim is essentially false. Error theory differs from other metaethical stances significantly because of its skeptical view on truth in moral judgments.

Each metaethical perspective presents a unique outlook on moral judgments, ultimately shaping one’s moral framework. However, no single perspective provides a definitive, one-size-fits-all answer to questions surrounding the nature of ethics. Understanding these views and their implications empowers individuals to craft their personal moral philosophies, contributing to enriching and nuanced moral discourses. In doing so, we endeavor to glean a deeper understanding and navigate life’s most perplexing moral dilemmas.
