Chemical Safety Documentation: Essential Records and Logs


Chemical safety documentation is a crucial aspect of maintaining a safe and compliant workplace. It is essential to keep accurate and up-to-date records of all chemicals used, stored, and disposed of within the facility. This documentation serves as a valuable resource for employees, regulators, and emergency responders, providing critical information in the event of an accident or spill.

Record Keeping Requirements

The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) have established specific record keeping requirements for chemical safety documentation. OSHA’s Hazard Communication Standard (HCS) requires employers to maintain a written hazard communication program, including a list of hazardous chemicals, safety data sheets (SDSs), and employee training records. The EPA’s Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) requires facilities to maintain records of hazardous waste generation, storage, and disposal.

Essential Records and Logs

To ensure compliance with OSHA and EPA regulations, it is essential to maintain the following records and logs:

Chemical Inventory

A chemical inventory is a comprehensive list of all chemicals used, stored, and disposed of within the facility. This inventory should include the following information:

  • Chemical name and CAS number
  • Chemical classification and hazard information
  • Quantity and location of the chemical
  • Storage and handling procedures
  • Disposal and waste management procedures

Safety Data Sheets (SDSs)

SDSs are documents that provide detailed information on the safe handling, use, storage, and disposal of hazardous chemicals. SDSs should be maintained for all hazardous chemicals used within the facility and should include the following information:

  • Chemical name and CAS number
  • Hazard classification and warning information
  • Physical and chemical properties
  • Safety and health precautions
  • Emergency procedures
  • Disposal and waste management procedures

Employee Training Records

Employee training records are essential for ensuring that employees are properly trained on the safe handling, use, storage, and disposal of hazardous chemicals. Training records should include the following information:

  • Employee name and job title
  • Chemicals trained on
  • Training date and duration
  • Training topics and objectives
  • Evaluation results and certification

Incident and Accident Reports

Incident and accident reports are critical for identifying and correcting hazards within the facility. Reports should include the following information:

  • Date, time, and location of the incident or accident
  • Chemical involved and quantity
  • Cause and contributing factors
  • Injuries or illnesses sustained
  • Corrective actions taken

Spill Response and Cleanup Records

Spill response and cleanup records are essential for documenting the procedures used to respond to chemical spills and clean up hazardous materials. Records should include the following information:

  • Date and time of the spill
  • Chemical involved and quantity
  • Procedures used to respond to the spill
  • Cleanup methods and materials used
  • Disposal and waste management procedures

Waste Management Records

Waste management records are critical for ensuring that hazardous waste is properly generated, stored, and disposed of. Records should include the following information:

  • Waste generation and storage procedures
  • Waste disposal and transportation procedures
  • Waste treatment and recycling procedures
  • Waste management permits and certifications

Compliance and Audits

Regular compliance audits and inspections are essential for ensuring that chemical safety documentation is accurate and up-to-date. Audits should include the following:

  • Review of chemical inventory and SDSs
  • Verification of employee training records
  • Inspection of storage and handling procedures
  • Review of incident and accident reports
  • Review of spill response and cleanup records
  • Review of waste management records

By maintaining accurate and up-to-date chemical safety documentation, facilities can ensure compliance with OSHA and EPA regulations, reduce the risk of accidents and spills, and protect the health and safety of employees.
