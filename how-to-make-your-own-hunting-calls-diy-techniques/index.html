How to Make Your Own Hunting Calls: DIY Techniques


Hunting calls are an essential tool for both novice and seasoned hunters. These devices mimic animal sounds to attract prey, making hunting more effective and exciting. Whether you’re hunting waterfowl, deer, or predators, having the right hunting call can significantly improve your chances of success. Instead of purchasing expensive calls, why not create your own? In this guide, we’ll walk you through various DIY techniques to craft your own hunting calls using easy-to-find materials.

Understanding the Basics of Hunting Calls

Before diving into the craft of making hunting calls, it’s essential to understand the different types of calls and their purposes. Hunting calls can generally be classified into two categories:

  1. Mimicry Calls: These calls replicate the sounds made by animals, such as the quack of a duck or the grunt of a deer.
  2. Locating Calls: These calls are designed to help hunters locate animals, such as the loud howls used for calling coyotes or the turkey’s gobble used during spring hunts.

By creating your own hunting calls, you not only gain a deeper understanding of the animals you’re hunting but also have the satisfaction of crafting a tool tailored to your needs.

Materials You’ll Need

The materials required to make hunting calls depend on the type of call you wish to create. Here’s a list of common materials used for a variety of calls:

  • Wood: Popular for making duck, goose, and turkey calls. Hardwoods such as walnut, maple, and cherry are commonly used due to their resonance and durability.
  • Reeds: Used primarily for waterfowl calls, reeds create the distinctive sound that mimics the calls of ducks and geese.
  • Plastic Tubing: This material is often used for predator calls, as it’s easy to shape and can be altered to fine-tune the pitch.
  • Rubber Bands or Elastic: These can help create tension for reed-based calls, particularly for waterfowl.
  • Screws or Nails: Used to secure the reed or mouthpiece in place.
  • Super Glue: For holding together various parts of your call, especially if you’re working with small components.
  • Knife or Chisel: To shape and carve the materials.
  • Sandpaper: For smoothing rough edges and achieving the right sound quality.

Making a Simple Turkey Call

Turkey calls are one of the most popular DIY hunting calls because they are relatively easy to make and can be highly effective in the field. Here’s a basic method to craft a friction-style turkey call.

Materials:

  • A piece of hardwood (cherry, maple, or walnut works well)
  • A small rubber band or elastic
  • A piece of softwood (for the striker)
  • Sandpaper
  • A chisel or knife

Steps:

  1. Cut the Wood: Start by cutting your hardwood into a small, round disk (about 3 inches in diameter). This will be your call’s base.
  2. Shape the Striker: Cut a piece of softwood into a stick about 6-8 inches long. The end of the striker should be slightly rounded to ensure it glides smoothly over the surface of the call.
  3. Carve the Surface: Use a knife or chisel to carve a groove or a series of shallow notches into the surface of the hardwood disk. These grooves will help produce the specific tones and pitches needed for a turkey call.
  4. Smooth the Edges: Use sandpaper to smooth any rough edges, making sure the surface is even and free from splinters.
  5. Add the Tension: Stretch a small rubber band or elastic around the edge of the hardwood disk to add tension. This tension will create more resonance when you strike the call with the striker.

To use the call, lightly drag the rounded end of the striker over the surface of the call, varying the pressure to produce different pitches.

Creating a Basic Deer Grunt Call

Deer grunt calls are another essential tool in any hunter’s arsenal. A basic grunt call mimics the sound of a buck or doe and is used primarily during rutting season to attract or communicate with deer. Making a simple deer grunt call is straightforward, and you can craft it using basic materials.

Materials:

  • A piece of PVC pipe (about 4-6 inches in length)
  • A reed (this can be made from a thin strip of plastic or wood)
  • Super glue
  • A small rubber band

Steps:

  1. Prepare the PVC Pipe: Cut the PVC pipe to the desired length. The diameter of the pipe should be about the size of your finger to create the right sound quality.
  2. Create the Reed: Cut a thin strip of plastic or wood that fits snugly inside the open end of the pipe. The reed will vibrate when air is blown through the pipe, creating the grunt sound.
  3. Attach the Reed: Position the reed inside the pipe at a slight angle, ensuring it’s secure but still able to vibrate freely. Use a small amount of super glue to hold the reed in place.
  4. Add the Tension: To fine-tune the pitch, wrap a small rubber band around the open end of the PVC pipe. This will help hold the reed in place and add tension.
  5. Test the Call: Once your call is assembled, test it by blowing through the pipe. Adjust the reed’s position or the rubber band tension to modify the grunt’s pitch and tone.

By experimenting with different materials and reed thicknesses, you can create a call that mimics the sound of a buck or doe, whether you’re calling in a buck during the rut or signaling to a doe.

Crafting a Predator Call

Predator calls, such as those used for coyotes, bobcats, or foxes, are incredibly effective in luring animals in for a shot. They typically create distress or mating sounds that predators find irresistible. Making a basic predator call involves creating a tube or barrel that can produce a high-pitched sound.

Materials:

  • A piece of plastic tubing (approximately 6-8 inches long)
  • A rubber band
  • A piece of string or small cord
  • A small piece of foam or fabric
  • Scissors or a knife

Steps:

  1. Prepare the Tubing: Cut the plastic tubing to the desired length. A longer tube can produce a deeper, more resonant sound, while a shorter tube will generate a sharper, higher-pitched noise.
  2. Create the Mouthpiece: Use a small piece of foam or fabric to create a seal at one end of the tube. The material should be thin enough to allow air to pass through but thick enough to create a slight vibration.
  3. Attach the Rubber Band: Wrap a rubber band around the tube at the open end to help create tension against the foam. The rubber band will also provide some control over the volume of the call.
  4. Test the Call: Once everything is in place, try blowing through the tube. If needed, adjust the tension of the rubber band or the position of the foam to achieve the desired sound.

Predator calls are versatile and can be used to mimic various distress or mating calls depending on the situation. With a little practice, you’ll be able to fine-tune your call to sound like a distressed rabbit, fawn, or even a coyote.

Tips for Fine-Tuning Your Calls

Creating your own hunting calls can be a fun and rewarding experience. However, the effectiveness of your call depends on the sound quality, so here are some tips to help improve your calls:

1. Experiment with Materials

The material you choose affects the quality of the sound. Hardwoods, such as maple or walnut, provide better resonance for calls like turkey and deer, while plastic or PVC tubing works best for predator calls. Try different materials to find the best sound for each type of call.

2. Adjust the Tension

The tension of the reed, rubber band, or other components greatly influences the sound quality. If your call sounds too harsh, loosen the tension slightly. If the call is too quiet or dull, tighten the tension to increase the resonance.

3. Practice Makes Perfect

The more you practice using your DIY hunting calls, the better you’ll become at controlling pitch, volume, and tone. Experiment with varying your blow strength and how you strike the call to create more lifelike sounds.

4. Test Your Calls in the Field

After crafting your calls, always test them in the field. Bring along a few different calls to see which one works best for your specific hunting environment. Pay attention to how animals respond to each call and make adjustments accordingly.

Conclusion

Making your own hunting calls is a fantastic way to enhance your hunting experience and connect more deeply with the animals you’re targeting. With a few simple tools and materials, you can craft effective calls for a variety of game, from waterfowl to predators. Remember to be patient and experiment with different techniques until you get the perfect sound. In the end, your DIY hunting calls will not only give you an edge in the field but also provide a unique sense of satisfaction knowing that you’ve made your own gear from scratch. Happy hunting!
