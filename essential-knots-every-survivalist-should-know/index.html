Essential Knots Every Survivalist Should Know


In survival situations, your ability to secure, build, and manipulate your environment can mean the difference between life and death. Among the most vital skills to master is knot tying. Whether you’re building a shelter, securing gear, or performing first aid, knowing the right knot for the job is essential. In this guide, we’ll explore the most essential knots every survivalist should know to handle various survival tasks efficiently.

1. Bowline Knot: The King of Loops

When it comes to knots, the bowline knot stands as one of the most versatile and reliable options. It’s simple to tie, secure under tension, and forms a loop that doesn’t slip or jam. The bowline is especially useful for creating non-slip loops at the end of a rope, ideal for securing items, climbing, or making a rescue rope.

How to Tie the Bowline Knot:

  1. Form a small loop in the rope.
  2. Pass the working end of the rope through the loop.
  3. Bring the working end around the standing part of the rope.
  4. Thread the working end back through the loop.
  5. Pull tight to secure the knot.

Use Cases:

  • Creating fixed loops for securing gear
  • Rescuing or pulling someone
  • Tying a rope to a post or anchor

Why It’s Important:

In a survival situation, you may need to secure shelter, create a harness for climbing, or even rescue someone. The bowline’s strength and ability to stay secure make it indispensable for any survivalist’s knot-tying toolkit.

2. Square Knot: The All-Purpose Binding Knot

The square knot, or reef knot, is essential for tying two ropes of similar size together. It is incredibly simple to tie and untie, and when done correctly, it’s strong enough to hold under significant load. However, unlike some other knots, it’s not suitable for use in extreme conditions where heavy tension is applied—this knot can slip if overloaded.

How to Tie the Square Knot:

  1. Lay two ends of rope side by side, crossing them over each other.
  2. Tie the first half knot by passing the right end over the left, then under it.
  3. Tie the second half knot by passing the right end over the left again, and then under it.
  4. Tighten the knot securely.

Use Cases:

  • Joining two ropes together
  • Securing a bandage in first aid
  • Tying packages or bundles

Why It’s Important:

A square knot can be a lifesaver when you need to secure materials quickly and efficiently. Whether it’s for first aid purposes, tying a temporary shelter, or bundling firewood, this knot is as practical as they come.

3. Taut-Line Hitch: The Adjustable Knot

The taut-line hitch is a crucial knot for any survivalist who needs to adjust tension in ropes. Unlike most knots that lock in place, the taut-line hitch can slide to adjust tension but holds firm under load. This makes it ideal for securing tent lines, tarps, or shelters when setting up camp.

How to Tie the Taut-Line Hitch:

  1. Wrap the rope around the anchor point (such as a tree or stake).
  2. Make a loop around the standing part of the rope.
  3. Pull the working end of the rope through the loop twice to create two coils.
  4. Slide the knot along the rope to adjust tension, then pull tight to secure.

Use Cases:

  • Tying down tents or tarps in varying weather conditions
  • Creating adjustable lines for securing gear
  • Setting up temporary shelters or shade

Why It’s Important:

When you’re in a survival situation and need a flexible and reliable way to secure things, this knot gives you the ability to adjust tension quickly, making it an excellent knot for dynamic environments.

4. Clove Hitch: The Quick-Release Knot

The clove hitch is an excellent knot for quickly securing a rope to a post, tree, or other anchor point. It’s particularly useful when you need to tie something down temporarily, as it’s easy to tie and untie, even under tension. It is a versatile knot and works well for many different survival applications.

How to Tie the Clove Hitch:

  1. Pass the rope around the object (like a tree or post).
  2. Cross the rope over itself, forming a loop.
  3. Pass the working end of the rope over the standing part, and then loop it around again.
  4. Pull the knot tight against the anchor point.

Use Cases:

  • Securing tarps or tents to trees
  • Tying down objects in a hurry
  • Temporary securing of ropes to poles or other structures

Why It’s Important:

In many survival scenarios, you’ll need to quickly secure something to a tree, pole, or other anchor. The clove hitch allows you to do this quickly, and it’s easy to adjust or release when needed.

5. Figure-Eight Knot: The Safety Knot

The figure-eight knot is often used in climbing and rescue situations due to its strength and security. This knot is known for its clear shape and reliability, making it easy to inspect, ensuring the knot hasn’t come undone. It is frequently used for tying ropes to harnesses or creating loops for climbing.

How to Tie the Figure-Eight Knot:

  1. Create a loop by passing the working end of the rope over the standing part.
  2. Twist the rope and pass the working end under the standing part, forming the number 8 shape.
  3. Pass the working end through the first loop and tighten the knot.

Use Cases:

  • Tying a rope to a harness for climbing or rappelling
  • Creating secure loops for rescue situations
  • Attaching ropes to heavy equipment or anchor points

Why It’s Important:

In a survival environment, safety is key, especially when dealing with dangerous heights or water rescues. The figure-eight knot ensures that ropes stay in place and won’t slip under stress, making it vital for anyone who might find themselves in hazardous conditions.

6. Prusik Knot: The Knot That Climbs

The prusik knot is a life-saving knot that’s used in climbing and rescue operations. It’s unique because it allows a rope to slide when there’s no weight on it but holds firm when weight is applied. This knot is particularly useful in ascending or descending ropes, making it an essential skill for any survivalist who might need to climb trees or rocks.

How to Tie the Prusik Knot:

  1. Take a smaller loop of rope and wrap it around the main rope two to three times.
  2. Pass the smaller loop through itself to form a knot.
  3. Tighten the knot, ensuring that it grips the main rope.

Use Cases:

  • Climbing or ascending ropes
  • Emergency self-rescue in climbing or high-altitude situations
  • Creating a foothold or handhold in rope systems

Why It’s Important:

If you’re in a situation where you need to climb or descend a rope, the prusik knot can help you do so safely. It provides a way to ascend a rope when you don’t have additional gear or safety harnesses.

7. Double Fisherman’s Knot: The Secure Connection

The double fisherman’s knot is used for tying two ropes together. It’s especially useful when you need to create a longer rope by joining two shorter sections or when you need to tie a knot that can withstand heavy tension. It’s secure, reliable, and, once tied, is incredibly difficult to untie without cutting the rope.

How to Tie the Double Fisherman’s Knot:

  1. Lay two ropes parallel to each other.
  2. Take the end of one rope and wrap it around the other rope twice.
  3. Pass the end of the rope through the two loops created.
  4. Repeat the process with the second rope.
  5. Pull both ropes to tighten the knot.

Use Cases:

  • Joining two ropes to create a longer rope for climbing or securing
  • Making a rope harness for an animal or for emergency situations
  • Tying together ropes under extreme tension

Why It’s Important:

In extreme survival situations, you may need to extend your rope for various tasks. The double fisherman’s knot ensures that your ropes stay securely connected, even under heavy load, which can be critical for things like climbing, securing shelters, or other emergency scenarios.

8. Slip Knot: The Knot that Tightens Automatically

The slip knot is a useful knot when you need to quickly tie something but also want the option of quickly releasing it. It’s especially beneficial in situations where you need a temporary, adjustable knot that can tighten under pressure.

How to Tie the Slip Knot:

  1. Make a loop with the rope.
  2. Pass the working end of the rope through the loop.
  3. Tighten the working end to form a slip knot.

Use Cases:

  • Securing a temporary shelter
  • Quick tie-down for gear or supplies
  • Adjustable knots for securing tarps or ropes

Why It’s Important:

In survival situations, you need flexibility. The slip knot offers the ability to tie something down temporarily without the need for a permanent knot, which can be essential for quick changes in setup or when you need to dismantle your shelter quickly.

Conclusion

Mastering these essential knots can give you a distinct advantage in any survival situation. Whether you’re securing shelter, building a fire, or performing a rescue operation, having the right knot at your disposal can save time, effort, and even lives. By incorporating these knots into your survival toolkit, you’ll be better prepared for the challenges nature throws your way. The best part is that these knots don’t require fancy equipment—just a rope and your knowledge of how to tie them. Keep practicing, and these knots will soon become second nature.
