Fantasy Tropes You Should Avoid in Your Writing


It’s impossible to deny the appeal of fantasy: grand adventures, magical creatures, and worlds brimming with the impossible. Readers flock to the genre for escapism, wonder, and complex storytelling. But all that wonder fades fast when your world feels like a patchwork of the same tired clichés they’ve seen a hundred times before.

If you want your story to rise above the noise, it’s worth sidestepping the well-worn pitfalls that can make or break a fantasy novel. Here’s an in-depth look at some overused fantasy tropes—and why you should consider dodging them in your writing. Your prose and your readers will thank you.

1. The Farm Boy Who Is Secretly the Chosen One

If there’s one character everybody expects in a fantasy narrative, it’s the unsuspecting youth from humble beginnings who turns out to be a legendary hero. This trope is one of the oldest in the fantasy canon—and also one of the stalest.

Repeatedly, stories center around a nondescript farm boy (or girl), orphaned or otherwise marginalized, who stumbles upon a hidden destiny. Their path is often predictable: mysterious stranger arrives, reveals ancient prophecy, adventure ensues. Even if the execution is crisp, readers spot what’s coming a mile away.

Why Avoid It?
Relying on the Chosen One narrative puts your protagonist on rails. The tension evaporates when readers know fate protects this character. Instead, give your hero agency and vulnerability. Let them earn their victories rather than inherit them. And if you must use a “Chosen One,” add complexity: perhaps there are many chosen, or the prophecy is wrong, or being chosen is a curse.

2. The Wise Old Mentor Who Dies

Picture this: your hero, confused and overwhelmed, turns to an older, mystical figure who provides guidance—until, predictably, the mentor perishes (often to motivate the hero). This trope, popularized by everything from Tolkien’s Gandalf to Star Wars’ Obi-Wan Kenobi, tends to flatten the narrative.

Why Avoid It?
Assuming the death of a mentor is a necessary rite of passage removes agency from your protagonist. It’s lazy emotional shorthand that gives the illusion of growth. Consider letting your mentor stick around, subvert their wisdom, or reveal them as unreliable. Their impact will be far more meaningful if they surprise readers—and your protagonist—by subverting expectations.

3. Meddling Prophecies and Destinies

Nothing kills intrigue faster than a prophecy that spells out the plot. When a seer or oracle lays out who will win and how, the tension dissipates. Prophecy can be a powerful literary device, but it’s too often used as a crutch.

Why Avoid It?
Prophecies can shackle characters to predictable outcomes. Challenge yourself: let your characters create their futures through choices and blunders, not preordained fate. If you must include prophecy, play with ambiguity or misdirection. Allow destiny to be uncertain, or make fulfillment an unintended consequence of the hero’s flaws.

4. Tolkien’s Legacy: Elves, Dwarves, and Orcs

J.R.R. Tolkien revolutionized fantasy, infusing it with cultures sprung from ancient myth. Unfortunately, his blueprint led to the proliferation of elves with silver hair and pointed ears, dwarves who love mining and ale, and evil, brutish orcs.

Why Avoid It?
There’s nothing wrong with elves and dwarves—when done well. The problem arises when they’re carbon copies. Stock races flatten your world-building and signal to readers that you’re borrowing, not inventing. Design your own cultures, complete with quirks, flaws, and original histories. If you want fae or subterranean folk, consider what makes yours truly unique.

5. Dark Lords With No Depth

A shadowy figure sits atop a dark tower, orchestrating chaos and commanding armies of monsters. Why? Because they’re evil. This classic villain has all the subtlety of a Saturday morning cartoon antagonist.

Why Avoid It?
One-dimensional villains rob your story of impact. Readers crave complexity; a believable antagonist is driven by motives, not pure malice. Flesh out your villains with relatable goals, scars from the past, conflicted loyalties, or even sincerely good intentions twisted astray. Sometimes, the darkest villain thinks they’re saving the world.

6. Magical MacGuffins

Another quest, another ancient artifact that must be found, destroyed, or protected. The magical object-with-unlimited-power trope litters fantasy history. While a legendary item can kickstart a plot, making it the focal point often leads to shallow storytelling.

Why Avoid It?
If what matters is the item, not the characters, readers lose interest. Make your quests character-driven rather than object-driven. If there’s a relic, tie its significance personally to your heroine. Let it symbolize something deeper or create unexpected complications, but never let it become a shortcut to move the plot.

7. Evil Empires and Noble Rebels

The oppressive empire faces off against ragtag rebels—a setup employed from classic epics to recent bestsellers. Over time, this black-and-white approach to political conflict has turned into a shortcut rather than truly evocative world-building.

Why Avoid It?
Nuance electrifies a fictional world. Empires needn’t always be demonic; rebels needn’t always be pure-hearted underdogs. Add shades of gray: maybe the empire protects its citizens, while the rebels have their own dark history. Challenge your characters with moral ambiguity, and you’ll engage your readers far more deeply.

8. Overly Convenient Magic

Magic systems in fantasy often operate under murky rules—or worse, break those rules whenever the plot hits a snag. If spells solve every problem or lack established constraints, stakes disappear.

Why Avoid It?
Magic becomes interesting when it has limits, costs, and consequences. Define how your magic works, how it can go wrong, and why characters would choose not to use it. Forces that fix every problem without repercussions make for dull, tension-free stories. Readers want to understand the risks—otherwise, why worry about anything?

9. The Medieval Pastiche

So many fantasy worlds look like a blurred photograph of medieval Europe. The trappings are familiar: castles, monarchs, peasants, knights, all functioning within a vaguely feudal society.

Why Avoid It?
Leaning into a pseudo-medieval setting can set your world apart as generic rather than fascinating. Consider inspiration from different times and places: West African empires, Polynesian seafaring civilizations, or imagined futures where magic and technology intertwine. Reimagine your setting’s architecture, food, fashion, and politics—your world will feel richer for it.

10. Female Characters With No Agency

Women in fantasy too often exist as prizes, love interests, or afterthoughts. Token representation or sidelined characters cheapen your story, and modern readers spot these flaws immediately.

Why Avoid It?
If your female characters orbit male protagonists or possess no ambitions beyond romance, you’re squandering storytelling potential. Flesh them out: give women goals, agency, flaws, humor, and the power to succeed or fail on their own terms. The same holds true for representing marginalized identities with nuance instead of stereotypes.

11. Inexplicable Language and Naming Conventions

There’s a unique joy in naming everything in a fantasy world—but gibberish names or unpronounceable languages can bog down even the best prose. If your narrative is bursting with apostrophes and umlauts, or character names are interchangeable, it can snap readers out of the experience.

Why Avoid It?
Unmemorable names make characters harder to distinguish or root for. Effective naming offers flavor, context, and a sense of culture, not simply a collection of alien syllables. Think about how language, culture, and history would shape what inhabitants call themselves and their world.

12. Plot Armor for Main Characters

It’s hard for readers to maintain suspense when they realize your protagonists will survive explosions, curses, and assassins unscathed by virtue of their “main character energy.” Too much plot armor transforms epic narratives into shallow power fantasies.

Why Avoid It?
Consequences create drama. Don’t shield your heroes from harm; wound them, let them experience failure, give them irreversible setbacks. When a reader fears for a character’s fate, their investment multiplies. Take risks, and allow your narrative to veer into the unexpected.

13. Romantic Subplots Without Chemistry

Fantasy frequently shoehorns in romance for the sake of marketability. Characters destined for each other from page one rarely spark; forced pairings feel more like placeholders than heartfelt connections.

Why Avoid It?
Let romance develop organically. Chemistry comes from shared experiences, conflict, and mutual respect—not simply proximity. If you’re adding romance, ask: does it change the story? Does it reveal facets of the characters? If not, consider focusing on friendship, found families, or other bonds central to your narrative.

14. Races as Moral Monoliths

Fantasy species are often painted in broad strokes: elves are wise and good, goblins are greedy and bad. This essentialism not only feels lazy, it can replicate harmful real-world stereotypes.

Why Avoid It?
Treat every culture and species as varied. Let characters within groups disagree, betray, evolve, and surprise you. A world in which everyone from X race shares a single motivation or moral alignment is both unbelievable and, frankly, boring. Nuance creates authenticity.

15. The Overlooked Power of Subtlety

Finally, fantasy’s greatest weakness—and strength—is its ability to go epic. Massive armies, reality-shaking spells, and world-ending threats can be dazzling… but overreliance on spectacle risks dwarfing what really matters: character growth, stakes grounded in emotion, the power of everyday moments.

Why Avoid It?
The best fantasy balances grandeur with intimacy. Show how wars scar individuals, not just kings and queens. Focus on what’s lost and gained in the small victories. The avalanche of tropes can avalanche right over what makes a story memorable—so slow down, get personal, and let your imagination do the rest.

Conclusion

Subverting or avoiding familiar tropes isn’t about shunning everything that came before. Sometimes, an old idea can be done brilliantly with a fresh twist or a dash of sincerity. But leaning too heavily on cliché is like casting a spell with no magic left in it: the incantation falls flat.

As you build your next fantasy world, view these tropes not as hard lines to never cross, but as warning signs on the road to truly original storytelling. Take risks. Dig deeper. Make your worlds feel lived-in, your characters unforgettable, and your readers endlessly curious. After all, the best fantasy isn’t about dragons or magic—it’s about surprising us with stories we’ve never heard before.
