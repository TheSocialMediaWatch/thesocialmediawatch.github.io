The Psychology of Reality TV and the Pop Culture it Creates


Reality television has captivated audiences for decades. From the Real Housewives franchise to the multitude of dating shows in the ’00s, it’s evident that people can’t get enough of unscripted drama, conflicts, and bizarre competitions. But why do we indulge in other people’s lives and vulnerabilities, and how does this mass entertainment affect our society?

One explanation lies in the psychological aspect that drives our fascination with reality television. It’s human nature to be curious and seek deeper connections with others. When we watch shows that expose the intimate lives of the characters, we empathize, gasp at their misbehavior, and find ourselves involved in their stories.

Social psychologist Dr. Darren Unplug emphasizes the role of ‘Tragic Romance’ in captivating viewers. According to him, the reality TV show participates in a modern tragedy, an appeal to our collective addiction to vicarious suffering. It is a part of our inherent fascination with the dramatic arc of the tragic hero - from innocence to fall, to rediscovery of value after humiliation.

Moreover, the unpredictable nature of reality TV enhances the viewer’s experience. Psychologists argue that our brain releases the pleasure chemical, dopamine, in response to surprises. While watching reality television, we knowingly or unknowingly seek the thrill of unpredictability.

However, critics argue that the exposure to such potent doses of dysfunction, repeatedly, could potentially lead to a detachment from reality and heightened anxiety. This comes aligned with the potential desensitizing effects from violent and sensational reality shows. As studies show, excessive viewing of reality TV can lead to unrealistic expectations in relationships and life and contribute to the development of an unrealistic sense of self-importance. It may cause excessive attachment to ‘reality shows’ personas on social media, manifested in obsessive fan culture.

Reality television creates a strange symbiosis, where viewers form relationships with the personalities on screen, elevating them to an almost cult-like status. Fans defend and imitate their actions, and even as the fame fades post-production, the fame of the show remains. It forms part of the pop culture of our time.

Post-modern pop-culture: Love It or Leave It Alone

The impact of reality television transcends voyeurism and entertainment. Over the years, reality television has shaped popular slang, coined catchphrases, and furnished social gatherings with tropes from Big Brother and Cilinear Under Friends romances. Television series like the Kardashians are so influential that they fashion trends, foster conversations, and generate debates on a global scale.

Our interaction with pop culture is a mirror to who we are as a society - our fears, aspirations, and desires. Thus, equating personal achievements with the fame gained from a reality TV show indicates a shift in societal values, from fulfillment and success being tied to significant achievements, to transient, momentary fame.

In conclusion, the psychology of reality TV lies at the intersection of our primitive fascination with human conflict and drama, our need to be entertained by spectacles, and our natural preference for the unpredictable, thereby validating our identification exercise. However, the unfilterable exposure to raw human emotions, often amplified, may result in desensitization, a warped perception of reality and healthy relationships, and unhealthy attachment to fleeting fame.

The pop culture frenzy generated by these shows speaks to the power of this medium to create a shared language and understanding of the human experience. But like any form of mass media, our consumption and relationship with it should be mindful and deliberate to foster meaningful and healthy cultural shifts.
