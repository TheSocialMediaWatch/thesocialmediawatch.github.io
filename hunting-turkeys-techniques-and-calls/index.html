Hunting Turkeys: Techniques and Calls


Turkey hunting is a popular and exhilarating outdoor activity that requires skill, patience, and knowledge of turkey behavior. Whether you are a seasoned hunter or a beginner, understanding the techniques and calls used in turkey hunting can greatly increase your chances of success. In this blog post, we will explore various techniques and calls that can help you become a more effective turkey hunter.

Understanding Turkey Behavior

Before diving into the techniques and calls, it is essential to have a basic understanding of turkey behavior. Turkeys are highly social animals that live in flocks consisting of hens, juvenile turkeys (also known as jakes), and dominant gobblers. During the spring mating season, gobblers become more vocal and active, making it an ideal time for turkey hunting.

Scouting

The first step in successful turkey hunting is scouting. Scouting involves familiarizing yourself with the hunting area, identifying turkey roosting sites, feeding areas, and travel routes. Look for signs such as scratch marks on the ground, droppings, and feathers to determine turkey activity in the area. This information will help you strategize your hunting approach.

Setting Up

Once you have identified a promising hunting spot, it’s time to set up. Find a suitable location that provides cover and allows for a clear line of sight. Remember, turkeys have exceptional eyesight, so blending into your surroundings is crucial. Use natural camouflage or a ground blind to conceal yourself effectively.

Decoys

Decoys play a vital role in turkey hunting. They attract the attention of gobblers and can help lure them into range. There are various types of decoys available, including hen and jake decoys. Experimenting with different decoy setups and positions can be beneficial, as turkeys may respond differently to various configurations.

Turkey Calls

Turkey calls are essential tools for any turkey hunter. They mimic the sounds made by turkeys to communicate with each other. There are three primary types of turkey calls:

1. Box Calls

Box calls are popular among turkey hunters due to their ease of use and versatility. They consist of a rectangular box and a paddle. By moving the paddle across the box, you can produce a wide range of turkey sounds, including clucks, purrs, and yelps. Practice using a box call before heading out into the field to ensure you can produce realistic sounds.

2. Slate Calls

Slate calls, also known as pot calls, are another common type of turkey call. They consist of a circular piece of slate or glass set in a pot or frame. By rubbing a striker against the surface, you can create realistic turkey sounds. Slate calls are known for their versatility and ability to produce soft, subtle calls that can entice wary gobblers.

3. Mouth Calls

Mouth calls, also called diaphragm calls, are placed inside the hunter’s mouth. They require practice to use effectively but offer the advantage of keeping the hunter’s hands free. Mouth calls produce a wide range of turkey sounds, including yelps, clucks, and purrs. Experiment with different mouth calls to find one that suits your style and produces the desired sounds.

Calling Techniques

When it comes to calling techniques, there are several strategies you can employ:

1. Locator Calls

Locator calls, such as owl hoots or crow calls, can be used to locate turkeys. These calls imitate natural sounds that can trigger a response from gobblers, revealing their location. Use locator calls early in the morning or late in the evening to locate roosting turkeys.

2. Soft Calls

Soft calls, including purrs and clucks, are used to mimic contented turkeys. These calls can be effective when turkeys are in close proximity but may be hesitant to approach. Use soft calls sparingly to create a sense of curiosity in gobblers.

3. Aggressive Calls

Aggressive calls, such as yelps and cuts, imitate dominant turkeys and can trigger a territorial response. These calls are particularly effective during the mating season when gobblers are actively seeking hens. Aggressive calling can provoke a gobbler into approaching your location.

Conclusion

Mastering turkey hunting techniques and calls takes time and practice. Remember to prioritize safety and always follow local hunting regulations. By understanding turkey behavior, scouting effectively, setting up strategically, and using the appropriate calls, you can increase your chances of a successful turkey hunt. So grab your gear, head out into the wild, and embrace the thrill of turkey hunting!
