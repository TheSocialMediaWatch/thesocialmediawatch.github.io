Top Survival Skills for Long Hiking Trips


When embarking on a long hiking trip, the right preparation is essential to ensure a safe and enjoyable experience. Whether you’re trekking through rugged mountain ranges, dense forests, or remote wilderness, developing and honing key survival skills can make all the difference. Knowing what to do in emergency situations and understanding the basics of wilderness survival will help you tackle any challenges that come your way.

This post covers some of the most critical survival skills every long-distance hiker should master before hitting the trail.

1. Navigation and Orienteering Skills

A major aspect of any hiking trip is navigation. Whether you’re exploring well-established trails or venturing into the backcountry, knowing how to use a map and compass, or a GPS device, is crucial for getting from point A to point B safely.

Map and Compass: Learning how to read a topographical map and understand the symbols and contours is a foundational skill for any hiker. A map can give you a detailed overview of the terrain, elevation changes, and natural features, which will guide your decisions when navigating through the wilderness.

A compass, on the other hand, will help you orient yourself and stay on course. Make sure to practice using these tools before your hike so that you can quickly and efficiently determine your direction.

GPS Technology: While traditional navigation tools like maps and compasses are invaluable, a GPS device is an excellent complement for more complex trips. Devices like handheld GPS units or smartphone apps (with offline capabilities) can offer real-time location tracking. However, it’s essential to remember that electronic devices may fail due to battery depletion or loss of signal, so always have a backup plan.

2. Fire Starting Techniques

Starting a fire is one of the most important survival skills for hikers. It can provide warmth, light, a means to purify water, and a way to cook food. In emergency situations, it can be a life-saver. Understanding different methods of fire starting will ensure you’re prepared in any weather condition.

Matches and Lighters: The easiest way to start a fire is with waterproof matches or a reliable lighter. Always carry extra matches stored in a waterproof container. However, relying solely on these tools can leave you unprepared if they fail or become wet.

Fire Starting Kit: A more robust option is to carry a fire starter kit, which includes items like fire starter sticks, a magnesium fire starter, or a ferrocerium rod. The magnesium fire starter, for instance, produces intense sparks when scraped with a steel tool, even in wet conditions.

Fire Lay: Knowing how to build a proper fire lay can significantly affect your success in starting and maintaining a fire. A basic fire lay involves arranging tinder (dry leaves, grass, or pine needles) in a pyramid shape, followed by kindling (small twigs and branches) and larger pieces of wood. Make sure your fire is contained and avoid starting fires in areas with dry grass or leaves to reduce the risk of wildfires.

3. Shelter Building

Whether you’re facing a sudden storm or need to rest for the night, having the ability to build a shelter can be crucial. In the wilderness, it’s essential to understand how to use natural resources to create protection from the elements.

Simple Shelters: The simplest form of shelter involves using a tarp or emergency bivy sack. A tarp can be hung between trees or set up in a lean-to formation, offering protection from rain and wind.

If you don’t have a tarp, knowing how to build a shelter from natural materials can save your life. A debris hut, for example, can be constructed by piling leaves, branches, and moss over a framework of larger sticks, providing insulation and protection.

Insulation and Comfort: When building a shelter, prioritize insulation from the ground. Using pine boughs, dry leaves, or other materials under your shelter will help prevent heat loss to the cold ground. Additionally, a good shelter should be built in a location that offers some protection from wind and rain, such as near large rocks or dense trees.

4. Water Sourcing and Purification

Water is a basic human need, and having access to safe drinking water is paramount while hiking. You must be able to identify reliable sources of water and know how to purify it.

Finding Water: Streams, rivers, and lakes are common sources of water in the wilderness, but not all water is safe to drink. Avoid drinking directly from standing water, as it can be contaminated with harmful bacteria, parasites, or chemicals.

Purification Methods: If you don’t have access to bottled or treated water, you’ll need to purify any water you find. There are several effective methods:

  • Boiling: Boiling water for at least one minute (or three minutes at higher altitudes) will kill harmful microorganisms.
  • Water Purification Tablets: These tablets, which are often iodine- or chlorine-based, can effectively disinfect water. However, they can alter the taste, so carry a few extra tablets to ensure you’re covered for the entire trip.
  • Filtration Systems: Portable water filters are lightweight and easy to use, removing both large particles and bacteria from the water. Some advanced filters even remove viruses, making them a reliable choice for serious backcountry hikers.

5. First Aid and Medical Knowledge

Injuries or illnesses can occur at any time on a hiking trip, and knowing how to administer first aid can make a significant difference in outcomes. Always carry a well-stocked first aid kit with items such as bandages, gauze, antiseptic wipes, pain relievers, and blister care materials.

Wound Treatment: Knowing how to properly treat cuts, scrapes, and burns is vital. Clean any wound with water and antiseptic before dressing it with a sterile bandage to reduce the risk of infection.

Dealing with Blisters: Blisters are a common issue during long hikes, especially when you’re covering significant distances. To prevent blisters, wear well-fitted shoes and moisture-wicking socks. If a blister does form, treat it immediately by cleaning it, applying a blister pad, and keeping it covered to avoid further irritation.

Hypothermia and Heatstroke: Both extreme cold and heat can lead to life-threatening conditions such as hypothermia and heatstroke. Learn to recognize the signs and symptoms of these conditions and understand how to respond. For hypothermia, warmth, dry clothing, and high-calorie foods are essential for rewarming. In cases of heatstroke, cool the body with water, shade, and electrolyte-rich drinks.

6. Food Sourcing and Survival Skills

While hiking, it’s important to know how to manage your food supply and possibly find food in the wild if necessary. Carry enough food to sustain you throughout the trip, including lightweight, high-calorie snacks such as energy bars, nuts, and dried fruit.

However, if you find yourself in a situation where food becomes scarce, having basic foraging and hunting skills can help.

Foraging for Edible Plants: Many plants in the wilderness are edible, but others can be toxic. Familiarize yourself with common wild edibles, such as berries, roots, and edible mushrooms. However, be cautious, as misidentifying plants can have severe consequences.

Fishing and Hunting: If you’re in an area with access to streams or rivers, fishing may be a viable food source. Knowing how to catch fish with a basic fishing kit or improvised spear can provide an additional source of protein. For hunting, some survivalists learn to trap small animals using snares or basic traps, but these methods require practice and patience.

7. Mental and Emotional Resilience

Physical survival skills are important, but mental resilience can be just as critical, especially during extended hiking trips. It’s not uncommon to face feelings of isolation, exhaustion, or even fear when venturing into the wilderness.

Stay Calm and Focused: In a survival situation, staying calm is paramount. Panicking can cloud your judgment and waste valuable energy. Practice mindfulness and focus on the immediate tasks at hand. Take things one step at a time and maintain a positive outlook.

Keep a Routine: During long hikes, try to maintain a routine. This helps break the monotony and gives you a sense of control over your environment. Regular breaks, hydration, and meals are essential for keeping your energy up.

Stay Connected (If Possible): If you have a satellite phone or a personal locator beacon (PLB), these devices can be lifesaving in an emergency. Even when hiking solo, staying connected through technology (when available) can provide peace of mind.

8. Wildlife Awareness and Safety

Encounters with wildlife are inevitable in certain regions, and understanding how to react to different animals can prevent dangerous situations.

Bears, Coyotes, and Mountain Lions: In bear country, always store food in bear-proof containers or hang it high in trees to avoid attracting bears to your campsite. If you encounter a bear, stay calm, avoid direct eye contact, and back away slowly. For predators like mountain lions or coyotes, make yourself appear larger, make loud noises, and never run.

Snakes and Insects: Snakes, especially venomous ones, are often found in rocky or grassy areas. Wearing long pants and sturdy boots is the best defense against snake bites. Additionally, be cautious of insects like ticks, which can transmit diseases such as Lyme disease. Always check yourself for ticks after hiking in areas known for tick populations.

Conclusion

Mastering the essential survival skills for long hiking trips can significantly increase your chances of safely navigating the wilderness. By being prepared and practicing these skills ahead of time, you can face challenges with confidence and enjoy the beauty of nature without compromising your safety. Always remember, the best way to survive is to prepare in advance. Carry the right gear, know the terrain, and keep a level head in emergency situations. Your safety and success in the wilderness depend on it.
