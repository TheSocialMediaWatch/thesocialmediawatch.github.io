Subterranean Wonders: The Exciting World of Gardening Underground


The world of gardening is vast and diverse, offering opportunities to cultivate a variety of flora in unique environments. One such environment that you may not initially consider is below the surface of the earth. Underground gardening, or subterranean horticulture, is a fascinating and lesser-known aspect of the plant world. In this blog post, we’ll dive deep into the exciting, diverse, and often surprising world of underground gardening.

The Importance of Soil

At the heart of underground gardening is soil. While most gardening exposes plants to the air, sun, and standard soil mixtures, underground gardening exposes plants primarily to soil, promoting a symbiotic exchange that is quite different from gardening above the ground. Soil has unique properties for various plants and promoting their vitality, and cultivating a thriving underground garden requires an understanding of soil pH, moisture levels, and nutrient content.

Soil texture, or the arrangement of mineral particles and organic materials, plays a significant role in underground gardening. The ideal soil has balanced proportions of sand, silt, and clay, providing air pockets for root growth while retaining adequate moisture and nutrients. Additionally, consider soil acidity, which can range from highly acidic, with a pH of 3.0-4.5, to slightly acidic, with a pH of 6.0-6.5, to neutral, with a pH of 6.6-7.3. Different plants require varying optimal soil pH levels, so research the desired level for your chosen plants.

Choosing Plants for Underground Gardens

Not all plants are well-suited to life under the earth. However, some thrive in subterranean conditions. Root vegetables and tubers, such as carrots, potatoes, and beets, are excellent candidates for underground gardening as they naturally grow underground, and they require less light than most vegetables. Edible and ornamental plants like ferns and mosses are also well-adapted to underground gardening, flourishing in the cold, damp environment.

Herbs like ginger, horseradish, and turmeric grow beautifully underground, while many shrubs and trees require specific conditions to be successful in underground settings. For example, some bonsai species, such as juniper and pine, respond positively to root pruning, encouraging growth and compact forms reminiscent of their above-ground counterparts. Furthermore, carnivorous plants such as Venus flytraps can be adapted to underground settings, given their captive air-filled cells that allow them to detect and consume airborne insects.

Techniques and Strategies

To create an underground garden, you’ll need to consider several techniques and strategies. First, it’s essential to choose an appropriate site, whether planting underground in a section of your yard, utilizing a raised bed, or constructing a purpose-built underground greenhouse. Ensure adequate drainage, as waterlogging can lead to root rot and other issues. In addition, you’ll need to pay close attention to light requirements for your chosen plants, as it’s often minimal with underground gardening. Placing windows, mirrors, or lights within your underground structure can help facilitate successful plant growth.

In terms of container size, bigger is almost always better for underground gardens. This allows more soil volume, providing a more stable environment for lower root zones, as well as the accommodation for root development. Understand that plants in underground gardens typically grow more slowly and produce fewer underground fruits, like tubers or bulbs, than those grown above ground.

The Benefits of Underground Gardening

There are many reasons to venture into underground gardening. For one, it offers a fascinating look into the botanical world that’s less explored than traditional gardening. Experimenting with underground plants can also help you develop a deeper appreciation for the life cycles of various organisms. Moreover, underground gardening offers a level of protection to tender plants from harsh weather conditions, making it the ideal oasis for plants like citrus, banana, and palm trees.

Conclusion

Though it may seem unconventional, underground gardening is a rewarding and engaging method of cultivating a diverse array of flora. By understanding the unique requirements of underground gardening, you can convert your backyard, raised bed, or purpose-built greenhouse into a lush, bountiful subterranean wonderland. So, take that leap underground - who knows what plant treasures you’ll unearth?
