The Most Scenic Kayaking Routes in North America


Kayaking offers a unique way to immerse yourself in nature’s beauty, combining the adventure of paddling with spectacular views that vary from rugged coastlines to serene lakes. North America, with its diverse landscapes and abundant waterways, boasts some of the most breathtaking kayaking routes in the world. Whether you are a seasoned paddler or a beginner seeking tranquil waters, this guide will take you through the most scenic kayaking routes across the continent, each offering a distinct experience that combines natural splendor, wildlife encounters, and unforgettable moments on the water.

1. Apostle Islands, Wisconsin — A Coastal Water Wonder

Located in Lake Superior, the Apostle Islands National Lakeshore is a hidden gem characterized by crystal-clear waters, sea caves, and lush forests. The 22 islands create a maze of routes where paddlers can explore mystical sea caves, especially stunning in the summer when sunlight filters through the crystal waters.

  • Highlights: Paddling through the sea caves near Devils Island, exploring sandstone cliffs, and camping on secluded beaches.
  • Difficulty: Intermediate; water conditions can be variable due to open lake exposure.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Late spring through early fall.

Kayakers here will witness sheer cliffs carved by millennia of wave action, towering forests, and opportunities to spot bald eagles, otters, and harbor seals. The Apostle Islands offer a mix of flatwater and open-water kayaking for a dynamic experience.

2. San Juan Islands, Washington — Orcas and Oceanic Beauty

Nestled in the Salish Sea between mainland Washington and Vancouver Island, the San Juan Islands provide a spectacular paddling environment filled with marine wildlife and scenic vistas.

  • Highlights: Orca whale sightings, paddling near bluffside forests, and stopping off at small archipelagos.
  • Difficulty: Beginner to intermediate; protected waters but can be windy.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Summer months, when wildlife activity and visibility peak.

The San Juan Islands’ mix of rocky shoreline, quiet bays, and sprawling waters challenge and reward paddlers with unforgettable sights of orcas breaching and seals sunning themselves on rocks. The region is award-winning for eco-tourism and has well-maintained campsites and kayak rental shops.

3. Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness, Minnesota — The Ultimate Inland Paddle

For those who crave wilderness and solitude, the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness (BWCAW) offers over 1,000 lakes connected by rivers and streams, bordered by lush forests and rugged cliffs.

  • Highlights: Lake-to-lake route planning, camping, fishing, and the chance to see moose and loons.
  • Difficulty: Intermediate to advanced; requires portaging and navigation skills.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Late spring to early fall.

The BWCAW is legendary among paddlers for its pristine conditions and quiet beauty. The routes offer varying lengths, from single-day excursions to multi-day trips with backcountry camping, allowing you to disconnect and experience North America’s true wilderness.

4. Hood Canal, Washington — Fjords and Calm Waters

A quieter and less-known waterway, Hood Canal is a fjord forming part of the Puget Sound. It offers deep, calm waters with stunning views of the Olympic Mountains to the west.

  • Highlights: Sea kayaking in calm fjord waters, spotting seals and eagles, and paddling through narrow inlets.
  • Difficulty: Beginner-friendly; sheltered waters.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Late spring through early fall.

This route is ideal for those looking for a relaxing kayak route enveloped by dramatic mountain vistas and rich marine life. The fjord’s sheltered waters are perfect for novices, and plenty of tidal flats and beaches offer tranquil stopping points.

5. Lake Tahoe, California/Nevada — Alpine Serenity on the Water

Lake Tahoe is known for its crystal-clear, deep blue waters and panoramic mountain views, making it a premium kayaking destination in the Sierra Nevada.

  • Highlights: Paddling alongside towering granite cliffs, exploring secret coves, and soaking in alpine sunsets.
  • Difficulty: Beginner to intermediate; weather can change rapidly.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Summer to early fall.

This alpine lake offers a unique blend of freshwater kayaking with the dramatic presence of surrounding peaks. Kayakers can explore sandy beaches and outcrops, ideal for picnics and relaxing breaks.

6. Clearwater River, Idaho — Mountain Rapids and Peaceful Pools

Flowing through the lush forests and steep canyons of north Idaho, the Clearwater River combines peaceful stretches with thrilling rapids, ideal for adventurous kayakers seeking variety.

  • Highlights: Scenic canyons, wildlife sightings, and varied water conditions.
  • Difficulty: Intermediate to advanced; includes class II-III rapids.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Late spring to early summer when the water is high.

The Clearwater River is not just about fast-moving water; many sections feature calm pools where paddlers can stop and absorb the surrounding wilderness. From emerald forests to soaring eagles, this route challenges while delighting with spectacular nature.

7. Everglades National Park, Florida — South Florida’s Paddling Paradise

The Everglades offer an entirely different kayaking experience — paddling through a vast network of mangrove tunnels, sawgrass marshes, and freshwater sloughs, teeming with exotic wildlife.

  • Highlights: Manatees, alligators, wading birds, and mangrove forests.
  • Difficulty: Beginner to intermediate; watch for tides and currents.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Winter and spring, avoiding hurricane season.

Kayaking here means slow, exploratory paddling through the famed “River of Grass.” Wildlife encounters are frequent, and the still waters provide evocative reflections of vast skies and sprawling plant life.

8. Cape Breton Island, Nova Scotia — Atlantic Coastline Elegance

The rugged coastline of Cape Breton Island provides stunning ocean kayaking routes, with dramatic cliffs, pebble beaches, and historic lighthouses lining the route.

  • Highlights: Sea caves, puffin and seabird colonies, and Celtic culture.
  • Difficulty: Intermediate; strong ocean currents and winds possible.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Summer months for calmer weather.

This route blends paddling with cultural richness, as paddlers can detour to coastal villages or explore the island’s rich heritage. Keep an eye out for seals lounging by the shoreline and impressive waterfalls cascading into the sea.

9. The Thousand Islands, New York/Ontario — A Maze of Islands and History

Situated where the St. Lawrence River meets Lake Ontario, the Thousand Islands comprise over 1,800 islands, offering complex routes for those who love exploration.

  • Highlights: Castles on islands, quiet coves, and historic landmarks.
  • Difficulty: Beginner to intermediate.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Late spring through early fall.

This area is perfect for paddlers who love discovering small islands and quaint harbors while gliding on calm waters subsidized by natural barriers. The botanical diversity and bird life add another dimension to the journey.

10. Shuswap Lake, British Columbia — A Serene Lakeside Escape

Known for its sandy beaches and warm waters, Shuswap Lake is a tranquil spot for paddlers who prefer a calm and scenic lake environment amidst mountainous backdrops.

  • Highlights: Clear waters, forested shores, and small island explorations.
  • Difficulty: Beginner-friendly.
  • Ideal Time to Visit: Summer months.

Many local outfitters offer kayak rentals, making this a great destination for family outings or multi-day paddling excursions with lakefront camping.

Tips for Planning Your Scenic Kayaking Adventure

  • Weather and Water Conditions: Check forecasts and water levels before setting out. Conditions can vary greatly between open lakes, rivers, and coastal areas.
  • Safety First: Always wear a life jacket, notify someone of your route, and carry communication devices where possible.
  • Respect Wildlife and Environment: Keep a safe distance from animals and practice Leave No Trace principles to preserve these stunning natural habitats.
  • Gear and Preparation: Tailor your gear to the environment—wetsuits for colder places, sun protection for exposed waters, and dry bags for essentials.

Kayaking these routes is more than a physical activity; it is an invitation to discover North America’s rich diversity of landscapes, ecosystems, and stories. From the quiet majesty of interior lakes to the awe-inspiring oceanic coasts, each paddle stroke brings you closer to nature’s grandeur. Whether gliding past towering cliffs, threading through emerald forests, or floating alongside wildlife, these scenic routes promise unforgettable experiences that every kayaker should add to their bucket list. The water is calling—grab your paddle and set off on an adventure that stirs the soul.
