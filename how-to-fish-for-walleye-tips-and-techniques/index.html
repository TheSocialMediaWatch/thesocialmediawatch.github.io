How to Fish for Walleye: Tips and Techniques


Fishing for walleye can be an exciting and rewarding experience for anglers of all skill levels. These predatory fish, known for their elusive nature, require specific techniques, knowledge, and gear to catch successfully. Whether you are a seasoned fisherman or a novice looking to target walleye, understanding their behavior, preferred habitats, and the best fishing methods is crucial.

In this guide, we’ll cover everything you need to know about fishing for walleye, including where to find them, the best times to fish, the ideal equipment, and proven techniques to reel in this popular fish.

Understanding Walleye Behavior

Before heading out on the water, it’s essential to understand walleye behavior. Walleye are nocturnal predators that typically feed at dawn and dusk. During the day, they prefer deeper waters or areas with low light, such as the shadows of underwater structures, while at night, they move toward shallower waters to hunt.

Their diet primarily consists of smaller fish like minnows, perch, and shad, making these prey species a good starting point when selecting bait. Walleye are also known to be temperamental, meaning they might not always strike immediately. Patience and persistence are key when targeting this species.

Best Locations to Fish for Walleye

Finding the right location is one of the most critical aspects of walleye fishing. These fish are often found in lakes, rivers, and reservoirs with cool, clear waters. They tend to prefer areas with structure, such as rocky bottoms, submerged vegetation, drop-offs, and points where the depth changes abruptly. These areas provide both cover and food, which is why walleye tend to congregate here.

Popular Walleye Hotspots

  • Shorelines and Drop-offs: Look for steep drop-offs along shorelines, as these areas are often prime hunting grounds for walleye.
  • Points and Submerged Islands: Walleye love to hang around points where deep water meets shallow water. Submerged islands and underwater ridges can be great places to cast.
  • Weed Beds and Structure: Walleye are also found near weed beds and submerged logs or rocks, which provide cover and attract baitfish.
  • River Mouths: Many walleye travel into rivers to spawn in spring, so fishing near river mouths can yield good results.

The Best Time to Fish for Walleye

Timing is crucial when fishing for walleye. Since these fish are more active at specific times of day, fishing during the right periods increases your chances of success. Walleye are most active at dawn and dusk, when they venture into shallow waters to feed. During these times, the low light conditions provide the cover they need to hunt effectively.

However, walleye can also be caught during the night, especially when using artificial lighting or fishing in areas where light penetration is minimal. On overcast days, walleye are also more likely to be found in shallow areas as the diffused light makes them feel more secure.

In terms of seasons, walleye are often most active during the spring and fall, when water temperatures are moderate. During the summer, they tend to retreat to deeper, cooler waters to avoid the heat. In winter, ice fishing can be a popular method for catching walleye, though it requires specific techniques due to the colder conditions.

Essential Gear for Walleye Fishing

Choosing the right gear for walleye fishing is crucial for success. The right combination of rods, reels, lines, and tackle will help you fish effectively and increase your chances of landing a walleye.

Rod and Reel

When selecting a rod and reel for walleye fishing, a medium-action spinning rod is ideal. A 6.5 to 7-foot rod offers the right balance of sensitivity and strength, allowing you to detect subtle bites while also handling the fish when it strikes. Pair the rod with a quality spinning reel, preferably one that can handle light to medium weight lines.

Fishing Line

A 6-10 pound test fishing line is typically the best choice for walleye fishing. Monofilament and fluorocarbon lines are great options because they provide good sensitivity while maintaining a low visibility in the water. Fluorocarbon is particularly effective because it is virtually invisible under water, which can increase your chances of a successful strike.

Lures and Baits

Walleye can be caught using a variety of lures and live bait. Live minnows are one of the most effective baits, but other options like nightcrawlers, shad, and perch can also work well. When fishing with artificial lures, consider using jigs, crankbaits, or spinners.

Jigs

Jigs are among the most versatile and popular lures for walleye. They can be fished vertically or horizontally, depending on the depth and structure of the water. Walleye are often attracted to jigs that mimic the movement of small prey species. Try a variety of colors and sizes to see which works best for your specific location.

Crankbaits

Crankbaits are excellent for covering larger areas, especially when fishing in deeper waters. These lures are designed to mimic the movement of small fish and can be retrieved at different speeds to simulate injured prey. They are particularly effective when trolling or fishing at varying depths.

Spinners

Spinners, particularly those with a willow leaf blade, are effective in attracting walleye, especially in clear waters. The flash created by the spinning blade helps draw walleye in from a distance. They can be used with or without live bait, depending on the situation.

Live Bait Rigging

When using live bait, it’s essential to use the proper rigging technique. A simple slip-sinker rig can work wonders, allowing the bait to move naturally along the bottom while remaining in the strike zone. Alternatively, a crawler harness or a live bait rig with a bobber can be used for suspended fishing or targeting walleye in deeper water.

Fishing Techniques for Walleye

Now that you’ve got the right gear, it’s time to learn the most effective techniques for catching walleye. Here are some proven methods to help you land a trophy fish:

Trolling

Trolling is a common and effective technique for walleye fishing, especially when targeting fish that are located in deeper waters. By slowly dragging lures or baits behind a moving boat, you can cover a large area and increase your chances of finding active walleye. Trolling can be done with crankbaits, spinners, or live bait, depending on the depth and water conditions.

Drift Fishing

Drift fishing is a technique that involves allowing your boat to drift with the current while keeping your bait in the strike zone. This method is often used when fishing in rivers or lakes with gentle currents. The key to drift fishing is controlling the boat’s speed and ensuring that your bait stays near the bottom, where walleye are most likely to strike.

Vertical Jigging

Vertical jigging involves lowering a jig straight down into the water and bouncing it off the bottom. This technique is especially effective when fishing for walleye in deeper waters or when they are holding close to the bottom. Jigging can be done with a variety of jigs, such as lead-head jigs, or soft plastic jigs, and can be adjusted based on the fish’s depth and activity level.

Casting

Casting is a more traditional technique for walleye fishing, and it can be highly effective when targeting fish in shallow areas or near structure. Cast your baited hook or lure near drop-offs, submerged rocks, or weed beds, and retrieve it slowly to mimic the movement of injured prey. Be patient, as walleye tend to be cautious and may take some time to strike.

Common Walleye Fishing Mistakes to Avoid

While fishing for walleye can be relatively straightforward, there are several common mistakes that anglers make that can decrease their chances of success. Here are a few to keep in mind:

1. Using Too Heavy of a Line

Using a line that is too heavy for walleye can lead to poor presentations and fewer strikes. Walleye are often wary of thick lines, so it’s important to use a line that is light but strong enough to handle the fish.

2. Fishing at the Wrong Depth

Walleye are bottom dwellers, and fishing too high in the water column can result in missed opportunities. Ensure that your bait or lure is at the right depth by adjusting your technique and equipment accordingly.

3. Not Matching the Hatch

Walleye feed primarily on smaller fish, so it’s important to use bait that mimics the types of prey species present in the water. Be sure to match your bait’s color, size, and movement to that of local fish populations to increase your chances of success.

4. Ignoring Water Conditions

Walleye are sensitive to changes in water temperature, clarity, and current. Always take note of the conditions before you fish and adjust your tactics accordingly. For example, if the water is murky, consider using brighter or more visible lures.

5. Not Being Patient

Walleye can be finicky eaters, and patience is key. Don’t rush your presentation, and be prepared to wait for a bite. Many anglers make the mistake of reeling in too quickly or giving up too soon. Instead, take your time and wait for the walleye to strike.

Conclusion

Fishing for walleye is an enjoyable and rewarding activity that requires a mix of patience, skill, and knowledge of the fish’s behavior. By understanding their habits, choosing the right gear, and employing proven techniques, you can increase your chances of landing this elusive and prized fish. Whether you’re fishing from a boat, the shore, or through the ice, these tips will help you fish effectively and catch more walleye.

Happy fishing!
