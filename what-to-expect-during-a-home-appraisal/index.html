What to Expect During a Home Appraisal


When selling or refinancing a property, one of the crucial steps in the process is the home appraisal. This procedure helps determine the current market value of your home, which can influence the sale price or the loan amount you qualify for. For many homeowners, an appraisal is a mysterious and sometimes nerve-wracking event. Knowing what to expect during a home appraisal can help you feel more confident and possibly enhance the outcome.

What is a Home Appraisal?

A home appraisal is a professional assessment of a property’s value conducted by a licensed appraiser. Lenders typically require appraisals to verify that the home’s worth justifies the loan amount requested. In essence, the appraisal protects both the buyer and the lender by ensuring the loan is rooted in the actual value of the home.

While appraisals are commonly associated with mortgages and refinancing, they’re also useful for establishing a fair asking price during a sale, settling property taxes, or even handling estate cases.

Who Conducts the Home Appraisal?

An independent, third-party appraiser conducts the home appraisal. These experts have certifications and licenses that require them to follow strict industry standards. They visit the property, gather data, and then compile a detailed report that includes an estimated value based on comparable sales and property features.

Because appraisers are impartial, they won’t be influenced by either the buyer or seller. Their focus is purely on factual information and market trends, ensuring the valuation is fair and unbiased.

Preparing for the Home Appraisal: Tips to Increase Value

Before the appraiser arrives, a bit of preparation can go a long way in maximizing your home’s appraised value:

  • Clean and declutter: A tidy home looks well-maintained and can make a better impression.
  • Complete minor repairs: Fix leaky faucets, cracked windows, or squeaky doors.
  • Enhance curb appeal: Maintain your lawn, trim bushes, and clean walkways.
  • Gather renovation receipts: If you recently upgraded your kitchen, bathroom, or installed new appliances, have documentation ready.
  • Provide access: Make sure all areas, including the attic, basement, and garage, are accessible.
  • Prepare a list for the appraiser: This might include recent upgrades, unique features, or neighborhood amenities.

The Day of the Appraisal: What Happens?

When the appraiser arrives, they typically spend anywhere from 30 to 60 minutes evaluating your home, though larger properties or homes with extensive features might take longer. Their inspection covers several aspects:

Exterior Inspection

The appraiser begins outside, assessing the condition of your property from the curb. This examination includes:

  • Size of the lot
  • Condition of the landscaping and driveway
  • Integrity of the siding, roofing, gutters, and foundation
  • Functionality and aesthetic of exterior fixtures like doors and windows
  • Presence of additional structures (garage, shed, pool, decks)

Interior Inspection

Once inside, the appraiser will:

  • Measure the square footage to verify it matches public records
  • Count and evaluate the bedrooms and bathrooms
  • Inspect the condition of floors, walls, ceilings, and fixtures
  • Assess kitchens and bathrooms for updates and quality of materials
  • Check heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems
  • Look for signs of damage such as water stains, mold, or pests
  • Note overall cleanliness and maintenance standards

Neighborhood and Location Evaluation

Beyond your home itself, the appraiser considers:

  • Proximity to schools, shopping, transportation, and employment centers
  • Neighborhood safety and curb appeal
  • Trends in local real estate market values
  • Comparable homes recently sold in your area

Data Analysis and Reporting

Using all this information, the appraiser will perform a comparative market analysis (CMA). They will compare your home to similar properties in the area that have sold recently, adjusting for differences in size, features, and condition.

The final step is compiling the data into a comprehensive appraisal report. This document includes:

  • The appraiser’s estimated value of the home
  • Descriptions and photographs of the property
  • Notes about upgrades or unique features
  • Comparables used for value estimation

This report typically takes a few days to be finalized and then is sent to the lender or whoever requested the appraisal.

How Does the Appraisal Affect Your Sale or Loan?

The appraisal has a significant impact on real estate transactions and financing. If the home appraises at or above your sale price, it usually means the process moves forward smoothly. However:

  • If the appraisal comes in below the sale price, the lender may reduce the loan amount, requiring the buyer to make up the difference in cash or renegotiate the price.
  • In refinancing, a low appraisal could limit the amount you qualify to borrow.
  • For sellers, a low appraisal might mean needing to lower your asking price or invest in repairs to boost value.

Understanding this dynamic gives you a clearer picture of your negotiation leverage.

Common Questions About Home Appraisals

How Accurate Are Home Appraisals?

While appraisers use rigorous methods, their valuation isn’t an exact science. Market conditions, unique features, and even the appraiser’s judgment can cause some variance. Typically, the appraisal offers a snapshot of value within a reasonable range rather than a precise figure.

Can You Be Present During the Appraisal?

Yes, homeowners are generally allowed to be present during the appraisal. Meeting the appraiser can be helpful to point out improvements or unique features that might not be obvious. However, keep interactions professional and avoid pressuring them.

How Much Does an Appraisal Cost?

Appraisal fees vary by location and property size but often range from $300 to $600 for typical single-family homes. The party requesting the appraisal generally pays these fees.

How Long Is an Appraisal Valid?

Appraisals typically reflect current market conditions and are considered valid for about 90 days. After that, changes in the market might require a new appraisal.

Beyond the Basics: Understanding Different Types of Appraisals

While the standard appraisal covers most homes, there are specialized versions:

  • Drive-by appraisal: The appraiser evaluates only the exterior without entering the home. Usually faster and cheaper but less detailed.
  • Desktop appraisal: An appraisal done primarily using public records and online data, with no physical visit.
  • Commercial appraisals: These focus on properties used for business purposes and involve different criteria.

Know which type applies to your situation and how it might affect the valuation process.

How to Challenge an Appraisal You Disagree With

If you believe your appraisal undervalues your home, you can take these steps:

  • Review the report carefully for errors in property details.
  • Compare the selected comps to ensure they are truly comparable.
  • Provide additional information or documentation of improvements to your lender.
  • Request a second appraisal or reconsideration from the lender.

While not always successful, a thoughtful challenge can sometimes lead to a reassessment.

Final Thoughts: Demystifying the Home Appraisal Process

A home appraisal brings important clarity and fairness to real estate transactions. By understanding what happens during the appraisal, preparing your home thoughtfully, and knowing how to interpret the results, you’ll approach the process with confidence.

Remember, an appraisal is a professional opinion grounded in data but influenced by market realities beyond your control. Keeping a positive mindset and open communication with your lender or real estate agent can help ensure the appraisal serves as a helpful step towards your financial goals.

If you’re buying, selling, or refinancing soon, showing your home in its best light and knowing what to expect can make the appraisal process an asset rather than an obstacle.
