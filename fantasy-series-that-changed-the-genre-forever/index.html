Fantasy Series That Changed the Genre Forever


The world of fantasy literature has been forever reshaped by a select group of series that not only captivated readers but also redefined storytelling conventions, inspired new generations of writers, and broadened the scope of imagination. These groundbreaking series proved that fantasy could be as complex, diverse, and profound as any literary genre, introducing innovative themes, world-building techniques, and character archetypes that continue to influence the genre today.

In this exploration, we delve deep into some of the most influential fantasy series that have transformed the landscape of fantasy fiction. From classics that established the foundation of modern fantasy to newer works that injected fresh energy and perspectives into the genre, these series highlight the evolution of fantasy storytelling and its enduring appeal.

1. The Lord of the Rings by J.R.R. Tolkien

Any discussion about fantasy literature inevitably begins with J.R.R. Tolkien’s The Lord of the Rings. Published in the mid-20th century, this epic saga laid the cornerstone for modern fantasy writing. Tolkien’s meticulous world-building, complete with languages, mythologies, and a richly detailed geography, established a new benchmark for immersive storytelling.

The series not only popularized the trope of a fellowship on a perilous journey but also introduced iconic elements such as hobbits, powerful rings, and dark lords that have become staple motifs across fantasy fiction. Beyond plot and setting, Tolkien’s exploration of themes such as friendship, sacrifice, and the corrupting influence of power added layers of depth to a genre often dismissed as escapist.

His influence extends beyond literature into film, gaming, and popular culture, proving the lasting power of a well-crafted fantasy world.

2. A Song of Ice and Fire by George R.R. Martin

George R.R. Martin’s A Song of Ice and Fire series shook the foundations of traditional fantasy with its gritty realism and complex political intrigue. Beginning with A Game of Thrones in 1996, Martin introduced readers to a medieval-inspired realm where noble houses vie for power, and where the struggle is as brutal and ambiguous as it is fantastical.

What set this series apart was its moral ambiguity and willingness to subvert conventional fantasy tropes. Heroes may fall, villains may redeem themselves, and the line between good and evil is often blurred. Martin’s commitment to realistic character development, intricate plotting, and unexpected twists challenged readers’ expectations and diversified the genre.

The layered storytelling and mature themes attracted a broader audience, proving that fantasy could be sophisticated, multi-dimensional, and resonate deeply with contemporary issues of power, loyalty, and survival.

3. The Wheel of Time by Robert Jordan (and completed by Brandon Sanderson)

Robert Jordan’s The Wheel of Time series, beginning in 1990, is another seminal work that expanded the scope of fantasy in both scale and complexity. Spanning fourteen books, this sprawling epic combines intricate character arcs, an expansive cast, and a richly detailed magic system.

Jordan’s approach to time and destiny—conceptualized as a “wheel” turning through repeated ages—introduced a cyclical view of history that added philosophical weight to the narrative. The series also showcased a nuanced portrayal of gender dynamics, with powerful female characters breaking traditional molds.

Crucially, Jordan set a new standard for serialized storytelling in fantasy, demonstrating how long-form narratives could sustain reader engagement over decades while exploring deep themes like fate, prophecy, and sacrifice.

Brandon Sanderson’s completion of the series after Jordan’s passing not only honored but also elevated the original vision, providing a satisfying conclusion to a complex saga.

4. Harry Potter by J.K. Rowling

While often categorized as young adult or middle-grade literature, Harry Potter has undeniably transformed the fantasy genre on a global scale. Rowling’s seven-book series chronicles the magical coming-of-age story of the titular boy wizard, blending traditional fantasy elements with contemporary settings and themes.

Its extraordinary success can be attributed to its accessibility combined with thematic depth, exploring friendship, identity, prejudice, and the fight against evil. The series also rejuvenated interest in fantasy among younger readers, spawning an entire generation of fans who continue to embrace the genre in adulthood.

Rowling’s creation of a hidden magical world coexisting with the mundane one inspired countless authors to imagine new forms of magical realism and urban fantasy, broadening the genre’s horizons beyond medieval-inspired worlds.

5. His Dark Materials by Philip Pullman

Philip Pullman’s His Dark Materials trilogy introduced a darker, more philosophical dimension to fantasy, challenging readers with questions about religion, consciousness, and authority. Its narrative follows Lyra Belacqua through parallel universes, blending fantasy, science, and theology.

The unique concept of daemons—physical manifestations of characters’ souls—and the series’ refusal to shy away from complex and sometimes unsettling ideas distinguished it from more traditional fantasy fare. Pullman’s critique of dogma and his exploration of free will and innocence subverted expectations and provoked thoughtful discourse.

The series’ intellectual ambition expanded the possibilities of fantasy, demonstrating that the genre could engage with profound moral and existential questions while still captivating readers with imaginative storytelling.

6. The Kingkiller Chronicle by Patrick Rothfuss

Patrick Rothfuss’s ongoing series, beginning with The Name of the Wind, offers a fresh take on epic fantasy by blending lyrical prose with a deeply personal narrative. The story of Kvothe, a gifted musician and magician recounting his life’s trials and adventures, places character and voice at the forefront.

Rothfuss’s meticulous attention to language, music, and storytelling craft illustrates how fantasy can be reflective and introspective without sacrificing narrative momentum. The meta-narrative structure—stories within stories—add layers of complexity and invites readers to think about the nature of myth and memory.

Though the series remains unfinished, its impact is unmistakable, inspiring writers to foreground emotion and artistry in fantasy while maintaining the genre’s classic elements.

7. The Stormlight Archive by Brandon Sanderson

Brandon Sanderson’s The Stormlight Archive is a modern epic that showcases inventive magic systems, intricate world-building, and richly developed characters. Since the first book, The Way of Kings, was published in 2010, it has steadily redefined what contemporary fantasy can achieve.

Sanderson’s hallmark is his “hard magic” approach: magic with clearly understood rules and limitations, which he integrates seamlessly into political and cultural settings. The series also explores themes of leadership, mental health, and redemption, approaching fantasy with both innovation and heart.

This ambitious work underscores Sanderson’s reputation for refreshing classic fantasy tropes with creativity and sensitivity, setting a new standard for future contributions to the genre.

8. The Earthsea Cycle by Ursula K. Le Guin

Ursula K. Le Guin’s Earthsea series predates many modern fantasies but remains influential for its philosophical depth and pioneering diversity. Beginning with A Wizard of Earthsea in 1968, the series introduced a nuanced exploration of balance, power, and identity within a richly imagined archipelago world.

Le Guin’s focus on moral dilemmas, the consequences of power, and the journey toward self-understanding laid groundwork for later fantasy authors looking to explore character complexity beyond good versus evil.

Additionally, her subversion of racial and cultural norms in fantasy settings challenged the genre’s Eurocentric tendencies, inspiring more inclusive and reflective storytelling.

Conclusion: The Ever-Evolving Tale of Fantasy

Fantasy series that changed the genre forever have one thing in common: they dared to dream bigger, push boundaries, and transform familiar elements into something new and resonant. Each of these series—not simply stories of magic and adventure but profound explorations of human nature and the world—expanded the possibilities for what fantasy could be.

Whether through grand world-building, complex characters, moral ambiguity, or innovative structures, these landmark series collectively weave a rich tapestry of influence, setting the stage for future storytellers to continue evolving the genre.

Fantasy remains a living, breathing form of art—constantly renewing and reshaping itself. For readers and writers alike, revisiting these monumental works offers both inspiration and a reminder of the genre’s limitless potential.
