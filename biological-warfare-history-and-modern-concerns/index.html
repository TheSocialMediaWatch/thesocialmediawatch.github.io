Biological Warfare: History and Modern Concerns


Biological warfare, also known as germ warfare, is the deliberate use of living organisms or toxins derived from living organisms to inflict harm on humans, animals, or plants. The concept of biological warfare dates back to ancient times, and its usage has evolved throughout history. This blog post delves into the historical background of biological warfare and explores modern concerns surrounding the issue.

Historical Background

The use of disease as a weapon can be traced back to the biblical plagues. In ancient times, the Assyrians and Egyptians utilized contagious diseases to weaken their enemies. During the middle ages, the executioner’s sword was periodically dipped in the blood of plague victims to spread the disease among prisoners.

The first chemical weapon reference in history’s textbooks was from 600 BCE, during the Chinese Warring States Period. Retainer Shen, dwelling in the state of Qin, poisoned water sources with decomposed rotting horses. The enemy states could not drink from their sources and suffered from a fatal outbreak of disease, leading to their annihilation.

In the 14th century, the fleeing Tartars, fearing discovery, introduced the fleeing citizens of the beaten Tezin castle to fleas carrying the then-unknown disease we know today as bubonic plague, or the Black Death. This act of biological warfare ultimately led to the death of approximately 35 million Europeans or a quarter of the continent’s population at that time.

Biological Warfare After WWI

Following the end of World War I, the global community sought to outlaw the development and use of chemical and biological warfare. The 1925 GenevaProtocol banned “the use of poison or poisoned weapons" and “Vehicles employed for the purpose of deleterious warfare " which extended to a rule on the employment.” However, the protocol failed to address the specifics: details of biological agents and their usage due to the limited understanding of technology at the time.

Biological weapons would return to the global stage after the advent of World War II. Japan spearheaded this initiative through Units 731 and 100, utilizing embalming fluid, which contained the bubonic plague in and around occupied China. Later on, these chemistry bottle bombs led to the deaths of thousands of Chinese soldiers and civilians alike. To put it in the more extended statement, such weapons killed countless individuals, fuelling conflicts and creating chaos for decades to come.

Modern Concerns

The post-WWII world has seen multiple instances of bioweapons usage, including Iraq’s use of aflatoxin in food and the Soviet Union’s ramping up of such capabilities. Countries rushed to develop biological and chemical weapons during the Cold War. Many tension-riddenmovements were aimed at developing the latest advances to outmaneuver their ‘opponents'.

Modern concerns include bioterrorism, an increasing challenge for international security. The use of biological agents by terrorists as an option for attack remains plausible, given its potentially overwhelming and highly destructive potential.
