The Intriguing World of Orange Wine


When we think of wine, the colors that usually come to mind are red, white, and rosé. However, there’s a lesser-known category of wine that’s been gaining attention in recent years – orange wine. No, it’s not made from oranges, but rather from white wine grapes that are left in contact with their skins during the winemaking process, resulting in a unique and intriguing beverage that defies traditional categorization.

What is Orange Wine?

Orange wine, also known as skin-contact wine, is made from white wine grapes that are fermented with their skins, seeds, and sometimes even stems. This extended skin contact gives the wine its characteristic orange or amber hue, as well as a complex flavor profile that sets it apart from conventional white wines. The process of making orange wine is more akin to red winemaking, as the skin contact imparts tannins, texture, and a distinct aroma to the final product.

History and Origins

While orange wine may seem like a recent trend, its origins can be traced back thousands of years to the country of Georgia, where winemaking traditions have been preserved through generations. The practice of fermenting white grapes with their skins in clay vessels called qvevri dates back to ancient times and has experienced a resurgence in the modern wine world. From Georgia, the production of orange wine has spread to other parts of the world, including Italy, Slovenia, and beyond, where winemakers are experimenting with different grape varieties and techniques to create their own interpretations of this ancient style.

Characteristics and Flavor Profile

One of the most intriguing aspects of orange wine is its diverse flavor profile, which can vary greatly depending on the grape varietal, terroir, and winemaking methods employed. Generally, orange wines exhibit a range of flavors that may include notes of dried fruits, nuts, honey, and even a hint of tannic grip, similar to red wines. The extended skin contact also contributes to a textural richness and a slightly oxidative quality, giving the wine a complex and often polarizing character that challenges conventional expectations.

Food Pairing and Serving

Due to its robust and complex nature, orange wine has found a place at the table alongside a wide array of dishes. Its bold flavors and tannic structure make it a versatile companion to a variety of foods, from rich and savory dishes to spicy and aromatic cuisines. When serving orange wine, it’s recommended to decant it to allow the flavors to fully unfold and to serve it at a slightly cooler temperature than room temperature to enhance its refreshing qualities.

The Future of Orange Wine

As the world of wine continues to evolve, orange wine has carved out a niche for itself as a unique and captivating category that appeals to adventurous wine enthusiasts and sommeliers alike. Its ancient origins, complex flavors, and distinct appearance have garnered attention from both traditional and natural wine aficionados, sparking a renewed interest in this age-old winemaking technique. Whether you’re a seasoned wine connoisseur or a curious newcomer, the world of orange wine invites exploration and discovery, offering a sensory journey that transcends the boundaries of conventional wine styles.

In conclusion, orange wine stands as a testament to the rich tapestry of winemaking traditions and the enduring allure of pushing the boundaries of what wine can be. Its enigmatic nature and bold flavors make it a captivating addition to the diverse landscape of wine, inviting us to embrace the unexpected and savor the complexities that lie within each amber-hued glass. So, the next time you’re perusing the wine aisle or visiting a vineyard, consider venturing into the intriguing world of orange wine and experience a taste of history, innovation, and adventure in every sip. Cheers!
