The Best Plants for Pollinators and How to Attract Them


Attracting pollinators to your garden isn’t just a wonderful way to enliven your outdoor space—it’s a vital contribution to biodiversity and food production. Pollinating insects such as bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, and hummingbirds play an essential role in the reproduction of many plants, including those crucial to human and wildlife diets. By carefully selecting certain plants, you can create a thriving habitat that supports these busy creatures all season long. This guide delves into the best plants to include for various pollinators and practical tips to attract and support them year-round.

Understanding Pollinators and Their Needs

Pollinators are attracted to plants primarily for nectar and pollen, which provide the energy and protein necessary for their survival. Different pollinators have varying preferences in flower shape, color, scent, and bloom time. Knowing these preferences helps in designing a garden that offers an abundant buffet and a safe haven.

  • Bees prefer flowers rich in pollen and nectar, with shapes easy to land on or access.
  • Butterflies are drawn to brightly colored flowers with landing platforms.
  • Moths work mostly at night and favor pale or white flowers with strong scents.
  • Beetles gravitate towards large, bowl-shaped flowers.
  • Hummingbirds seek tubular, brightly colored flowers, mostly in red or orange hues.

Top Plants to Attract Bees

Lavender (Lavandula spp.)

Lavender’s fragrant, purple flowers bloom during summer and early fall, attracting honeybees and native bees alike. Its dense flower spikes are rich in nectar, and lifespans can extend for several weeks, providing a consistent food supply.

Coneflower (Echinacea spp.)

Coneflowers offer a blend of pollen and nectar. Their central spiky cones attract bees looking for easy pollen access. These plants are drought-tolerant and bloom from mid to late summer, prolonging pollinator support.

Sunflowers (Helianthus annuus)

Sunflowers are a beacon for many pollinators with their large heads packed with nectar and pollen. They bloom in mid to late summer and also provide seeds for birds afterward, enhancing habitat value year-round.

Borage (Borago officinalis)

Known for vibrant blue star-shaped flowers, borage produces copious nectar that’s irresistible to bees. Its long blooming period ensures continuous forage across the growing season.

Wildflowers Mix

A mix of native wildflowers such as black-eyed Susan, goldenrod, and asters can massively enhance pollinator attraction by providing diversity and succession in bloom times.

Favorite Plants for Butterflies

Milkweed (Asclepias spp.)

Milkweed is vital for monarch butterflies, serving as both nectar source and host plant for caterpillars. Its clusters of sweetly scented flowers provide nectar for many butterfly species.

Butterfly Bush (Buddleja davidii)

True to its name, this shrub produces long, cone-shaped flower clusters packed with nectar. While it blooms from summer to early fall, care should be taken in some regions as it can be invasive—opting for native alternatives is recommended.

Lantana (Lantana camara)

Lantana’s rich orange, red, and yellow flower clusters attract a variety of butterflies and nectar-feeding insects. It thrives in warm climates and blooms heavily over a long period.

Zinnia (Zinnia elegans)

Zinnias are easy to grow, colorful, and loved by butterflies. Their open-faced flowers provide an excellent landing platform and accessible nectar.

Joe-Pye Weed (Eutrochium purpureum)

With tall purple flower clusters, Joe-Pye Weed attracts large numbers of butterflies and other pollinators late into the season.

Moths’ Preferred Plants

Evening Primrose (Oenothera spp.)

Opening its pale yellow night flowers in the evening, this plant provides nectar for moths and other nocturnal pollinators.

Nicotiana (Nicotiana spp.)

Adding sweet fragrance in the evening, Nicotiana flowers are tubular and emit scents that can travel far, perfect for moth pollination.

Jasmine (Jasminum spp.)

Certain jasmines bloom at night, releasing intense fragrance that attracts moths and some bees.

Campion (Silene latifolia)

With white flowers that open in the night, campion attracts night pollinators such as moths effectively.

Plants for Beetles and Other Pollinators

Magnolia (Magnolia spp.)

Magnolia flowers appeal to beetles with their large, bowl-shaped blooms filled with pollen.

Pawpaw (Asimina triloba)

The unique bloom of the pawpaw attracts beetle pollinators, supporting biodiversity in woodland gardens.

Buttercup (Ranunculus spp.)

Buttercups’ bright yellow flowers attract beetles and other flying insects seeking pollen.

Designing a Pollinator-Friendly Garden

Provide Continuous Blooming

To support a wide range of pollinators throughout the growing season, include plants with staggered bloom times from early spring to late fall. Mixing early bloomers like crocus with summer staples such as coneflowers and fall favorites like asters ensures steady food availability.

Incorporate Native Plants

Native plants are adapted to your local climate and ecosystems, often making them the best choiceto support indigenous pollinator species. They usually require less maintenance and are more resistant to pests and diseases.

Avoid Pesticides and Herbicides

Chemical treatments can severely impact pollinator health. Opt for natural pest control methods and companion planting whenever possible.

Create Shelter and Nesting Sites

Pollinators need more than food—they also require shelter and nesting materials. Consider leaving patches of bare soil for ground-nesting bees and plant dead wood or shrubs where solitary bees can nest. Incorporating a small water source like a shallow dish with pebbles can provide hydration.

Use Diverse Plant Structures

Incorporate a variety of flower shapes, colors, and heights to attract a range of pollinator species. This diversity supports not just major pollinators but also lesser-known and specialized ones.

Seasonal Pollinator Garden Calendar

  • Spring: Crocus, Apple blossoms, Cherry blossoms, Willow catkins, Dandelions
  • Summer: Lavender, Coneflower, Milkweed, Sunflowers, Borage
  • Fall: Asters, Goldenrod, Joe-Pye Weed, Sedum, Solidago

Implementing plants from these categories ensures a vibrant environment for pollinators year-round.

Beyond Flowers: Additional Pollinator Attraction Tips

  • Companion Planting: Pair vegetables and herbs with flowering plants to attract pollinators that support your food crops.
  • Avoid Double-Flowered Varieties: Many double-flowered cultivars have reduced nectar and pollen.
  • Plant in Clusters: Grouping the same species together creates a larger visual signal for pollinators, making it easier for them to locate resources.
  • Leave Weeds: Plants like clover and dandelions are often the first food sources in early spring.

Conclusion

Crafting a garden that welcomes and supports pollinators is a rewarding endeavor, benefiting wildlife, your home environment, and even the broader ecosystem. By planting a variety of favorites like lavender, milkweed, and sunflowers, and maintaining a diverse and pesticide-free habitat, you’ll create a haven buzzing with life. Not only will you witness the mesmerizing dance of bees and butterflies, but you’ll also contribute to the essential cycle that sustains much of the world’s plant diversity and food supply. Start small, observe, and expand your pollinator-friendly garden—your efforts will ripple outward, inviting nature’s best workers to flourish.
