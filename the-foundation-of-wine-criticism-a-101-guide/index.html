The Foundation of Wine Criticism: A 101 Guide


Wine criticism often seems like an esoteric pursuit, something exclusive to the well-versed gourmand or the high-end sommelier. But at its base, wine criticism is an analysis of the aroma, taste, and overall texture of the wine. And, like any other art, it’s subjective. In this guide, we’ll explore how to establish a foundational understanding of wine criticism.

The Anatomy of Wine

Understanding aroma is a crucial first step. It’s how our senses perceive wine, often the most typical point of assessment when evaluating vintages. Wine aroma can be broken down into three categories: primary (volatile compounds that come from the grape itself), secondary (esters produced during fermentation), and tertiary (more complex aromas derived from aging).

When analyzing taste, it’s crucial to consider both fruit and tannin levels. Fruitiness refers to the naturally-occurring flavors in the grape, such as apple, peach, or berry. Tannins, on the other hand, are natural compounds found in grape skins, seeds, and stems. A higher tannin content can result in a dry, astringent sensation in the mouth.

Acidity provides wine with balance and structure. Appropriate acidity enhances the sensation of tastes while providing a refreshing sensation in warmer wines.

Finally, consider body and mouthfeel. The wine’s body refers to its weight on the palate and can range from light to heavy. Similarly, mouthfeel considers the sensations in our mouth upon swallowing; this could be silky or oily, for example.

The “5 Sights” of Wine Evaluation

Just like how the Elements of Art provide guidelines for painting, the 5 Sights offer parameters for evaluating wine visually.

  1. Clarity relates to the wine’s transparency. A wine with a clear body suggests good clarification.
  2. Color is subjective but can reveal much about a wine. Brighter colors, especially in red wines, are an indication of a fresher grape.
  3. Intense Color refers to the depth of color in a wine. A deeper color can suggest a higher level of antioxidant polyphenols.
  4. Brightness is the sheen or shimmer of a wine. A brighter hue can suggest a youthful wine.
  5. Internal Emulsion refers to the ’legs’ or tears a wine produces. These can indicate a well-balanced wine.

Adopting a Personal Approach to Wine Criticism

Though wine appraisal follows a general format, the most important step is developing your palate. The best approaches involve both education and consistency. Letting your taste buds be the guiding light through trial and error will eventually result in a deeper understanding of wine.

Remember, though, that this is not a competition. Wine is subjective. Your tastes and preferences might not align with a critic’s. Embrace this. Enjoy wine as you would any other art: based on individual opinion and your own sensibilities.

By cultivating an intimate connection with wine, you will create a deeper understanding that only comes with experience and exposure. So remember: be patient with yourself, keep an open mind, and enjoy the journey. After all, wine is not solely about the end product but rather the experience.
