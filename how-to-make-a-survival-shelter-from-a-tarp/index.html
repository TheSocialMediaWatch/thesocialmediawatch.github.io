How to Make a Survival Shelter from a Tarp


In survival situations, building a shelter is a fundamental skill that can significantly increase your chances of staying safe, dry, and warm. A tarp is one of the most versatile and effective materials for creating a survival shelter. It’s lightweight, compact, and can be used in a variety of environments, making it an essential tool in any survival kit.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through how to create a reliable and functional survival shelter using a tarp. From selecting the right tarp to understanding different shelter configurations, we’ll cover everything you need to know to build a shelter that can protect you from the elements.

What You’ll Need to Build a Tarp Shelter

Before we dive into the different shelter designs, let’s go over the basic materials you’ll need to build a shelter with a tarp.

1. A Tarp

A good-quality tarp is the foundation of your shelter. Ideally, choose a tarp that is large enough to accommodate you and any gear you have. The size should be at least 8 feet by 10 feet, though larger tarps will offer more room. The material should be durable, waterproof, and resistant to tearing. Polyester, nylon, or polyethylene are excellent choices.

2. Rope or Paracord

Rope or paracord is essential for securing the tarp to trees, stakes, or other sturdy anchors. You’ll need enough length to create ties for the tarp corners and edges, typically 30 to 50 feet of rope.

3. Stakes or Rocks

If you’re in an area without readily available trees or other anchors, you may need stakes or rocks to secure the tarp. Wooden stakes are commonly used, but large, heavy rocks can also do the job in a pinch.

4. Tree Branches or Poles (Optional)

If you need extra structure or support for your tarp shelter, you can use tree branches or poles to create a frame. This step is optional but can be useful for larger shelters or in windy conditions.

5. Survival Tools

It’s always a good idea to have a few essential survival tools on hand when building a shelter, such as a knife for cutting rope or trimming branches, and a fire starter for warmth once your shelter is set up.

Choosing the Right Location for Your Shelter

Before you set up your tarp shelter, it’s crucial to choose the right location. A poor location can render your shelter ineffective, so take the time to assess your surroundings.

Look for Shelter from the Elements

Pick a spot that offers natural protection from wind and rain. This might include being near a large rock, under a tree canopy, or in a ravine. Avoid areas that are prone to flooding, such as low-lying ground or places where water tends to collect.

Consider Safety

Ensure that your shelter is far from potential hazards, such as wild animals, sharp rocks, or unstable terrain. Also, avoid locations near cliffs or steep hills, as these can present dangers during weather changes.

Evaluate Ground Conditions

Look for flat, dry ground to set up your shelter. Avoid areas with lots of debris, as this could make it difficult to secure the tarp. If you can, clear the ground of sticks and rocks to create a more comfortable sleeping area.

Proximity to Resources

While not absolutely necessary, being near a water source or firewood can make your survival situation much easier. Just make sure to set up your shelter far enough away from water to avoid flooding or attracting insects.

Tarp Shelter Configurations

There are several different tarp shelter designs you can use, depending on your needs and the resources available to you. Each design has its own advantages, and the best one for you will depend on the environment and weather conditions.

1. A-Frame Shelter

The A-frame shelter is one of the simplest and most effective tarp shelters. It provides excellent protection from rain and wind, making it a great option in stormy conditions.

How to Build an A-Frame Shelter:

  1. Choose two sturdy anchor points: Find two trees or poles that are at least 8-10 feet apart. The trees should be about 5-6 feet tall to provide adequate coverage.
  2. Secure the tarp at the peak: Tie a rope to the center of the tarp and drape it over the anchor points. Make sure the tarp hangs evenly on both sides, creating a triangular shape.
  3. Stake the corners: Pull the corners of the tarp down to the ground and stake them securely into the earth. If you’re using rocks instead of stakes, place large rocks on each corner of the tarp to weigh it down.
  4. Adjust the tarp: If necessary, adjust the height of the tarp by tightening or loosening the rope at the peak. The A-frame design should create a sloping roof that channels water off the shelter.

This design works well for light to moderate rain and offers good wind protection.

2. Lean-To Shelter

The lean-to shelter is another versatile option that’s easy to set up and offers good protection from the elements. This shelter design is particularly useful if you’re in an area with strong winds or if you need a larger sleeping area.

How to Build a Lean-To Shelter:

  1. Find a sturdy anchor point: Select a tree or sturdy post that is 6-8 feet tall. The higher the anchor, the more space you’ll have inside the shelter.
  2. Secure one edge of the tarp: Tie one corner of the tarp to the anchor point, making sure it’s tight and secure. The tarp should hang down at an angle to create the lean-to effect.
  3. Stake down the other edge: Stretch the opposite side of the tarp out and stake it to the ground. Ensure that the tarp is pulled tightly to create a slanted roof that sheds rain.
  4. Use extra poles for support (optional): If you want additional stability, you can use a pole or tree branch to prop up the middle of the tarp. This will create more headroom and prevent the tarp from sagging in the middle.

A lean-to shelter offers great coverage from the elements, but it’s best suited for mild to moderate wind conditions.

3. Tarp Tent Shelter

The tarp tent shelter is a more advanced design that offers better protection from the wind and rain, especially in harsh conditions. It’s ideal for those who need extra privacy or security.

How to Build a Tarp Tent Shelter:

  1. Create a frame: Use two poles or sturdy branches to create an A-frame structure. The frame should be about 6-8 feet tall at the peak and should span the width of your tarp.
  2. Drape the tarp over the frame: Lay the tarp over the frame, making sure it covers the entire structure. The tarp should be long enough to cover the entire tent area, including the floor.
  3. Secure the corners: Tie the corners of the tarp to the ground, ensuring that the structure is tight and stable. You may need additional ropes to keep the tarp in place.
  4. Close the ends: If you want to increase privacy and weather protection, you can tie the ends of the tarp together, creating a more enclosed space.

This shelter provides excellent protection from the elements and can be used in most weather conditions. However, it’s more complex to set up than the other designs.

4. Tarp Hammock Shelter

If you’re in an area with a lot of insects or wet ground, a tarp hammock shelter can provide both comfort and protection. This design uses the tarp to create a suspended shelter, keeping you off the ground and away from pests.

How to Build a Tarp Hammock Shelter:

  1. Set up a hammock: Find two sturdy trees that are spaced far enough apart to accommodate your hammock. Secure the hammock between the trees as you normally would.
  2. Drape the tarp over the hammock: Once the hammock is set up, drape the tarp over the top, making sure the tarp is large enough to cover the entire hammock.
  3. Tie the tarp to the trees: Use ropes to secure the tarp to the trees, ensuring that it’s pulled tight and won’t blow away in the wind.
  4. Adjust the tarp: If necessary, adjust the tarp’s height and position to ensure that it provides adequate coverage from rain and wind.

This shelter design offers excellent protection and comfort, especially in areas with lots of insects or damp conditions.

Tips for Improving Your Tarp Shelter

  • Use a groundsheet: Placing a groundsheet under your shelter can provide extra insulation and keep moisture from seeping through the tarp.
  • Insulate with natural materials: If it’s cold outside, use leaves, pine needles, or grass to insulate the floor of your shelter. This will help retain heat and keep you warm.
  • Ventilation is key: Ensure your shelter has adequate ventilation to prevent condensation buildup. This will help you stay dry and reduce the risk of hypothermia.
  • Test your shelter: Before relying on your shelter in a survival situation, test it in less critical conditions to make sure it holds up and provides the protection you need.

Conclusion

Building a survival shelter from a tarp is a skill that every outdoor enthusiast and survivalist should master. With the right tarp and some basic tools, you can create a variety of shelters that offer protection from the elements, whether you’re caught in a rainstorm or seeking refuge from the wind. Remember to practice these techniques before you need them, and always be prepared with the necessary gear to keep you safe and comfortable in the wild.
