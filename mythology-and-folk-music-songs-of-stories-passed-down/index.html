Mythology and Folk Music: Songs of Stories Passed Down


Human beings have always been storytellers. From the very beginning, we’ve used stories to explain the world around us, to teach our children, and to preserve our heritage. One of the most enduring and fascinating forms of storytelling is mythology, which explores the human experience through the lens of the divine and the magical.

Over the millennia, stories from mythology have been passed down orally, transforming and evolving with the cultures that carry them. As such, it should come as no surprise that these age-old tales have found their way into the versatile and evocative world of folk music.

Folk music is an incredibly diverse genre, with roots in traditional songs and ballads that tell stories of love, loss, heroism, and tragedy. This genre of music often serves as a conduit for preserving the heritage and legends of a particular region or culture. It’s no coincidence that many songs draw from the deep well of myths and folklore.

In this post, we’ll explore the rich tapestry of connections between mythology and folk music. We’ll delve into the ways these two art forms intersect, and the unique ways in which songs can breathe new life into ancient stories.

Mythology in Folk Music: A Timeless Connection

The relationship between mythology and folk music is a symbiotic one. While many folk songs tell stories that are rooted in mythology, some songs even inspired directly from myths. Let’s take a closer look at a few examples.

One of the most iconic examples of mythology in folk music is the ballad of Orpheus and Eurydice. This tragic tale, originally from Greek mythology, tells the story of a musician who ventures into the underworld to rescue his beloved. The poignant love story has inspired countless iterations in poetry, art, and music, with one of the most famous being Benjamin Britten’s opera “Orpheus and Eurydice.” While this particular work of music is not strictly folk, its roots in traditional storytelling make it a powerful example of the close bond between mythology and folk music.

Another excellent example is the famous folk song “Scarborough Fair.” The melody and lyrics of this song come from an older traditional English ballad dating back to at least the 17th century. While its original version tells the tragic tale of a young man seeking redemption in a love triangle, it has links to both Celtic and English mythology. The song features themes of magic, prophecy, and even a hint of the supernatural.

Preserving Myths Through Song

One of the reasons why folk music is the perfect vehicle for preserving mythology is that it encourages the active engagement of the listener. Songs are deeply emotional and evocative, which cultivates connections between the audience and the story being told. When you listen to a song, it becomes more than just a story; it becomes a shared experience.

Moreover, through the art of storytelling, mythologies take on a new life. The underlying themes and lessons of the myths become more accessible and clearer through folk music. As we learn more about the characters and their stories, we gain a deeper understanding of our shared human experiences.

Folk Music’s Impact on Mythology

While the most obvious connection between folk music and mythology is the storytelling aspect, the reverse is also true: mythology has had a profound impact on the development of folk music. There are countless songs that draw from these ancient stories, and the mythical figures whose tales resonate across generations serve as enduring sources of inspiration.

Consider, for instance, the character of Robin Hood. This legendary English outlaw has inspired countless songs, ballads, and stories through the centuries. Robin Hood is, at his core, a romanticized hero, and his exploits have provided endless inspiration for folk musicians to sing of derring-do, loyalty, and justice.

Or take the case of the wily and cunning figure of Loki from Norse mythology. Loki has captured the imagination of a new generation of musicians, leading to everything from metal bands that celebrate his mischief-making to comedic takes on his adventures.

In both cases, these mythical characters have lived on thanks to the power of storytelling and, subsequently, the artistry of musicians that bring these characters to life.

In Conclusion: The Vibrant World of Folk Music and Mythology

As we’ve explored throughout this post, the world of folk music and mythology are intricately linked. This bond serves to preserve the ageless stories of our heritage while re-imagining them and making them contemporary and relatable.

Through the universal language of song, mythology continues to captivate and inspire us. As we continue to tell these stories, we strengthen the bonds among us, exploring our shared history and evolving together. And in that sense, mythology and folk music serve as beautiful reminders that our stories are, at their core, reflections of the human experience.
