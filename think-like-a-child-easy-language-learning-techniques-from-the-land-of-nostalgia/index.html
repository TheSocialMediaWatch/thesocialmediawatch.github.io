Think Like a Child: Easy Language Learning Techniques from the Land of Nostalgia


Childhood is a phase filled with innocence and boundless curiosity. It’s a time when we start learning languages naturally, without fear or judgment. As we grow up, learning a new language becomes a considerably daunting task. But why should the simplicity of language learning of our early years slip away as we age?

In this blog post, we’ll explore some life-hacks inspired by our childhood to make language learning easier, more effective, and fun!

1. Mimicking and Role-Playing

Any child knows how enthralling it is to imitate their parents, teachers, or cartoon characters. Bring that enthusiasm into language learning by mimicking native speakers. This technique helps internalize pronunciation, rhythm, and intonation without sounding mechanical. Plus, role-playing different scenarios with dialogues adds an interactive element to your learning journey.

2. Creating Visual Stories

Remember how you used to make up stories using your toys or crayon drawings? Storytelling is an excellent communicative tool in language acquisition. Draw scenes or use photographs, and create a story around them. Alternatively, write down your day’s events in both your native language and the new one you’re learning. Doing this spurs creativity, vocabulary acquisition and builds narrative fluency.

3. Singing and Rhymes

Singing nursery rhymes can be a great way to get comfortable with the rhythm and sound of new language. It doesn’t hurt that they’re typically melodic and catchy. As you become more adept, move on to songs you enjoy, focusing on favorite tunes in your target language. This not only helps with language absorption but also evokes positive emotions and improves your mood while you learn.

4. Join a Language Learning Club

Ever created your own club with friends? Carry on this spirit to learn a new language. Find a group of similarly motivated individuals or enroll in a language learning club. This dynamic encourages collaborative learning and provides opportunities to practice your new skills with peers, which can be more enjoyable and fulfilling than learning alone.

5. Sticking Post-Its in Your New Language

Consider your room or house as your personal ‘vocabulary playground’. To boost recognition and usage of crucial words, pick up some colorful post-it notes. For example, label objects around your living space in your target language. This makes your daily environment an interactive, language learning tool. Moreover, reaching for that mug now requires you to remember its translated term – practice!

6. Using Flashcards

Don’t overlook the old childhood standby - Flashcards! Create something appealing or humorous to accumulate less stress and build your vocabulary. Attempt varying techniques to study them, mix up your routine, and gamify your learning – each remember for challenging your mind in different ways.

7. Drawing and Art

Embrace childlike creativity by expressing your thoughts through drawings. When trying to remember a new word, sketch it out, conjugate verb charts into a colorful poster, construct creative sentences, and turn them into a comic strip. Artistic activities spark associative memory links.

8. Engage with Interactive Media and Games

Remember spending countless hours playing computer or board games? Rekindle the joy of gaming by engaging in learning apps or mobile games, aimed at teaching different languages. These resources are perfect for learning in stages and involve repetitive activities, reinforcement, and immediate feedback.

Wrap Up

The list above showcases just a few ways that childlike curiosity and play can be utilized to enhance your language learning experience. Letting go of intimidation and embracing the innocence behind early language acquisition can open new doors to learning. Whether it’s mimicking, storytelling, or drawing mind maps, techniques inspired by nostalgia lend an interactive edge to language learning. The goal here is to befriend the language you’re trying to learn, transforming the process into a joyous adventure rather than a grueling, endless task. Remember, language learning is a voyage - and ‘stepping into the shoes of a child’ might just reveal a world of untapped potential for fluent communication!
