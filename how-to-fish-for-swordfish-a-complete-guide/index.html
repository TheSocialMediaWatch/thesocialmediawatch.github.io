How to Fish for Swordfish: A Complete Guide


Swordfish, known for their long, flat bills and their strong, exhilarating fight, are one of the most sought-after species for both sport and commercial fishing. These majestic creatures can grow to impressive sizes and are a challenge to catch, making them a prized catch for many anglers. Whether you’re an experienced fisherman or a novice looking to try your hand at swordfishing, this complete guide will help you understand the techniques, gear, and tips needed to land a swordfish.

Understanding Swordfish: The Basics

Swordfish are large, predatory fish found in both temperate and tropical oceans worldwide. They are known for their long, sharp bills, which they use to slash at schools of fish, stunning or injuring their prey. This unique adaptation allows them to hunt effectively in deep waters, which is why they are often found at depths of 200 to 1,000 feet below the surface.

Due to their deep-water habitats and strong, fast swimming abilities, swordfish are not easy to catch. They are often found in offshore waters, far from shore, making it a challenging pursuit. However, their size and the thrill of the catch are what make swordfishing so rewarding.

Essential Gear for Swordfishing

Before embarking on a swordfishing trip, it’s crucial to have the right gear. Swordfish are powerful creatures, and without the appropriate equipment, it can be nearly impossible to land one. Below are some of the most essential tools you’ll need:

1. Rod and Reel

The rod and reel setup is critical for swordfishing, as you’ll need something robust enough to handle the weight and strength of a swordfish. A heavy-duty trolling rod with a length between 6 and 8 feet is ideal. The rod should be capable of withstanding the strain of long, drawn-out fights, and it should have a sensitive tip to detect subtle bites.

Pair your rod with a reel that has a high line capacity and a strong drag system. Many experienced swordfish anglers recommend using reels designed for big game fishing, such as a Shimano Tiagra or a Penn International reel. These reels should have a line capacity of at least 500 yards of heavy monofilament or braided line.

2. Line

Swordfish are known for their endurance and strength, so your line needs to be able to handle the fight. For swordfishing, you should use a strong, durable line. Many anglers use 80 to 130-pound monofilament or braided line. Monofilament is preferred for its stretch, which helps absorb the shock of a swordfish’s powerful runs.

Some anglers opt for a combination of monofilament for the main line and fluorocarbon leader material for the leader line. Fluorocarbon is nearly invisible underwater, providing an advantage when fishing in clear water, which is common in deep-sea swordfishing.

3. Hooks

Swordfish have large, tough mouths, so you’ll need a strong hook. Circle hooks are often used for swordfishing, as they tend to hook the fish in the corner of the mouth, reducing the chances of losing the fish during the fight. Choose a hook that is at least 9/0 to 12/0 in size, depending on the size of the swordfish you’re targeting.

4. Bait

Choosing the right bait is essential for attracting swordfish. Swordfish are carnivores and prefer live or dead bait that mimics the movement and size of their natural prey. Common bait choices include:

  • Live Squid: Swordfish are often attracted to the movement of live squid.
  • Live Mackerel or Baitfish: These fish are commonly used as bait due to their natural presence in the diet of swordfish.
  • Dead Bait: If live bait is not available, dead squid or mackerel can work well.

Using natural bait will often yield better results, but some anglers also use artificial lures to entice swordfish.

5. Deep Drop Equipment

Swordfish are typically found in deeper waters, often beyond 1,000 feet. To target them effectively, you’ll need to use deep-drop fishing techniques, which involve using specialized equipment designed to reach these depths. A deep-drop rig consists of a heavy sinker, which ensures that your bait gets to the bottom, and a fishing line strong enough to withstand the pressure of deep-sea conditions.

How to Find Swordfish

Swordfish are typically found in deep, offshore waters, often around underwater structures like drop-offs, canyons, and seamounts, where they hunt for smaller fish and squid. While they can be found at various depths, they are most commonly encountered at night, when they come up from the depths to hunt.

1. Night Fishing

Swordfish are nocturnal hunters. During the day, they remain at deep depths, where the water is cooler and where they can conserve energy. At night, they ascend to the upper layers of the ocean to hunt, making nighttime the best time to target them.

To increase your chances of success, plan your fishing trips around the full moon when swordfish are most likely to be feeding. The moonlight enhances visibility and makes it easier for swordfish to locate their prey. Anglers often fish between dusk and dawn, when swordfish are most active.

2. Using a Fish Finder

A fish finder is an essential tool for locating swordfish. These devices use sonar to detect the location of fish and underwater structures. While swordfish can be found at varying depths, having a fish finder will help you pinpoint areas where swordfish are likely to be located, increasing your chances of a successful catch.

Swordfishing Techniques

Swordfishing requires patience and precision. Here are some of the most common techniques used to catch swordfish:

1. Trolling for Swordfish

Trolling is one of the most effective methods for swordfishing. The idea is to use your boat to slowly drag bait through areas where swordfish are known to be feeding. While trolling, keep your bait at varying depths to attract swordfish from different levels of the water column.

Many anglers prefer using two rods when trolling. One rod is set to fish at a shallower depth, while the other is set to fish deeper. This increases the chances of encountering swordfish at different depths, especially considering that swordfish can move between depths depending on their hunting patterns.

2. Deep Dropping

Deep dropping is another technique commonly used for swordfishing. This method involves using a weighted rig to lower the bait to deep-water areas where swordfish are known to reside during the day. Once the bait reaches the desired depth, the angler waits patiently for a bite.

To deep drop successfully, it’s essential to have the right gear. As mentioned earlier, using a deep-drop rig with a strong, durable line and heavy sinker is crucial. Patience is key in deep dropping, as it may take time for swordfish to locate the bait.

3. Spot and Stalk

Spot and stalk is a more active approach to swordfishing. This method involves scanning the water’s surface for signs of swordfish activity, such as splashing or the sight of fish jumping. Once a swordfish is spotted, the angler positions the boat in the vicinity and drops bait into the water.

While this method is less commonly used, it can be effective in certain situations, particularly when swordfish are feeding closer to the surface.

How to Hook a Swordfish

Once you’ve located a swordfish and it takes the bait, the next step is to set the hook. The key to successfully hooking a swordfish is to let the fish take the bait fully before you make a move.

1. Wait for the Right Moment

Swordfish are often cautious feeders, so it’s important to wait for the fish to take the bait fully before setting the hook. If you set the hook too early, you risk pulling the bait away from the fish or causing it to spit the hook.

Many anglers wait until they feel a solid pull or see the rod tip bend before engaging the reel and setting the hook.

2. Setting the Hook

Once the swordfish has taken the bait and begins swimming away, it’s time to set the hook. Reel in any slack in the line and then give a sharp tug to drive the hook into the fish’s mouth. Swordfish have hard mouths, so setting the hook properly is crucial to ensure that it stays hooked throughout the fight.

The Fight: How to Land a Swordfish

Swordfish are known for their incredible strength and endurance. Once hooked, they can fight for hours, making for an intense and thrilling battle. Here’s how to handle the fight:

1. Stay Calm and Let the Fish Run

Swordfish often make long, powerful runs once hooked. While this can be intimidating, it’s important to stay calm and let the fish run without trying to reel in too quickly. Allowing the swordfish to expend energy on its runs will help tire it out, making it easier to reel in over time.

2. Use Your Rod and Reel Wisely

Throughout the fight, keep steady pressure on the fish, but don’t overexert yourself. Use the rod to maintain tension on the line while letting the reel do the work. When the swordfish runs, keep your rod tip down to avoid snapping the line. When it tires, use short, quick cranks to reel it in.

3. Be Patient

Swordfish can take hours to land, so patience is essential. If the fish is not showing signs of tiring, don’t rush the process. Let the swordfish wear itself out, and over time, it will begin to swim more slowly. Only then should you bring it close to the boat for the final struggle.

4. Gaffing and Boating the Swordfish

Once the swordfish is sufficiently exhausted, it’s time to gaff it. Gaffing is the process of using a large, sharp hook to secure the fish and bring it on board. Be careful when gaffing, as swordfish can still be dangerous, even after a long fight. Always ensure you have a good grip on the gaff and bring the fish aboard swiftly.

Conclusion

Catching a swordfish is a thrilling and challenging experience. With the right gear, techniques, and patience, you can increase your chances of landing this prized fish. Whether you’re trolling, deep dropping, or using spot and stalk methods, each approach has its merits. Understanding the behavior of swordfish, knowing when and where to fish, and mastering the techniques outlined in this guide will ensure your swordfishing adventure is a success.

Happy fishing!
