How to Fish with Spinners: Tips and Tricks


Fishing with spinners is a time-honored technique favored by anglers worldwide for its effectiveness and simplicity. Spinners are versatile lures that can attract a variety of fish species, making them a staple in many tackle boxes. Whether you’re a beginner eager to learn or an experienced fisherman looking to hone your skills, this guide will dive deep into the art of spinner fishing, sharing expert tips and tricks to boost your success on the water.

Understanding Spinners: What Makes Them Effective?

Before jumping into techniques, it’s essential to understand what a spinner lure is and why it works so well. Spinners consist of a metal blade that spins around a wire shaft when retrieved, creating vibrations and flashes that mimic the movement of small prey fish or insects. This combination of visual flash and vibration entices predatory fish, triggering their instinct to strike.

Spinners come in a variety of designs, including:

  • Inline Spinners: The blade spins around a central shaft.
  • Spinbait: A blade attached to a jighead, often with a skirt.
  • Buzzbaits: Surface spinners that create noise and disturbance atop the water.

The choice of spinner depends on your target species, water conditions, and fishing environment.

Selecting the Right Spinner for Your Adventure

The ideal spinner matches the size, shape, and color that appeal to the fish you are targeting and complements the water clarity and light conditions.

Size and Weight

  • Smaller spinners, such as in the 1/16 to 1/8-ounce range, are perfect for trout, panfish, and bass in clear water or when fish are finicky.
  • Medium-sized spinners (1/4 to 3/8 oz) are versatile and work well for bass, walleye, and pike.
  • Larger spinners, upwards of 1/2 oz, are suitable for murky water and bigger predators like muskie or larger pike.

Additionally, water depth and current influence weight choice. Heavier spinners sink faster, allowing you to fish deeper zones or work against strong currents.

Blade Design and Color

Blades come in various shapes, including Colorado, Willowleaf, and Indiana styles:

  • Colorado Blades: Round and produce strong vibrations, ideal for murky water or low light.
  • Willowleaf Blades: Slim and elongated, generate less vibration but more flash, great for clearer water and when fish are feeding visually.
  • Indiana Blades: A balance between Colorado and Willowleaf, offering moderate flash and vibration.

Color selection can mimic the local prey or stand out to fish. Bright, reflective finishes work well in low-light or stained water, while natural, subtle patterns suit clear conditions.

Gear Setup for Spinner Fishing

Having the right gear amplifies your chances of success.

Rod and Reel

A medium to medium-light spinning rod with fast action works best with spinners, giving you the sensitivity to detect subtle bites and the backbone to set the hook firmly. Lengths of 6 to 7 feet offer excellent casting distance and control.

Pair this with a spinning reel sized 2500 to 3000 for most freshwater spinner fishing scenarios. Match your reel’s drag settings to reduce line breakage during aggressive strikes.

Line Selection

Monofilament line around 6-12 lb test is common for spinner fishing, offering enough abrasion resistance with suitable stretch to protect the fish during the fight. Fluorocarbon is a great option for clear water due to its low visibility and decent abrasion resistance. Braided lines deliver excellent sensitivity and casting distance, but using a fluorocarbon leader can reduce visibility near the lure.

Proven Spinner Fishing Techniques

Steady Retrieve

The most straightforward approach. Cast your spinner out and reel it steadily, allowing the blade to rotate continuously. This creates consistent flash and vibration that attract fish.

Experiment with retrieval speed. Sometimes slower, subtle presentations trigger bites, while other times a faster retrieve entices aggressive strikes.

Stop-and-Go Retrieve

Mimic prey movement by pausing intermittently during the retrieve. Reel for a few seconds, then pause and let the spinner fall, then retrieve again. This start-stop action can provoke reaction strikes from fish following your lure.

Fluttering Descent

Cast your spinner and let it sink with minimal or no retrieve, allowing it to flutter as it drops. Spin blades will continue to rotate, enticing fish holding at mid-water or near the bottom to strike as the lure moves naturally downward.

Bottom Bouncing

For bottom-feeding species like bass or walleye, bounce your spinner off the lake or riverbed. Use a slightly heavier spinner and a slow retrieve, letting it tap rocks or gravel. The vibrations coupled with erratic movement can tempt fish lurking nearby.

Targeting Structure and Cover

Fish often lurk near submerged structures such as fallen trees, rocks, docks, or weed lines. Cast your spinner close to these areas and retrieve past them to provoke strikes from fish hiding in ambush.

Optimizing Spinner Use by Species

Bass

Bass are aggressive and respond well to spinners, especially in spring and fall. Use smaller-sized spinners with Willowleaf blades in clear water and switch to Colorado blades and brighter colors in stained water or low light. Employ stop-and-go or bottom-bouncing retrieves to entice strikes.

Trout

Smaller inline spinners in 1/16 or 1/8-ounce are effective for trout. Focus on streams and rivers with clear water, using light line and small-bladed spinners that flash subtly. Cast upstream and let your spinner drift naturally with or slightly against the current.

Pike and Muskie

Use large, heavy spinners with robust wire frames and strong hooks to handle toothy predators. Bright colors and larger blades work well in murky water. Maintain a steady retrieve or stop-and-go near weed beds and reeds.

Walleye

Walleye respond to medium-sized spinners with less flashy but more vibration-heavy blades like Colorado styles. Fish low light hours or deep waters with a slow to moderate retrieve, occasionally pausing the lure.

Seasonal Strategies for Spinner Fishing

  • Spring: As fish become more active after winter, spinners are excellent for drawing out territorial strikes. Focus on shallow water near spawning areas.
  • Summer: Fish may stay deeper or in shaded areas. Use heavier spinners and slow retrieves to reach them.
  • Fall: Fish feed aggressively before winter. Larger spinners and faster retrieves can trigger competitive strikes.
  • Winter: Spinner fishing is less effective in cold water as fish slow down, but using small, subtle blades with ultra-slow retrieves can entice hesitant bites.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Fishing with Spinners

  • Overpowering the Presentation: A spinner that’s too heavy or spun too fast can deter fish rather than attract them.
  • Using the Wrong Blade: Selecting the wrong blade shape or size for water conditions limits effectiveness.
  • Neglecting Gear Match: Using heavy rod setups or thick line reduces sensitivity and makes it harder to detect strikes.
  • Ignoring Fish Behavior: Not adjusting retrieval speed or color based on fish mood and water conditions is a missed opportunity.

Maintenance Tips for Your Spinners

Spinners are durable but require routine care:

  • Rinse after use, especially in saltwater environments.
  • Sharpen hooks regularly or replace them to maintain hooking power.
  • Check blade rotation; clean and lubricate with light oil if it becomes sluggish.
  • Store spinners in a dry, organized tackle box to prevent rusting and tangling.

Advanced Spinner Fishing Tips

  • Combine Spinners with Soft Plastics: Attach a small grub or trailer to increase profile and movement.
  • Use Split Shot Weights: Add weight above the spinner to fish deeper zones without changing lures.
  • Experiment with Line Retrieval Angles: Changing your position relative to the lure improves presentation and hook-up ratios.

Conclusion: Mastering Spinner Fishing for Consistent Success

Fishing with spinners blends simplicity with strategic nuance. By understanding spinner types, tuning your tackle setup, and practicing various retrieval techniques, you’ll unlock many opportunities to catch fish across species and environments. Whether casting on tranquil lakes, flowing rivers, or weed-choked bays, spinners remain a trusted companion for anglers seeking excitement and success.

Approach each fishing session with curiosity, adapt to conditions, and keep fine-tuning your skills. Spinner fishing is as much about reading the water and fish as it is about the lure itself. With patience and practice, you’ll soon enjoy the exhilarating strikes and rewarding catches that spinner fishing delivers. Tight lines!
