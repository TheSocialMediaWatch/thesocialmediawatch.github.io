The Best Fantasy Novels for Fans of Historical Fiction


Historical fiction and fantasy have long captivated readers with their ability to transport us beyond the mundane, whether to the realms of ages past or to entirely imagined worlds. While historical fiction freezes time, drawing us into poignant tales of bygone eras, fantasy unleashes creativity, reshaping history into something wondrous and new. For those who relish the intricacy of historical narratives but yearn for a touch of the fantastical, the fusion of these two genres offers an unparalleled reading experience.

The intersection of history and fantasy is a literary playground. Here, authors weave rich tapestries of magic and mythology into narratives inspired by real periods, cultures, and conflicts. From reimagined medieval kingdoms to magical interpretations of ancient empires, these novels evoke not only the nostalgia of familiar historical settings but also the exhilaration of limitless imagination. If you’re a fan of historical fiction but also crave the thrill of the extraordinary, here are some of the best fantasy novels to add to your reading list.

1. “Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell” by Susanna Clarke

Set in England during the Napoleonic Wars, Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell is a masterful blend of historical fiction and fantasy. Clarke recreates Regency-era England with a historian’s precision, from its political intrigues to its societal norms. But layered on top of this historical canvas is an exploration of magic—a force both revered and lost—in British history. The book delves deep into the lore of a world where magicians once flourished and now participate in shaping events of momentous importance.

Clarke’s use of footnotes enhances the historical depth and lends the narrative an academic touch, reinforcing its historical grounding even as it delves into fantastical domains. For readers drawn to tales of war, diplomacy, and slow-building conflict but eager for the enchantment of magical possibilities, this novel is an unforgettable experience.

2. “The Priory of the Orange Tree” by Samantha Shannon

Samantha Shannon’s sprawling epic combines elements of medieval Europe and Eastern lore with a fantasy twist. This standalone novel follows a dragon-fueled history spanning continents, cultures, and ideologies. Shannon crafts a richly imagined world reminiscent of classic historical conflicts, layering it with intricate political alliances, religious tensions, and gender dynamics.

While steeped in a fantastical premise—ancient dragonkind awakening—the novel’s characters grapple with very human themes: loyalty, ambition, and the lasting consequences of history. Shannon’s meticulous attention to detailing rival queens, fierce warriors, and daring sorceries makes this book perfect for historical fiction enthusiasts who are ready to embrace sweeping fantasy narratives tied to imagined histories.

3. “The Daughters of Izdihar” by Hadeer Elsbai

Inspired by 1920s Egypt, The Daughters of Izdihar interweaves feminist narratives with magical realism in a historical fantasy that’s as poignant as it is empowering. Elsbai conjures a fictional kingdom on the brink of societal revolution, where elemental magic exists alongside suffrage movements and political upheavals. Readers who enjoy historical nuances—such as women fighting for their voices and marginalized communities navigating oppressive systems—will resonate with the interplay between history and fantasy in this novel.

The author’s attention to cultural details, from locations to social hierarchies, creates an immersive experience. Whether driven by fiery sorcery or human resilience, the characters in Elsbai’s narrative feel grounded in reality even as the magical world blooms around them.

4. “The Golem and the Jinni” by Helene Wecker

Set in turn-of-the-century New York City, The Golem and the Jinni is both magical and deeply historical. Helene Wecker’s writing brings to life the immigrant experience during a pivotal era in American history while seamlessly weaving in folkloric magic. Chava, a golem, and Ahmad, a jinni from Syrian myths, exist both as fantastical creatures and cultural metaphors, representing outsiders struggling to navigate a world of human desires, religions, and prejudices.

While this story is born of fantasy, its historical framework—the bustling Jewish and Syrian neighborhoods of old Manhattan—grounds it firmly in reality. Wecker’s portrayal of community, migration, and innovation will satisfy any historical fiction fan eager to explore a multicultural narrative imbued with the otherworldly.

5. “A Brightness Long Ago” by Guy Gavriel Kay

Few authors blend history and fantasy as seamlessly as Guy Gavriel Kay. A Brightness Long Ago is set in a world closely modeled after Renaissance Italy, but with light fantastical touches that enrich the storytelling. Kay’s novel paints a deeply emotional portrait of political intrigue, ambition, and the fleeting moments that shape lives intertwined with history. Readers who enjoy the grit and glamour of Renaissance-era society will find themselves immersed in Kay’s nuanced characters and the complex weight of their choices.

The magic in Kay’s books is subdued, appearing more as echoes from the past or whispers of prophecy than overt displays of power, making his work perfect for readers who favor historical fiction layered with subtle fantasy.

6. “The Bear and the Nightingale” by Katherine Arden

Drawing inspiration from Russian folklore and medieval history, Katherine Arden crafts an atmospheric story in The Bear and the Nightingale. Set in a snow-laden village on the edge of the wilderness, this novel combines historical realism with Slavic mythology to stunning effect. Its protagonist, Vasilisa, belongs to an era where tradition and superstition rule—but as ancient spirits and fantasy elements come to life, she must navigate both human and otherworldly dangers.

Arden enchants with her depiction of Russia’s feudal society, and fans of historical fiction will appreciate the authenticity of her portrayal of religion, family dynamics, and the harshness of rural life. Coupled with the novel’s magical undercurrents, it becomes a story of resilience and transformation, perfect for readers drawn to darker historical fantasies.

7. “The City of Brass” by S.A. Chakraborty

For those charmed by history’s golden ages, The City of Brass transports readers to a fantastical version of the Islamic world, with deep roots in historical culture and mythology. The story follows Nahri, a con artist who accidentally summons a djinn and becomes entangled in the politics of an ancient magical city. Chakraborty’s richly drawn setting reflects the opulence of real historical cities like Baghdad or Cairo, combining architecture, traditions, and societal divides with mythical intrigue.

The balance of historical depth and vibrant fantasy makes this trilogy irresistible for fans of historical fiction seeking to expand their horizons into the world of magic and mythical creatures.

8. “The Winter Sea” by Susanna Kearsley

While Susanna Kearsley’s works often fall closer to historical fiction, The Winter Sea edges into fantasy through its exploration of ancestral memory and past lives. Set in the Jacobite era of 18th-century Scotland, this haunting tale intertwines a modern writer’s experiences with vivid flashbacks to a turbulent time in history. Kearsley uses the backdrop of historical events while adding surreal layers of connection between present and past.

Readers who enjoy richly researched historical fiction may find themselves surprised by the gentle fantasy woven throughout this story, offering a unique perspective on time, fate, and the legacies we inherit.

Why the Blend of History and Fantasy Works Perfectly

Fantasy novels rooted in historical settings strike a perfect balance between escapism and familiarity. The historical context offers a sense of grounding—giving readers rich settings they can relate to or recognize—while the fantastical elements elevate the narrative, breaking free from real-world constraints. This synergy allows authors to explore timeless themes like power, identity, and resilience while speculating on “what if?” scenarios that transcend fact.

These novels also appeal to readers who enjoy both genres but seek more variety in their storytelling. Histories can feel static, locked into established narratives; fantasy loosens those constraints, allowing authors to reinterpret well-known time periods with vivid imagination. The result is an irresistible fusion of the meticulous detail of history and the boundless creativity of fantasy.

Final Thoughts

For fans of historical fiction ready to embrace the extraordinary, these fantasy novels offer a gateway to worlds where history meets magic. Each of the titles mentioned merges the rigor of historical storytelling with the enchantment of fantasy, crafting tales that are as thought-provoking as they are fantastical. Whether you’re captivated by medieval Europe, ancient lore, or cultural revolutions, these books promise unforgettable adventures through the past and the unreal.

So, if you’re looking to enrich your reading list with stories that balance the familiar with the fantastic, dive into one of these exceptional novels. History is just the beginning—let these authors show you where imagination can take it next.
