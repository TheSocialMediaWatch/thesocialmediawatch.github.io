The Unpredictable Power of a Change in Perspective in Comics


When we look at a comic strip, we often think of it as a simple form of art, aimed at making us laugh, think, or feel deep emotions. However, comics have an incredible power that transcends their appearances. They can radically alter our perspectives on the world around us, and we often fail to recognize this immense power.

In comics, the change in perspective can happen on multiple levels, from the character’s internal thought bubbles to the artist’s choice in depicting the scenes and using symbolism. Let’s explore how these changes in perspective have transformed comic stories.

The Art of Relatability

One of the primary functions of comics is to provide relatability to the reader. By presenting characters who undergo internal and external struggles, we, as readers, get lost in their stories to bond with their journeys. The intricacies of their personality, emotions, and thoughts become a way to showcase a new perspective that can change our view on life.

Brian Fries’ “The Thing” is a perfect example of this concept. The comic centers around the main character’s concept of self as an alien, with an appearance that isolates him from society. When his outer appearance changes, his outlook on life becomes increasingly more optimistic as a result. This change of inner perspective leads the reader to reflect on their own perceptions of identity and the impact of first impressions.

The power of relatability forces us to perceive the world differently as we try to place ourselves in the characters’ situations. This change of perspective can result in heightened empathy, social awareness, and understanding.

Using Symbolism For Effect

Symbolism in comics is a powerful storyteller’s tool. The use of objects, colors, and images adds depth to the narrative, altering the reader’s perspective on the story.

One prime example is Alan Moore’s “Watchmen,” a comic concerned with themes of social anxiety and politicization even during seemingly mundane moments. A single, iconic scene of a couple dancing in a60s-style nightclub, accompanied by the perfect symbolism of a actual smiley face button being worn by the man, made its way into the historical consciousness. That moment symbolizes the fateful end of their era, a time of optimism now marred by the violence and darkness of modern times.

This change in perspective shapes our understanding of the story, forcing the reader to dig into the layers of symbolism and hidden meanings to truly grasp the narrative.

The Shift of Power Dynamics

Comics can also reshape power dynamics as they explore gender, race, class, and other forms of inequality.

“Fun Home,” by Alison Bechdel, is a touching, semi-autobiographical tale about the author’s relationship with her father. The story is a masterclass in how comics change perspective as this work undermines patriarchal power dynamics. By retelling the tale from multiple perspectives and challenging the traditional father-daughter relationship, Bechdel unravels decades-old mysteries and allows readers to see the world from different angles.

These changes in perspective challenge our understanding of power dynamics, exposing the flaws in societal norms and affirming our need for critical thinking and understanding.

A Flight Into New Worlds

Comics are extremely versatile, enabling readers to explore new worlds and ideas. By shifting perspectives, they help readers to develop a profound understanding of the world around them and a heightened sense of empathy for others.

The unpredictable power of a change in perspective in comics allows us to question everything we think we know about life, love, and our existence. So, the next time you flip through a comic book, remember that this small form of art is capable of changing perspectives in ways bigger medium may not be able to, and grasp the full potential it holds.
