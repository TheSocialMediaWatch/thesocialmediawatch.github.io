The Ethics of Friendship: Philosophical Perspectives on Relationships


Friendship has long been a fascinating subject for philosophers and theologians alike. It is an essential human bond that transcends boundaries and brings together individuals with shared values and ideals. This post delves into the philosophical perspectives of friendship, exploring its role in our lives, the moral duties it imposes, and the impact it has on our relationships.

Friendship, as Aristotle proposed, is one of the most vital components of a flourishing and well-rounded life. He believed that friendships foster upstanding character traits, such as temperance, courage, and wisdom. In his view, friendships should be built on mutual virtue and respect, with both parties actively seeking the good for each other.

Virtue ethicists argue that moral choices revolve around developing and nurturing virtues, such as honesty, empathy, and loyalty. In friendships, these virtues often come into play. Trustworthy friends demonstrate these virtues by being honest, supporting one another, and possessing a genuine concern for the well-being of the other.

Contrary to popular belief, friendship isn’t always reciprocal. In his book The Art of Friendship, British author and philosopher C.S. Lewis touches upon the idea that friends may have varying levels of giving and receiving. However, a true friend recognizes the value of such an unbalanced relationship and continues to invest in their friendship. Lewis highlights that the merit of friendship is not confined to actions that result in mutual psychological satisfaction but rather lies in nurturing bonds for the sake of the individuals involved.

The modern shift in thinking about friendships has drawn attention to the idea of friendships beyond one’s social circles, nations, and even species. Michael Dummett, a philosopher, and Peter Singer, an ethicist, have both championed the notion of universal friendship. Singer takes it a step further, emphasizing that friendships that do not acknowledge obligations to others, such as animals, lack moral coherence. However, Singer’s view is met with resistance, as genuine friendships advocate for an attachment to those we know.

Viewing relationships from an existentialist perspective, friendships acquire meaning and significance through personal choices and experiences. In particular, the dialogue of a friendship defines its essence. Martin Buber, a Jewish philosopher, emphasized that true friends must share a sense of dialogue, openness, and understanding. Through such dialogue, friends bridge the gap between their subjective worlds, enriching their experiences and solidifying their bond.

As the world evolves, so do our friendships. In today’s digital age, friendships take on new forms, transcending geographical boundaries and offering new paths for human connection. However, social media often portrays a sanitized, idealized version of friendships, which can diminish the depth and rich context that genuine friendships offer.

In the complexities of life, friendships serve as a unique and powerful force. From Aristotle’s perspective on virtue to the existentialist’s emphasis on dialogue, philosophical insights elucidate the importance of friendships in our lives and the moral implications associated with our choices in fostering these bonds.

Whether it’s a playmate from childhood, a mentor through tough times, or a confidant with whom you share your deepest recesses, friendships offer a profound representation of our human capacity for connection and understanding. It is vital to recognize and nurture these bonds, as they offer a richness and value that extends far beyond superficial connections, enriching our lives and shaping our character.
