The Most Underrated Fantasy Books You Should Read


Fantasy is a genre that often transports readers to imaginative worlds filled with magic, mythical creatures, and epic battles between forces of good and evil. While blockbuster series like The Lord of the Rings, Harry Potter, and A Song of Ice and Fire dominate the mainstream conversation, there are countless hidden gems in the fantasy genre that often go unnoticed. If you’re a fan of enchanting tales and are eager to discover stories that deserve as much attention, here’s a list of the most underrated fantasy books you should read.

1. The Kingdom of Liars by Nick Martell

In the crowded fantasy genre, The Kingdom of Liars stands out as a masterpiece of storytelling that balances intrigue, mystery, and emotional depth. Set in a world where memory is currency, Nick Martell spins an intricately woven tale centered around Michael Kingman, a young man accused of betraying his kingdom. With unreliable narrators, unexpected twists, and a sprawling plot, this book is perfect for readers who crave complexity and morally ambiguous characters.

What makes The Kingdom of Liars so compelling is its layered world-building. The city of Hollow—a place where fractured moons hang in the sky and magic exacts a cost—feels both fresh and immersive. Martell’s ability to combine gripping political drama with emotional nuance makes this story deserving of far more readers.

2. The Forgetting Moon by Brian Lee Durfee

If you’re longing for an epic fantasy saga that doesn’t shy away from its dark and gritty undertones, The Forgetting Moon is the book you’ve been searching for. The first book in The Five Warrior Angels series, it plunges the reader into a world on the brink of chaos. Durfee crafts a richly detailed tapestry of knights, assassins, gods, and myths, offering a reading experience that is both thrilling and emotionally satisfying.

Durfee’s storytelling is unapologetically brutal, yet it shines with complex characters and multi-layered conflicts. His ability to weave religion, regional politics, and fractured alliances into the narrative brings depth to the unfolding drama. The series hasn’t received the attention it deserves, but those who give The Forgetting Moon a chance will find themselves immersed in one of the finest examples of epic fantasy.

3. The Stone and the Void by Andrew Rowe

Andrew Rowe is perhaps best known for his Arcane Ascension series, but his standalone masterpiece, The Stone and the Void, deserves recognition. Combining elements of traditional high fantasy with game-inspired mechanics, Rowe creates a highly inventive world that will appeal to anyone who loves the idea of magic systems grounded in logic and rules.

The story dives deep into concepts like ethical dilemmas of power, the burden of responsibility, and the cost of perfection. With vivid character development and a compelling plot that keeps readers guessing, this book has all the makings of a cult classic. Despite its brilliance, The Stone and the Void often gets overshadowed by Rowe’s other works—a fate that should change.

4. The Queen of Tearling by Erika Johansen

Erika Johansen’s The Queen of Tearling is an intriguing fusion of fantasy and dystopia. The novel follows Kelsea Raleigh Glynn, a young woman stepping up to become queen of a beleaguered kingdom plagued by corruption, greed, and looming threats from neighboring nations. What sets the book apart is Johansen’s skillful exploration of social inequality, the burdens of leadership, and the moral compromises required for survival.

Full of political intrigue and gripping tension, The Queen of Tearling is as much about character growth as it is about dramatic storytelling. Kelsea’s journey is a refreshing subversion of the “chosen one” trope, with the strong, flawed protagonist finding her own way amid adversity. Though the trilogy has gained a loyal following, it remains woefully underrated, especially considering it offers rich, thought-provoking themes alongside riveting action.

5. Tigana by Guy Gavriel Kay

Guy Gavriel Kay is a master of blending historical inspiration with fantasy elements, and Tigana is perhaps his finest achievement. This standalone novel tells a poignant tale of a country erased from existence—not just geographically, but from memory itself. A group of citizens fights to reclaim their heritage in a world where memory and identity are fragile constructs.

Kay’s prose is achingly beautiful, his characters painfully human, and his exploration of loss, loyalty, and perseverance offers a lens through which readers can better understand their own lives. Though Kay is renowned among dedicated fantasy readers, Tigana remains one of his more underappreciated works, striking a unique chord for anyone willing to immerse themselves in its lyrical storytelling.

6. Blackwing by Ed McDonald

Ed McDonald’s Blackwing introduces readers to the Grimdark fantasy of the Raven’s Mark series, beginning with this riveting first installment. Set in a harsh and unforgiving world scarred by a magical war, the story follows bounty hunter Ryhalt Galharrow as he uncovers secrets that could threaten the fragile alliance holding civilization together.

What makes Blackwing truly stand out is its atmospheric setting—The Misery, a hostile wasteland filled with mutated monstrosities—is as much a character as the people inhabiting the story. The book also excels in blending fast-paced action with philosophical introspection, asking questions about survival, loyalty, and trust. Fans of gritty worlds similar to Glen Cook’s The Black Company will find themselves hooked by McDonald’s imaginative and razor-sharp writing.

7. The Traitor Baru Cormorant by Seth Dickinson

Seth Dickinson’s The Traitor Baru Cormorant is a riveting tale of empire, rebellion, and revenge. This intricate political fantasy introduces Baru, a brilliant young woman determined to dismantle the oppressive power that colonized her homeland—but, as the title implies, her path is anything but straightforward.

Dickinson’s strength lies in his ability to create morally complex characters and situations, forcing readers to grapple with questions of ethics and sacrifice. The detailed political maneuvering, combined with the rich cultural backdrop, makes this novel a standout in a genre often dominated by action-driven tales. Though highly praised by its readers, this book deserves a broader audience to appreciate its nuanced storytelling and innovative approach to fantasy.

8. A Brightness Long Ago by Guy Gavriel Kay

Another entry by Kay, A Brightness Long Ago is a rich and poignant standalone novel that showcases the fragility of human ambition. Taking inspiration from Renaissance Italy, the book teems with political rivalry, betrayal, and fleeting moments of humanity. If epic battles and fantastical creatures are not what you seek, this story offers a quieter, yet no less compelling, exploration of choices and their ripple effects.

Kay’s careful attention to historical context and character depth makes each page resonate—his ability to ground the fantastical within reality is unparalleled. It’s a novel that blurs the lines between fantasy and historical fiction, and for those willing to explore its nuances, A Brightness Long Ago promises unforgettable moments.

9. The Rook by Daniel O’Malley

Daniel O’Malley’s The Rook is an urban fantasy unlike any other. The story kicks off with Myfanwy Thomas waking up in a park surrounded by corpses—and no memory of how she got there. As she navigates the supernatural bureaucracy of the Checquy, a secretive organization protecting Britain from paranormal threats, Myfanwy discovers secrets about her own abilities—and her past.

With a sharp wit and a narrative that seamlessly combines humor, action, and suspense, The Rook is a refreshing departure from traditional fantasy tropes. Its originality, paired with distinctive world-building and engaging characters, makes it a must-read for fans looking for something truly unique.

Fantasy as a genre offers boundless possibilities for exploration, yet many incredible works remain hidden beneath the towering shadow of mainstream blockbusters. The books listed above deserve a place on your shelf—not simply because they haven’t achieved the widespread fame of their peers, but because of their superb stories that redefine what fantasy can offer. Dive into these magical worlds, and you may just discover your new favorite book.
