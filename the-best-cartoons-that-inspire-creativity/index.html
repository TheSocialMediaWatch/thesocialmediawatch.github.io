The Best Cartoons That Inspire Creativity


There’s something about an animated world that makes anything possible. Maybe it’s the fluidity of reality. Maybe it’s the ability to shape, paint, and bend dimensions. Or maybe it’s simply that energetic spark—the irrepressible urge to make, build, or imagine something new—that cartoons ignite in people of all ages. Whether you’re brushing up on fresh ideas, need a jolt of inspiration, or just miss the thrill of possibility, the right cartoon can set off fireworks in your creative brain.

Let’s jump into a playful gallery of shows and films, spanning decades and styles, that punch boldly above their genre. These are the cartoons with stories, worlds, and characters guaranteed to inspire inventors, artists, writers, daydreamers, and anyone craving a burst of pure originality.

1. Adventure Time: The Sweet Absurdity of Imagination

The Land of Ooo doesn’t just tickle your funny bone; it shoves you headlong into surreal color-splotched landscapes where anything goes. Creators Pendleton Ward and his team let ideas flow as freely as the rainbow rivers in Ooo. There are sentient candy people, haunted swords, musical luminaries, and heartbreaks that worm their way deep.

The series’ world-building is a masterclass in creative risk-taking. Every episode experiments with visual styles, storytelling shapes, and genre-bending twists. Characters like Jake the Dog, who can morph into any shape, and Princess Bubblegum, the ultimate mad scientist (in bubblegum form), absolutely dare you to see the world sideways.

If you want to unlock the part of your brain that tosses “what ifs” like fireworks, Adventure Time is a template. It’s not just an animated series—it’s a reminder that art thrives farthest from the borders of logic.

2. Steven Universe: Empathy Is Its Own Superpower

Look past the glowing gemstone adventures and musical showdowns, and you’ll find Steven Universe is a gentle, radical invitation to create with your whole heart.

Rebecca Sugar’s creation is saturated with empathy, identity, and radical acceptance—an underlying power that makes any creator feel seen. The environments are rendered with painterly pastels, while characters grapple with complex relationships and self-concept. Each “fusion” (where Gems combine identities and powers) is outwardly a magical gimmick and inwardly a lesson in teamwork, trust, and opening up.

The storytelling is a mosaic of themes: from sci-fi drama to slice-of-life comedy to full blow-out musical. There are extended art sequences, inventive perspectives, and songs that become earworms for their emotional core, not just their catchiness. For storytellers, animators, songwriters, or anyone hoping to express big feelings, Steven Universe is a beacon.

3. Over the Garden Wall: Folk Tales and Surreal Whimsy

Sometimes, the best creativity emerges when you dare to walk the path less traveled. Over the Garden Wall is a short, autumnal epic on the power of the unknown. Cartoon Network’s mini-series, created by Patrick McHale, weaves together American folklore, fairy tales, and timeless myth.

Visually, each frame feels painted in cozy, melancholy storybook hues. The journey of lost brothers Wirt and Greg, assisted by a talking bird and pursued by a shadowy threat, radiates both warmth and unease. The odd, meandering structure encourages pausing, reflecting, and getting lost in side stories. If you’re looking to emulate the unpredictable logic of dreams or the lush ambiguity of old tales, this is your spark.

Storytellers and artists can drink in Over the Garden Wall’s details: trompe-l’oeil backgrounds, hauntingly simple songs, and recurring symbols. It’s a gentle nudge to take creative detours and trust in strangeness.

4. Gravity Falls: Secret Codes and Conspiracy Puzzles

Creativity flourishes in curiosity, and Gravity Falls is the ultimate animated ode to curiosity-driven discovery. Alex Hirsch’s creation is overflowing with cryptic codes, runes, hidden gags, and clever puzzles inviting viewers to join the mystery-hunt.

Every corner of Gravity Falls brims with in-jokes, blink-and-you’ll-miss-it foreshadowing, and lovingly outlandish creatures. The show’s greatest feat, though, is showing that questions are the heart of creative life. Mabel’s boundless crafting (of sweaters, parties, and friendships) and Dipper’s obsessive research (into secret societies and supernatural entities) model two complementary approaches to making something out of nothing: wild play and rigorous inquiry.

For aspiring writers and world-builders, Gravity Falls is a cult favorite, with dedicated communities puzzling over hidden messages long after the credits roll. It’s a show that doesn’t just reward creativity; it actively invites you to participate.

5. Studio Ghibli Films: Limitless Oil Paint, Limitless Possibilities

If there were an Oscar for Most Beautiful Daydreams, Studio Ghibli would be the repeat champion. Hayao Miyazaki and Isao Takahata have collectively crafted a universe where the fantastic and the mundane blend seamlessly. Whether you’re journeying with Chihiro through a spirit bathhouse, riding a catbus, or tagging along with a grumpy witch-in-training, Ghibli films channel the core of pure creative energy.

The attention to littleness—a pot boiling, laundry drying, dust spiraling in sunbeams—grounds even the wildest flights of magical realism. The visuals, impossibly lush and hand-drawn, celebrate imperfection and detail in a way that digital aesthetics rarely capture. For illustrators, animators, and dreamers, Ghibli’s animation is a guidebook to world-building that feels immersive, lived-in, and endlessly possible.

But the films’ greatest achievement? Normalizing wonder. They suggest that curiosity isn’t just for children, and that sometimes sitting quietly with your thoughts is the beginning of a creative journey.

6. The Amazing World of Gumball: Absurdity as Art Form

Gumball Watterson’s world is literally a collage—2D animation crash-landing into CGI, puppetry, live-action, and stop-motion backdrops. The art direction alone is a whirlwind tour of what cartoons can do when all rules are thrown out.

But it’s in the writing that Gumball pushes boundaries. Episodes veer from video game parodies to existential comedy to fourth-wall breaking havoc. Each bit of visual inventiveness primes your mind to see that genres are tools, not walls; stories and jokes become even sharper when packed into a new shape.

For creators stuck on format or style, Gumball is a permission slip to try anything. It’s a show that leaps out of the box so frequently, the box simply can’t contain it anymore.

7. Avatar: The Last Airbender: The Art of World-Building

If you want a masterclass in harmonizing world-building, character arcs, and visual storytelling, look no further. Tapping into East Asian influences, martial arts, and spiritual traditions, Avatar: The Last Airbender creates a sweeping mythos that feels as old as the mountains.

The element “bending” itself is a creative lens—each type with its own choreography, rules, and limitations. Backgrounds glow with meticulously researched architectural and cultural detail. Characters mature, struggle, and change in ways that make even the most fantastical moment resonate deeply.

Whether you’re a plotter or a pantser, an illustrator or a cosplayer, Avatar’s dedication to consistent, ever-surprising creativity offers endless inspiration.

8. Foster’s Home for Imaginary Friends: Embracing the Wild Idea

Take every childhood imaginary friend and give them a physical form; suddenly, you have a madhouse teeming with personalities and impossibilities. Foster’s Home for Imaginary Friends, shaped by Craig McCracken, is an exuberant call to nurture even the most outlandish ideas.

The design language keeps reinventing itself: no two imaginary friends are alike. Some are towering blobs, others tiny and sharp-toothed, all visually echoing the sheer wildness of childhood invention. The stories—equal parts slapstick, heart, and chaos—remind everyone that good ideas need space to run wild, bump into walls, and maybe knock over a few vases.

For teachers, parents, or creators hoping to unstifle their inner child, Foster’s says: make room for the ridiculous, because that’s where real creativity is born.

9. Samurai Jack: Minimalism and Maximalism Collide

Genndy Tartakovsky’s Samurai Jack strips storytelling down to its core, then undoes the seams again with outrageous visual flourish. Minimal dialogue meets maximal action; flat, graphic design collides with epic, cinematic vistas. The show’s confidence is a visual artist’s manifesto: let the tone, palette, and shape tell the story.

Episodes range from nearly silent meditations to bombastic battles bursting with color and motion. Unconventional narrative structure? Absolutely. No pressure to spoon-feed exposition? None at all.

Jack’s journey is as psychological as it is physical—a lonely odyssey where past, present, and future merge. For anyone who wants the motivation to take risks—to pare back, trust the medium, and find new storytelling shapes—Samurai Jack is an unmissable blueprint.

10. Animaniacs: Meta Mayhem and Musical Mischief

If there’s a cartoon that proves intelligence and absurdity don’t just coexist but thrive together, it’s Animaniacs. The Warner siblings bounce through medleys of puns, parodies, and pop culture lampoonery that continually wink at the audience. Segments like “Yakko’s World” or “Good Idea, Bad Idea” are masterclasses in writing that’s both tightly structured and radically freewheeling.

There’s musical prowess too—many songs rival Broadway showtunes for cleverness and energy. The show champions meta-humor and intertextuality, blending classic cartoon anarchy with whip-smart satire.

For writers, comedians, musicians, or anyone bored of coloring within the lines, Animaniacs is a fuel injection of zany creativity—proof that breaking the fourth wall or remixing old tropes doesn’t just entertain, it reinvents.

11. Craig of the Creek: Everyday World-Building

Not all creativity glows with fantasy neon or pulses with epic soundtracks. Sometimes, imagination blooms in the backyard. Craig of the Creek is Cartoon Network’s love letter to childhood, friendship, and turning ordinary places into playgrounds of endless possibility.

The “Creek” becomes a self-contained world, governed by rules, cliques, and legends—a patchwork of adventures built from childhood resourcefulness. The characters invent games, build forts, broker alliances, and host epic battles with DIY charm.

For creators looking to bottle nostalgia, or to remember how ordinary settings can be stages for extraordinary acts, Craig of the Creek is a beautiful example. It shows that even the everyday holds creative magic for those willing to see—and build—it.

12. BoJack Horseman: Blending Comedy, Tragedy, and Visual Metaphor

Not every creative spark is childlike. BoJack Horseman smuggles enormous pathos and inventive visual metaphor into its caustic Hollywood satire. The show’s skill in emotional storytelling is matched by its restless animation—sight gags, color coding, and surreal asides that flesh out the inner lives and outer facades of its cast.

BoJack isn’t a guide to hopefulness, but a testament to storytelling that mingles tragedy with farce, heartbreak with hilarious self-destruction. Sometimes, creative fuel comes from seeing how narrative, visual symbolism, and reflective humor can dovetail. For adult artists and writers, BoJack Horseman is proof that even the ugliest inner struggles can become art.

How Cartoons Shape Creative Thinking

Cartoons offer a toolset that other art forms often can’t. They bend perspective, exaggerate emotion, and throw reality in the spin cycle. Here’s how animated storytelling feeds creative drive, no matter your age or background:

Unconstrained Visuals

In animation, a house can float away, a cat can talk, color rules evaporate. These visual impossibilities encourage outlandish invention in other mediums, too.

Radical Empathy

Well-written cartoons make empathy contagious. Characters reveal their vulnerabilities and strengths, suggesting that emotion is a valid foundation for artistic creation.

Playfulness in Failure

Animated worlds thrive on trial and error. When ideas flop, characters bounce back—sometimes literally. This resilience is a lifeline for artists learning to respect their own failed experiments.

Breaking Structure

Cartoons are notorious for bending rules of time, genre, and narrative. They play with pacing, framing, and continuity in ways that challenge assumptions and prime the brain for lateral thinking.

Accessible Mastery

Animation’s impact is democratic. You don’t need to be a research scholar or museum-goer—just open your eyes and let the stories unfold.

Wrapping Up: Animated Inspiration for All

The most inspirational cartoons share a restlessness, refusing to be boxed in by what came before. From the anything-goes worlds of Adventure Time and Gumball, to the painterly serenity of Studio Ghibli, to the muscular world-building of Avatar, each show and film in this list offers a unique invitation: try something new.

Get swept up in a story, marvel at a wild design, play with a new technique, remix an old trope, or just let the infectious weirdness of cartoons color your next project. No matter your age or skill, there’s always more room for creative mischief. The next time you’re stuck, let a cartoon lead you into the unknown—you never know what new worlds you’ll build.

And if all else fails, just picture a singing dog jumping over a rainbow river. Your imagination will do the rest.
