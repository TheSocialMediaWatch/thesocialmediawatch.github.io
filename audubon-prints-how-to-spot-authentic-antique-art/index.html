Audubon Prints: How to Spot Authentic Antique Art


Audubon prints are a testament to the seamless fusion of art and science. Created in the early 19th century by John James Audubon, who is often hailed as one of the most influential ornithologists and nature artists of his time, these works capture the exquisite details of birds in their natural habitats. For collectors and enthusiasts, owning an authentic antique Audubon print is a prized possession, but determining whether a piece is genuine requires careful scrutiny and knowledge of the telltale signs.

In this guide, we will delve deep into the world of Audubon prints, explore the history that makes them so coveted, and equip you with invaluable tips to spot authentic antique art. Whether you’re a seasoned collector or new to the fascinating realm of antique prints, understanding these distinguishing factors can save you from costly mistakes and enrich your appreciation for these timeless masterworks.

The Historical Significance of Audubon Prints

John James Audubon’s magnum opus, The Birds of America, stands as one of the greatest achievements in wildlife art. Published between 1827 and 1838, this ambitious project featured life-sized bird illustrations rendered with remarkable precision and artistry. Collaborating with skilled engravers and colorists, Audubon created a collection of 435 plates that showcased his singular ability to marry scientific observation with artistic flair.

Unlike traditional illustrations of wildlife during that time, Audubon’s depictions emphasized dynamic poses, natural settings, and lifelike detail. These prints were issued in large-format volumes, typically measuring 39 x 26 inches—a deliberate choice to accommodate life-sized representations of even the largest birds. The rarity of these original prints, coupled with their beauty and historical importance, has made them highly sought-after in the global art and antiques market.

Understanding the Editions of Audubon Prints

Before diving into authentication techniques, it’s important to understand the different editions of Audubon prints. Each edition carries its own nuances and characteristics that can impact both value and authenticity.

1. Original Havell Edition

The Havell Edition refers to the original set of prints published between 1827 and 1838 in London by Audubon in collaboration with engraver Robert Havell Jr. These are the most valuable and historically significant prints. They were produced using copperplate engraving and aquatint techniques, followed by meticulous hand coloring.

2. Bien Edition

Published in the mid-1850s, the Bien Edition was printed under the direction of Julius Bien using lithography instead of engraving. While these prints were still large in format, they differ in appearance and technique from the Havell Edition.

3. Octavo Editions

Recognizing the prohibitive cost of the larger formats, Audubon later released The Birds of America in a smaller “octavo” size. These editions were published in the mid-19th century, measuring about 10 x 6.5 inches. Though smaller, they were still beautifully detailed and remain collectible.

4. Later Reproductions

Twentieth-century reproductions of Audubon prints flood the market; while they can be beautiful art pieces in their own right, they lack historical authenticity and are worth considerably less. Modern processes like offset printing or digital replication are used for these prints.

Key Features of Authentic Antique Audubon Prints

Spotting an authentic Audubon print starts with understanding the materials, techniques, and subtleties unique to vintage artwork. Here are some critical aspects to investigate:

1. Paper Quality and Type

Genuine antique prints were created on high-quality, hand-laid paper or woven paper. Identifiable paper traits include visible chain lines and watermarks. For Havell Edition prints, look for the “J Whatman” watermark, commonly found on the paper—a hallmark of fine-quality linen-rag paper used during that era.

The texture of the paper can also reveal its age. Antique paper feels sturdier yet delicate, while modern reproductions tend to have machine-produced uniformity.

2. Printing Methods

Original Audubon prints from the Havell Edition were engraved using copperplates. You can identify copperplate engravings by examining the fine, recessed lines on the image. Run your fingers gently over the surface of the print (if allowed), and you may feel a slight texture from the engraving process.

Additionally, hand coloring was meticulously applied to Havell Edition prints. Look for slight variations or overlaps in the coloring, rather than the perfect uniformity typical of modern printed reproductions.

3. Dimensions

The authentic Havell Edition prints are known for their large dimensions (approximately 39 x 26 inches). Be cautious of prints that deviate significantly from this size unless they correspond to other known editions, like the octavo or Bien formats.

4. Font and Text

Original prints include engraver annotations and titling inscribed into the copperplate. Study the font style, spelling, and placement of the text below the image, as these can provide valuable clues. Replicas may include text inconsistencies or vary in alignment.

5. Age Indicators

Examine the overall condition of the print. Signs of aging such as slight foxing, natural toning, and edge wear can indicate authenticity. However, be wary of artificially aged reproductions designed to mimic antique characteristics.

Common Pitfalls in Authentication

Mistaking a reproduction for an original print is a common error among collectors. Often, these reproductions are heavily marketed as “vintage,” yet they lack historical significance. Here are some pitfalls to avoid:

  • Manufactured Wear: Some reproductions are artificially distressed to mimic the signs of age, like foxing or fading. Scrutinize the irregularity of aging patterns; authentic prints age naturally and unpredictably.

  • Modern Printing Characteristics: Check for glossy finishes or brightness in modern ink, which is incompatible with the muted tones created by period-accurate methods.

  • Incorrect Dimensions: Be cautious with sizes that don’t match known editions. Modern prints often come in dimensions meant to fit standard frames, sacrificing historical accuracy.

Seeking Expert Verification

When in doubt, it’s wise to consult a professional appraiser or expert in antique prints. Many established galleries, auction houses, and dealers specialize in Audubon prints and can provide a certificate of authenticity (COA). These evaluations may involve closer inspection, including analysis of the paper under ultraviolet light or magnification.

Preserving and Valuing Authentic Audubon Prints

Owning an authentic Audubon print goes beyond merely acquiring it; proper preservation safeguards its value for years to come. Here are a few tips to keep in mind:

  • Framing: Use archival-quality materials, including acid-free mats and UV-protective glass, for framing antique prints.

  • Environmental Factors: Protect your print from humidity, temperature fluctuations, and direct sunlight, which can deteriorate both the paper and ink.

  • Insurance and Documentation: For high-value prints, secure insurance and maintain detailed records of provenance and authenticity for future reference.

The Legacy of Audubon Prints

Audubon prints are more than just antique art; they are a window into history, nature, and one man’s extraordinary vision. By learning how to identify authentic pieces, you not only protect your investment but also honor the legacy of a remarkable artist and naturalist. Whether displayed on a wall or preserved in a collector’s archive, true Audubon prints continue to inspire and captivate audiences, just as they did nearly two centuries ago.

Engage with the rich history of Audubon’s work, marvel at the craftsmanship, and let your passion for antique art flourish as you embark on a journey into one of the most fascinating chapters of wildlife illustration.
