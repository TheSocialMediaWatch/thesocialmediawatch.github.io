Ethical Implications of Autonomous Vehicles: Philosophy and Technology


Autonomous vehicles have been hailed as the future of transportation. But with this exciting prospect comes a suite of challenging questions - both philosophical and technological - that must be addressed. In this article, we will delve into these issues, navigating the tricky terrain of autonomous vehicle ethics.

To start, let’s define autonomy. An autonomous vehicle is one that has the ability to operate without human intervention. This includes self-driving cars, drones, and even ships. The list is only limited by our creativity. While these technological advancements promise faster, safer, and more efficient transportation, they also pose a number of thorny questions.

The Trolley Dilemma

One prominent issue is a variation of the well-known “trolley problem.” Imagine a self-driving car facing an impossible choice: a child is about to be hit by the car or crossed by the train it is traveling on. The car can change its course and hit a group of adults, saving the child but risking several lives, or it can stay on its course, hitting the child but saving the adults on the train. If the car can avoid the entire situation, it would be equally acceptable.

This philosophical conundrum lends itself to a host of theorists’ analyses. Some believe it represents the sanctity of human life, to be preserved at all costs, while others argue for a more utilitarian approach, endangering and valuing fewer lives for the benefit of more.

The Algorithm of Right and Wrong

It’s not just the programming of the vehicle that requires careful consideration. How do we ensure the vehicle’s underlying algorithms are free from prejudice, bigotry and other societal ills? Autonomous vehicles rely heavily on algorithms that learn from data, but the data that these algorithms use, in turn, depends on the society it is trained on.

One example is the use of facial recognition technology. Facial recognition algorithms have been found to be more accurate in identifying white faces than Black faces, a clear artifact of the US criminal justice system’s historical prejudice. This suggests that the ads say that the algorithms we use might not be devoid of societal biases.

Whose Hands are on the Wheel?

Autonomous vehicles are deeply reliant on the data gathered by their sensors. The eyes, ears and nose of these machines are their sensors. But what happens when sensors fail?

The response to this question reveals much about the underlying philosophy of autonomous vehicle navigation. Originally, full autonomy was imagined as a ‘driverless’ solution - no one would own the responsibility of being behind the wheels. However, following several high-profile accidents, major car manufacturers and tech companies alike stated that a fully autonomous system isn’t inherently safe - instead, it must rely on the continuous improvement of sensor technology.

A Look Ahead

Given the complexity of the moral and technical questions we’ve discussed, it’s clear that the road ahead is not without its pitfalls. However, this also means that there is ample opportunity for thoughtful, wise decision making and evolution of technological capabilities in the domain of autonomous vehicles.

Like all significant advances, we mustn’t lose sight of the fact that what we’re building is and will continue to be a tool for human use, shaped by their needs and desires, as well as the need for future evolution to meet the inferable questions that emerge from these early days.

The challenge, therefore, is to ensure that we, as the creators and consumers of these technologies, are mindful of the path we tread. Our choices and actions have both short-and long-term consequences. The technology may be autonomous, but our responsibility to create and use these technologies wisely is not.

Summary

Autonomous vehicles present exciting prospects, but also force us to consider difficult questions about the moral implications of computer-driven decision-making. From the trolley problem to the need for fair and just decisions by automated entities, these challenges require careful consideration. As we move forward into a world of increased automation, it’s crucial that we remain aware of the complex dynamics at play. The journey ahead may be complex, but our actions have the potential to shape a future that is safe, efficient and free from human error.
