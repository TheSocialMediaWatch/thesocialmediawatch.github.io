The Best Fantasy Video Games for Story Inspiration


If you’re a writer, game designer, or anyone drawn to rich storytelling, fantasy video games can be an incredible wellspring of ideas. Their immersive worlds, intricate character arcs, and original lore offer a deep reservoir of inspiration. The key is knowing which titles push narrative boundaries and present complex, thought-provoking stories that spark creativity. In this post, we’ll dive into some of the best fantasy video games that can fuel your imagination and help you craft unforgettable stories.

Why Fantasy Games Are a Goldmine for Storytelling

Fantasy video games uniquely blend interactive storytelling with world-building, character development, and myth-making, which can provide a fresh perspective on narrative structure. Unlike passive media, games allow you to experience the story dynamically, often revealing lore gradually through exploration, dialogue, and choice-driven outcomes. This makes them ideal for understanding pacing, layered narratives, and multifaceted characters.

The power of fantasy lies in its freedom to bend reality, mix genres, and spread roots deep into archetypal themes like heroism, corruption, sacrifice, and redemption. Fantasy games immerse you in settings ranging from enchanted realms and dystopian futures to mythical kingdoms and alien landscapes, each offering intriguing plot devices and moral dilemmas that can be adapted or reimagined in your own stories.

Examining Game Worlds with Rich, Engaging Lore

1. The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt

Unless you’ve lived under a rock, The Witcher 3 is a hallmark of sophisticated storytelling in video games. Based on Andrzej Sapkowski’s acclaimed books, the game’s narrative weaves together folklore, politics, and personal drama, all set in a gritty, morally ambiguous world. It shows how to intertwine personal quests with epic confrontations, enriching both large-scale and intimate storytelling.

The characters—each layered with backstories, motivations, and moral conflicts—offer fertile ground for writers to study character-driven narrative arcs. Its branching dialogue and consequential choices also demonstrate how agency can shape story outcomes, a valuable lesson for interactive fiction.

2. Divinity: Original Sin 2

This game excels in building an original fantasy world filled with magic, social tensions, and political intrigue. The narrative design allows players to craft their characters’ destinies through choices that affect alliances, conflicts, and even reality itself. Divinity: Original Sin 2 pushes the boundaries of collaborative storytelling, making it a fantastic example of multifaceted perspectives and ethical complexity.

The extensive lore and creative magic systems inspire both world-building and plot mechanics. Writers can borrow ideas on portraying power struggles, the nature of divinity, and mutable morality in their narratives.

3. Dragon Age: Origins

A pioneer of deep role-playing storytelling, Dragon Age: Origins focuses on the classic fantasy conflict between humans and mythical creatures, interwoven with themes of prejudice, loyalty, and sacrifice. The game’s origin stories tied to different races/class choices offer multiple vantage points for storytelling, emphasizing the effect of background on character decisions.

The political machinations within the game’s kingdoms and the dense mythological fabric provide a blueprint for creating compelling, layered worlds. This title is especially useful for developing narrative factions, internal conflict, and dialogue nuance.

Exploring Unique Narrative Mechanics and World-Building

4. Hollow Knight

A masterclass in environmental storytelling, Hollow Knight is set in a beautifully hand-drawn world full of silent characters, forgotten histories, and subtle lore fragments. Rather than delivering exposition through dialogue, the game conveys story through architecture, item descriptions, and player discovery.

This minimalist storytelling approach teaches how to “show, not tell” elegantly and how atmosphere can amplify narrative impact. The mysterious world of Hallownest encourages exploring themes of decay, memory, and redemption without overt narrative spoon-feeding.

5. Elden Ring

From the legendary minds behind Dark Souls, Elden Ring offers a sprawling open world steeped in cryptic lore and mythic drama. Its storytelling is fragmented and often hidden beneath layers of obscure references, inviting players to piece together the story through subtle clues.

For creative writers, this highlights the power of ambiguity and non-linear narrative. The game’s epics of fallen kingdoms and godlike powers provide templates for grand-scale mythos construction, illustrating how incomplete narratives can engage and provoke readers’ imagination.

6. Final Fantasy VII Remake

A revamp of a classic, this title blends high-tech dystopia with fantasy elements, creating an emotionally charged narrative featuring environmentalism, identity, and rebellion. Known for its character-driven plot and cinematic style, it proves that familiar stories can be revitalized through character growth and modernized storytelling techniques.

The intricately crafted emotional arcs suggest techniques for pacing revelations and building empathy. Writers can study how to weave together action, suspense, and personal stakes in a fashion that appeals both to long-time fans and new audiences.

Crafting Nuanced Characters and Moral Dilemmas

7. Planescape: Torment

This cult classic is legendary for its philosophical explorations and profound narrative depth. Planescape: Torment challenges the player to question identity, memory, and the meaning of existence against a surreal fantasy backdrop.

Writing characters with philosophical layers and unresolved inner conflicts feels raw and authentic here. The game’s exploration of consequence and forgiveness is a useful reference for anyone fascinated by character psychology and morally gray storytelling.

8. Baldur’s Gate II: Shadows of Amn

Another cornerstone in fantasy role-playing, this title offers an expansive story with deep emotional and tactical complexity. It builds character relationships through diverse dialogue options and personal quests, while engaging players in epic confrontations.

Learning from its pacing, character development arcs, and world mythology can help writers create immersive narratives that balance plot momentum with character-driven scenes.

Why These Games Should Influence Your Unique Storytelling Voice

Each of these games carries unique storytelling strengths: some excel at sprawling epic narratives, while others finesse intimate character studies or minimalist environmental storytelling. Engaging with such titles reveals how interactivity, pacing, and player agency reshape conventional stories.

Gamers also become comfortable with non-linear, multi-perspective narratives and are more receptive to complex themes—elements that modern stories benefit from. The blending of dark and light elements challenges stereotypes about fantasy, proving it can address mature, nuanced human experiences.

When mining these games for inspiration, treat them as launches into your own creative cosmos. Adapt their storytelling techniques, borrow thematic elements, or reimagine characters and settings with your own voice to craft fresh, compelling fantasy narratives.

Tips on Using Fantasy Games for Story Inspiration

  • Focus on World-Building: Notice how environments, cultures, and histories interact to create believable, immersive worlds.
  • Analyze Character Arcs: Study protagonists and side characters, especially how their motivations evolve and clash.
  • Examine Narrative Structures: Observe how plot reveals, side quests, and multiple endings add narrative depth.
  • Think About Themes: Identify recurring themes like redemption, power, and sacrifice, and consider how they can shape your story.
  • Learn from Dialogue: Pay attention to how characters express personality and culture through dialogue.
  • Adapt Mechanics Creatively: Consider how game mechanics like choice or exploration inform narrative control.

Final Thoughts

Fantasy video games are not just about entertainment; they are treasure troves of storytelling innovation. Whether you want to build worlds as vast as those in Elden Ring or craft intimate character-driven tales like The Witcher 3, these games offer countless lessons in narrative design, character complexity, and thematic richness.

Dive into these stories not only as a player but as a storyteller. Let their mythologies, moral quandaries, and immersive worlds ignite your imagination, helping you create fantasy tales that resonate deeply with your audience. Your next epic might just begin with a controller in your hand.
