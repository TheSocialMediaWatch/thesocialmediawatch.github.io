The Most Popular Cartoon Franchises of All Time


Animated worlds have been captivating audiences for over a century. Cartoons are more than Saturday morning traditions or idle background noise—they’re cultural giants, shaping generations, sparking fashion trends, and selling billions in merchandise. Some have even sparked cinematic universes and theme parks, pulling fans into fantastic realities that transcend ink and pixels. Charting which cartoon franchises hold the crown for popularity is no easy feat, but some names stand so tall in the pop culture landscape that their legacy is undeniable.

Let’s travel from hand-drawn classics to digital juggernauts and explore the most popular cartoon franchises ever—an odyssey through nostalgia, innovation, and phenomenal global fandom.

The Simpsons: The Yellow Standard

Since 1989, Springfield’s most dysfunctional family has dominated television screens. “The Simpsons” redefined prime-time animation for adults, paving the way for countless imitators. With over 700 episodes, a blockbuster movie, and enough merchandise to fill multiple Kwik-E-Marts, this series is an undisputed colossus.

What’s the secret sauce? Universal humor, satirical takes on contemporary life, and a cast of colorful characters—Homer, Bart, Marge, Lisa, and Maggie have become household names. The impact goes beyond television; “The Simpsons” has influenced language (d’oh!), inspired internet memes, and even predicted future events with uncanny precision. The brand’s longevity illustrates its intergenerational reach, making it not just a cartoon franchise but a vital thread in the fabric of modern pop culture.

Pokémon: Gotta Catch Global Domination

It started in 1996 as a Game Boy title in Japan, but Pokémon quickly exploded into a world-conquering juggernaut. The TV anime, launched a year later, only propelled Pocket Monsters further into the stratosphere. The adventures of Ash Ketchum and Pikachu have enchanted kids and adults for decades, spinning off over a thousand episodes, numerous films, and several anime generations.

Pokémon’s success isn’t limited to the screen. The card game is a global craze. Video games routinely top sales charts. The franchise has invaded every corner of pop culture, from fashion to fast food. Pokémon GO, the mobile app, reignited Pokémania for a 21st-century audience, driving countless people outdoors in search of digital creatures.

Between games, television, and the constant evolution of its roster, Pokémon is widely considered the highest-grossing media franchise ever—proof that the electrifying appeal of Pokémon isn’t fading anytime soon.

Looney Tunes: The Masters of Slapstick

Before adult animation, before multiverses, there was “Looney Tunes.” This cartoon franchise, dating back to the 1930s, transformed figures like Bugs Bunny, Daffy Duck, Porky Pig, and Tweety into enduring icons. Their irreverent humor, clever wordplay, and wild physical comedy delighted young viewers and clever adults alike.

“Looney Tunes” isn’t just slapstick gags and catchphrases (“What’s up, Doc?”)—it’s foundational to the language of animation. The characters and shorts remain fresh, still popping up on streaming platforms, in new movies (like “Space Jam”), and in all forms of consumer products. The franchise helped launch the animation careers of legends like Mel Blanc and Chuck Jones while creating a template for character-driven comedy that persists throughout the industry.

SpongeBob SquarePants: The Pineapple Phenomenon

When SpongeBob SquarePants first aired in 1999, few could predict it would become Nickelodeon’s most successful property. Yet the zany exploits of the eternally optimistic sponge quickly conquered the hearts and minds of fans worldwide. Set in the bizarre underwater realm of Bikini Bottom, the series found the sweet spot between childlike whimsy and winking adult humor.

The franchise’s reach is impressive—multiple spin-offs, blockbuster films, a Broadway musical, and a staggering merchandising machine that runs from toys to home goods. SpongeBob memes regularly trend online, and phrases like “Is mayonnaise an instrument?” have entered the lexicon. Bikini Bottom’s positivity, absurdity, and distinct visual design have cemented its legacy as a modern cartoon titan.

Tom and Jerry: Cat-and-Mouse Chase Through the Ages

The timeless rivalry between Tom the cat and Jerry the mouse has charmed audiences since the 1940s. Originally created by William Hanna and Joseph Barbera, “Tom and Jerry” outlined the art of animated physical comedy: high-speed chases, elaborate schemes, and the kind of orchestrated chaos that defies dialogue.

Across hundreds of shorts, TV spin-offs, cinematic releases, and global adaptations, their frenetic battle has remained mesmerizing to kids and adults alike. The simplicity of the humor makes these animations nearly universal, transcending language barriers. Generations have grown up with the duo’s non-stop antics—testament to the franchise’s unmatched staying power.

Scooby-Doo: Mystery Solved

Somewhere between horror, humor, and campy adventure, “Scooby-Doo” found its home. Since 1969, a parade of rebooted series, animated films, and even live-action adaptations have thrust Scooby, Shaggy, Velma, Daphne, and Fred into new mysteries, all while unmasking generations of “meddling kids.”

Part of its enduring popularity stems from a perfect blend of spooky storylines, goofball comedy, and memorable catchphrases. “Scooby-Doo” is a merchandising giant, selling everything from cereals to costumes. Its formula—creepy settings, slapstick humor, and the satisfying reveal—has kept the franchise evergreen, with new iterations finding fresh audiences every decade.

Dragon Ball: The Super Saiyan Surge

Anime’s mainstream breakthrough in the West would be incomplete without “Dragon Ball.” From its origins as Akira Toriyama’s dynamic manga to the multi-decade anime phenomenon it spawned, this franchise is the touchstone of shonen action and spectacle.

With “Dragon Ball Z,” the saga of Goku and his companions battling iconic foes propelled anime into global consciousness. The series blends over-the-top martial arts, cosmic storylines, and themes of perseverance and friendship. Dragon Ball’s legacy includes video games, movies, toys, and mountains of fan content. “Dragon Ball Super” demonstrated the property’s enduring appeal, reenergizing fandom and introducing new generations to the world of Super Saiyans.

Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles: Heroes in a Half Shell

What started as a parody comic in the 1980s morphed into a relentless cultural sensation. Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles transformed Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael, and Donatello from underground oddities into global icons. The original animated series catapulted pizza-eating, wisecracking turtles into homes, igniting a craze that included live-action films, abundant merchandise, and multiple reboots.

The Turtles’ perpetual reinvention keeps them relevant, evolving with new audiences while retaining core elements: brotherhood, martial arts, and a sharp sense of humor. Their resilience in the pop culture ecosystem is hard to overstate—few franchises have cycled through so many iterations and maintained such a fiercely loyal fanbase.

Avatar: The Last Airbender - Elements of Legacy

Nickelodeon’s “Avatar: The Last Airbender” didn’t initially seem destined for legendary status, but its masterful blend of Asian-inspired fantasy, mature storytelling, and cinematic animation turned it into one of the most acclaimed cartoons ever. The epic story of Aang and his friends as they battle elemental forces of chaos managed to win over children and adults alike.

Critical adoration turned into cult fandom, fueling comics, a sequel series (“Legend of Korra”), and upcoming live-action adaptations. Its carefully crafted world-building and deeply empathetic characters set a high bar for Western animation and have inspired countless successors.

Disney’s House of Mouse: The Dynasty That Began It All

Arguably the original powerhouse of animated franchises, Disney’s empire began with “Mickey Mouse” in 1928. From there, it grew into a constellation of enduring characters—Donald Duck, Goofy, Minnie, and more—each starring in shorts, feature films, and television programming.

The staying power of Disney’s classic cartoons stems from more than nostalgia. The studio’s innovation redefined animation again and again, shifting from theatrical shorts to feature-length cartoons (“Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs,” “The Lion King”) and beyond. Disney’s animated properties continue to dominate box offices and streaming charts, and with vast merchandising, theme parks, and endless spin-offs, this dynasty remains the gold standard in cartoon longevity and profitability.

South Park: Satire Without Limits

What began as a crude paper-cutout pilot in 1997 rapidly evolved into a cultural battering ram. “South Park” is notorious for its unapologetic, often controversial humor—but for over 25 seasons, it’s managed to remain both relevant and wildly popular. Cartman, Stan, Kyle, and Kenny are part of a franchise that’s lampooned politics, pop culture, and the animated format itself.

Merchandising, video games, and an estimated streaming deal worth hundreds of millions have turned “South Park” into a juggernaut with a fiercely devoted fanbase. Its simple visual style hides razor-sharp wit and remarkable longevity, keeping it firmly rooted among animation’s true titans.

Peanuts: The Classic Strip That Grew Beyond Paper

Cartoon franchises aren’t just TV shows. “Peanuts,” born from Charles Schulz’s imaginative newspaper strips, evolved into a massive franchise thanks to timeless television specials and a lovable cast including Charlie Brown and Snoopy. “A Charlie Brown Christmas” and “It’s the Great Pumpkin, Charlie Brown” remain seasonal favorites the world over.

From plush toys to Macy’s Thanksgiving Parade balloons, Peanuts merchandise is ubiquitous. Its emotional resonance and gentle humor have endeared it to audiences for generations, putting it high on the list of history’s most beloved cartoon franchises.

The Transformers: More Than Meets the Eye

First arriving in the mid-1980s, “Transformers” captured imaginations by pitting Autobots and Decepticons against each other in an endless struggle between good and evil—this time, with robots who turned into cars, planes, and more. The original cartoon series sparked an action figure sensation and set the template for cross-promotional synergy.

Dozens of animated series, blockbuster movies, comic books, and a never-ending stream of new toys have kept Transformers at the forefront of pop culture. The franchise’s ability to transform itself—pun very much intended—has guaranteed its place among animation’s most successful and ever-adapting titans.

Family Guy: Irreverence in the Suburbs

It was canceled—twice—and yet, Seth MacFarlane’s “Family Guy” always found a way to return. The misadventures of the Griffin family, especially Peter and the evil-genius baby Stewie, tap into a vein of subversive, referential, and sometimes shockingly absurd humor.

“Family Guy” has inspired spin-offs (“The Cleveland Show”), video games, and massive meme culture. Its cutaway gags and acerbic commentary on American life ensure it remains a favorite among adults and teens, cementing its place as a relentless force in animated comedy.

Conclusion: Animation’s Immortal Champions

This journey through the most popular cartoon franchises underlines their role as more than simple children’s entertainment. Whether it’s slapstick legends, heroic transformations, or sharp satire, these franchises are woven into global culture. They unite fans across countries, generations, and languages, sparking creativity and nostalgia all at once.

The appeal never fades: from the ageless charm of classic looney animals and Disney’s magic to the millennial punchlines of “SpongeBob SquarePants” or the cosmic energy of “Dragon Ball.” New cartoons will rise, but the legends carved out by these franchises aren’t likely to be eclipsed—they’re cherished threads in the tapestry of collective memory.

Animated characters might come from ink, paint, or code, but their influence is very real. Which cartoon franchise shaped your childhood (or adulthood)? There’s a good chance you’ll be humming its theme song for years to come.
