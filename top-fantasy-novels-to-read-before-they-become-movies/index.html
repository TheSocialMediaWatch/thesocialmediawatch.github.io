Top Fantasy Novels to Read Before They Become Movies


In the book-to-film pipeline, anticipation runs high, and there’s a certain thrill in seeing beloved characters and worlds come to life on the big screen. Yet, nothing matches the magic of experiencing these stories first in their original form. If you’re a devoted fantasy reader, a film buff, or someone caught between the two, this list of fantasy novels on the cusp of cinematic adaptation is just the ticket. Take the chance to absorb their intricate plots, lush world-building, and unforgettable heroes before Hollywood weaves its own spell.

1. The Priory of the Orange Tree by Samantha Shannon

Picture an epic tale brimming with dragons, elemental magic, and a sprawling cast with motivations as twisty as the roots of an ancient tree. Samantha Shannon’s “The Priory of the Orange Tree” is a feminist reimagining of classic dragon fantasy, and it’s caught the eye of producers eager to replicate the success of sprawling high-concept adaptations. The story is set in a divided world threatened by the return of a draconic adversary. As queendoms teeter on the edge of war and ancient secrets brim beneath the surface, a diverse array of protagonists—all richly drawn, each with their own struggles and desires—fight for survival.

Why read it first? Rumors of a miniseries have swirled, but the book’s tapestry of perspectives and lore runs deep. Experience the full complexity of Shannon’s world before adaptation’s inevitable trimming of subplots.

2. The Broken Earth Trilogy by N.K. Jemisin

Hollywood is perpetually drawn to stories that blend profound human drama with speculative premise, and no trilogy does this better than N.K. Jemisin’s Hugo Award-winning “The Broken Earth.” Centered on a world riven by seismic disaster, the saga follows three women with power to move mountains—literally. Their destinies, heartbreaks, and transformations sweep across a setting as violent as it is beautiful.

Currently in development with a major studio, this series will no doubt boast impressive special effects and sweeping visuals, but its true force lies in nuanced relationships and sharply observed social commentary. Don’t wait for the credits to roll—immerse yourself in Jemisin’s original, propulsive prose.

3. The Rage of Dragons by Evan Winter

African-inspired fantasy isn’t new, but Evan Winter’s debut injects the subgenre with breakneck action and unforgettable intensity. “The Rage of Dragons” introduces readers to Tau, an outcast seeking vengeance in a war-soaked society where only the chosen few can wield the might of dragons. Winter’s world is harsh and unforgiving, blending martial intrigue, political maneuvering, and gripping battles.

Having been optioned for film and television, it’s only a matter of time before this unique take on epic fantasy ascends to the screen. The book’s relentless pacing and plot twists ensure a captivating ride—something that, in a visual medium, could hinge on spectacle over substance. Seize the chance to savor the depth behind Tau’s decisions.

4. The Name of the Wind by Patrick Rothfuss

For over a decade, fans have awaited news of adaptation for Patrick Rothfuss’s modern classic. “The Name of the Wind” tells the story of Kvothe, an enigmatic hero with a mysterious—and at times unreliable—past. The book is a meditation on storytelling itself, exploring themes of myth, truth, and the way legends are made.

Adaptation talk has ebbed and flowed, linked to big names and studios, but the story’s intimacy and lyrical prose are unparalleled. Read Rothfuss’s masterpiece before it becomes the next fantasy phenomenon on TV, and judge for yourself whether any actor can step into Kvothe’s shoes.

5. Red Sister by Mark Lawrence

Combat nuns, arcane relics, a dying sun—these are the ingredients that set “Red Sister” apart in a crowded field. Mark Lawrence’s coming-of-age saga introduces Nona Grey, a girl taken to a convent that trains deadly warriors. The intrigue of this cloistered world, pieces of lost technology, and the threat looming from the order’s enemies all make for a cinematic read.

With its tight plotting and character-driven storylines, “Red Sister” is ripe for adaptation, and rumors suggest that producers have already taken notice. The action, drama, and moments of reflection all beg to be experienced in the original prose.

6. The Poppy War by R.F. Kuang

With an adaptation reportedly in the works, “The Poppy War” by R.F. Kuang offers a gut-punch of military fantasy, rich Chinese history, and shamanic magic. Protagonist Rin battles both her own demons and the horrors of war as she rises from poverty to the heights of magical power. Kuang’s world is sharp-edged and uncompromising, vividly depicting the costs of ambition and the scars war leaves behind.

Where the story’s violence and moral ambiguity might be softened for mainstream audiences, the book invites readers to grapple with its full complexity. Kuang’s trilogy unfolds with escalating stakes—read it now, and enjoy the thrill of anticipation when casting news hits.

7. The Shadow of the Gods by John Gwynne

Viking-inspired fantasy meets dark adventure in John Gwynne’s “The Shadow of the Gods.” The reemergence of dragon gods and warring clans set the stage for betrayals, giant monsters, and reluctant alliances. Gwynne’s deft hand with action and his evocative world-building make for an immersive read.

Hollywood has shown growing interest in Norse-inspired worlds since the success of similar tales. Its ensemble cast and visual potential are primed for adaptation, but the internal narratives and shifting allegiances are best savored on the page.

8. The City of Brass by S.A. Chakraborty

Magic, political scheming, and djinn intrigue swirl in “The City of Brass,” the opening to S.A. Chakraborty’s “Daevabad Trilogy.” Set in a vibrant reimagining of 18th-century Cairo and the fabled city of Daevabad, the story follows Nahri, a conwoman with a mysterious past, as she dives into a world of tumbling loyalties and ancient magic.

Fans of lush settings and multidimensional characters should dive in before casting and costuming begin to shape public imagination. Chakraborty’s vision is filled with mythic depth and emotional resonance.

9. Fourth Wing by Rebecca Yarros

With dragons, an elite military academy, and deadly rivalries, “Fourth Wing” has already drawn comparison to juggernauts like “Game of Thrones.” It’s no wonder Hollywood has come knocking. Following Violet, a heroine out of her depth but determined to survive, the novel weaves romance, treachery, and high fantasy.

The breakneck pace and adrenaline-fueled trials of this hit make it compulsively readable, and while the inevitable adaptation will surely capture the action, the book’s details invite deeper investment. Get ahead of the curve and see for yourself why everyone’s raving.

10. Legendborn by Tracy Deonn

A richly layered blend of Arthurian legend and contemporary magical academy, “Legendborn” is a fantasy novel ripe for visual storytelling. Bree Matthews, a young Black woman at a predominantly white Southern university, finds herself caught up in a secret society, hidden magic, and her own complicated grief.

The novel’s fresh take on myth, combined with honest emotional beats and a propulsive plot, has seen it picked up for adaptation, with enthusiastic producers at the helm. Readers can dive into Bree’s journey and uncover secrets alongside her before her story gets a screen interpretation.

Why Read the Originals?

Adapting fantasy novels to film often means losing some depth and subtlety that only prose can provide. Nuanced character development, inner monologues, complex world-building, and rich linguistic texture are hard to replicate fully on screen—even with the most talented cast and dazzling effects. Whether it’s the riddle-like quality of Rothfuss’s writing or the bottomless pain in Jemisin’s dystopia, these nuances shine brightest in their original home.

Moreover, by reading the books first, you’ll find yourself in a privileged position when conversations turn to casting choices, changes to the plot, or the inevitable debate over the ‘true’ ending. You’ll also experience these imaginative worlds unfiltered by someone else’s vision, forming your own mental movie before the director has a say.

Bonus Picks: Honorable Mentions

Some novels hover just on the edge of adaptation buzz, or have suffered development hell for so long that progress feels glacial. Still, they’re well worth exploration, and you can be assured of your book hipster credentials if they do hit the screen.

  • “Foundryside” by Robert Jackson Bennett: A clever blend of heist action and innovative magic.
  • “Empire of the Vampire” by Jay Kristoff: Gritty, violent, and dripping with gothic excess.
  • “The Lies of Locke Lamora” by Scott Lynch: If oceans eleven met fantasy, it would be this rollicking tale of thieves in a Venice-inspired city.

Building Your TBR Before the Boom

New waves of adaptation mean that today’s book club read might be tomorrow’s binge-worthy obsession. From dragon-fueled warfare to cunning thieves and magical societies, the fantasy novels listed above represent the very best in the genre—complex, imaginative, and vivid.

So grab a copy or check out your local library. Lose yourself in worlds where magic is real, destiny is sharp-edged, and no epic tale remains untouched for long. There’s a certain satisfaction in being there before the trailer drops, when only readers know what surprises lie ahead.

Which of these are you most excited to see brought to life, and which do you hope stays your little secret for just a bit longer? Let the page be your portal, before the theaters open their doors.
