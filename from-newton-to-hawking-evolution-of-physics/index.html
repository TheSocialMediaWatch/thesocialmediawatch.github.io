From Newton to Hawking: Evolution of Physics


The evolution of physics, like the arc of any scientific discipline, has been a long, steady move from simple ideas to deeper, more complex theories. Starting with Newton’s groundbreaking work, physics has witnessed some remarkable developments that have not only expanded our understanding of the universe but also changed the way we perceive it.

The mechanical philosophy of Sir Isaac Newton

Newton’s revolutionary work laid the groundwork for classical physics. Working during the Scientific Revolution in the late 17th century, Newton developed the theories of motion, universal gravitation, and Newtonian mechanics, which became the foundation of classical physics. His iconic work, ‘Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy,’ better known as ‘Principia,’ builds upon the works of earlier philosophers like Galileo, refining the mechanical philosophy that views the universe as a giant machine.

The genius of Maxwell and the unification of forces

One of the most significant shifts in physics occurred in the 19th century when James Clerk Maxwell unified electricity, magnetism, and optics under a single framework. His theory of electromagnetic radiation predicts the behavior of light and electromagnetic waves, shaping our understanding of these phenomena.

The discovery of the universe’s composition

During the early 20th century, physics underwent another quantum leap with the realization that atoms existed and were composed of smaller particles. When Ernest Rutherford discovered the atomic nucleus in 1911, physics took a new direction. This development led to the framework of modern atomic theory and the concept of the electron.

The relativity revolution

Albert Einstein’s groundbreaking theory of relativity reinvented the perception of space and time. First, his special theory of relativity challenged the laws of electromagnetism. Breaking away from the accepted rules of classical physics, Einstein showed that the speed of light is a constant and that time and space are intertwined (Space-Time). Then, his general theory of relativity created a new theory about gravity, describing it as a curvature of space-time.

Quantum Mechanics: The rule of the small

After the dawn of the 20th century, new physical theories such as quantum mechanics became necessary for understanding the atomic and subatomic world. Niels Bohr’s model of the atom and Erwin Schrödinger’s work on wave mechanics led to the development of quantum physics. This new scientific rule reshaped the classical understanding of aspects like energy and matter, introducing the idea of wave-particle duality.

The quest for unification: From Standard Model to String Theories

Our current understanding of fundamental particles and forces is governed by the Standard Model. But physics still quests for a Theory of Everything, unifying all four fundamental forces (electromagnetic, strong nuclear, weak nuclear, and gravity), and explaining how the universe operates at a microscopic level.

Into the multiverse: The modern landscape

Particle physicists embarked on an ambitious project in the late 20th century-one that unveiled a multiverse theory. This modern landscape enriches our understanding of cosmos history and promises a better comprehension of the universe, taking us from the cosmic microwave background radiation to black holes.

Conclusion

From Newton’s mechanical philosophy to the quantum mechanical revolution, the evolution of physics has been nothing short of groundbreaking. As we continue to unravel the deepest mysteries of the universe, the scientists who have forged the path from Newton to Hawking inspire us to keep exploring and expanding our understanding.
