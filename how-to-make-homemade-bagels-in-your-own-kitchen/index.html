How to Make Homemade Bagels in Your Own Kitchen


There’s a moment in every home baker’s journey when the allure of bakery-fresh bagels becomes too enticing to resist. We all know what that first bite of a warm bagel feels like—the chewy crust, the slightly sweet and dense crumb, and the joy of slathering on your favorite cream cheese or butter. Why wait for a weekend run to your local shop when you can master this iconic bread in your own kitchen?

Making homemade bagels might sound intimidating, but it’s surprisingly achievable, even without professional equipment or years of experience. This comprehensive guide will take you through every step to create bagels that rival your favorite bakery’s, with tips along the way to troubleshoot, customize, and perfect your baking process.

What Makes a Bagel a Bagel?

Bagels are not just round bread with a hole in the middle. Their signature chewy texture and shiny crust set them apart. The unique process of boiling the dough before baking is the secret to their magic. Whether you prefer classic New York-style bagels, a slightly sweeter Montreal version, or a flavor-packed everything bagel, the basics remain the same: high-gluten flour, yeast, malt (or sweetener), a short ferment, a boiling step, and finally, a hot bake.

Essential Tools and Ingredients

Before diving into your homemade bagel adventure, gather the right equipment and the freshest ingredients.

Tools:

  • Large mixing bowl (or stand mixer with a dough hook)
  • Measuring cups and spoons
  • Kitchen scale (recommended for accuracy)
  • Baking sheet
  • Parchment paper
  • Large pot (for boiling)
  • Slotted spoon
  • Wire rack

Must-Have Ingredients:

  • High-gluten bread flour (critical for chewiness)
  • Instant or active dry yeast
  • Lukewarm water
  • Barley malt syrup, honey, or sugar
  • Coarse salt
  • Optional toppings: sesame seeds, poppy seeds, everything seasoning, cinnamon, onion flakes

The Best Flour for Bagels

The texture of your bagels depends almost entirely on the flour you choose. Bread flour, with a higher protein content than all-purpose flour, creates that classic chew. If you can find it, specialty high-gluten flour boosts elasticity even further, giving you bakery-level bagels.

Step-by-Step Homemade Bagel Recipe

Ready to roll up your sleeves? Here’s a tried-and-true homemade bagel recipe, detailed for clarity so you’ll feel confident and excited every step of the way.

Ingredients:

For 8 large bagels

  • 4 cups (500g) bread flour (plus extra for dusting)
  • 2 teaspoons (8g) kosher salt
  • 1 tablespoon (14g) barley malt syrup (or honey/sugar)
  • 1 tablespoon (9g) instant yeast
  • 1¼ cups (300ml) warm water

For boiling:

  • 2 quarts (2 liters) water
  • 2 tablespoons (28g) barley malt syrup or honey
  • 1 tablespoon baking soda (optional, increases crust shine)

Optional toppings:

  • Poppy seeds
  • Sesame seeds
  • Everything bagel spice
  • Dried onion flakes

1. Mix and Knead the Dough

Start by combining the flour, salt, malt syrup, and yeast. If using a stand mixer, fit with a dough hook and mix on low while slowly pouring in the warm water. Alternatively, mix by hand with a sturdy spoon until a shaggy dough forms.

Knead for 8–10 minutes by hand or 6–8 minutes with a mixer, until the dough is stiff, elastic, and barely tacky. Bagel dough should be firmer than most bread doughs—this is key to achieving the dense crumb.

If your dough feels very dry, add a teaspoon of water at a time. If sticky, sprinkle with a bit of flour. Resist the urge to over-flour.

2. First Rise (Bulk Fermentation)

Form your dough into a smooth ball and place it in a lightly oiled bowl. Cover with a clean kitchen towel or plastic wrap.

Let rise at room temperature until puffed and roughly doubled in volume, about 1 hour. You can also refrigerate the dough overnight at this stage; a slow ferment develops deeper flavor and complex notes. If refrigerating, allow the dough to come to room temperature before shaping.

3. Divide and Shape the Bagels

Once risen, turn your dough onto a clean, very lightly floured surface. Gently deflate and divide into 8 equal pieces (a kitchen scale ensures consistency).

Roll each portion into a ball, pinching any seams on the underside. For classic bagel shaping, poke your thumb through the center of each ball, then gently stretch and rotate to widen the hole to about 2 inches across. The hole will close up slightly during the next rises and bake, so don’t be shy.

Arrange shaped bagels on a parchment-lined baking sheet, spacing them a couple inches apart.

4. Proof, then Test Readiness

Let your shaped bagels rest for 20–30 minutes at room temperature. To test readiness, place one bagel in a bowl of cold water. If it floats, you’re ready to proceed; if not, let them rise longer and test again.

Refrigerating the proofed bagels uncovered for up to 24 hours is a pro move. This overnight rise develops flavor and creates a skin, which contributes to the signature bagel crust.

5. Prepare to Boil

When ready to bake, bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Stir in the malt syrup (or honey) and baking soda. The syrup delivers flavor and helps produce that deep, shiny crust; baking soda alkalizes the water for extra color and chew.

Preheat your oven to 425°F (218°C) so it’s hot as soon as the bagels hit the oven.

6. Boil the Bagels

Gently drop 2–3 bagels into the boiling water at a time, taking care not to crowd the pot. Boil for 45 seconds on one side, flip, and boil another 45 seconds.

Boiling gelatinizes the starches on the crust and gives bagels their chewy texture. The longer you boil, the chewier and puffier the bagels will be.

Remove with a slotted spoon to drain, then place back on the parchment-lined baking sheet.

7. Add Toppings

If you love seeded bagels, now’s the time! As soon as the boiled bagels come out of the water, dip the tops into a shallow bowl of seeds or sprinkle liberally. Press lightly to adhere.

Classic options include poppy seeds, sesame seeds, everything seasoning, or kosher salt. For sweet variations, cinnamon sugar is delicious.

8. Bake to Golden Perfection

Slide your baking sheet into the preheated oven and bake for 20–25 minutes, rotating the pan halfway through. Bagels should be deeply golden and hard to the tap.

Cool on a wire rack for at least 20 minutes before slicing. As difficult as it is to wait, this step helps set the crumb structure and finish the cooking.

Customizing Your Homemade Bagels

Once you’ve nailed the basic recipe, try these variations to make bagels tailored to your taste.

Flavorful Mix-Ins

  • Cinnamon raisin: Add 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon and ½ cup raisins during mixing.
  • Jalapeño cheddar: Add 1 finely chopped jalapeño and ¾ cup shredded cheddar to your dough.
  • Blueberry: Gently fold in ½ cup fresh or frozen blueberries after kneading.

Unconventional Toppings

Bagels aren’t limited to seeds. Try parmesan and black pepper, dried garlic flakes, or even crunchy fried onions.

Whole Wheat or Multi-Grain

Swap up to half of the bread flour for whole wheat or rye for a hearty, nutty twist. Increase your hydration slightly to keep the dough workable.

Tips for Perfect Bagels Every Time

  • Water temperature: Keep it lukewarm to activate your yeast without killing it.
  • Dough texture: Stiff dough ensures authentic chew. Soft dough can lead to bready, non-bagel results.
  • Boiling: Don’t skip or rush this step—this is where the magic happens.
  • Spacing: Bagels will expand during baking, so give them room on your sheet.
  • Baking: For extra shine and crispness, bake directly on a preheated baking stone or steel if you have one.

Troubleshooting Common Bagel Problems

Even the best bakers encounter hiccups. Here’s how to handle common challenges:

  • Bagels are too dense or flat: Under-proofing, overproofing, or weak flour are culprits. Make sure dough rises until just doubled and use bread or high-gluten flour.
  • Bagels stick to parchment: Lightly grease parchment or use silicone baking mats to prevent sticking, especially with overnight rises.
  • Bagels are bready, not chewy: Dough was too soft or you skipped the boiling. Stick to a stiffer dough and always boil!
  • Toppings fall off: Apply immediately after boiling, while surface is wet.

Storing and Freezing Homemade Bagels

Homemade bagels are best within 24 hours but can stay fresh for up to 3 days in a paper bag or sealed container. For longer storage, slice and freeze in a ziplock; reheat directly from frozen in a toaster or oven for a taste just like fresh.

Bagel FAQ

Can I make bagels gluten-free?
Bagels rely on gluten for texture, but there are gluten-free recipes using specialty flours and binders. Expect a slightly different crumb.

Can I make bagels without a stand mixer?
Absolutely. Hand kneading takes more time and elbow grease, but it works perfectly well.

What’s the best way to serve fresh bagels?
Classic toppings include cream cheese, lox, capers, tomatoes, or just a simple pat of salted butter. For sweet tooths, honey or fruit jam pairs beautifully.

Can I shape bagels a different way?
Some bakers roll dough into a rope and seal the ends to form the circle. Both the poke-and-stretch and rope methods work; use what feels natural.

Inviting Homemade Bagels into Your Routine

Baking your own bagels connects you deeper to the foods you love. The simple act of shaping dough and filling your kitchen with the aroma of baking bread is immensely satisfying. You’ll quickly see why fresh bagels outshine store-bought—the control over ingredients, the endless options for customization, and the irresistible taste. Plus, bagels freeze and reheat extraordinarily well, making them a perfect make-ahead breakfast.

Set aside a morning, queue up your favorite playlist, and enjoy the meditative rhythm of kneading and shaping. With just flour, water, yeast, salt, and a bit of time, your homemade bagels will become the toast of your kitchen—hot, chewy, and ready to be shared.

There’s nothing quite like a fresh bagel pulled from your own oven. Start your new baking tradition today and discover just how attainable (and delicious) homemade bagels can be.
