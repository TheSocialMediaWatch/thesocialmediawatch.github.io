The Chemistry Behind Common Household Products


The world of chemistry is all around us, even in the most mundane household items. While we may not realize it, these everyday objects conceal fascinating chemical reactions. Join us on a journey into the remarkable chemistry that makes everything from soap to the humble paper clip possible.

The Magic of Soap

The process of making soap dates back to ancient times, but its modern form became prevalent in the 19th century, after the introduction of colloidal soap, followed by the growth of the soap industry. Soap is naturally derived from fats and oils, combined with an alkali, resulting in a chemical reaction known as saponification.

Fats and oils consist of molecules made up of a glycerol backbone and three fatty acid chains. When a fat or oil combines with an alkali, such as sodium hydroxide, the fatty acids are liberated, and the glycerol remains. The fatty acids then combine with the hydroxide anions to form soap molecules. The soap molecules have a polar hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a nonpolar hydrophobic (water-hating) tail.

This dual nature allows soap to surround and lift dirt away from surfaces, emulsifying with water to rinse it away. The addition of fragrances, dyes, or other additives provides variety, giving us the wide range of soap options we enjoy today.

A Look at Aspirin

Acetylsalicylic acid, more commonly known as aspirin, is a common over-the-counter pain reliever. It is produced through the esterification of salicylic acid and acetic anhydride, a reaction which forms a new ester molecule and water.

Salicylic acid, a weak organic acid, has a hydroxyl group that makes it acidic. Acetylation of this group results in a less acidic, neutral molecule, thus reducing inflammation and reducing pain. Aspirin is also an antiplatelet, helping to prevent the formation of blood clots.

Despite its widespread use, aspirin can be dangerous for some individuals, particularly those with allergies or undergoing certain medical procedures. Always consult your healthcare provider before taking any new medication, even over-the-counter remedies like aspirin.

Drying with Silica Gel

Imbibition, or the process of absorption of a liquid into a solid, is what allows for the drying action of silica gel. Silica gel is made from silica (silicon dioxide) combined with an organic R-SO3H group, forming an organosilicone polymer gel.

This porous gel structure readily absorbs water through imbibition, making it an excellent desiccant. When you place silica gel in a moist environment, the water molecules adhere to the pores of the gel, effectively drawing out the moisture. Once saturated, silica gel can be regenerated by heating.

The Mystery of Paperclips

While paperclips may seem like simple, mundane objects, their construction and function are rooted in chemistry. The most common type of paperclip is made from a continuous strip of drawn wire, usually formed of steel or a steel alloy.

Steel is composed of iron and carbon, forming an alloy with unique mechanical and physical properties. These properties provide the paperclip with its springiness, allowing it to hold and clip papers together.

The shape and size of paperclips can vary depending on their intended purpose, some functioning as straight clips, while others have unique shapes, such as the so-called “No. 2” or “Gem” paperclip, which are coiled to form a loop.

In the end, the chemistry behind these common household items is complex and fascinating. These seemingly simple objects, from the soap that cleans our hands, to the aspirin that relieves our pain, the drying power of silica gel, and the simple paperclip, are all rooted in the remarkable field of chemistry.
