The Best Fantasy Novels with Female Protagonists


The world of fantasy is a universe of possibilities: dragons soar above misty mountains, dark sorcery crackles in hidden corners, and every shadow could hide a hero. For readers seeking fresh perspectives, fantasy stories led by unforgettable women deliver on every front. These tales feature heroines wielding magical powers, defying expectations, and shaping worlds with their choices. They inspire, challenge, and thrill—inviting us to imagine what might be possible in both story and life.

If you’re searching for epic adventures anchored by complex, dynamic heroines, you’ll find plenty to love in the novels below. Forget passive maidens or one-dimensional “strong female characters”—these books offer richly drawn protagonists who change their worlds as much as their worlds change them.

1. The Fifth Season by N.K. Jemisin

When Essun returns home to find both her son murdered and daughter missing, she’s thrust into a journey across a world on the cusp of collapse. N.K. Jemisin’s The Fifth Season—the first book in her immersive Broken Earth Trilogy—melds breathtaking world-building with unforgettable characters. Essun is more than a survivor: she’s a mother, a mage, and a revolutionary. Her journey through a land shattered by apocalyptic magic is both personal and epic in scale, marked by trauma, resilience, and the exhaustive challenge of survival in a prejudiced society.

What sets The Fifth Season apart isn’t just its intricate magic system or environmental catastrophe—but the raw humanity at its core. Jemisin’s exploration of pain, strength, and community is both moving and unflinching, with Essun at the helm as a protagonist readers won’t soon forget.

2. The Priory of the Orange Tree by Samantha Shannon

Samantha Shannon crafts an entire world teetering on the edge of destruction in this standalone epic fantasy. The novel features multiple points of view, but Ead Duryan stands out—a sorceress in disguise, tasked with guarding the heir to the throne. Ead’s story is one of quiet strength, resilience, and discovery, blending court intrigue with outlandish adventure and sapphic romance.

Dragons, assassins, and ancient secrets abound, but Shannon’s greatest triumph is her heroines: flawed, passionate, and deeply human women who shape the fate of nations. For epic fantasy fans craving both scale and heart, The Priory of the Orange Tree feels expansive and intimate all at once.

3. Alanna: The First Adventure by Tamora Pierce

No list would be complete without Tamora Pierce’s beloved Song of the Lioness series. Alanna: The First Adventure follows Alanna of Trebond as she disguises herself as a boy to become a knight in the kingdom of Tortall. Alanna’s determination, cleverness, and fiery spirit made her a classic heroine for generations of readers—and the series never loses sight of the complexities of growing up.

The books explore themes of gender, agency, and friendship, challenging social norms without ever sacrificing action or fantasy. Pierce’s legacy endures partly because Alanna’s journey resonates with anyone who’s ever dreamed of being more than the world expects.

4. A Court of Thorns and Roses by Sarah J. Maas

Sarah J. Maas’s A Court of Thorns and Roses series has become a cultural juggernaut, fusing fae politics, dangerous magic, and high-stakes romance. Feyre Archeron begins as a mortal girl struggling to provide for her family—and is soon swept into the magical and treacherous world of the Fae.

Feyre’s story is one of transformation: from survivor to warrior, prey to huntress, captive to queenmaker. Maas’s blend of lush settings, steamy romance, and a heroine forced to confront her own limits appeals to readers hungry for both fantasy and emotional depth.

5. The Bear and the Nightingale by Katherine Arden

Set against the backdrop of medieval Russia, The Bear and the Nightingale pulses with ancient folklore and haunting magic. Vasilisa “Vasya” Petrovna can see beings others cannot—spirits of hearth, land, and forest—and her connection to old traditions draws her into a clash between Christianity and the fading ways.

Vasya’s courage, curiosity, and wildness set her apart. Arden creates a subtle yet powerful arc for her protagonist: Vasya stands between worlds, challenging convention and refusing to abandon her identity for safety. If you love atmospheric fantasy spun from myth and fairytale, Vasya’s story offers both a chilling winter and the promise of spring.

6. Uprooted by Naomi Novik

Naomi Novik’s Uprooted blends Eastern European folklore and fairy tale sensibility into a tightly-woven fantasy tale. Agnieszka, a clumsy village girl, has always expected her beautiful best friend to be chosen by the Dragon—a powerful, reclusive sorcerer who protects their land in exchange for a human tribute.

But when Agnieszka is picked instead, she’s thrust into a web of magic, monsters, and political intrigue. Novik crafts a protagonist whose apparent ordinariness masks wells of strength and compassion. The dark forest at the novel’s heart, and Agnieszka’s evolving magic, capture the thrill of ancient stories retold for a new age.

7. A Wizard of Earthsea by Ursula K. Le Guin

Ged may be the titular wizard in A Wizard of Earthsea, but Ursula K. Le Guin’s Earthsea Cycle is marked by other powerful protagonists. Chief among them: Tenar, introduced in The Tombs of Atuan. Tenar, also known as Arha, is chosen as the high priestess of a mysterious cult from childhood, her identity stripped and rebuilt by those around her.

Le Guin’s nuanced portrayal of autonomy, identity, and compassion makes Tenar’s perspective essential to fantasy literature. Her journey from imprisoned child-priestess to self-possessed woman highlights the struggles—and triumphs—of finding one’s place in an often alienating world.

8. Sabriel by Garth Nix

Necromancy, lost kingdoms, and a protagonist with the will to cross into Death itself? Garth Nix’s Old Kingdom series is at its best with Sabriel, as its young heroine inherits the mantle of Abhorsen—a necromancer who lays the dead to rest.

Sabriel is both resourceful and vulnerable—far from invulnerable, she draws on savvy, courage, and compassion, matching wits with ancient evils instead of brute force alone. The worldbuilding is crisp and imaginative, blending boarding school echoes with chilling fantasy. Sabriel’s journey proves that bravery is as much about heart as it is about magic.

9. City of Brass by S.A. Chakraborty

Set in 18th-century Cairo and the legendary city of Daevabad, City of Brass introduces Nahri, a con artist and healer with mysterious powers, as she accidentally summons a djinn warrior. Thrust into a realm of politics, intrigue, and simmering magic, Nahri must navigate a court defined by secrets and shifting allegiances.

Chakraborty’s heroine is shrewd, ambitious, and deeply conflicted, creating a compelling lens through which to experience lush worldbuilding and tangled intrigue. The trilogy’s exploration of power, heritage, and the costs of revolution is anchored by Nahri’s evolution—a journey filled with as much danger as wonder.

10. Mistborn: The Final Empire by Brandon Sanderson

Brandon Sanderson’s Mistborn series turns fantasy conventions on their head, and it’s all thanks to Vin—a street thief who discovers extraordinary magical abilities when she falls in with a band of rebels. Vin’s growth from wary survivor to legendary Mistborn—and eventual leader—drives the trilogy’s twists and turns.

Sanderson excels at magic systems and epic battles. But Vin’s heart, doubts, and determination give the story its emotional resonance. She’s a heroine whose choices ripple outward, reshaping an empire and reimagining what it means to be powerful.

11. Seraphina by Rachel Hartman

Dragons take human form and courts buzz with suspicion in Rachel Hartman’s Seraphina. The titular protagonist is a gifted musician with a dangerous secret: half human, half dragon, her very existence could destabilize a fragile peace.

Seraphina’s journey, shaped by contingent identity and uncertainty, explores what it means to belong—and how music, empathy, and bravery can triumph even in the shadow of war. Hartman’s lyrical prose and inventive setting highlight the power of difference and defiance.

12. The Bone Season by Samantha Shannon

In a future London stalked by oppressive authorities, Paige Mahoney’s psychic powers make her both valuable and dangerous. The Bone Season launches a sprawling series where clairvoyant outcasts tangle with rebellions, otherworldly conspiracies, and their own haunted pasts.

Paige shines as a protagonist with grit and rebellious spirit, never content to accept the roles assigned to her. The intricate, layered world is a treat, but it’s Paige’s personal stakes and stubborn daring that elevate the story.

13. The Queen of the Tearling by Erika Johansen

Young Kelsea Raleigh Glynn, the last heir to her murdered mother’s throne, comes of age in a world rife with danger and deception. Erika Johansen’s The Queen of the Tearling tells a story about the burdens of inherited power and the quest for justice.

Kelsea is not your typical fantasy queen: insecure, bookish, and driven by idealism, she is forced to transform—sometimes painfully—into the ruler her people need. The trilogy’s blend of political intrigue and raw character growth resonates long after the last page.

14. An Ember in the Ashes by Sabaa Tahir

Sabaa Tahir’s acclaimed An Ember in the Ashes series stars Laia, a slave in a brutal, Rome-inspired empire. When her brother is arrested, Laia infiltrates a deadly military academy as a spy, drawing on very real terror and hope to survive.

Laia’s perspective brings immediacy and high stakes to the narrative, but her evolution—from frightened outsider to resolute leader—feels all the more dramatic against the novel’s grim, pulse-pounding world. Tahir’s series is unflinching in its violence and injustice, but steeped in undeniable heart.

15. Circe by Madeline Miller

Rooted in Greek myth, Madeline Miller’s Circe transforms a marginal enchantress into the hero of her own story. Left isolated on her island, Circe encounters monsters, mortals, and gods—navigating the price of power and the cost of compassion.

Miller’s lush prose and psychological insight reframe an ancient tale, making Circe’s journey a meditation on loneliness, strength, and self-discovery. Less about battles than about the transformation that comes from enduring, learning, and choosing to stay true to oneself, it’s a modern classic.

Why Female Protagonists Matter in Fantasy

These novels deliver more than escapism; they offer a mirror—and sometimes a challenge—to the world outside the page. When the heroine’s voice is at the center, the fantasy genre gets to bend, break, and remake the old archetypes: women as warriors and peacemakers, queens and wanderers, mages and monsters.

For readers, this means deeper empathy and a richer tapestry of stories to inhabit. Through protagonists who defy convention—sometimes quietly, sometimes at the tip of a sword—fantasy invites us to imagine new kinds of heroism. Whether you’re seeking magical quests, political intrigue, or character-driven sagas, these novels open doors to dazzling worlds and unforgettable journeys.

If you’re new to fantasy, start with one that calls to your curiosity—a novel with dragons, or dark secrets, or a touch of romance. If you’re a lifelong genre fan, there’s always a new heroine waiting to surprise you. The only question is: which adventure will you choose next?
