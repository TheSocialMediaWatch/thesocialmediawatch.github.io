Survival Skills: Making Cordage from Natural Materials


Cordage is an essential survival skill that dates back to ancient times. It involves creating strong and durable ropes, strings, and cords from natural materials found in the wilderness. In a survival situation, cordage can be used for various purposes, including building shelters, creating traps, and making tools. In this blog post, we will explore the art of making cordage from natural materials and how it can be a valuable skill for anyone venturing into the great outdoors.

Understanding the Importance of Cordage

When you find yourself in a survival situation, having a reliable source of cordage can make all the difference. It enables you to improvise and adapt to your environment, increasing your chances of survival. Natural cordage has been used by our ancestors for centuries, and its versatility and strength continue to prove invaluable in modern-day survival scenarios.

Selecting the Right Natural Materials

The key to making strong cordage lies in selecting the right natural materials. Different plants can be used, each with its own unique characteristics. Some common choices include:

  1. Stinging Nettle: Known for its fibrous stems, stinging nettle is an excellent choice for making cordage. Its fibers are strong and durable, providing reliable strength for your ropes.

  2. Dogbane: Dogbane, also known as Indian hemp, is a perennial plant that can be found in North America. Its long, slender stems yield fibers that are strong and flexible, making them suitable for various survival applications.

  3. Yucca: Yucca plants are abundant in arid regions and have been used for centuries to make cordage. Their leaves are rich in strong, flexible fibers that can be harvested and processed into ropes or strings.

  4. Cattail: Cattails are a versatile plant that can be found near bodies of water. Their leaves and stems can be used to create cordage that is relatively strong and durable.

Harvesting and Processing Natural Materials

Once you have identified the right plants for making cordage, it’s important to harvest them properly. Ensure that you have the necessary permissions and follow ethical guidelines when collecting plant materials. Here’s a step-by-step guide to processing natural materials for cordage:

  1. Gathering: Collect a sufficient amount of plant material, ensuring that it is long, straight, and free from any damage or decay.

  2. Drying: Allow the collected materials to dry naturally, preferably in a well-ventilated area. This will help remove excess moisture and make the fibers easier to work with.

  3. Stripping: Strip the outer bark or skin from the plant material using your fingers or a sharp tool. This will expose the fibers within.

  4. Separating: Separate the fibers by gently pulling them apart. Remove any unwanted debris or weak fibers to ensure the strength of your cordage.

  5. Twisting: Begin twisting the fibers together in a clockwise or counterclockwise direction, depending on your preference. This twisting action will create a strong and durable cord.

  6. Braiding: For even stronger cordage, you can braid three or more strands of twisted fibers together. This technique adds an extra level of strength and durability to your cordage.

Testing and Using Your Cordage

Before relying on your cordage in a survival situation, it’s crucial to test its strength. You can do this by subjecting it to various loads and evaluating its performance. Once you are confident in its strength, you can utilize your cordage for a multitude of purposes, such as:

  • Building shelters: Use your cordage to secure branches and create a sturdy shelter to protect yourself from the elements.

  • Setting traps: Construct snares and traps to catch small game for sustenance.

  • Making tools: Use your cordage to fashion tools such as fishing nets, bowstrings, or even makeshift bow drills for fire-making.

Conclusion

Mastering the skill of making cordage from natural materials is a valuable asset for anyone interested in survival and bushcraft. By understanding the process of selecting, harvesting, and processing natural materials, you can create strong and reliable cordage that will serve you well in the wilderness. So, why not take the time to learn this ancient craft? It may just save your life one day.

Remember, cordage-making is an art that requires practice and patience. So, get out into nature, explore the possibilities of natural materials, and embark on a journey of self-sufficiency and survival.
