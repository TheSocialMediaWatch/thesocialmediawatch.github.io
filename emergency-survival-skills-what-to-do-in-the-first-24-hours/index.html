Emergency Survival Skills: What to Do in the First 24 Hours


When disaster strikes, whether it’s a natural calamity, a sudden accident, or any unforeseen emergency, the first 24 hours are often the most critical. What you do in these initial moments can dramatically influence your survival chances and how well you can recover in the long run. The key to handling an emergency effectively lies in having the right survival skills, maintaining a calm mind, and knowing the proper steps to take immediately.

Here’s a comprehensive guide to essential survival skills and actions that should be taken during the first 24 hours of an emergency.

1. Assess the Situation

The first step in any survival scenario is to evaluate the situation and stay calm. Panic will only hinder your ability to think clearly. Take a moment to assess your surroundings and identify potential hazards or dangers. Look for:

  • Immediate threats such as fire, structural damage, or exposed electrical wires.
  • Safe spaces where you can shelter from environmental hazards or further risks.
  • Escape routes or ways to exit the danger zone, if necessary.

This moment of assessment is essential for understanding what actions you need to take next.

2. Ensure Your Safety and Shelter

Once you’ve assessed the situation, your priority is to ensure your safety and find shelter. If you are indoors during an emergency, stay inside until you know it is safe to leave. Evacuating too early or without adequate preparation can expose you to unnecessary risks. Here are some tips on creating a safe space:

  • Create a barrier between yourself and potential threats. If you’re in a building that has been damaged, stay away from windows and doors, and seek cover under sturdy furniture like tables or desks.
  • If outdoors, find shelter quickly. Look for natural protection such as caves, large trees, or any structure that can offer protection from harsh weather conditions.
  • Stay warm and dry. Hypothermia can set in quickly, so it’s important to insulate yourself from the cold. If you’re trapped in a place with no warmth, create makeshift insulation using clothing, blankets, or anything you can find.

Sheltering in place can be a life-saving measure when it’s unsafe to move around, but remember to stay alert and listen for any news updates or changes in the situation.

3. Communicate and Signal for Help

After securing your safety and shelter, your next task is to communicate with others and signal for help. In many emergency situations, you may be cut off from conventional communication methods. This is where knowing how to use alternative signaling techniques can be vital.

  • Use your phone if you can. Dial emergency services and provide as much information as possible. If phone networks are down, text messages might be more reliable than voice calls.
  • If you’re unable to use your phone, use loud sounds or visible signals to attract attention. Shouting, blowing a whistle, or using a mirror to reflect sunlight can help you get noticed.
  • Fire and smoke can be effective signals to alert rescuers of your location. If safe, creating a small, controlled fire can be a beacon of hope.
  • Use landmarks. If you’re stranded outdoors, identify landmarks that could help rescuers locate you. Mark trails or positions that others can follow.

Communication and signaling are critical in connecting with rescuers and coordinating with others who are also affected by the emergency.

4. Find Water and Food

Within the first 24 hours of an emergency, staying hydrated and nourished becomes paramount. Dehydration and starvation can quickly lead to physical and mental exhaustion, making it more difficult to survive and think clearly.

Water

  • Locate clean water sources. If you’re at home, tap water may still be available, but ensure it’s clean. In wilderness situations, avoid drinking from stagnant sources like ponds. Use any filtration methods you have at your disposal, such as water purifying tablets, a filter, or boiling water to kill bacteria and viruses.
  • Conserve your water. If water is limited, ration it. Avoid drinking too much at once, as this can quickly deplete your supplies.

Food

  • Foraging for food can be an option in certain situations, especially if you’re stranded outdoors. Look for edible plants, insects, or even small animals that can provide calories.
  • Focus on calorie-dense foods. If you have access to stored food, prioritize those that are high in energy and will keep you sustained, such as nuts, dried fruits, granola bars, and jerky.
  • Don’t waste energy searching for food if water is your immediate priority. A well-hydrated body can survive longer without food, but not without water.

If you have any food or water supplies with you, conserve them as much as possible while waiting for rescue or further instructions.

5. Provide First Aid

Injuries, whether minor or severe, are a common occurrence during emergencies. Knowing basic first aid can save lives and prevent injuries from worsening. Assess your body and the bodies of those around you for any injuries, and treat them promptly.

  • Stop bleeding. Apply direct pressure to wounds to stop bleeding. Elevate limbs if necessary.
  • Prevent shock. Lay the injured person down, keep them warm, and elevate their legs if possible to improve blood circulation.
  • Treat burns, cuts, and bruises. Use available supplies, such as bandages, gauze, or clean cloths, to cover and protect wounds from infection.
  • CPR. If someone stops breathing, CPR can be a lifesaving skill. Knowing how to perform chest compressions can keep someone alive until help arrives.

A well-equipped first-aid kit should be a staple of any survival kit. Ensure you have it on hand, and know how to use its contents effectively.

6. Stay Informed

In modern emergencies, keeping up with news and updates is crucial. If you’re able to access a phone, radio, or any device, make it a priority to stay informed about the situation.

  • Listen for updates from emergency services or local authorities to understand evacuation routes, shelters, or any changing conditions.
  • Social media can also be an invaluable tool for gathering information, especially if official channels are unavailable. Follow trusted news sources or emergency accounts for live updates.
  • Local emergency broadcasts on radios or TV stations provide essential instructions for survival and evacuation.

Understanding the broader scope of the emergency can help you make better decisions about your next steps and increase your chances of survival.

7. Manage Stress and Mental Health

Emotional and psychological resilience plays a crucial role in survival. In high-stress situations, it’s easy to panic or become overwhelmed by fear. The ability to manage these feelings will help you stay focused and take decisive actions when needed.

  • Keep a clear mind by focusing on practical tasks. Avoid dwelling too much on worst-case scenarios.
  • Take deep breaths to calm your nerves and center yourself. Mindful breathing can help prevent overwhelming feelings of panic.
  • Stay positive. Even in the most dire situations, a positive mindset can make all the difference. Optimism fosters resilience and improves your ability to adapt to changing circumstances.

If you’re with others, support one another by sharing duties, talking about your feelings, and encouraging each other. Teamwork can create a sense of unity and reduce the psychological toll of the situation.

8. Plan for the Next 24 Hours

After the first 24 hours, it’s important to reassess your situation. You might have found shelter, water, and food, but your circumstances will evolve. Planning ahead is key to making sure you remain safe and ready for whatever comes next.

  • Plan an escape route if your shelter becomes unsafe or if help doesn’t arrive.
  • Monitor your resources. Reevaluate how much water and food you have, and make plans to replenish them.
  • Stay alert. Continue listening for signals of nearby rescue teams or threats. You need to be ready to act at a moment’s notice.

Long-term survival depends on the steps you take in the first 24 hours. By maintaining a steady focus on the present moment and prioritizing your needs, you will improve your chances of overcoming the emergency.

Conclusion

The first 24 hours in any emergency can make the difference between life and death. By staying calm, assessing your situation, ensuring your safety, and securing necessary resources, you lay the foundation for survival. Remember, preparation is key. Knowing these essential emergency survival skills ahead of time can help you react quickly and effectively in the critical moments after a disaster.
