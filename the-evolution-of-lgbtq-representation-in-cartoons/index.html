The Evolution of LGBTQ+ Representation in Cartoons


As society has evolved, so too has the representation of the queer community in cartoons. From simple and subtle hints in early cartoons to fully realized, dimensional characters, the journey of LGBTQ+ representation in this medium has been an interesting and sometimes frustrating process. This evolution hasn’t always been linear, nor have there been lack of setbacks, but it is crucial to celebrate the strides made in representation.

The early days of animation saw little to no representation of queer individuals. However, as the 20th century progressed, and conversations about queerness entered mainstream discourse, it became more common to see same-sex pairings or queer-coded characters. In these instances, the subtlety of these cues was often a double-edged sword. While it allowed representation to sneak by censors, it also made it difficult for viewers to fully grasp and appreciate the relationship dynamics at play.

One of the earliest successful examples of a same-sex relationship in animated form can be found in the Japanese anime Graveyard Kitaro, which aired from 2007 to 2009. The hesitation to explicitly state the nature of the relationship foregrounded ambiguity, with viewers left to interpret and decipher the characters’ dynamic.

The hesitancy to explicitly highlight queer relationships extended to other forms of American animation. For instance, it wasn’t until powering through cultural and social hurdles that Adventure Time’s Princess Bubblegum and Marceline Malcoix’s relationship was depicted more directly.

The introduction of more diverse, queer characters in animation has also been an essential aspect of this evolution. These characters offer representation for young viewers, allowing them to imagine and empathize with members of the queer community. One such character is Steven Universe’s Garnet, providing vital representation not just for queer Gems (a concept within the show), but for queer folks of color.

The importance of diverse representation should not be underestimated. While these portrayals are far from perfect, they pave the way for a future where animation not only features but positively celebrates the rich tapestry of queer experience.

Despite this progress, there’s still work to be done. As recent as a few years ago, shows like She-Ra and the Princesses of Power made strides in queer representation. However, an attempt at an in-universe “Straight Pride” celebration inevitably led to backlash and a rethink, highlighting the importance of cultural sensitivity in representation.

In conclusion, while there have been undoubtedly positive advancements in the representation of the queer community within the animation medium, there is still a long way to go. Much of this progress is dependent upon a continuous push for more comprehensive and holistic representation, as well as greater awareness regarding cultural sensitivity in storytelling. We still have much to learn and explore within the ever-evolving landscape of queer representation in cartoons.
