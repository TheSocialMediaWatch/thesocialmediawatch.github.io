The Mythology of Ancient Anatolia: Hittite Gods and Goddesses


The ancient civilization of Anatolia, located in modern-day Turkey, holds a rich and fascinating mythology that often gets overshadowed by the more well-known mythologies of Greece and Egypt. The Hittite gods and goddesses, who were worshipped by the Hittite people during the Bronze Age, offer a unique insight into the beliefs and cultural practices of this ancient civilization.

The Pantheon of Hittite Gods and Goddesses

The Hittite pantheon consisted of numerous gods and goddesses, each with their own distinct powers and domains. Among the most prominent deities were:

  1. Teshub: The chief god of the Hittite pantheon, Teshub was associated with storms, thunder, and the weather. He was often depicted holding a thunderbolt, symbolizing his power over the forces of nature.

  2. Hebat: As the wife of Teshub, Hebat was the queen of the gods and the goddess of fertility and love. She was often depicted with a lion, symbolizing her protective nature.

  3. Kumarbi: Known as the father of the gods, Kumarbi was a primordial deity associated with creation and chaos. He was often portrayed as a serpent or a dragon, representing his immense power.

  4. Inara: The goddess of wild animals and the hunt, Inara was often depicted with a bow and arrow. She was also associated with healing and protection.

  5. Shaushka: As the goddess of love and beauty, Shaushka represented desire and passion. She was often depicted with a mirror, symbolizing her fascination with her own appearance.

Mythological Tales and Legends

The mythology of the Hittites is filled with captivating tales and legends that shed light on the relationships between the gods, as well as their interactions with mortals. One such tale is the myth of “The Storm God and the Dragon”.

In this myth, Teshub, the Storm God, battles a monstrous dragon named Illuyanka. The dragon, who has stolen the Storm God’s thunder and rain, poses a threat to the entire world. Teshub, with the help of other gods, engages in a fierce battle with Illuyanka, ultimately defeating the dragon and restoring balance to the natural order.

Another intriguing legend is “The Song of Kumarbi”, which tells the story of the rise and fall of the gods. Kumarbi, in an attempt to gain power, bites off the genitals of Anu, the sky god, and swallows them. From this act, three generations of gods are born, each one overthrowing the previous generation until the final god, Teshub, emerges as the ruler of the heavens.

Religious Practices and Rituals

The Hittites engaged in various religious practices and rituals to honor their gods and goddesses. One such ritual was “The Sacred Marriage”, a ceremony held to unite the divine couple, Teshub and Hebat. This ritual symbolized the union of the sky and the earth, ensuring fertility and prosperity for the Hittite people.

Another important religious practice was the offering of sacrifices to the gods. Animals, such as bulls and sheep, were commonly sacrificed to appease the deities and seek their favor. These sacrifices were performed by priests who acted as intermediaries between the mortal world and the divine realm.

Legacy and Influence

Although the Hittite civilization eventually declined and disappeared from history, their mythology and religious beliefs have left a lasting impact. Many aspects of Hittite mythology can be seen in subsequent civilizations of the region, including the Phrygians and the later Greeks.

The rich tapestry of Hittite mythology continues to captivate scholars and enthusiasts alike, offering a glimpse into the beliefs and cultural practices of an ancient civilization.
