An Introduction to Sherry: The Forgotten Wine


Sherry is a fortified wine that has long been overshadowed by its more popular counterparts, like Port and Madeira. However, this unique beverage has a rich history, diverse styles, and an exceptional pairing versatility that make it deserving of a renaissance in the world of wine. In this post, we will explore the fascinating journey of Sherry, its production methods, the various styles it encompasses, and how to appreciate this “forgotten” wine.

The History of Sherry

Sherry’s roots can be traced back to the ancient Phoenicians, who established the first vineyards in the region of Jerez in southern Spain, over 3,000 years ago. The city of Jerez de la Frontera, situated in the Andalusian province of Cádiz, became the heart of Sherry production. With its unique climate and soil, the region was ideal for growing the Palomino grape, which would become the primary grape variety used in Sherry production.

Throughout the centuries, Sherry gained popularity not only in Spain but also in international markets. By the 16th century, Sherry was being exported to England, where it became a favorite among nobility. The British even played a significant role in shaping the Sherry industry, introducing the use of barrels for aging and fortifying the wine with brandy. However, the 20th century saw a decline in Sherry’s popularity, as tastes shifted towards lighter wines, leading to its current status as a “forgotten” wine.

Understanding Sherry Production

The unique production process of Sherry is one of the factors that sets it apart from other wines. The primary grape used is Palomino, though Pedro Ximénez and Moscatel are also utilized, particularly for sweeter styles. The process begins with the harvest, typically taking place in late summer. After the grapes are pressed, the juice is fermented in stainless steel tanks or traditional oak barrels.

Following fermentation, the wine undergoes a fortification process, where grape spirit is added to increase the alcohol content. This step is crucial in defining the style of Sherry produced. There are two main categories of Sherry: biological aging and oxidative aging.

Biological Aging

Biological aging occurs when the wine is protected from oxygen by a layer of yeast called “flor.” This flor develops on the surface of the wine in barrels, creating a unique environment that imparts specific flavors and aromas. Wines that undergo biological aging are typically lighter and crisper, with styles including Fino and Manzanilla.

Fino is a pale, dry Sherry characterized by its delicate aromas of almonds, sea breeze, and fresh bread. Manzanilla, produced in the coastal town of Sanlúcar de Barrameda, shares similar traits but has a slightly salinity due to its proximity to the ocean.

Oxidative Aging

In contrast, oxidative aging allows the wine to come into contact with oxygen, resulting in a richer, fuller-bodied product. The absence of flor leads to a darker color and more complex flavors. Styles include Amontillado, Oloroso, and Cream Sherry.

Amontillado is a unique style that begins with biological aging before being exposed to oxygen, offering a balance of nutty and fruity notes. Oloroso is a deep, dark Sherry with robust flavors of dried fruits, chocolate, and spices. Cream Sherry, a blend of Oloroso and sweet wines like Pedro Ximénez, is rich and luscious, often enjoyed as a dessert wine.

The Diverse Styles of Sherry

Sherry is often classified into various styles, each with distinct characteristics and flavor profiles. Here’s a closer look at some of the most popular types:

Fino

Fino Sherry is the lightest and driest of the styles, known for its crisp freshness and subtle complexity. It typically presents flavors of green olives, almonds, and a hint of citrus. Ideal for tapas, Fino pairs beautifully with light seafood dishes, olives, and nuts.

Manzanilla

Manzanilla is similar to Fino but offers a saltier and more intense flavor profile due to its coastal origins. It has a bright, refreshing quality that makes it an excellent companion for seafood, particularly fried fish and shellfish.

Amontillado

Amontillado is a fascinating hybrid, combining the characteristics of both Fino and Oloroso. It begins as a Fino, aged under flor, and then undergoes oxidative aging. This results in a complex wine with nutty, caramel, and dried fruit notes. Amontillado pairs well with rich foods like roasted meats and strong cheeses.

Oloroso

Oloroso Sherry is rich and full-bodied, known for its intense flavors of dark fruit, nuts, and spices. With its higher alcohol content and absence of flor, Oloroso can stand up to boldly flavored dishes. It pairs excellently with roasted meats, grilled vegetables, and even chocolate desserts.

Cream Sherry

Cream Sherry is a sweetened version of Oloroso, blended with Pedro Ximénez. Its lush sweetness makes it a delightful after-dinner drink, often enjoyed with desserts like chocolate or nut-based dishes.

How to Taste Sherry

Tasting Sherry can be an enlightening experience, revealing its complex flavors and aromas. Here’s a simple guide to help you appreciate Sherry like a pro:

  1. Choose the Right Glass: Use a small wine glass to concentrate the aromas. For Fino and Manzanilla, a traditional copita is ideal, while a larger glass works well for Oloroso and Cream Sherry.

  2. Observe the Color: Take a moment to observe the color of the Sherry. Fino and Manzanilla will be pale straw-colored, while Oloroso and Amontillado will have deeper amber tones.

  3. Swirl and Smell: Swirl the wine gently and take a deep breath to appreciate its aromas. Note the different scents that come forward—nutty, fruity, or even floral notes.

  4. Taste: Take a small sip, allowing the wine to coat your palate. Pay attention to the flavors, acidity, and finish. Sherry often has a unique umami quality that makes it stand out.

  5. Pairing: Experiment with food pairings. Sherry’s versatility allows it to complement a wide variety of dishes, from tapas to hearty mains.

Sherry and Food Pairings

One of the most appealing aspects of Sherry is its remarkable versatility when pairing with food. Here are a few classic pairings to elevate your dining experience:

Appetizers

  • Fino with Tapas: Pair Fino with olives, marinated anchovies, and patatas bravas for a classic Spanish experience.
  • Manzanilla with Seafood: Enjoy Manzanilla with shrimp, calamari, or oysters to enhance the flavors of the ocean.

Main Dishes

  • Amontillado with Roasted Chicken: The nuttiness of Amontillado complements the rich flavors of roasted chicken and herbs.
  • Oloroso with Grilled Lamb: Oloroso’s bold flavors pair beautifully with grilled meats, making it an excellent choice for lamb or steak.

Desserts

  • Cream Sherry with Chocolate: The sweetness of Cream Sherry makes it a perfect match for chocolate desserts or rich cheeses.
  • Pedro Ximénez with Ice Cream: Drizzle Pedro Ximénez over vanilla ice cream for a simple yet decadent dessert.

The Revival of Sherry

In recent years, there has been a resurgence of interest in Sherry, especially among younger wine enthusiasts. Several factors contribute to this revival:

Innovative Producers

New, innovative producers are experimenting with traditional methods and modern techniques to create unique expressions of Sherry. These wines often reflect the terroir of the Jerez region, showcasing the unique characteristics of the local grapes and environment.

Greater Awareness

Social media and wine blogs have played a significant role in raising awareness about Sherry. Wine lovers are discovering the diversity of styles and flavors available and sharing their experiences online.

Trendy Cocktails

Sherry is making its way into the cocktail scene, with bartenders using it to create refreshing and complex drinks. From sherry cobblers to low-alcohol spritzers, Sherry is finding its place in the contemporary beverage landscape.

Conclusion

Sherry, once considered a forgotten wine, is making a comeback with its unique characteristics, rich history, and versatility. With various styles ranging from the dry and crisp Fino to the sweet and luscious Cream Sherry, there’s a flavor profile to suit every palate. By exploring the fascinating world of Sherry, wine enthusiasts can appreciate the complexities and joys this remarkable beverage offers.

So, whether you’re a seasoned wine aficionado or just starting your journey, don’t overlook Sherry—this delightful fortified wine is ready to reclaim its place on your table!
