How to Build Tension in a Fantasy Story


Tension is one of the most critical elements in any story, especially in fantasy. It keeps readers hooked, urging them to turn the pages and stay invested in the narrative. Tension in fantasy stories isn’t just about physical danger or epic battles between good and evil; it’s about creating an atmosphere that makes readers feel on edge, even in moments of peace. The best fantasy stories have an underlying sense of suspense, conflict, and anticipation that makes every page feel charged with potential.

In this post, we’ll explore how to effectively build tension in a fantasy story, ensuring that your readers remain on the edge of their seats.

Understanding Tension in Fantasy

Before diving into how to create tension, it’s important to understand what it is. Tension in storytelling refers to the feeling of uncertainty, unease, or expectation that keeps the reader invested. It can come from various sources, such as:

  • Conflict: External or internal struggles between characters, races, or forces.
  • Uncertainty: When readers don’t know what will happen next.
  • Suspense: Keeping readers in anticipation of a key event or outcome.
  • Mystery: Introducing questions that need answers.
  • Moral Dilemmas: Forcing characters to make tough choices with no easy answers.

In fantasy, the world itself can play a significant role in creating tension. The unique setting, magical systems, and mythical creatures all present opportunities to enhance the tension. However, regardless of the genre, the techniques of building tension remain consistent.

1. Create High Stakes

One of the most effective ways to build tension in your fantasy story is by establishing high stakes. If nothing of importance is at risk, then the story will feel flat, and tension will quickly dissipate. The higher the stakes, the more tension you can create.

In a fantasy world, the stakes don’t necessarily have to be about life and death—though those can work too. The stakes can also involve:

  • The fate of entire kingdoms or empires.
  • The survival of magical creatures or races.
  • The success or failure of an ancient prophecy.
  • The balance of good and evil in the world.

When these stakes are introduced early on and maintained throughout the story, readers are left wondering how the characters will overcome the enormous challenges they face. This uncertainty is a key ingredient in creating tension.

2. Introduce Conflicting Motivations

A compelling way to increase tension is by giving your characters conflicting motivations. In fantasy, this often happens on a grand scale. Characters may be caught between different factions, moral dilemmas, or loyalty to conflicting ideals.

Consider a scenario where a hero is forced to choose between saving their homeland and honoring a promise made to a close friend. The tension arises from the emotional weight of the choice and the consequences that follow. If the character’s decision could lead to an irreversible change in the world, the tension will only escalate.

Creating internal conflicts within your characters is also a great way to heighten tension. A protagonist might feel torn between their duty and their personal desires, adding layers of uncertainty about their actions. This conflict gives readers a sense of anticipation as they try to figure out what the character will do next.

3. Utilize Cliffhangers and Unanswered Questions

Another powerful tool in building tension is the use of cliffhangers. These can be chapter-ending moments that leave readers hanging, making them want to continue reading to find out what happens next. In fantasy, cliffhangers are especially effective when tied to magical or supernatural events that leave the characters (and readers) unsure of the outcome.

For example, imagine a scenario where the protagonist is about to enter an ancient ruin, and the last line of a chapter is: “The door creaked open, revealing an empty hall, but something was waiting in the shadows.” This creates immediate tension, as readers are desperate to know what lies ahead.

Cliffhangers are best used when there are unanswered questions that intrigue the reader. These questions can be related to the fate of characters, mysteries in the world, or unresolved plot points. The more critical the unanswered question, the greater the tension it creates. Readers will feel compelled to keep reading in hopes of uncovering the answer.

4. Slow-Burn Suspense

Building tension doesn’t always require dramatic action or immediate danger. Sometimes, the most effective form of tension comes from a slow-burn buildup. This method involves gradually revealing information, making readers feel increasingly uneasy as the story progresses.

In a fantasy setting, you can build this kind of suspense by:

  • Dropping subtle hints about an impending disaster, like strange weather patterns or cryptic warnings from ancient beings.
  • Introducing characters who seem too perfect or too ominous, leading readers to question their true intentions.
  • Revealing the flaws and weaknesses of seemingly invincible characters, making readers wonder when these vulnerabilities will come to the forefront.

Slow-burn suspense works by keeping readers in a state of uncertainty. It’s not about immediate danger, but rather about the creeping sense that something terrible is about to happen. This technique keeps readers invested, as they’re constantly anticipating the moment when everything will unravel.

5. Use the Setting to Your Advantage

The setting in a fantasy story is more than just a backdrop; it can be a source of tension in and of itself. In many fantasy worlds, settings are often filled with magic, danger, and mystery, which can be harnessed to build tension.

Here are some ways you can use your setting to enhance tension:

  • Unfamiliar Worlds: If your characters are in a strange, unfamiliar world, this creates immediate tension. The unknown is a powerful force in storytelling, and fantasy settings are ripe for exploration. As your characters encounter bizarre creatures or magic they don’t understand, the sense of unease can escalate.
  • Dangerous Landscapes: Imagine your characters traveling through a dangerous, magical forest where the trees seem to watch them. Or perhaps they journey across a wasteland filled with monsters and ancient traps. The environment itself can be a constant source of tension, as the characters must navigate its hazards.
  • Magical Rules: In many fantasy stories, magic is an unpredictable force. By introducing elements of magical unpredictability or dangerous magic systems, you can create tension. Perhaps there’s a curse that no one fully understands, or a powerful artifact that may not be what it seems.

By carefully choosing how you present your setting, you can ensure that the world your characters inhabit contributes to the overall tension of the story.

6. Incorporate Time Pressure

A sense of time running out can also significantly increase tension. In a fantasy story, this could manifest as:

  • A deadline to stop an impending disaster.
  • A prophecy that is supposed to be fulfilled at a specific time.
  • A character racing against time to save another person or complete a mission.

The closer the characters get to the deadline, the more pressure they will feel, and the more tension the reader will experience. This pressure also creates room for mistakes, which can heighten tension further. Characters who are rushing or under pressure are more likely to make poor decisions, and these mistakes can lead to dire consequences.

7. Raise the Stakes with Antagonists

A well-developed antagonist is crucial for building tension. Whether it’s a dark sorcerer, an oppressive regime, or a rival faction, the antagonist’s actions and motivations must feel like a real threat to the protagonist and the world.

The best antagonists in fantasy are those whose goals conflict directly with the protagonist’s, creating a sense of opposition that keeps tension high throughout the story. But beyond this direct conflict, a truly great antagonist will also have the power to create doubt and fear. For example:

  • A villain who can manipulate people’s minds, making it difficult to trust anyone.
  • An antagonist with a vast network of spies, who can strike at any time.
  • An enemy whose ultimate plan is shrouded in mystery, with only hints scattered throughout the story.

The more unpredictable and dangerous the antagonist, the greater the tension. Readers will constantly wonder when and how the antagonist will make their next move, keeping them on edge until the final showdown.

Conclusion

Building tension in a fantasy story is about creating an emotional rollercoaster for your readers. By establishing high stakes, introducing conflicting motivations, and using suspenseful techniques like cliffhangers and slow-burn tension, you can keep your audience hooked. Don’t forget the power of your setting and antagonist, and always remember that tension is about more than just physical conflict. It’s about keeping readers on the edge of their seats, eagerly awaiting what comes next. By using these techniques, you’ll be able to craft a fantasy story that captivates readers from beginning to end.
