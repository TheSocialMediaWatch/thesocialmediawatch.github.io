Legal Steps to Take If You're Wrongfully Terminated


Wrongful termination can be one of the most stressful and confusing experiences an employee can face. Being fired from a job can feel like an attack on your livelihood, but when it happens unfairly, it can be devastating. Understanding your legal rights and knowing the steps to take can help you regain control of the situation and potentially recover compensation for damages caused by the wrongful termination.

In this post, we’ll explore the key legal steps to take if you’ve been wrongfully terminated. This guide will give you the knowledge you need to protect your rights and pursue the best course of action to rectify the situation.

Understanding Wrongful Termination

Before diving into the legal steps, it’s crucial to first understand what constitutes wrongful termination. A wrongful termination occurs when an employee is fired for illegal reasons, which may include but are not limited to:

  • Violation of Employment Contracts: If you have a written contract that specifies the terms of your employment and the conditions under which you can be terminated, firing you in violation of this contract is considered wrongful.

  • Discrimination: If you were fired based on race, gender, religion, age, disability, or other protected characteristics, this is considered discrimination and can lead to a wrongful termination lawsuit.

  • Retaliation: If you were fired for engaging in legally protected activities like reporting workplace harassment, filing a workers’ compensation claim, or whistleblowing on illegal activities, this is retaliation and can be grounds for a wrongful termination claim.

  • Violation of Public Policy: Firing an employee for reasons that violate public policy, such as firing someone for taking time off to serve on jury duty or filing a complaint about unsafe working conditions, can also be grounds for wrongful termination.

  • Constructive Discharge: In some cases, an employer’s behavior may create a work environment that forces you to resign. If you resign under such conditions, it may be treated as a wrongful termination.

Now that we have a clearer understanding of what wrongful termination is, let’s move forward with the steps you can take if you find yourself in this unfortunate situation.

Step 1: Review Your Employment Contract or Employee Handbook

The first step after being terminated is to review any employment contracts or company policies that might apply to your situation. Many employees sign an employment contract that outlines the terms of their employment and the grounds for termination. If your contract includes specific termination clauses, and the reasons for your termination don’t align with those clauses, you may have a strong case for wrongful termination.

Additionally, check your employee handbook or any other documents that outline the company’s policies. These documents may contain guidelines on disciplinary actions, performance reviews, and termination procedures. If the company did not follow these procedures or breached its own policies, you may have a claim.

Step 2: Document Everything

Documentation is key in any legal case, and wrongful termination is no different. Start by writing down everything you can remember about your termination, including:

  • The exact date and time of your termination.
  • The reason given by your employer for your termination.
  • Any communications (emails, memos, letters) that relate to your termination.
  • Any previous performance reviews or disciplinary actions, especially if they were unfair or contradictory to the reason given for your termination.
  • The names of anyone who was present during your termination or any meetings that led to it.

This documentation will be essential in helping your attorney build a case and understand whether your employer violated any laws or contractual terms.

Step 3: Understand Your State’s Employment Laws

Employment laws can vary significantly from one state to another, and understanding these laws can help you decide how to proceed. In some states, employees are considered “at-will,” which means that an employer can terminate them for almost any reason, as long as it is not illegal. However, even in at-will employment states, wrongful termination claims can still arise if the firing is based on discrimination, retaliation, or violation of public policy.

In other states, employment laws may offer more protection to workers, and it may be harder for an employer to fire an employee without cause. Be sure to research your state’s specific laws to understand your rights and options. You may also want to consult with a legal expert in your state to ensure you understand how local laws apply to your situation.

Step 4: File a Complaint with the Appropriate Agency

If you believe your termination was the result of discrimination, retaliation, or another illegal reason, you may need to file a complaint with a government agency before pursuing legal action in court. The two primary agencies that handle employment discrimination and wrongful termination claims are:

  • The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC): If you believe you were fired due to discrimination based on race, gender, age, disability, religion, or national origin, you should file a charge with the EEOC. The EEOC will investigate your claim and, if they find merit, may offer mediation or legal action.

  • State Agencies: Many states have their own agencies that handle wrongful termination claims. These agencies may deal with local laws and offer additional protections that the EEOC does not cover. You should check with your state’s labor department to determine if you need to file a complaint there as well.

In some cases, you may have to file your complaint within a certain time frame (often 180 days to 1 year), so it’s important to act quickly.

Step 5: Consider Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

Before heading to court, it may be worth considering alternative dispute resolution (ADR) methods such as mediation or arbitration. Many employers require employees to agree to ADR as part of their employment contract, which means you may be obligated to resolve the dispute outside of court.

Mediation involves a neutral third party helping both sides come to a resolution without the need for litigation. Arbitration is more formal and involves a neutral third party making a binding decision on the dispute.

ADR can be less expensive and time-consuming than a full trial, but it may not always lead to the best outcome, depending on the specifics of your case. An experienced employment lawyer can help you understand whether ADR is a good option for your situation.

Step 6: Consult an Employment Lawyer

If you believe you’ve been wrongfully terminated, one of the most important steps is to consult with an experienced employment lawyer. An attorney specializing in employment law can provide valuable insights and help you navigate the complex legal process.

When selecting a lawyer, look for someone who has experience with wrongful termination cases and who is familiar with both state and federal laws. Your lawyer will help you understand whether you have a case, help you gather necessary evidence, and guide you through the process of filing a lawsuit if necessary.

What a Lawyer Can Do for You:

  • Assess Your Case: A lawyer can evaluate whether you have a valid claim for wrongful termination and discuss your options with you.
  • Help with Documentation: Lawyers know what documentation is needed to support your case and can help you organize your evidence.
  • Negotiate on Your Behalf: If your case goes to mediation or arbitration, a lawyer can represent you and negotiate on your behalf to ensure you get a fair settlement.
  • Litigate: If the case goes to court, a lawyer will represent you during the legal process, present evidence, and fight for your rights.

Step 7: File a Lawsuit (If Necessary)

If attempts to resolve the matter through ADR or a government agency are unsuccessful, your next option may be to file a lawsuit against your employer for wrongful termination. A lawsuit allows you to seek compensation for damages caused by the termination, which may include:

  • Back Pay: Compensation for the wages you would have earned if you had not been wrongfully terminated.
  • Emotional Distress: Damages for the emotional pain and suffering caused by the wrongful termination.
  • Punitive Damages: In cases of particularly egregious behavior, punitive damages may be awarded to punish the employer and deter others from similar actions.
  • Reinstatement: In some cases, a court may order that you be reinstated to your job if it finds that your termination was illegal.

The process of filing a lawsuit can be lengthy and expensive, so it’s important to consult with your attorney to determine if pursuing legal action is the best course of action for you.

Conclusion

Wrongful termination is a serious issue that can have lasting effects on your career and personal well-being. If you find yourself in this unfortunate situation, it’s important to take swift action to protect your rights. By understanding the legal steps involved—from reviewing your employment contract to consulting with an attorney—you can better navigate the complexities of a wrongful termination case and pursue the best possible outcome.

The legal process can be overwhelming, but with the right support and guidance, you can take control of the situation and ensure that you are treated fairly. If you believe you’ve been wrongfully terminated, take the necessary steps today to protect your future.
