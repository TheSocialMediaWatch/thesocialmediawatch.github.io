The Art of Tracking: How to Follow Animal Trails


Tracking animals is a fascinating and ancient skill, one that has been practiced by hunters, naturalists, and outdoor enthusiasts for thousands of years. The ability to follow animal trails can provide valuable insights into an animal’s behavior, habitat, and movement patterns, making it an essential tool for anyone interested in wildlife observation or understanding the natural world more deeply. Whether you are in the wilderness looking for wildlife or simply seeking a more intimate connection with nature, mastering the art of tracking animals opens up a new dimension of adventure.

Understanding Animal Tracks

To begin tracking animals, it’s crucial to understand the basics of animal tracks. Every creature leaves unique signs behind, from the size and shape of footprints to the disturbances they cause in their environment. Tracks often reveal not only the species of the animal but also its behavior, such as whether it was walking, running, or pausing to rest. Learning to identify these tracks accurately is the first step in becoming a proficient tracker.

The Different Types of Tracks

Animal tracks come in various forms, including footprints, scat (feces), broken branches, and even scent markings. Each type of track can provide a different clue as to what the animal was doing at the time. For example:

  • Footprints: These are the most common and often the easiest to spot. They can be clear indicators of an animal’s size, weight, and movement pattern. In soft soil, mud, or snow, footprints can be extremely detailed.
  • Scat: Animal droppings can tell you what the animal has been eating, whether it’s herbivore, carnivore, or omnivore. They can also offer clues about the animal’s health and diet.
  • Scent Markings: Many animals leave behind scent markers, either from glands or urine, which can indicate territory or mating behavior.
  • Scrapes and Nests: Some animals, such as foxes and rabbits, leave behind scrapes in the dirt where they’ve been digging. Birds and mammals may also create nests or make other marks to indicate their presence.

By understanding the differences in these markers, you can interpret the context and meaning of the trails you encounter.

How to Read Animal Tracks

Once you start encountering animal tracks, the next step is to learn how to interpret them. Animal tracks provide a wealth of information, from species identification to behavioral insights. The key is to observe carefully and piece together the clues.

Step 1: Look for Size and Shape

The first thing to do when you spot an animal track is to observe its size and shape. The general size can often tell you the species, while the shape will give you further clues. For example, canine tracks (like wolves or dogs) typically have a triangular shape, while feline tracks (such as those of a cat or a cougar) are rounder with retractable claws often not leaving impressions.

  • Canine Tracks: These typically have four toes with distinct pads, and the claws may or may not be visible. The prints are often elongated and resemble an oval or triangle.
  • Feline Tracks: Feline tracks tend to be rounder and more compact, with sharp claws that rarely show in the prints, as they are retractable.
  • Hoofed Animals: Hoof prints, like those from deer or elk, are often oval or heart-shaped and lack toes.

Step 2: Observe the Gait

The way an animal walks, runs, or trots can also reveal much about its behavior and activity. Look at the spacing between tracks and how they are aligned.

  • Stride Length: Long strides indicate an animal that is running or traveling at speed, while short strides suggest walking or trotting.
  • Pacing vs. Diagonal: Some animals, such as cats and birds, tend to pace their tracks (the left front foot and left rear foot move together), while others, like horses, use a diagonal gait (left front and right rear move together).

The way tracks are spaced and arranged will tell you not just the animal’s pace, but also its intent. For instance, a haphazard pattern of tracks may suggest an animal is agitated or in distress, while a more uniform pattern could indicate calm or purposeful movement.

Step 3: Look for Clues in the Environment

Animal tracks don’t exist in isolation. The surrounding environment offers further insights that can enhance your tracking experience. Look for other signs that can give context to the animal’s actions:

  • Feeding Signs: Look for disturbed vegetation or evidence of feeding, such as broken twigs or half-eaten fruit. This can help you understand what the animal was doing before or after leaving the tracks.
  • Water Sources: Animals often leave tracks near water sources. Observing where animals drink or bathe can help you identify habitual trails and predict future movements.
  • Shelter Locations: Some animals leave marks around the entrances of dens, nests, or resting sites. Identifying these areas is crucial for understanding animal behavior.

Tools for Tracking Animals

While a keen eye and patient observation are the most important tools for tracking animals, there are a few other tools that can make the process easier and more accurate. Here are some commonly used tools for animal tracking:

  • Field Guidebooks: A good field guide for animal tracks can help you identify footprints and other signs in the field. These guides typically include pictures and detailed descriptions, making identification easier.
  • Tracking Stick: A simple stick or branch can help you keep track of where you’ve been, mark certain spots, or even help measure the size of a track.
  • Camera: Taking photos of tracks and signs can be helpful for later analysis. High-quality, close-up pictures will allow you to compare the tracks with guidebooks or use them for further research.
  • Binoculars: While binoculars are often associated with bird watching, they can also be useful for spotting distant wildlife and tracking animals from afar without disturbing them.

Common Animal Trails to Follow

Now that you know how to read tracks and use tools to enhance your observations, it’s time to start exploring different types of animal trails. Here are a few animals whose trails are commonly tracked:

1. Deer

Deer are one of the most common and easily tracked animals in many regions. Their tracks are often large, heart-shaped, and clearly visible, especially in softer ground. Deer typically create clear trails that follow consistent paths between feeding, drinking, and bedding areas.

  • Key Features: A split-hoof print, with a heart-shaped or oval shape. The tracks usually show a consistent pattern of movement, especially near areas of dense cover or water sources.

2. Bears

Bears are large creatures, and their tracks are substantial. They tend to leave large, round paw prints, often with visible claw marks. Following bear tracks can be more challenging since bears often travel great distances, but they are easily identified due to their size and the impression of claws.

  • Key Features: Large, round prints with visible claws. Bears often create a “sloppy” track pattern due to their heavy body and wide stance.

3. Coyotes and Wolves

These canids leave tracks that are similar to domestic dogs but more refined and pointed. The tracks of a coyote or wolf may vary based on the terrain but are often found near hunting grounds, water sources, or dens.

  • Key Features: Four toes, visible claws, and an elongated shape. Their tracks are often more spaced out and indicate a steady pace.

4. Birds of Prey

Birds such as hawks, eagles, and owls leave unique marks, typically in the form of talon prints or feathers. These tracks can be found around perches or hunting grounds.

  • Key Features: Sharp talon marks, often accompanied by feathers, blood trails, or animal remains.

Conclusion

Tracking animals is a rewarding and enriching activity that enhances our understanding of the natural world. By learning to read tracks, observe behavior, and identify signs in the environment, you can unlock a whole new realm of knowledge about wildlife. Whether you’re tracking for practical purposes, like hunting, or simply seeking a deeper connection with nature, following animal trails offers a fulfilling and educational experience.

With practice, patience, and an eye for detail, anyone can become a skilled tracker. The next time you find yourself in the wilderness, take a moment to look at the ground beneath your feet – you never know what animal trail you might uncover.
