How to Fish for Redfish: A Complete Guide


Fishing for redfish is an exciting and rewarding experience, especially for those who enjoy the challenge of catching a powerful, hard-fighting fish. Known for their beauty and strength, redfish are a favorite among anglers in coastal areas. Whether you’re a seasoned angler or just starting out, this guide will take you through everything you need to know to successfully fish for redfish, from understanding their behavior to selecting the right gear and techniques.

What Are Redfish?

Redfish, also known as red drum (scientific name: Sciaenops ocellatus), are a popular species of fish found primarily along the Gulf of Mexico, Atlantic coast, and other warm coastal waters of the United States. They get their name from their reddish-brown coloration and distinctive black spot near their tails. Redfish are highly sought after by sport anglers due to their impressive size, strength, and the thrilling fight they put up when hooked.

These fish are known to grow quite large, with some reaching lengths of up to 50 inches or more. They are also known for their resilience, making them an exciting target for anglers. Redfish typically live in shallow, brackish waters, where they feed on a variety of prey, including shrimp, crabs, small fish, and other aquatic organisms.

Best Time to Fish for Redfish

Timing plays a crucial role when fishing for redfish. While redfish can be caught year-round in many areas, there are certain seasons that are more favorable for catching these fish. The best time to fish for redfish is typically during the warmer months when the water temperature is between 70°F and 85°F (21°C to 29°C). During this time, redfish are more active and feeding aggressively.

Early mornings and late afternoons are also prime times to fish for redfish, as these are the times when they are most likely to be feeding near the shore. Additionally, fishing during a rising or falling tide can increase your chances of success, as redfish tend to move into shallow areas to feed during these times.

Where to Fish for Redfish

Redfish are typically found in shallow coastal waters, including estuaries, marshes, and mangroves. They are often found near structures like oyster beds, submerged rocks, drop-offs, and grassy flats. These habitats provide cover and food sources for redfish, making them ideal areas to target.

Look for redfish in the following locations:

  • Estuaries and Tidal Creeks: Redfish love the brackish waters where fresh and saltwater mix. Tidal creeks and estuaries are prime feeding grounds, especially during incoming or outgoing tides.

  • Shallow Flats: In the warmer months, redfish often feed in shallow water, especially in areas with seagrass, mud, or sand flats. These areas are perfect for sight fishing.

  • Mangroves and Oyster Beds: Redfish are known to hang around structures such as mangrove roots or oyster beds. These areas offer shelter and a variety of prey for the fish.

  • Inshore and Offshore Reefs: In deeper waters, redfish can be found around reefs and wrecks. These areas are ideal for anglers looking for larger fish.

Essential Gear for Redfish Fishing

Fishing for redfish requires the right gear to ensure you can land these powerful fish without losing them. The type of gear you choose will depend on your fishing environment, target size, and personal preferences. Below are the essential components you’ll need to have in your fishing arsenal:

Rod and Reel

When choosing a rod and reel for redfish fishing, you want a setup that is both strong and sensitive. A medium to heavy action spinning or baitcasting rod is ideal, as it will allow you to handle the size and strength of redfish. A rod in the 7 to 8-foot range is recommended for casting longer distances and getting good leverage on the fish.

Pair your rod with a high-quality reel with a smooth drag system. A reel with a 2500 to 4000 size is suitable for most redfish fishing situations. Make sure the reel is saltwater-resistant to withstand the harsh conditions of coastal waters.

Fishing Line

For redfish, a 10- to 20-pound braided fishing line is recommended. Braided line provides excellent strength and sensitivity, which is essential for feeling bites and setting the hook on these powerful fish. You’ll also need a leader line, typically made from fluorocarbon, in the 20- to 30-pound test range. Fluorocarbon is virtually invisible in the water and has good abrasion resistance, which is important when fishing in areas with rocks or oyster beds.

Hooks

Redfish are typically caught using circle hooks or J-hooks. Circle hooks are ideal for catch-and-release fishing because they tend to hook the fish in the corner of the mouth, which reduces the chance of injury. The recommended hook sizes for redfish range from 2/0 to 5/0, depending on the size of the bait and the fish you’re targeting.

Bait and Lures

The best bait for redfish depends on what’s available in the area, but redfish are known to be opportunistic feeders. They are often caught using both natural bait and artificial lures. Here are some options to consider:

  • Live Bait: Redfish love live shrimp, mullet, and menhaden. These are great options for attracting redfish, especially in shallow waters. Live bait can be fished on the bottom, under a popping cork, or free-lined.

  • Cut Bait: Redfish also bite on cut bait such as chunks of mullet, pinfish, or other local baitfish. This can be especially effective when fishing in deeper waters or when live bait is hard to come by.

  • Artificial Lures: If you prefer artificial lures, soft plastics such as paddle-tail swimbaits, jerkbaits, and shrimp imitations are great choices. Choose lures that resemble the local prey species in the area. When fishing with lures, it’s best to use a slow, steady retrieve or a twitch-and-pause method to mimic the movement of injured baitfish.

Tackle Box

A well-organized tackle box is essential for carrying all your gear. Make sure it’s stocked with hooks, lures, weights, swivels, and pliers. It’s also a good idea to carry a few different types of rigs, such as Carolina rigs or fish-finder rigs, so you can quickly adapt to changing conditions.

Fishing Techniques for Redfish

There are several fishing techniques you can use to catch redfish, depending on the location, time of day, and water conditions. Below are some effective methods:

1. Sight Fishing

Sight fishing for redfish is one of the most exciting ways to catch them. This method involves spotting redfish in shallow waters, often in grassy flats or near the shoreline. Once you spot a redfish, you’ll cast your bait or lure just ahead of it and wait for the fish to swim toward the offering.

Sight fishing requires patience and precision. You need to approach the fish slowly and quietly, making sure not to spook them. A light rod and fluorocarbon leader are ideal for this technique, as they offer better stealth.

2. Fishing with a Popping Cork

A popping cork is an excellent tool for attracting redfish, especially in deeper or murkier water. The cork creates noise and movement on the surface, which mimics the sound of injured baitfish. The idea is to make the cork “pop” by giving it a quick tug, creating a disturbance that attracts redfish.

This technique works well when fishing with live shrimp or soft plastics beneath the cork. Redfish are attracted to the noise, and the bait sits just below the surface, allowing you to fish at different depths.

3. Bottom Fishing

Redfish often feed on the bottom, especially in deeper waters or when the tide is low. When bottom fishing, you’ll need to use a weight to keep your bait or lure near the ocean floor. The Carolina rig is one of the best rigs for bottom fishing for redfish, as it allows for natural bait movement and minimal resistance.

Make sure to fish during the right tide conditions, as redfish are more likely to feed in shallow areas during rising or falling tides.

4. Drift Fishing

Drift fishing involves slowly moving your boat or kayak across an area while allowing your bait to drift with the current. This technique is especially useful when fishing in large, open areas like flats or channels. It allows you to cover a wide range of water while keeping your bait in the strike zone for an extended period.

5. Fishing Over Structure

Redfish often congregate around structures like oyster beds, submerged rocks, or mangrove roots. When fishing over structure, make sure your bait or lure is presented close to the structure. A slower retrieve is often necessary to allow the bait to sink to the right depth.

Handling Redfish

Once you’ve caught a redfish, it’s important to handle it properly. If you’re practicing catch-and-release, make sure to wet your hands before touching the fish to avoid damaging its slimy protective coating. Use circle hooks to minimize injury, and release the fish gently back into the water as quickly as possible.

If you’re keeping the fish for consumption, make sure to follow local regulations regarding size and bag limits. Redfish are delicious, with a mild, sweet flavor that makes them a popular choice for cooking.

Conclusion

Fishing for redfish is an enjoyable and rewarding experience that requires patience, skill, and the right techniques. Whether you’re targeting them in shallow flats or near deep structures, understanding the best times, locations, and methods to fish for redfish will increase your chances of success. With the right gear and a bit of practice, you’ll be reeling in these powerful fish in no time. Happy fishing!
