The Top 5 Kitchen Myths Debunked


The kitchen is often seen as a place where creativity and practicality merge, but it’s also where countless myths and misconceptions have found footing over the years. While some of these myths have been passed down through generations or emerged as “common sense,” many don’t hold up under scrutiny. In fact, believing and perpetuating these myths can impact how we cook, prepare food, and even maintain our kitchens. It’s time to separate fact from fiction and debunk the top 5 kitchen myths that have been pulling the wool over home cooks’ eyes.

Myth #1: Adding Oil to Pasta Water Prevents It from Sticking

If you’ve ever cooked pasta, chances are you’ve heard the advice to drizzle a bit of oil into the boiling water to keep the noodles from sticking together. This common “tip” sounds logical at first glance, but it’s nothing more than a myth. The truth is, adding oil to your pasta water does very little to solve the issue. Why? Because oil and water don’t mix. The oil simply floats on the surface while your pasta cooks below—and when drained, that minimal contact with the oil fails to have any lasting effect.

What actually causes pasta to stick together is starch—and the solution isn’t oil, but proper technique. Stir your pasta regularly as it cooks, especially in the first few minutes when the starch begins to activate. Additionally, use plenty of water so the pasta has room to move and release its starch without clumping. Once drained, avoid rinsing the pasta unless you’re making a cold dish like pasta salad. Rinsing washes away the starch that helps sauces stick to the noodles, and that leads us to why adding oil can even backfire: oil-coated pasta won’t absorb sauces properly, making your dish less flavorful.

The verdict? Skip the oil and focus on stirring, timing, and proper preparation for perfectly cooked pasta every time.

Myth #2: Sharp Knives Are More Dangerous Than Dull Knives

It’s easy to think that a sharp knife poses a greater risk of injury—after all, it can cut through food with precision and ease. However, the opposite is true. Dull knives are, in fact, far more dangerous than sharp ones. When a knife is dull, you need to apply extra force to slice through food, which increases the risk of slipping and losing control. A slip with a dull knife often leads to accidents that can be far more harmful than you’d face with a well-honed blade.

Sharp knives require less effort, offering more control and precision with every cut. They also produce cleaner slices, which can prevent you from hacking away at food and inadvertently cutting yourself. Regular sharpening is essential not just for kitchen performance, but for safety, too. Investing in a quality knife sharpener or taking your knives to a professional will ensure your blades are in top condition. It’s also wise to learn proper knife skills and cutting techniques to maximize safety in the kitchen.

So, remember: it’s dull knives that demand caution. Keep your knives sharp and you’ll reduce both the danger and frustration in your food prep.

Myth #3: Salt Prevents Water From Boiling Over

Do you sprinkle salt into boiling water thinking it’ll magically keep it from spilling over? This myth is another one of those persistent kitchen misconceptions that doesn’t actually hold up under scientific analysis. While it’s true that salt raises the boiling point of water slightly due to a phenomenon called boiling point elevation, this effect is minimal at best. The amount of salt you’d need to meaningfully change the boiling process—and in turn prevent spillovers—would result in water too salty to cook with.

Boiling over isn’t caused by unsalted water, it’s caused by excessive heat and surface tension. When starch and water collaborate in the pot, bubbling occurs—and if you’re not controlling the heat, those bubbles can rise quickly and flow over the edges. To prevent boil-over incidents, try using less water so there’s more room in the pot. You can also lay a wooden spoon across the rim to break the surface tension of the bubbles. Lowering the heat slightly can be another simple yet effective solution.

While salt plays an important role in enhancing flavor during cooking, it won’t save you from messy stovetops or boiling overpots. Instead, tackle the real cause with better heat control and spacing.

Myth #4: You Should Only Use Cold Water to Boil Faster

Have you ever started boiling water by reaching for the cold tap, believing it’ll heat up quicker? Many people operate under the assumption that starting with cold water will somehow speed up the boiling process, but this is actually a kitchen myth rooted in outdated thinking. Here’s what really happens: boiling depends on temperature change over time, not the starting temperature of the water itself.

In truth, hot tap water will boil faster because it is already closer to the boiling point. However, many experts advise against using hot tap water for cooking due to potential contaminants. Hot water can pick up minerals and residues from your water heater that might affect taste or purity. For the best results, stick to cold tap water—even if it starts behind temperature-wise. Though it may take slightly longer, cold water is often cleaner and safer for consumption, especially in recipes where purity matters.

If you’re looking to make your boiling faster, a stovetop kettle or induction cooktop can help dramatically reduce the wait time. And if your water really won’t boil fast enough, it may be time to assess your cookware, stove settings, or batch size—as these factors matter far more than whether the water started out cold or warm.

Myth #5: Wooden Cutting Boards Are Unsanitary

The idea that wooden boards are inherently unsanitary has been circling kitchens for ages, primarily because wood is a porous material. Many believe that bacteria and juices from raw food get trapped in those pores, making wooden surfaces unsafe for food prep. However, studies have shown that wooden cutting boards can actually be safer than plastic ones when properly maintained.

Here’s why: wood has natural antimicrobial properties that fight bacteria. Additionally, while bacteria may initially sink into the pores of a wooden board, they eventually die as the wood dries out. In comparison, plastic cutting boards tend to develop grooves from knife cuts over time, and bacteria can accumulate in those scratches where they are harder to remove—even during washing.

To keep wooden boards sanitary, clean them thoroughly after each use with warm, soapy water, and periodically disinfect them with a vinegar solution or by sprinkling salt and scrubbing with a lemon. Always let them air-dry completely, and apply mineral oil occasionally to maintain their condition. Avoid soaking wooden boards in water for extended periods, as this can cause them to warp or crack.

So next time you reach for your cutting board, consider wood as a practical, durable, and sanitary option. Keep it clean and treat it well, and it will serve you reliably for years to come.

Final Thoughts

The world of cooking is filled with traditions, advice, and handy tips—but not everything we hear in the kitchen is gospel. By debunking these myths, you can elevate your cooking game, avoid unnecessary challenges, and make your kitchen more efficient. Whether it’s understanding how pasta behaves in boiling water or learning the truth about cutting board hygiene, approaching these myths with a critical eye can truly make a difference.

Cooking is as much about understanding your tools and processes as it is about creating delicious meals. By letting go of misconceptions and embracing evidence-based practices, you’ll be well on your way to mastering the art and science of cooking.
