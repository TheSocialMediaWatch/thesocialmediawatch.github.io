How to Train Your Cat to Use a Scratching Post


Scratching is a natural and essential behavior for cats. It helps maintain the health of their claws, provides a form of exercise, relieves stress, and marks their territory. But when your furry friend decides to sink their claws into your couch, curtains, or other furniture, it can be frustrating. The good news is that you can guide your cat to use a scratching post instead. While it takes some patience and understanding, most cats can be successfully trained. Here’s how to do it, step by step.

Understand Why Cats Scratch

Before diving into training techniques, it’s important to understand why your cat scratches. Knowing their motivation will help you approach training with greater empathy and effectiveness. Cats scratch for several reasons:

  • Claw Maintenance: Scratching removes dead layers of nails, keeping their claws healthy.
  • Stretching and Exercise: The motion of scratching engages the muscles in their legs, shoulders, and back, providing an essential stretch and workout.
  • Marking Territory: Cats have scent glands in their paws and leave both visual and olfactory markers when they scratch.
  • Stress Relief and Play: Scratching can be a way for cats to release pent-up energy or handle stress.

Recognizing these natural instincts will make it clear that your goal isn’t to stop the scratching altogether – it’s to redirect it.

Choose the Right Scratching Post

Not all scratching posts are created equal. To effectively train your cat, you’ll need to choose a scratching post they’ll actually want to use. Here are some tips for selecting the right one:

  1. Material Matters
    Cats have strong preferences when it comes to texture. Most cats enjoy scratching materials like sisal rope, sisal fabric, cardboard, or carpet. Sisal is particularly durable and satisfies their need for a rough texture that mimics tree bark.

  2. Height and Stability
    Make sure the scratching post is tall enough for your cat to fully stretch out while scratching. If the post is too short, they may ignore it in favor of taller options—like your furniture. Stability is just as important; a wobbly or tipping post is unlikely to win your cat’s favor.

  3. Variety of Options
    Some cats prefer vertical posts, while others enjoy horizontal scratchers. Try offering a combination of shapes and orientations to find which one your cat responds to most.

Placement is Key

Even the best scratching post won’t get much use if it’s placed in the wrong spot. Cats tend to scratch in high-traffic areas where they spend the most time. Here’s how to make your post more appealing by choosing the right location:

  • Near Sleeping Areas: Cats love to scratch as part of their stretching routine after waking up, so placing a post near their favorite nap spot is often a good idea.
  • Close to Problem Areas: If your cat has been scratching a piece of furniture, place the scratching post directly next to or in front of that spot. It’s easier to transition them to the new location from there.
  • Central Locations: Scratching is also tied to marking their territory, so posts placed in central household locations may attract more interest than those tucked away in a quiet corner.

Once your cat is regularly using the scratching post, you can gradually move it to a more convenient location if necessary. Just make sure to move it a short distance at a time.

Introduce the Scratching Post

Simply plopping a scratching post in front of your cat and expecting them to use it isn’t enough. You’ll need to actively encourage their curiosity and use some tricks to get them acquainted with the new addition.

  1. Use Positive Reinforcement
    Encourage your cat to investigate the scratching post by rewarding them when they show interest. Whenever they approach, sniff, or scratch the post, praise them and offer a small treat. Positive reinforcement helps reinforce the connection between the scratching post and good outcomes.

  2. Add Catnip or Cat Attractants
    Many cats are drawn to the smell of catnip. Sprinkle some fresh catnip on or around the scratching post to make it more appealing. Alternatively, you can use sprays that contain feline attractants to encourage exploration and scratching.

  3. Mimic Scratching Behavior
    Cats are natural copycats and may learn by observing you. Run your nails down the post to mimic scratching behavior. This gesture can pique their curiosity and inspire them to try it themselves.

  4. Incorporate Playtime
    Play near the scratching post using toys such as feather wands or strings. Direct the toy or string so that it brushes against the post. When your cat pounces on the post during play, they’re more likely to start scratching as part of their natural behavior.

Prevent Unwanted Scratching

While training your cat to use a scratching post, you’ll also want to discourage them from targeting unwanted areas. Here’s how:

  1. Make Furniture Less Appealing
    If your cat has a favorite scratching spot that you’d like to protect, make it undesirable for scratching. Cover it with double-sided tape, aluminum foil, or a protective mat. Cats dislike the sticky or slick texture, which can deter them from scratching.

  2. Redirect Immediately
    If you catch your cat in the act of scratching an off-limits area, redirect them to their scratching post. Avoid yelling or punishing them—this may frighten your cat or cause confusion, making training more difficult.

  3. Use Deterrent Sprays
    Some sprays are designed to keep cats away from furniture without harming them. Test the spray on a small area of your furniture beforehand to ensure it doesn’t cause damage.

  4. Reward Alternative Behaviors
    Always reward your cat with praise, treats, or petting when you see them using the scratching post instead of forbidden areas.

Stay Consistent

Consistency is key when training your cat. It may take several weeks for a new behavior to stick, so be patient and persistent. Reinforce positive behavior every time your cat uses the scratching post, and don’t forget to address bad habits early to prevent them from becoming ingrained.

Maintain the Scratching Post

Once your cat is happily using their scratching post, it’s important to keep it in good condition. If the post becomes too worn or unstable, your cat may lose interest and resume scratching elsewhere. For cardboard scratchers, replace them once they’re shredded beyond use. For sisal posts, check for loose material or wobbling and address any issues promptly.

Final Thoughts

Training your cat to use a scratching post isn’t just about saving your furniture—it’s about promoting a healthy and happy environment for your feline friend. By understanding your cat’s natural instincts, choosing the right post, and providing encouragement, you can effectively redirect their scratching behavior in a way that works for both of you.

Remember, every cat is unique. With some patience, observation, and plenty of positive reinforcement, you’ll soon enjoy a home with scratch-free furniture and a very satisfied cat.
