Quantum Theory and Mathematics: Unveiling the Mysteries of the Microscopic World


Quantum mechanics, a fundamental theory in physics, describes the behavior of individual particles at microscopic scales. It’s a branch of science that covers everything from atoms to subatomic particles, such as electrons, photons, and quarks. And at the heart of quantum mechanics lies the interplay between mathematics and physics.

But how exactly does mathematics help us understand the mysterious quantum world? To answer that, we need first to understand some of the core principles of quantum theory.

In classical physics, an object is either in one state or another. A ball is either red or blue. However, in quantum theory, a particle can be in several states, not just two, simultaneously. This mind-boggling concept is known as superposition.

To put it in a more intuitive way, imagine a person who is not only at a bus stop but also halfway to the next stop and halfway back to the previous one. This means that when you ask the person where they are, they will say both that they are at the current stop and that they are traveling to the next one. Further, quantum mechanics tells us that, without measuring them, objects do not have definite states. Instead, they occupy a “superposition” of all possible states.

Superposition is one of several weird quantum phenomena described by the Schrödinger’s Equation. That equation, which bears the name of Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger, quantitatively describes how a quantum system evolves over time. Schrödinger’s equation is a statement about the probability distribution that arises for particles in quantum systems.

Another essential quantum mechanical principle is wave-particle duality. Before measurement, light behaves as both a wave and a particle, thanks to its wave-particle duality. This fact is at the core of photoelectricity, a phenomenon explained by Einstein’s photon theory, which won him a Nobel Prize.

While these quantum principles may seem utterly confusing, mathematical concepts such as linear algebra and wave functions provide us with the necessary tools to observe and understand the quantum world.

Linear algebra, a field of mathematics, helps express these quantum states and describe the transformations that give rise to many of the counterintuitive phenomena. Waves functions, which form a central part of quantum mechanics, describe the behavior of quantum particles.

For example, the electron-in-a-box problem illustrates the beginnings of quantum mechanics. In this case, the mathematics allows us to calculate the energy levels that grow out of our knowledge of the wave function. The energy levels occur in increments or “quanta,” which explains the term quantum.

What about entanglement, a concept that Einstein called “spooky action at a distance”? Here, the mathematics of complex numbers comes into play. In quantum mechanics, you describe particles in terms of a wave function that is a complex number. Lastly, the Bell test experiments of 2015 demonstrated that some aspects of quantum mechanics are indeed non-LOCAL. The researchers have managed to rule out all possible loopholes in the Bell test experiments and proven conclusively that quantum mechanics is non-local.

As fascinating as it may be, the world of quantum physics is also incredibly complex, with possibly infinite hidden variables, something Einstein couldn’t accept. The consequences of these complex rules shape how we understand the stars, the subatomic world, and, crucially, the technological breakthroughs they bring.

From quantum cryptography that can protect our digital communications to quantum computing capable of finding answers to some of our biggest scientific questions too, the understanding of quantum mechanics relies heavily on our mathematical models. Mathematics and physics understand the behavior of particles, providing ways we can exploit this knowledge.

In essence, the interplay between mathematics and quantum theory unveils mysteries of the microscopic world that would otherwise remain hidden, spurring our curiosity and pushing the boundaries of our understanding. Yet, many new unsolved problems lie in the realm of quantum physics, waiting to be unraveled by future generations of mathematicians and physicists working together, hand in hand, towards unveiling even deeper secrets of our universe.
