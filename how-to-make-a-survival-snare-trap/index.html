How to Make a Survival Snare Trap


When you find yourself in a wilderness survival situation, securing a reliable food source becomes a top priority. One of the most effective and low-effort techniques to catch small game is using a snare trap. This ancient trapping method leverages simple materials and clever mechanics to capture animals without constant human presence.

In this detailed guide, we’ll walk through the essentials of making a survival snare trap, including selecting materials, choosing an ideal location, crafting the snare loop, setting the trigger mechanism, and tips for safety and legality.

Understanding the Snare Trap Concept

A snare trap is a looped device designed to tighten around an animal as it passes through or steps into it. The loop is usually made from a strong, flexible material such as wire or cordage. When the animal moves through the loop, the trap’s design causes the loop to constrict, thereby capturing the animal by the neck, leg, or body.

Snares are effective primarily for small- to medium-sized animals such as rabbits, squirrels, birds, and sometimes even larger game like foxes or raccoons. Because they require minimal energy once set, snares are ideal in survival scenarios where conserving strength is critical.

Materials You’ll Need

Before gathering or crafting a snare, identify the materials around you or carry essentials in your survival kit:

  • Wire or Paracord: The key element. Thin-gauge wire is ideal because it is strong and less visible. Twine or natural cordage can also work but may be less durable.
  • Support Stakes or Trees: The snare must be anchored securely. Choose sturdy sticks, branches, or nearby trees.
  • Knife or Sharp Tool: For cutting, sharpening points, or aiding in adjustments.
  • Optional Trigger Components: Depending on the snare design, you might want simple triggers such as bent saplings or toggles.

If wire is not available, you can fashion cordage from natural materials like grapevines, inner bark fibers, or roots, though their strength varies.

Step 1: Selecting the Snare Location

Choosing the right location drastically increases your chances of success. Small animals follow predictable paths in the forest or grassland, which helps in setting an effective snare.

Tips for Location:

  • Animal Trails: Look for well-trodden paths, game trails along fence lines, or easy routes between shelter and water.
  • Feeding Areas: Near berry patches, nut trees, or natural food sources.
  • Dense Vegetation: Areas where animals take cover or are more likely to pass.
  • Near Burrows or Dens: Entrance paths can be prime spots.

Look for signs such as footprints, droppings, fur, or disturbed foliage. Placing your snare strategically along these corridors increases the likelihood of capture.

Step 2: Crafting the Snare Loop

The fundamental part of the trap is the loop, which tightens as the animal moves through it.

To Make the Loop:

  1. Cut a length of wire or paracord around 3 to 5 feet long. The length depends on the target animal and environment.
  2. Form a loop at one end, about 4-6 inches in diameter, large enough for the animal’s head or legs but small enough to tighten efficiently.
  3. Twist or tie the loose ends securely to keep the loop intact while allowing it to slide freely to constrict.

Tips:

  • If using wire, twisting it around itself a few times adds security.
  • For cordage, a slip knot or noose knot works well.
  • Sharpen one side of wire or insert a small sharp wire piece inside the loop to ensure quicker catching by penetrating the animal’s fur or skin slightly — this is more effective but requires care.

Step 3: Creating the Anchor and Trigger

The snare must be anchored firmly to prevent the animal from freeing itself or escaping with the trap.

Simple Anchor Points Include:

  • Sharpened sticks driven into the ground.
  • Heavy rocks tied to the snare end.
  • Strong trees or thick branches.

Since the main function of the trap is the loop tightening on the animal, elaborate triggering mechanisms aren’t always needed. However, incorporating a bent sapling as a spring pole can increase effectiveness by snapping up or tightening the loop rapidly.

Basic Trigger Setup:

  1. Spring Pole Snare: Secure one end of the snare to a bent and flexible sapling. The other end is attached to the loop. When the animal triggers the loop, the sapling springs upright tightening the noose forcefully.
  2. Tripwire Snare: Set the snare loop near the ground where it is held open by a simple stick trigger connected to a tripwire. When the animal trips the wire, the stick is released, freeing the snare to tighten around the animal.

For survival situations where simplicity favors reliability, a snugly anchored noose with the animal’s movement providing tightening is often sufficient.

Step 4: Setting and Camouflaging the Snare

Once the snare is built and anchored, place it carefully in the chosen location. Consider the height and angle:

  • The loop should be positioned roughly at the height the animal’s head or legs pass through.
  • The loop should hang freely but close enough to the ground to catch the intended prey.

Camouflaging Your Snare:

  • Use leaves, grass, branches, or dirt to blend the snare into the environment.
  • Avoid covering the loop completely; it must remain invisible but functional.
  • Scent can disturb animals, so handle all parts with minimal human scent. Wearing gloves or wiping down materials with natural elements helps.

Step 5: Monitoring and Maintaining Your Snare

It’s important to check snares regularly for two reasons: to retrieve caught animals promptly and to reset or adjust the trap.

  • Check traps at least twice daily if possible.
  • Handle caught animals carefully; some may still be alive.
  • Dispose of any spoiled game or damaged snare parts responsibly.
  • Reset the trap as needed in the same or improved locations.

Avoid leaving animals suffering for prolonged periods; humane trapping practices minimize suffering and maintain respect for wildlife.

Safety and Ethical Considerations

While snares are invaluable survival tools, they should be used responsibly:

  • Avoid setting snares in areas populated by larger predators or protected species unless strictly for survival.
  • Be aware of local laws related to trapping and hunting.
  • Use snares only when necessary and in survival or controlled subsistence situations.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

  • Snare Loop Too Large: Animals may step over or escape. Reduce loop size.
  • Loop Doesn’t Tighten Smoothly: Lubricate wire or cord or replace materials.
  • Poor Anchorage: Try bigger stakes or anchor to a tree.
  • Animals Destroy Snare: Camouflage better or relocate.

Conclusion

Mastering the art of creating and setting a survival snare trap greatly bolsters your ability to procure food in wilderness conditions. It combines the use of simple materials, knowledge of animal habits, and patience. With practice and respect for nature, snares can be a reliable addition to your survival skills toolbox.

Always remember to adapt your designs based on the environment and animal behavior, and prioritize safety for both yourself and the wildlife.

By following this guide, you equip yourself with practical knowledge crucial for emergency scenarios or backcountry ventures where every resource counts. Happy trapping, and may your wilderness endeavors be safe and successful!
