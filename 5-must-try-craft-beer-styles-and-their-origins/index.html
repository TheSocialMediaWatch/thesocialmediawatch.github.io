5 Must-Try Craft Beer Styles and Their Origins


The world of craft beer is a vast and wondrous place, with a dizzying array of styles, flavors, and brewing traditions. From the hoppy, citrusy flavors of New England IPAs to the rich, malty depths of German-style bocks, there's a craft beer out there to suit every taste and preference. In this post, we'll delve into the origins and characteristics of five must-try craft beer styles, and explore the cultural and historical contexts that have shaped them over time.

  1. New England-Style India Pale Ale (NEIPA) Hailing from the autonomous region of New England in the United States, NEIPAs have taken the craft beer world by storm in recent years. These beers are characterized by their hazy, murky appearance and their bold, citrusy flavors, which are often described as tropical and fruity. The key to a great NEIPA lies in the brewery's ability to balance the bitter, floral flavors of the hops with a smooth, creamy mouthfeel and a subtle sweetness. The origins of NEIPAs can be traced back to the pioneering work of breweries like Tree House and Alchemist in Massachusetts and Vermont, respectively. These brewers experimented with innovative hop varieties and brewing techniques to create beers that were both refreshing and complex. Today, NEIPAs are enjoyed by craft beer enthusiasts around the world, and their popularity shows no signs of waning.
  2. German-Style Bock Bock beers originated in the 16th-century Franconia region of Germany, where they were brewed as a robust, full-bodied style for monks and other discerning drinkers. Characterized by their rich, malty flavors and deep, dark color, German bocks are often aged for a long time to allow their flavors to mature and develop. There are several sub-styles of bock, including the classic Weihenstephaner Festbier and the more robust Einbecker Eiterasarler. These beers are often brewed with traditional German malts and hops, which provide a distinctive flavor and aroma that is both malty and slightly sweet.
  3. Belgian-Style Quadrupel Quadrupels are a type of strong, dark ale that originated in the West Flanders region of Belgium. These beers are characterized by their powerful, complex flavors and their high ABV, which can range from 9-12% or more. Quad babies are often aged for a long time in oak barrels, which imparts a rich, vanilla-like flavor and a smooth, velvety texture. Quadrupels are brewed using traditional Belgian techniques, including the use of dark malts and an extensiveoppel of yeast. This fermentation process allows the flavors of the beer to mature and develop over a long period, resulting in a rich, complex flavor profile.
  4. Dry-Hopped Pale Ale Dry-hopped pale ales are a style that's gaining popularity around the world, thanks to the efforts of innovative breweries like Bell's and Stone. These beers are characterized by their bright, citrusy flavors and their hoppy, floral aromas, which are often described as herbs and spices. The key to a great dry-hopped pale ale lies in the brewery's ability to balance the bitter, resinous flavors of the hops with a smooth, refreshing mouthfeel and a subtle sweetness. This is achieved through an innovative brewing technique called dry-hopping, which involves adding hops to the fermenter during the secondary fermentation process.
  5. Smoke-Roasted Stout Smoke-roasted stouts are a type of dark, roasted beer that originated in the United Kingdom, where they were brewed as a robust, full-bodied style for working-class drinkers. Characterized by their rich, smoky flavors and deep, dark color, these beers are often brewed with roasted malts and smoked over an oak or beechwood fire. Today, craft breweries around the world are experimenting with various types of smoke, from traditional beechwood to more unusual varieties like applewood and cherrywood. The result is a rich, complex flavor profile that's both smoky and bold. Conclusion Craft beer is a dynamic, ever-changing world, with a dizzying array of styles, flavors, and brewing traditions to explore. From the hoppy, citrusy flavors of NEIPAs to the rich, malty depths of German-style bocks, each style has its own unique character and cultural heritage. In this post, we've delved into the origins and characteristics of just five must-try craft beer styles, and explored the cultural and historical contexts that have shaped them over time. Whether you're a seasoned beer aficionado or just starting to explore the world of craft beer, we hope that this post has given you a deeper appreciation for the rich heritage of this vibrant and ever-changing industry.
