How to Increase Your Running Speed in 4 Easy Steps


Increasing your running speed is a goal many runners share, whether you’re training for a race or just want to enhance your fitness. Speed isn’t just about raw power; it’s a blend of technique, strength, endurance, and smart training. By focusing on a handful of fundamental strategies, you can boost your pace safely and effectively. Let’s dive into four approachable steps that will help elevate your running speed and transform your workouts.

Step 1: Enhance Your Running Form

Efficient running form is the foundation of speed. Small adjustments can lead to significant improvements by reducing wasted effort and injury risk.

Posture and Alignment

Maintain an upright posture with a slight forward lean from the ankles, not the waist. This forward lean leverages gravity to propel you forward rather than forcing your legs to do all the work. Keep your head aligned with your spine, looking about 10 to 20 feet ahead, avoiding downward gazing which can restrict breathing and cause tension.

Arm Movement

Your arms power your stride. Keep them bent at about 90 degrees and swing them naturally from the shoulders. Avoid crossing your arms over the body’s midline, which can waste energy and disrupt balance. A loose but controlled arm swing helps maintain momentum and rhythm.

Foot Strike and Cadence

Aim for a midfoot strike just below your hips, allowing your legs to absorb impact efficiently. Overstriding, where the foot lands too far ahead, can slow you down and increase injury risk. Increasing your cadence—the number of steps you take per minute—towards 170-180 tends to promote a quicker turnover and more efficient stride. Use a metronome or music with a matching beat to help train your cadence.

Breathing Technique

Deep, rhythmic breathing supports oxygen delivery and endurance. Practice belly breathing rather than shallow chest breaths to maximize lung capacity. Exhale fully to clear carbon dioxide and prepare for the next breath.

Step 2: Incorporate Speed Workouts

Speed workouts challenge your body to adapt to faster paces, improving muscle power, neuromuscular coordination, and overall speed. Introducing these workouts gradually reduces injury risk while maximizing gains.

Interval Training

Intervals involve running at a high intensity for a brief period followed by a recovery phase. For example, sprint for 30 seconds, then jog or walk for 60 seconds, repeating several cycles. Intervals develop fast-twitch muscle fibers, increase VO2 max (maximal oxygen uptake), and train your body to sustain faster speeds.

Start with 4-6 intervals and progress as your fitness improves. Track your times to monitor progress.

Tempo Runs

Tempo runs are sustained efforts at a “comfortably hard” pace—usually about 75-85% of your maximum effort or your lactate threshold pace. These runs improve your body’s ability to clear lactic acid and sustain speed over longer distances.

Try starting with 10-15 minutes at tempo pace during a 30-40 minute run and build up from there.

Hill Repeats

Running uphill develops strength and power in your legs, specifically your glutes, hamstrings, and calves, all crucial muscles for speed. Find a moderate hill (4-6% incline) and sprint or run hard uphill for 20-30 seconds, then recover by jogging back down.

Incorporate around 6-8 repeats, ensuring you recover fully between efforts.

Fartlek Training

Fartlek, Swedish for “speed play,” combines continuous running with periodic bursts of faster-paced effort. Unlike intervals, fartlek is less structured and focuses on varying intensity based on how you feel.

For example, sprint to a landmark, jog to the next tree, then pick up speed again. This diversity improves speed endurance and keeps workouts enjoyable.

Step 3: Build Strength and Flexibility

Speed isn’t just about your legs moving fast; it’s about a powerful, balanced body that supports efficient motion. Strengthening relevant muscle groups and enhancing flexibility protects against injury and boosts your stride.

Strength Training

Focusing on lower body strength pays huge dividends. Key exercises include:

  • Squats: Build power in quads, glutes, and hamstrings.
  • Lunges: Improve unilateral leg strength and hip stability.
  • Deadlifts: Target hamstrings and glutes while reinforcing posture.
  • Calf Raises: Strengthen calf muscles for better push-off.

Incorporate 2-3 strength sessions per week, using moderate weights and focusing on form and control.

Core Stability

A strong core stabilizes your torso and aids efficient force transfer during running. Planks, Russian twists, and bicycle crunches target the core muscles directly involved in maintaining good running posture.

Flexibility and Mobility

Tight muscles limit stride length and increase risk of strains. Pay special attention to hip flexors, hamstrings, calves, and quadriceps through dynamic stretching pre-run and static stretching post-run.

Yoga and foam rolling can also enhance mobility and reduce muscle tightness.

Step 4: Optimize Recovery and Nutrition

Improving running speed isn’t just about pushing harder; it’s also about giving your body what it needs to repair, rebuild, and improve.

Prioritize Rest

Speed workouts are taxing, so allow adequate recovery between hard sessions. This can mean full rest days or easy runs with light pace.

Listen to your body—persistent soreness or fatigue can signal you need more recovery.

Sleep Quality

Sleep is when much of the muscle repair and hormone regulation for performance occurs. Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night, and establish a consistent bedtime routine.

Nutrition for Speed

Fuel your running with balanced nutrition. Carbohydrates provide energy, proteins support muscle repair, and healthy fats aid overall function.

  • Pre-run: Consume easily digestible carbs 30-60 minutes before workouts.
  • Post-run: Within 30-60 minutes, consume a mix of protein and carbs to replenish glycogen and promote recovery.

Hydration is also crucial—dehydration impairs muscle function and endurance.

Final Thoughts

Increasing your running speed is an achievable goal by focusing on technique, engaging in targeted workouts, strengthening your body, and respecting recovery. Progress may feel gradual at first, but consistency pays off.

Remember, speeding up your runs isn’t about reckless sprinting every day—it’s a smart balance of effort, form, strength, and rest. Start by integrating these steps into your training plan, listen to your body, and enjoy the thrill of seeing your pace improve steadily.

Happy running!
