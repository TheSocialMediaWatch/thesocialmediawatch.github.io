Choosing the Perfect Title for Your Fantasy Novel


When embarking on the journey of writing a fantasy novel, one of the most thrilling — and daunting — tasks is choosing the perfect title. A well-crafted title not only captures the essence of your story but also sparks readers’ curiosity, compelling them to pick up your book from a crowded shelf or click that tempting “buy” button online. But how do you go about selecting a title that stands out in a genre brimming with enchanting worlds, complex characters, and epic quests?

In this comprehensive guide, we’ll explore the art and strategy behind choosing a fantasy novel title that resonates with your story and attracts your ideal readers.

Why the Right Title Matters

Your title is your story’s first impression. It influences how potential readers perceive your book before they’ve even read the first sentence. In fantasy, where readers often seek a portal into imaginative and mysterious realms, a compelling title acts as a key opening that gateway.

Here’s why investing time and thought into your title is vital:

  • Grabs attention: Amid thousands of fantasy titles, yours must cut through the noise.
  • Sets expectations: It hints at the genre, tone, and themes.
  • Enhances discoverability: In online stores and search engines, a memorable title aids visibility.
  • Encapsulates themes: A great title reflects core story elements and symbolism.

Starting Points For Fantasy Novel Titles

Before brainstorming titles, reflect on your story’s most defining features. Ask yourself:

  • What is the central conflict or theme of the novel?
  • Does your novel have a unique mythology or magic system?
  • Are there significant characters, places, or artifacts that could inspire a title?
  • What atmosphere or tone permeates your story — dark and brooding, whimsical and light, epic and grand?

Answering these can fuel your creative thinking and prevent generic or misleading titles.

Types of Titles Common in Fantasy

Understanding different approaches authors use can help you find the right angle:

  1. Character Names
    Titles named after protagonists or antagonists (“Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone,” “Eragon”)
    Pros: Immediate focus on a character’s journey.
    Cons: Less descriptive if readers aren’t already familiar with the character.

  2. Place Names or Settings
    Naming your book after a realm, city, or landmark unique in your world (“The Hobbit,” “The Name of the Wind”)
    Pros: Grounding the story in a setting gives readers context.
    Cons: Might not convey theme or plot if obscure.

  3. Objects or Artifacts
    Titles inspired by magical items or important objects (“The Sword of Truth,” “Mistborn”)
    Pros: Evokes mystery and adventure.
    Cons: Might sound generic without a unique spin.

  4. Concepts or Themes
    Abstract concepts or central thematic vocabulary (“The Silmarillion,” “A Storm of Swords”)
    Pros: Captures the tone and depth.
    Cons: Can be vague or poetic without clear connection.

  5. Evocative Phrases
    Using compelling phrases or combinations of words that hint at story elements (“The Lies of Locke Lamora,” “The Priory of the Orange Tree”)
    Pros: Unique and mysterious, intriguing readers.
    Cons: Needs to carefully balance memorability and clarity.

Brainstorming Techniques to Generate Titles

Coming up with a perfect title often requires a mix of creativity and methodical thinking. Here are some effective approaches:

1. Free Association

Write down key story elements: character traits, settings, important objects, magical terms, emotions, or themes. Then jot down any words or phrases that come to mind related to these elements without judgment. Allow your mind to wander creatively. Once your list grows, look for interesting combinations or words that jump out.

2. Use Thesauruses and Etymology

Explore synonyms and word origins to find evocative alternatives that match your story’s mood. Fantasy often benefits from archaic or myth-sounding words which can set a tone of mysticism or antiquity.

3. Combine Words or Invent New Terms

Hyphenations, portmanteaus, or newly coined words rooted in your world-building can make your title uniquely yours. Consider how Tolkien created names steeped in his fabricated languages to add depth and authenticity.

4. Explore Symbolism

Identify symbols or metaphors in your narrative. Using such symbolic language in a title can add layers of meaning and provoke curiosity.

5. Reflect on Tone and Genre Conventions

Titles in fantasy cover a broad spectrum from high epic fantasy to urban fantasy and magical realism. Make sure your title reflects the subgenre to attract the right audience.

Crafting Titles that Sell

Once you have a list of potential titles, critically evaluate each one through these lenses:

Memorable and Catchy

Does it stick in the mind? Avoid overly long or complex titles unless they serve a purpose and are easy to recall.

Unique but Clear

Ensure it stands apart from similar titles or well-known franchises but isn’t so obscure that it confuses potential readers.

Searchable and Market-Friendly

Consider how the title will appear in search engines and online marketplaces. Avoid titles that are too generic, as they will get lost in search results. Incorporating terminology related to your story’s themes or setting can help with SEO naturally.

True to Your Story

Your title should reflect the heart of your narrative and not mislead readers about the book’s content or genre.

Testing Your Title

Before settling on a final choice, try these methods to validate your options:

  • Feedback from Beta Readers: Share your title ideas with fans of the genre or writing groups.
  • Search for Similar Titles: Look up your title candidates online to check for duplication or confusing similarity.
  • Visualize the Cover: Imagine how the title will look on your book cover design—does it complement the artwork and grab attention?

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Overly Generic Titles: “The Quest,” “Dark Magic,” or “The Dragon’s Curse” might be accurate but lack uniqueness.
  • Confusing or Vague Titles: Titles that don’t hint at what the reader can expect might scare away those browsing.
  • Overly Long Titles: Lengthy titles can be cumbersome and hard to remember.
  • Clichés: Try to steer clear of overly used fantasy tropes in titles unless you have a fresh take.

Examples of Effective Fantasy Titles and Why They Work

  1. “The Way of Kings” by Brandon Sanderson

    • Epic and grand, this title hints at both the action (the path the kings take) and the grandeur of the story.

  2. “Mistborn” by Brandon Sanderson

    • A unique invented term encapsulating a key concept in the magic system, intriguing and brandable.

  3. “The Name of the Wind” by Patrick Rothfuss

    • Poetic and mysterious, the title hints at the underlying mystery and sets a lyrical tone.

  4. “Sabriel” by Garth Nix

    • A simple character name that evokes curiosity and centers the story around a protagonist.

  5. “A Court of Thorns and Roses” by Sarah J. Maas

    • An evocative phrase combining contrasting images, hinting at danger and beauty mixed in the story’s intrigue.

Wrapping Up

Choosing the perfect title for your fantasy novel is an art that marries creativity with marketing savvy. It embodies the magic of your world, the essence of your characters, and the allure of your plot in just a few evocative words. Your title is more than a label—it’s a promise to your readers that stepping into your book will be a journey worth taking.

Take your time, experiment fearlessly, and listen to feedback. In doing so, your title will soon become the magical beacon that draws readers into the vivid realm you’ve imagined.

Happy titling!
